# Qualitative Analysis

**The Legal Question:**
The petition presents three questions:
1. Whether a district court may consider objective video evidence at the Rule 12(b)(6) stage when it blatantly contradicts the plaintiff's complaint in a § 1983 excessive-force action, given a circuit split (6th/11th vs. 10th Circuit).
2. Whether a § 1983 plaintiff can satisfy the Iqbal/Twombly plausibility standard by strategically omitting known, adverse facts (here, that the decedent fired a weapon at officers and fled toward civilians).
3. Whether *Tennessee v. Garner* alone can clearly establish a Fourth Amendment violation at the pleading stage in a factually complex shooting, contrary to the Supreme Court's repeated warnings against defining clearly established law at a high level of generality (e.g., *White v. Pauly*, *Rivas-Villegas*).

**The Governing Standard:**
Under Rule 12(b)(6), courts generally accept the complaint's allegations as true. However, the Supreme Court in *Scott v. Harris* held that at summary judgment, courts should view facts in the light depicted by a videotape if it blatantly contradicts the plaintiff's version. The 6th and 11th Circuits have extended this logic to the motion-to-dismiss stage in qualified immunity cases, given that qualified immunity is an immunity from suit (and the burdens of discovery). The 10th Circuit here declined to do so, strictly adhering to the four corners of the complaint.
Furthermore, the Supreme Court has continually reversed lower courts that rely on the generalized standards of *Garner* or *Graham* to deny qualified immunity, insisting on particularized precedent.

**Facts Driving the Outcome:**
The 10th Circuit's decision (2-1) acknowledged the plaintiff omitted the fact that the decedent shot a BB gun at the officers. By strictly applying 12(b)(6) rules, the majority ignored the video evidence and found that relying on *Garner* was sufficient to defeat qualified immunity at the pleading stage.
This directly implicates two areas the Supreme Court polices aggressively: the protection of qualified immunity from early discovery burdens, and the misuse of *Garner* to establish "clearly established law." 
The petition highlights a clean, acknowledged circuit split on the procedural question (video evidence at 12(b)(6)).
The Court signaled its interest by requesting a response from the respondent on May 15, 2026.

**Reasoning for Probability:**
While the base rate for modern discretionary cert petitions is low (~3%), this petition hits all the marks for Supreme Court intervention. It involves a structural circuit split on federal procedure (Rule 12(b)(6) and video evidence), paired with a qualified immunity denial that contravenes the Court's repeated admonitions about defining clearly established law specifically. The Court's request for a response demonstrates that the petition has already cleared the dead list. The combination of a sharp circuit split, a procedural vehicle that implicates the purpose of qualified immunity, and the 10th Circuit's reliance on *Garner* makes this a prime candidate for a plenary grant (or a summary reversal/GVR). I estimate a 45% probability of a grant, making it the most likely disposition among the non-denial outcomes, though denial remains statistically prominent. Given the strength of the vehicle and the Court's demonstrated interest, I will predict a grant.

**Stakes / Significance:**
This is a high-stakes case for civil rights litigation. Allowing video evidence at the 12(b)(6) stage would significantly alter pleading standards and the trajectory of § 1983 excessive-force lawsuits nationwide, allowing many cases to be dismissed before discovery.
