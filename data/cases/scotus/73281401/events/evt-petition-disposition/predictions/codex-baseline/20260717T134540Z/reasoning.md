## Record and legal questions

The prediction uses the July 17 snapshot, `questions-presented.txt`, and the complete 33-page `petition.txt`. The snapshot identifies a paid 2025-Term petition from a published Tenth Circuit decision, records an initial May 21 conference distribution followed by a requested response, and shows the response was submitted July 15. The provisioned document manifest contains the petition and extracted questions presented but not the brief in opposition, so I did not infer the respondent's arguments or concessions.

The petition presents three questions arising from a Rule 12(b)(6) denial of qualified immunity in an excessive-force suit: whether a court may consider central video evidence that allegedly contradicts a complaint; whether known but omitted facts can defeat plausibility under *Twombly* and *Iqbal*; and whether *Tennessee v. Garner* alone clearly establishes the governing Fourth Amendment rule on the pleaded facts. Under Supreme Court Rule 10, the strongest basis for review is a square and consequential circuit conflict. At the cert stage, four Justices must vote to grant; error correction and disputes tied to a particular record ordinarily carry much less weight.

The officers' strongest argument is the first question. The petition says the Sixth and Eleventh Circuits permit courts to consider central video at the pleading stage when it blatantly contradicts the complaint, while the Tenth Circuit refused to do so. CourtListener confirmed the cited published authorities *Bell v. City of Southfield*, 37 F.4th 362 (6th Cir. 2022), and *Johnson v. City of Atlanta*, 107 F.4th 1292 (11th Cir. 2024). The frequency of body-camera evidence and qualified immunity's protection against litigation burdens make the issue recurring. Judge Tymkovich's dissent and the denial of rehearing add useful conflict and vehicle signals.

The difficulty is that ordinary Rule 12 practice generally confines review to the complaint and incorporated materials, with Rule 12(d) providing conversion to summary judgment for outside material. The petition asks the Court to recognize a video-specific route around that framework even though the complaint did not refer to the videos. The petition's own discussion of Sixth Circuit cases reveals factual and doctrinal qualifications concerning incorporation, centrality, authenticity, and whether a recording truly discredits the pleaded account. Those qualifications give the Court room to view the asserted split as immature or case-specific.

The other questions do not cure that weakness. A plaintiff ordinarily need not plead adverse facts, making the proposed "false plausibility" rule a poor vehicle for clarifying *Twombly* and *Iqbal*. The qualified-immunity question depends on whether the Court can consider facts omitted from the complaint. On the complaint-only account accepted below, the decedent was unarmed and nonthreatening when shot; on the petition's video-based account, he had just fired what appeared to be a gun at officers and was running toward an occupied vehicle. That sharp dependence on the disputed factual universe makes the third question less clean than the petition suggests.

A June 2026 published Tenth Circuit decision, *Estate of Ward v. Lucero*, applied the blatant-contradiction doctrine to video at summary judgment and held that ambiguous recordings did not utterly discredit the plaintiff's version. It shows that video and qualified-immunity disputes continue to recur, but its different procedural posture does not independently establish the Rule 12 split this petition needs.
