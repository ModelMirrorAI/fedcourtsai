# Prediction: denied

I assign an 18% probability to any grant and predict that the petition will be denied. The case is exceptionally important and has received exceptional attention, but the record-specific barriers make it a weak vehicle for resolving the underlying question.

## Question and governing considerations

The petition asks whether Connecticut may prohibit possession of AR-15-style rifles and magazines holding more than ten rounds when millions of law-abiding Americans possess those arms for lawful purposes. Petitioners read *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* to make common lawful possession dispositive: a commonly possessed arm cannot be "dangerous and unusual," and courts cannot sustain a ban by balancing the arm's perceived dangerousness against its utility. Connecticut responds that common ownership does not end *Bruen*'s historical inquiry and that restrictions on unusually dangerous weapons fit a historical tradition of responding to new weapons and unprecedented harms.

At the certiorari stage, the practical standard is whether the case presents a recurring, important federal question or a conflict warranting review, and whether this record cleanly permits the Court to answer it. The importance is unusually high. The vehicle quality is unusually poor.

## Grant signals

The snapshot records 18 distribution entries from January 7 through June 29, 2026, interrupted initially by a reschedule and then followed by repeated conference redistributions. That is a powerful signal of sustained attention rather than a routine denial. The committed statpack places modern petitions with three or more relists at a 21.8% grant rate, compared with 2.5% for all Term 2025 petitions, 5.4% for paid Term 2025 petitions, and 5.4% for modern cert petitions from the Second Circuit.

The merits issue is also certworthy in the abstract. The petition describes disagreements over whether these firearms and magazines are "Arms," where common use belongs in the *Bruen* framework, whether ownership numerosity matters, and whether actual defensive use or suitability is required. It invokes Justice Thomas's dissent and Justice Kavanaugh's statement respecting denial in *Snope v. Brown*. Those writings indicate that at least some Justices regard the AR-15 question as important and likely to merit review soon. The issue affects laws in numerous jurisdictions and is highly salient.

## Denial and vehicle signals

The brief in opposition identifies barriers that likely dominate the choice of this vehicle:

- The appeal concerns denial of a preliminary injunction, not a final judgment.
- The Second Circuit independently held that petitioners failed the balance-of-equities and public-interest requirements for preliminary relief. The petition's question presented does not challenge those grounds, so a merits ruling may not alter the judgment.
- The lower courts did not finally resolve disputed facts about ownership, lawful purpose, actual defensive use, or suitability. Petitioners and respondents characterize the evidentiary record very differently.
- The Second Circuit assumed the weapons were presumptively protected and did not decide several methodological conflicts emphasized by the petition. That makes this case a poor vehicle for resolving those asserted splits.
- Other assault-weapon and magazine cases have proceeded on fuller records and final judgments. CourtListener metadata confirms that the Seventh Circuit issued opinions in the related *Barnett v. Raoul* litigation on July 9, 2026, strengthening the reason to await a cleaner petition. The MCP response did not provide the opinion text, so I do not rely on its merits or result.

These defects also explain why extended redistribution need not imply a grant. The Justices may be considering a statement, dissent from denial, or coordination among related petitions. The Court previously declined review in *Snope* despite the issue's importance, and the reasons for preferring a final-record case are stronger here.

## Calibration and disposition

I begin with the 21.8% historical grant rate for petitions with at least three relists rather than the much lower whole-docket base rate. I adjust upward modestly for the extraordinary number of redistributions, national significance, and explicit interest expressed in *Snope*, but downward more substantially for the interlocutory posture, unchallenged alternative grounds, unresolved facts, and availability of cleaner vehicles. The resulting probability is 18% for any grant. A plenary grant is much more plausible than a GVR if the Court acts on the grant side, because no identified intervening Supreme Court rule appears to require reconsideration; nevertheless, denial is the likeliest disposition.

I do not provide individual cert votes. Cert votes are not public, and the pre-decision materials support only broad inferences: Justice Thomas has expressed a clear preference for review, Justice Kavanaugh has signaled interest on a suitable timetable, and the remaining votes needed under the Rule of Four are uncertain, particularly for this vehicle.

The big-case score is 0.94 because a merits ruling would affect prominent firearm restrictions across many jurisdictions and define the reach of *Heller* and *Bruen* beyond handguns. That stakes assessment is separate from the substantially lower probability that this petition is granted.
