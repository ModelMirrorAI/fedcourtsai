# Prediction: denied

I assign an 8% probability to any grant and predict denial. A plenary grant or summary reversal is possible because the petition targets a recurring qualified-immunity issue, but the vehicle and issue-presentation problems make four votes unlikely.

## Question and governing standard

The officers ask whether the Tenth Circuit denied qualified immunity by relying on a circuit-specific reckless-creation theory after *Barnes v. Felix*, by defining clearly established law too generally, and by avoiding a legal interlocutory question as factual. At certiorari, the Court generally looks for an important recurring legal conflict, a clean vehicle, and a preserved question. On the merits of qualified immunity, the plaintiff must show a constitutional violation and law sufficiently particularized to put the question beyond debate; an interlocutory appellate court ordinarily must accept the facts a district court says a reasonable jury could find.

## Factors supporting review

The Court has repeatedly corrected lower courts for defining clearly established excessive-force law too generally, including the Tenth Circuit in *City of Tahlequah v. Bond*. *Barnes* expressly left open how an officer's creation of danger bears on reasonableness. The call for a response after waiver and two national law-enforcement amici are real interest signals. The Court therefore could relist the petition and summarily reverse or use it to clarify the role of pre-seizure conduct.

## Factors supporting denial

The BIO persuasively argues that the first question is not outcome-determinative. Although the panel discussed reckless creation in rejecting one appellate argument, its clearly-established-law holdings rested on separate Tenth Circuit precedents addressing taser use without warning and shooting an unarmed, nonthreatening person. The petition identifies no developed circuit split on the post-*Barnes* issue and largely disputes how the lower courts characterized a contested record.

The third question is also impaired by interlocutory-jurisdiction limits, and the panel found broader *Graham* arguments waived because the officers did not adequately present them in their opening brief. The case therefore combines disputed facts, waiver, alternative grounds, and an uncertain causal link between the proposed doctrinal clarification and the judgment. The unanimous denial of rehearing without a requested poll adds no visible lower-court conflict.

Narrow CourtListener research found no clear post-*Barnes* split. Published Fourth and Sixth Circuit decisions continued to consider the whole sequence while applying established immediate-threat and taser rules; a Fifth Circuit officer-friendly decision involved an active gunfight and materially different facts. Those decisions reinforce that outcomes remain intensely record-specific rather than reveal a clean doctrinal conflict presented here.
