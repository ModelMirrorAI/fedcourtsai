# Pitts v. Burke, No. 25-1062 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.10.**

## The case

Officers Jessica Pitts and William Lewis (Bartlesville, Oklahoma) seek review
of the Tenth Circuit's published decision in *Burke v. Pitts*, No. 24-5134,
157 F.4th 1326 (10th Cir. Nov. 4, 2025), which affirmed the denial of
qualified immunity at summary judgment in a § 1983 excessive-force suit. On
the summary-judgment record taken in respondent's favor: the officers were
called by a father to remove his adult son (Thomas Gay), who was reportedly
high but — as the father told the officers — unarmed; within roughly three
minutes Lewis tased Gay repeatedly without warning after a single command to
drop a ventriloquist doll and a picture, and Pitts shot Gay twice, fatally, as
he moved toward the bedroom door holding nothing. Rehearing en banc was denied
with no judge calling for a poll.

The petition presents three questions (from `questions-presented.txt`):
(1) whether a circuit-specific "reckless creation"/escalation theory can
supply clearly established Fourth Amendment law given the express reservation
in *Barnes v. Felix*, 145 S. Ct. 1353 (2025); (2) whether clearly established
law was defined at too high a level of generality, contra *City of Tahlequah
v. Bond*, *Kisela v. Hughes*, and *White v. Pauly*; and (3) whether the panel
evaded interlocutory jurisdiction by recasting legal issues as factual
disputes. Petitioners ask for plenary review or, "at a minimum," summary
reversal.

## Signals toward a grant

- **Call for response.** Respondent waived (Apr 3, 2026); the Court requested
  a response on Apr 20, 2026, after the petition had been distributed for the
  Apr 24 conference. A Court-initiated CFR is the single strongest docket
  signal here — it means at least one chambers took interest — and
  empirically lifts the grant odds roughly an order of magnitude above the
  paid-petition base rate.
- **Petition-stage amici.** Two national police organizations (National
  Sheriffs' Association; National Fraternal Order of Police) filed supporting
  briefs — institutional interest beyond the parties.
- **A live post-*Barnes* question.** *Barnes* (2025) expressly reserved
  whether an officer's own creation of danger factors into Fourth Amendment
  reasonableness; the Tenth Circuit's *Ceballos*/*Tenorio*/*Carr* line is on
  one side of a genuine, long-simmering disagreement among circuits. The
  petition is an early vehicle candidate for that reserved question.
- **Track record.** The Court has repeatedly summarily reversed Tenth Circuit
  qualified-immunity denials (*White v. Pauly*, *City of Tahlequah*), and the
  petition is pitched directly at that practice. Paid petition, published
  opinion, competent counsel.

## Signals toward a denial

- **Vehicle problems, forcefully briefed.** The BIO (Georgetown ICAP) argues
  the panel did *not* rest on the reckless-creation theory — it discussed that
  doctrine only in rejecting an evidentiary argument, and resolved both prongs
  on the moment-of-force analysis under *Graham*/*Garner* and particularized
  circuit precedent (*Cavanaugh*, *Lee*, *Ceballos*, *Tenorio*, *Carr*). If
  that reading holds, QP1 — the petition's best hook — is not presented.
- **Fact-bound and jurisdictionally awkward.** The petition's core grievance
  (the autopsy report showing an "anterior" thigh wound versus the father's
  testimony that Gay was moving toward the door) is an evidence-sufficiency
  dispute that *Johnson v. Jones* puts outside interlocutory review. The BIO
  also documents forfeiture below of the *Graham*-factor arguments and a
  concession at CA10 oral argument that respondent's version of the facts
  likely shows a constitutional violation.
- **No split briefed.** The petition cites no out-of-circuit decision at all —
  no circuit-split showing, which the current Court effectively requires for
  plenary review of a QI application case.
- **Facts are hostile to summary reversal.** The Court's QI summary reversals
  (*Kisela*, *Tahlequah*, *Rivas-Villegas*) involved suspects wielding or
  reaching weapons. Here, on the record the Court would have to accept, an
  unarmed man holding a doll was tased without warning and shot while trying
  to leave the room. That record reads closer to *Tolan v. Cotton* than to
  *Kisela*, and the Court has largely abandoned the per curiam QI reversal
  practice since 2021–22.
- **No dissent below.** Rehearing en banc was denied without a poll — no CA10
  judge flagged the panel's approach.

## Probability

Anchors from the committed statpack (`metrics/statpack.md`): modern
discretionary-cert grant rate ~2.5–3% per Term (Terms 2023–2025); CA10
petitions in the modern slice grant at ~5%; zero-relist petitions grant at
under 1% (this petition sits at zero relists, but its first conference —
Sept. 28, 2026, the long conference — has not yet occurred, so the relist cut
is not yet informative). The statpack has no CFR cut; external experience puts
CFR'd paid petitions at roughly 8–12× the base rate, i.e. mid-teens percent
before case-specific adjustment.

Starting from a paid, CFR'd, amici-supported petition (~15–20%), I discount
for the strong BIO vehicle arguments (QP1 arguably not presented; QP3
fact-bound), the absence of any briefed split, the absence of an en banc
dissent, and a factual record that makes both plenary reversal and — more
importantly — summary reversal unattractive. A grant would most plausibly come
as a plenary grant on the *Barnes*-reserved question if the Justices want this
vehicle despite the BIO's reading of the panel opinion; a GVR path is largely
absent (no intervening decision — the panel already applied *Barnes*).

**P(grant, incl. GVR) ≈ 0.10; predicted disposition: denied**, most likely
after the September 28, 2026 long conference, possibly after a relist or two
given the CFR and amici. If the petition is relisted more than twice this
fall, the live probability should be revised sharply upward.
