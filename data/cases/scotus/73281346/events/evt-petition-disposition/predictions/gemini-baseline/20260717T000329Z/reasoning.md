# Reasoning

## Legal Question and Standard
This case involves the denial of qualified immunity to police officers in a 4th Amendment excessive force claim. The central question is whether the Tenth Circuit erred in denying qualified immunity by relying on a circuit-specific "reckless creation" or "escalation" theory, and whether the court defined clearly established law at an impermissibly high level of generality. The Supreme Court recently and explicitly reserved judgment on the validity of the "escalation" theory in *Barnes v. Felix*, 145 S. Ct. 1353 (2025).

The case has now been distributed for the September 28, 2026 "long conference". The petition has drawn amicus support from major law enforcement organizations (National Sheriffs' Association, Fraternal Order of Police).

## Prediction and Probability
I predict a `gvr` (summary reversal or merits vacatur) or plenary grant, which both count as a grant on the binary axis, with a **65% probability**. 

The Supreme Court has a long-standing practice of summarily reversing circuit courts—particularly the Ninth and Tenth Circuits—for denying qualified immunity in excessive force cases based on generalized definitions of clearly established law or idiosyncratic circuit doctrines like "provocation" (e.g., *County of Los Angeles v. Mendez*, *City of Tahlequah*). The fact that the Tenth Circuit allegedly relied on a doctrine that the Supreme Court *expressly reserved* just a year ago in *Barnes v. Felix* makes it logically difficult to maintain that the law was "clearly established" such that every reasonable officer would have known their conduct was unlawful. If the Supreme Court itself is unsure whether the 4th Amendment encompasses an escalation theory, it cannot be clearly established law for qualified immunity purposes.

Given the Court's affirmative request for a response and the direct connection to the reserved question in *Barnes*, the likelihood of intervention is extremely high relative to the base rate for paid cert petitions. The conservative majority polices qualified immunity deviations aggressively. A summary reversal (`gvr`) or plenary grant is highly likely.
