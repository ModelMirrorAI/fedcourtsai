Discretionary certiorari review under Supreme Court Rule 10 is reserved for cases involving compelling reasons, such as circuit splits, conflicts with Supreme Court precedent, or important federal questions that have not been, but should be, settled.

## Analysis and Probability
The absence of a response from the respondent is highly predictive of the outcome. The Supreme Court almost never grants a petition for certiorari without first having a response on file. If the Court were seriously considering granting the petition, it would typically issue a Call for Response (CFR) before voting to grant. Because this case was distributed for the long conference without a response, the petition is exceedingly likely to be denied. In addition, the base rate for cert grants overall is low (historically ~2.5-3.5% for paid petitions in recent Terms), and the fact that it comes from a state intermediate appellate court on issues that appear heavily bound up in state anti-SLAPP laws further reduces the likelihood of review.
