# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.3% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. No individual-Justice votes are predicted because the record provides no sound basis for allocating votes beyond the aggregate disposition forecast.

## Question and posture

The petition arises from a Washington private tort action following the death of the petitioner's dog after emergency veterinary treatment. It alleges that the respondents later altered medical records, contacted law enforcement, removed online reviews, and sent a cease-and-desist letter threatening civil and criminal consequences. The Washington Court of Appeals affirmed summary judgment on intentional-infliction-of-emotional-distress and fraud theories, and the Washington Supreme Court denied discretionary review.

The petition presents two broad questions: whether threats intended to suppress social-media speech can be discounted despite the First Amendment and state anti-SLAPP protections, and whether a court may grant summary judgment while disregarding evidence or imposing novel burdens. The petition relies principally on the First and Fourteenth Amendments, federal and Washington summary-judgment principles, Washington's Uniform Public Expression Protection Act, and state tort law.

## Certiorari considerations

The strongest argument for review is that coercive threats against consumer speech are recurring and that the petition alleges unusually troubling post-treatment conduct. The petition also invokes the Court's occasional summary reversals for failure to apply the nonmovant-favorable summary-judgment standard.

Those points are outweighed by substantial certworthiness and vehicle defects:

- The respondents are private parties. The petition does not identify government action suppressing the petitioner's speech; it instead asks that alleged threats be considered as conduct supporting state tort liability. That makes the First Amendment framing attenuated and dependent on state-law elements.
- Washington's anti-SLAPP statute is state law and, on the petition's account, is triggered by a filed claim against expression rather than a pre-litigation demand. The court below did not resolve a clean federal question about that statute.
- The summary-judgment question is overwhelmingly record-bound. The petition's principal contention is that an unpublished intermediate state appellate decision misread a 635-page record and Washington tort law. Supreme Court review ordinarily does not correct factual error or enforce Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56 in state-court litigation.
- The petition asserts a divide between state and federal courts but does not establish a developed conflict among federal circuits or state courts on either question presented. Its discussion of other jurisdictions concerns varied tort treatments of post-treatment conduct, not a square conflict on a controlling federal rule.
- The snapshot shows no merits response, CVSG, or relist. The case was distributed for its first conference months later, so the classic positive selection signals are absent at this forecast date.

The petition appears final enough for review, but an adequate state-law basis for the judgment, the private-party posture, and the fact-intensive record make this an exceptionally poor vehicle for resolving either abstract question.
