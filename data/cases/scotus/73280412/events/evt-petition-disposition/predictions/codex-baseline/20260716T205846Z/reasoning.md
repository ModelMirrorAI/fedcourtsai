# Prediction: denial

I assign a 36% probability to any grant-side disposition, including a GVR, and predict denial as the single most likely disposition.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether an Equal Protection racial-gerrymandering claim becomes moot when a different court replaces the challenged district through a Voting Rights Act remedy that allegedly continues or intensifies the plaintiff's race-based classification, while review of that remedy remains ongoing.

The certiorari inquiry is whether the petition presents an important federal question, a conflict among lower courts, or a departure from Supreme Court precedent warranting review. Four Justices are needed to grant. A GVR is also possible when an intervening Supreme Court decision may materially affect the judgment below without requiring plenary review.

## Case-specific signals

The January 23, 2026 petition and extracted questions presented were available in full. They report that Garcia challenged Washington Legislative District 15 as a racial gerrymander; a single judge in the parallel *Soto Palmer* VRA case invalidated that district and ordered a replacement; the three-judge panel then dismissed Garcia's claim as moot; this Court previously vacated and remanded for a fresh judgment appealable to the Ninth Circuit; and the Ninth Circuit affirmed in an unpublished three-page memorandum. The petition argues that the replacement district continued the same racial sorting and that reversal in the parallel litigation could restore the original map.

The petition has meaningful merits support. *North Carolina v. Covington*, 585 U.S. 969 (2018), states that racial-gerrymandering claims arise from the plaintiffs' alleged segregation rather than line-drawing as such and remain live when a remedial district continues that segregation. *Moore v. Harper*, 600 U.S. 1 (2023), supports continued adversity when appellate review could cause a challenged map to take effect again. The petition also points to Judge VanDyke's detailed dissent and the Ninth Circuit's failure to discuss *Moore*.

The docket supplies an unusually positive screening signal: after Washington waived a response, the Court rescheduled the petition and requested a response. The matter is a paid petition from the Ninth Circuit and is now fully briefed for the September 28, 2026 conference. Those facts raise the forecast materially above the ordinary paid-petition base rate.

The most important additional context is *Louisiana v. Callais*, decided April 29, 2026, before the provisioned snapshot. *Callais* held 6-3 that a map drawn to satisfy an asserted Section 2 obligation was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander because Section 2 did not require it. That decision does not itself decide mootness, but it validates the petition's broader concern that a VRA remedy can perpetuate rather than cure an Equal Protection injury. It also increases the chance of a GVR in this case or in the related *Soto Palmer* litigation, which could undermine the premise of the mootness ruling.

## Reasons denial remains more likely

The petition identifies no developed circuit split. The decision below is unpublished and highly posture-bound, and the case presents vehicle complications: the replacement map was drawn by a different court, Garcia's original suit challenged the commission's map, and the Ninth Circuit understood him not to have specifically established that the new district was a continuation of the old one. *Covington* involved concrete district-level findings that remedial districts continued the prior racial gerrymanders, making its application here contestable. *Callais* concerns the merits of Section 2 and strict scrutiny, not Article III mootness, so a lower court could reach the same mootness result after reconsideration. The Court may also prefer to address any consequences through the direct challenge to the *Soto Palmer* judgment rather than grant this derivative petition.

No relist or CVSG appears in the snapshot. The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions, 3.0% for modern Ninth Circuit cert petitions overall, and only 0.8% for petitions with no relist. The response request and intervening *Callais* decision justify a large upward adjustment, but not enough to make a grant or GVR more likely than denial.
