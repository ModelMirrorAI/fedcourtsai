# Prediction: denial, with a substantial grant possibility

I estimate a 24% probability of a grant (including any grant limited to one question) and therefore predict denial as the most likely disposition. If the Court takes the case, a plenary grant is much more likely than a GVR because no intervening controlling decision supplies a rule for remand.

## Legal questions and governing standard

The petition presents two questions under the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA). First, it asks when a land-use restriction imposes a “substantial burden” on religious exercise under 42 U.S.C. § 2000cc(a), particularly when a religious institution could use another location or knew of the restriction before acquiring or seeking to use the land. Second, it asks how RLUIPA's Equal Terms Provision, 42 U.S.C. § 2000cc(b)(1), applies when an ordinance expressly subjects places of worship to an arterial-street condition that does not apply to several secular assemblies and institutions.

At the merits stage, the Substantial Burden Provision subjects a qualifying burden to strict scrutiny, while the Equal Terms Provision forbids less-equal treatment of a religious assembly or institution. At the cert stage, the central considerations are the acknowledged disagreement among appellate courts, the importance and recurrence of the statutory questions, the suitability of this record for resolving them, and whether at least four Justices want to address them now.

## Record-specific signals

The July 16 snapshot and the complete, untruncated petition show several unusually strong grant signals:

- The Kentucky Supreme Court adopted the Sixth Circuit's multi-factor substantial-burden framework and treated a smaller shrine elsewhere and petitioner's foreknowledge of the ordinance as defeating a substantial burden. The petition plausibly contrasts that approach with more protective standards in other circuits and with *Holt v. Hobbs*' rejection of alternative religious exercise as an answer to a RLUIPA burden.
- The ordinance, as described in the petition, expressly permits several secular uses without the arterial-street condition imposed on churches. The lower court's conclusion that equal terms are satisfied because some secular institutions are also restricted tees up the recurring disagreement over the required comparator and burden-shifting framework.
- The questions are statutory, recurring, and nationally consequential, and the petition says the Supreme Court has never construed either land-use provision. The docket shows a paid petition, experienced Supreme Court counsel, a response requested after respondents initially waived, and extensive amicus support from a cross-faith group of religious organizations, religious-liberty groups, and twenty states. Those signals materially distinguish the case from an ordinary petition.

The grant case nevertheless has important weaknesses:

- The Court has repeatedly declined opportunities involving the same general RLUIPA disputes. The petition itself identifies recent denials in *Canaan Christian Church*, *New Harvest Christian Fellowship*, and *Spirit of Aloha Temple*, among others. That history is the strongest reason not to turn the doctrinal split into a near-even forecast.
- The posture is unusual: private neighbors invoked local zoning rules, and the religious institution raised RLUIPA as a defense in state-court litigation. Although the Kentucky Supreme Court held the defense properly presented, the Court could prefer a conventional claim against a governmental zoning authority.
- The two questions implicate different, already fragmented lines of circuit doctrine. The Court may view the case as overbroad, fact-bound, or a less clean vehicle than the petition claims. The record also permits arguments about the size and location of the proposed grotto and the comparability of the exempt secular uses.
- No merits response was provisioned because the response is not due until August 14. The petition's characterization of the vehicle and conflicts therefore has not yet been tested adversarially.

## Probability calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term. The docket has no completed post-response conference and thus no meaningful relist signal yet; treating its current zero relists like a conference-ready zero-relist petition would be misleading. I move well above the paid-petition baseline for the requested response, exceptional amicus participation, clean statutory text issue, acknowledged lower-court conflicts, and the Court's strong contemporary interest in religious-exercise protections. I stop at 24% because the repeated denials of related RLUIPA petitions, the defensive state-court posture, and the absence of the forthcoming opposition leave denial clearly more likely than grant.

I do not predict individual cert votes. The conference vote is private, the response is outstanding, and the record does not support justice-by-justice probabilities. The big-case score of 0.72 reflects the potential nationwide effect on religious institutions and municipal zoning, not the likelihood of a grant.
