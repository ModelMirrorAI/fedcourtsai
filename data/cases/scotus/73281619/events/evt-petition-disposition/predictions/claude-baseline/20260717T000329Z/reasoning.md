# Missionaries of Saint John the Baptist, Inc. v. Frederic, No. 25-1131 — cert prediction

## The case

Petitioner, a Catholic nonprofit in Park Hills, Kentucky, sought to build a
16-by-39-foot grotto shrine to the Virgin Mary on a lot adjacent to its
grandfathered church. The local Board of Adjustment granted a conditional-use
permit and variances; neighbors (the Frederics) sued, and the Kentucky Supreme
Court ultimately held the permit unlawful under the zoning ordinance's
arterial-street requirement and rejected petitioner's RLUIPA defense
(727 S.W.3d 400, Dec. 18, 2025). Two questions presented:

1. Whether denying a religious institution permission to build a religious
   structure on its own property is a "substantial burden" under RLUIPA's
   Substantial Burden Provision, 42 U.S.C. § 2000cc(a).
2. Whether a zoning code that expressly restricts religious assemblies in ways
   it does not restrict nonreligious assemblies (schools, parks, libraries,
   golf courses, country clubs are exempt from the arterial-street rule)
   violates the Equal Terms Provision, § 2000cc(b)(1).

The Kentucky Supreme Court adopted the Sixth Circuit's *Livingston* factors and
held there was no substantial burden because petitioner could build a smaller
shrine elsewhere and knew of the ordinance beforehand ("self-imposed" burden),
and no equal-terms violation because *some* secular uses (universities, police
stations) are also subject to the arterial-street rule.

## Grant-side signals (unusually strong)

- **Call for response after waiver.** Respondents waived (Apr 27, 2026); the
  case was distributed for the May 28 conference; on May 14 the Court
  **requested a response**. A CFR means at least one chamber is seriously
  engaged — it is the classic first step of the grant funnel and by itself
  lifts a paid petition well above the ~2.5–3% modern grant base rate (statpack
  Term rows), empirically into the high single digits.
- **Seventeen cert-stage amicus briefs**, including **20 states** (Kentucky
  et al.), Notre Dame, Yale, and Harvard religious-liberty clinics, ACLJ,
  Manhattan Institute, and an ecumenical coalition (Muslim Public Affairs
  Council, Sikh Coalition, Coalition for Jewish Values, American Hindu Jewish
  Congress, evangelical and Catholic organizations). This volume of cert-stage
  support is top-percentile and historically correlates with sharply elevated
  grant rates.
- **Elite counsel on both sides.** John Bash (Quinn Emanuel) with First Liberty
  Institute for petitioner — the same shop that built *Groff v. DeJoy* and
  *Kennedy v. Bremerton* as vehicles; respondents, after waiving, retained
  Skadden's Supreme Court practice (Parker Rider-Longmaid) and took two BIO
  extensions (now due Aug. 14, 2026) — behavior consistent with a perceived
  real grant threat.
- **A genuine, long-acknowledged split, never resolved.** The Court has never
  construed either land-use provision in RLUIPA's 25 years. The petition
  documents a multi-way split on the substantial-burden standard (Sixth/Fourth
  circuits' restrictive multi-factor tests vs. Ninth/Second/Seventh's broader
  readings, plus state high courts) and the well-known equal-terms
  methodological split (Eleventh's textual approach vs. Third's "regulatory
  purpose" vs. Seventh's "accepted zoning criteria"). Judge Thapar's *Tree of
  Life* dissent (2018) called for the Court's intervention.
- **Sympathetic, clean-looking facts.** A church barred from building a small
  shrine on its own land while golf courses and country clubs are exempt from
  the same requirement is a compelling narrative for this Court's
  religious-liberty docket, and *Holt v. Hobbs* supplies ready-made reasoning
  that the Kentucky court arguably defied ("alternative means" and
  "self-imposed burden" rationales that *Holt* rejected in the prison context).
- **Final judgment from a state court of last resort** — no interlocutory
  posture problem, and the Kentucky Supreme Court expressly held the RLUIPA
  defense "properly before us," so the federal question is preserved.
- The Court remains actively engaged with RLUIPA: *Landor v. Louisiana Dept.
  of Corrections* (decided June 23, 2026) addressed RLUIPA damages this Term
  (different provision; legitimate forward context, not this case's outcome).

## Deny-side considerations

- **This QP has been a graveyard.** The Court has denied cert on these exact
  questions repeatedly for two decades — the petition's own table of
  authorities lists denials in *Midrash Sephardi* (2005), *Greater Bible Way*
  (2008), *Lighthouse* (2008), *Rocky Mountain* (2011), *Foursquare Gospel*
  (2011), *Chabad Lubavitch* (2015), *Livingston* (2018), *Tree of Life*
  (2019), *Canaan Christian* and *New Harvest* (2023), and *Spirit of Aloha*
  (2025, just months before this petition). Repeated past denials on a
  well-known split are evidence that four votes have not materialized, even
  with occasional separate writings. *Apache Stronghold* (2025) shows this
  Court can relist a high-profile religious land case many times and still
  deny.
- **Vehicle wrinkles Skadden will press.** The RLUIPA issue arises as a
  *defense* in litigation between the church and private neighbors — the
  government entity (the Board) actually *granted* the permit and the state
  courts reversed on state-law grounds. Respondents will argue the case is a
  poor vehicle: whether a court judgment enforcing an ordinance in
  neighbor-initiated litigation is government "imposition or implementation"
  of a land-use regulation, antecedent state-law questions (the dissent below
  was on state-law grounds), and the undeveloped record on the ordinance's
  remaining RLUIPA elements (the Kentucky court never reached compelling
  interest / least restrictive means).
- The two BIO extensions push resolution to the long conference (late
  September 2026) at the earliest, with relists likely either way — a long
  horizon over which denial remains the modal outcome for any single petition.

## Weighing

Base rate for a paid modern petition is ~2.5–3% (statpack, Terms 2023–2025).
Conditioning moves this cell far off the base rate: a CFR issued sua sponte
after waiver (~8–10x lift), top-decile amicus support including a 20-state
coalition, elite repeat-player counsel who chose this vehicle deliberately, a
square and acknowledged split the Court has never resolved, and a Court whose
revealed preference in religious-liberty cases is strong. Petitions carrying
this full cluster of signals historically grant somewhere in the 25–50% range.
Against that: the Court's long revealed reluctance on precisely these QPs and
real (if answerable) vehicle objections from a capable BIO shop.

I put **P(grant, including any grant-in-part) at 0.38**. Denial is still the
single likeliest disposition (~0.55–0.60, likely after relists and possibly
with a dissent from or statement respecting denial), so the point prediction
is **denied** with `granted = 0`. A GVR is implausible (no intervening
decision on these provisions — *Landor* is off-point), and dismissal/withdrawal
signals are absent. If the Court grants, a limited grant on QP1 alone is
plausible but I fold that into the grant-side probability.

## Big-case score

0.65 — a grant would yield the first-ever Supreme Court construction of
RLUIPA's land-use provisions, restructuring church-zoning litigation
nationwide (the 20-state amicus coalition and ecumenical breadth of support
attest to the stakes). Decided, it would be a marquee religious-liberty case
of its Term, though below blockbuster constitutional fare.
