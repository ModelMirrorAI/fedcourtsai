# Prediction: denied

I assign a 1.8% probability to any grant and predict denial. This includes the possibility of a plenary grant or GVR, although a plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR because *Barnes v. Felix* preceded, rather than postdated, the Fifth Circuit judgment.

## Questions and governing standards

The petition presents four questions arising from a federal officer's convictions under 18 U.S.C. §§ 242 and 1519: whether the Fifth Circuit applied an impermissibly time-confined Fourth Amendment excessive-force analysis after *Barnes*; what subjective culpability § 242 willfulness requires under *Screws*; whether § 242 bodily injury must exceed a de minimis threshold; and whether the evidence showed contemplation of a federal investigation under § 1519.

Certiorari ordinarily requires a square conflict, an important unsettled federal question, or a serious departure from Supreme Court precedent. Even where a legal issue is important, the Court generally disfavors a fact-bound sufficiency dispute, a question not cleanly passed upon below, or a vehicle in which alternative grounds and disputed facts complicate resolution.

## Record-specific signals

The complete petition and its appended opinion materially favor denial:

- The Fifth Circuit reviewed only evidentiary sufficiency after a bench trial, applying the highly deferential rational-factfinder standard. Its published, unanimous opinion rested on video evidence and testimony from a supervisor, an eyewitness officer, an expert, and an investigator. That posture makes the case a poor vehicle for announcing refined constitutional or mens-rea rules.
- The *Barnes* theory is not a clean conflict. Although the opinion did not cite *Barnes*, it discussed events before the force, including resistance, the parties' verbal exchanges, the alleged threat, Delgado's decision to open a secure barrier, and his failure to await help or check for weapons. The petition's contention is thus substantially about how the panel weighed the totality, not an express revival of a temporal cutoff.
- The willfulness question is potentially important, but the court recited the *Screws* open-defiance-or-reckless-disregard standard and treated training violations, angry demeanor, and concealment as circumstantial evidence. The petition describes differing emphases among circuits, yet does not show a clean outcome-determinative conflict on materially identical facts.
- The bodily-injury argument is entangled with the underlying excessiveness determination and the record contains more than abstract or technical injury: dizziness, headache, ringing in the ears, confusion, pain, bleeding, and a nose laceration. The opinion did not squarely select among the three asserted statutory tests, weakening preservation and vehicle quality.
- The § 1519 question is derivative in the petition's own presentation. The panel also relied on immediate false statements, a later false written account contradicted by video, and training to document excessive force, providing an independent factual basis for the intent inference.
- The United States waived a response, and no brief in opposition was provisioned. The case was distributed for its first conference with no relist or CVSG. A Justice can request a response, but the docket presently lacks the procedural signals that commonly precede a grant.
