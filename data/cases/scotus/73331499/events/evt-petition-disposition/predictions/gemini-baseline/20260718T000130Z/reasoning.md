# Reasoning for Petition Disposition Prediction

## Case Facts and Posture
This is a paid petition for certiorari arising from a criminal conviction in Pennsylvania state court. The petitioner seeks review of a July 24, 2025 decision of the Superior Court of Pennsylvania (an intermediate appellate court). The Pennsylvania Supreme Court denied discretionary review on February 4, 2026. 

The petition raises two questions under the Fourth Amendment:
1. Whether a John Doe DNA Complaint and Warrant that includes only genetic markers common to all humans, without a specific DNA profile, violates the specificity and particularity requirements.
2. Whether the warrantless search, seizure, and extraction of DNA from trash items to create a DNA profile (often as part of investigative genetic genealogy) violates the Fourth Amendment.

The petition was docketed on May 12, 2026. The respondent (Pennsylvania) did not file a Brief in Opposition by the June 11, 2026 deadline. Consequently, the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 "long conference" without a response.

## Legal and Practical Context
The substantive issue—the Fourth Amendment implications of extracting DNA from abandoned items to match against genetic genealogy databases—is highly salient. Law enforcement's increasing use of genetic genealogy (famous for solving the Golden State Killer and Idaho murders cases) has spurred significant litigation in state courts and federal circuits over the past few years. Recent retrieval indicates courts are divided on the nuances, though many state high courts and federal circuits have upheld the practice of analyzing abandoned DNA.

However, the procedural posture and case-specific signals overwhelmingly dictate the outcome here:
1. **Lack of Response:** The respondent did not file a Brief in Opposition. The Supreme Court almost never grants a petition without either receiving a response or calling for one (CFR). While the Court could issue a CFR after the September conference, predicting a terminal grant based on this docket snapshot would buck severe base rates. 
2. **Vehicle Problems:** The petition comes from a state intermediate appellate court. Although the Pennsylvania Supreme Court denied allocatur (exhausting state remedies), the U.S. Supreme Court strongly prefers cases where the state's highest court has fully analyzed the federal constitutional issue. 
3. **Base Rates:** The base rate for paid cert petitions is low (roughly 1-5% depending on circuit/state origin). Without early markers of interest (like an ordered response, relists, or a strong cert-stage amicus presence), the probability of a grant collapses toward zero.

## Conclusion
Despite the novel and highly salient Fourth Amendment questions presented regarding genetic privacy and DNA profiling, the absence of a Brief in Opposition and the posture of the case as an appeal from a state intermediate appellate court make it an exceedingly poor vehicle for certiorari. The overwhelming statistical likelihood is that the petition will simply be denied at the September 28 conference. 

**Probability of Grant:** 2% (accounting for the slim chance of a Call for Response leading to a grant).
**Predicted Disposition:** Denied.
