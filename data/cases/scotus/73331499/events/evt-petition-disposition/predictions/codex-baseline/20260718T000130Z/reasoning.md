# Prediction: denial

I assign a 2.5% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. A plenary grant is the principal conceivable grant outcome; the record supplies no obvious intervening decision or mootness development that would make a GVR likely.

## Questions and governing considerations

The petition presents two Fourth Amendment questions. First, it challenges a 2000 John Doe complaint and arrest warrant that identified genetic loci common to all people but did not set out the unknown suspect's allele profile or its statistical rarity. Second, it argues that police needed a warrant not merely to collect discarded objects, but to extract DNA and construct profiles from petitioner's discarded chew-spit bottles and his minor son's used tableware.

On the merits, particularity requires a warrant to identify its object with enough specificity to constrain execution. The petition contrasts decisions invalidating generic DNA descriptions with decisions sustaining John Doe warrants that supplied an actual profile and rarity statistics. The profiling question sits at the intersection of the abandonment rule for exposed trash, the treatment of biological sampling and analysis as searches, and the Court's recognition that technologically extracted information can implicate privacy interests beyond the physical object collected. The certiorari inquiry, however, is not simply whether those arguments are substantial: the Court ordinarily looks for a square and mature conflict, an important recurring federal question, and a clean vehicle requiring resolution.

## Record signals

The provisioned petition describes a 1995 sexual assault, a DNA-based complaint filed in 2000, renewed investigative-genetic-genealogy work in 2020, surreptitious collection of the son's tableware in February 2021, collection of petitioner's residential trash in June 2021, and a later DNA match. After suppression litigation and a stipulated bench trial, petitioner was convicted of rape and aggravated assault. The Pennsylvania Superior Court affirmed in a published decision, and the Pennsylvania Supreme Court denied discretionary review.

The subject is important and recurrent as genetic genealogy and DNA analysis become more capable. The petition also identifies meaningful doctrinal disagreement about what a John Doe DNA warrant must contain and about whether laboratory profiling is a distinct search. Those features support a probability above the lowest tier of paid criminal petitions.

The vehicle and docket signals are substantially weaker:

- The petition combines two analytically distinct questions, and the second introduces separate issues involving petitioner's own trash, his son's tableware, and petitioner's standing to assert interests associated with his son.
- Existing abandonment doctrine gives the Commonwealth a strong response concerning the physical collection of the trash, while the petition must persuade the Court to separate that collection from later forensic analysis.
- The claimed conflicts are not cleanly framed. The cited John Doe cases turn on materially different warrant contents, and the petition itself describes the DNA-profiling authorities as divided across differing custodial and collection settings.
- The questions-presented text is poorly finished: the second question ends with “from,” which reinforces that this is not an especially polished vehicle for a major constitutional rule.
- The snapshot shows a paid petition filed May 5, 2026 and distributed June 24 for the September 28 conference. No response, amicus filing, CVSG, or relist is shown, and no brief in opposition was provisioned. The absence of those positive selection signals as of the snapshot weighs heavily against a grant.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, versus 2.49% across paid and IFP filings combined. It also reports only a 0.8% grant rate among petitions with no relist and a 3.0% rate where there has been no CVSG, although those cuts are descriptive and the case has not yet reached its first scheduled conference. I start from the paid-petition rate, add modest weight for the novelty and importance of genetic privacy, and subtract more for the lack of institutional support or a response, the mixed questions, and the vehicle complications. That yields 2.5% rather than the paid-petition baseline.

No individual votes are predicted because certiorari votes ordinarily are not disclosed and the present record offers no justice-specific signal. The 0.62 big-case score reflects potentially broad stakes for DNA privacy and investigative genealogy if the Court reached the merits; it is deliberately separate from the much lower grant probability.
