# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.6% probability to any grant of relief and predict denial of the petition for an extraordinary writ of mandamus.

## Question and posture

This is not an ordinary petition for certiorari. More than 120 property owners ask the Supreme Court to direct the Southern District of Texas to recall and vacate an order remanding their consolidated inverse-condemnation actions to Texas state courts. The owners contend that the remand defies the mandate in *DeVillier v. Texas*, 601 U.S. 285 (2024), which said they should be permitted to pursue their federal Takings Clause rights through the cause of action available under Texas law.

The provisioned snapshot, dated July 17, 2026, records a paid Rule 20 petition filed April 24, 2026 and distribution for the September 28, 2026 conference. It contains no relist, CVSG, or requested-response signal beyond the ordinary response deadline. No filed-document text was provisioned, so I consulted the petition PDF directly through the Supreme Court link in the snapshot.

## Governing standard

Supreme Court Rule 20 makes an extraordinary writ discretionary and requires relief to aid the Court's appellate jurisdiction, exceptional circumstances, and the absence of adequate relief elsewhere. Mandamus can enforce the Court's own mandate when a lower court obstructs it, but it is not a substitute for ordinary review and is granted only in exceptionally clear cases. The petition has a substantial argument on the no-alternative-remedy element because 28 U.S.C. § 1447(d) generally bars review of remand orders. The decisive weakness is instead whether the district court clearly disobeyed the 2024 mandate.

## Case-specific assessment

The 2024 unanimous opinion supports the owners only up to a point. It held that Texas law supplied a vehicle through which they could pursue federal Takings Clause rights, and it remanded for proceedings consistent with that conclusion. But the opinion did not expressly guarantee a federal forum. Its reasoning avoided deciding whether the Takings Clause itself supplies a direct cause of action and emphasized the availability of a Texas-law cause of action. Pursuing that cause of action in the state courts where the owners originally filed remains textually consistent with being “permitted to pursue” the claims.

The intervening procedure makes a clear mandate violation still harder to show. After initially directing state-court remand, the Fifth Circuit withdrew that instruction and remanded to the district court for proceedings consistent with the Supreme Court's decision, expressly stating that withdrawal did not reject the validity of returning the litigation to state court. The district court then considered briefing and adopted the recommendation to remand. That record looks less like defiance of an unmistakable command than resolution of an issue the Supreme Court left open. The petition's argument that federal jurisdiction was necessarily settled relies heavily on implications from the prior disposition and oral-argument assurances, while the operative mandate itself did not specify the forum.

The owners do have favorable equities: Texas removed the cases, represented that the state-law vehicle could be used in federal court, and § 1447(d) may prevent immediate appellate correction. Precedents recognize mandamus as a means to protect a Supreme Court mandate. Those considerations keep the probability above a purely nominal value. They do not overcome the demanding Rule 20 standard, the ambiguity in the mandate, and the extraordinary rarity of original mandamus relief.

## Calibration and significance

The committed statpack's 2025 Term estimate gives ordinary modern cert petitions a 2.5% grant rate, and unrelisted petitions without a CVSG have still lower rates. Those cert figures are only contextual here: an original mandamus petition faces a materially more demanding and rarer route. I therefore place the probability of any grant at 0.6% and expect a summary denial without recorded individual votes.

I score the case's significance at 0.52. A ruling could matter for federal-question jurisdiction, mandate enforcement, removal tactics, and more than 120 owners' constitutional compensation claims, but the requested decision is procedurally narrow and could turn on the wording of a single prior mandate.
