## Posture behind the petition (from the underlying district-court docket)

Petitioners are the property owners from *Devillier v. Texas*, 601 U.S. 285
(2024), where a unanimous Court held their Fifth Amendment takings claims
against Texas (for flooding caused by the I-10 median barrier) could proceed
on remand under Texas's state-law inverse-condemnation cause of action.
Counsel of record here (Daniel Charest, Burns Charest LLP) is plaintiffs'
counsel in the underlying case, S.D. Tex. No. 3:20-cv-00223.

The remand history — retrieved from the district-court docket
(CourtListener docket 17305392) — explains the mandamus:

- Dec. 5, 2024: the Fifth Circuit (No. 21-40750) remanded to the district
  court "for further proceedings consistent with the decisions of the
  Supreme Court."
- Dec. 13, 2024: **Texas moved to remand the case to state court** — the
  same case Texas itself had removed to federal court in 2020.
- Jan. 5, 2026: Magistrate Judge Edison issued a Memorandum and
  Recommendation on the remand motion; plaintiffs objected (Jan. 20, 2026).
- **Apr. 9, 2026: Judge Brown adopted the M&R**, and the docket then shows
  documents transmitted out (delivery confirmation of case documents,
  Apr. 10–14, 2026) — i.e., the case was remanded to Texas state court.
- Apr. 24, 2026: this mandamus petition was filed in the Supreme Court,
  two weeks after that order.

So the petition evidently asks the Supreme Court to enforce its own
*Devillier* mandate: petitioners' theory must be that Texas — having removed
the case, litigated it through a unanimous Supreme Court loss, and then
obtained a remand to its own courts — is evading the mandate, and that
mandamus from this Court is the only avenue because 28 U.S.C. § 1447(d)
bars review of a subject-matter-jurisdiction remand order "on appeal or
otherwise" (no CA5 mandamus docket appears in RECAP, consistent with
petitioners going straight to the Court under its All Writs Act /
aid-of-jurisdiction power).

## Governing standard and why denial is overwhelmingly likely

Rule 20 requires "exceptional circumstances warrant[ing] the exercise of
the Court's discretionary powers" and that "adequate relief cannot be
obtained in any other form or from any other court." The modern Court
receives dozens of extraordinary-writ petitions each Term and grants
essentially none — original writs of mandamus have not issued in decades.
This floor is far below even the ~2–3% modern cert grant rate in the
committed statpack (Terms 2023–2025: 2.5–3.3%), and the relist-driven
uplift buckets do not apply: the petition sits un-relisted, headed to the
long conference, apparently without a response.

Substantively, the "defiance of mandate" framing is weaker than it first
appears. *Devillier* held that the plaintiffs' takings claims could proceed
**under Texas's state-law inverse-condemnation cause of action**; the Court
expressly declined to decide whether the Fifth Amendment is self-executing.
A federal district court concluding that, so reframed, the case no longer
belongs in federal court is at least a colorable application of — not
obvious defiance of — the Court's decision, and Texas state courts
routinely adjudicate inverse-condemnation claims. The equities (Texas's
removal-then-remand two-step, six years of litigation without a merits
hearing) are sympathetic and could draw a statement respecting denial from
one or more Justices, but sympathy is not the Rule 20 standard when a state
forum stands open to hear exactly the claim the Court preserved.

Residual grant probability reflects: (a) the Court's institutional
sensitivity to perceived gamesmanship around its own recent, unanimous
mandate in this very case; (b) § 1447(d) making this Court's extraordinary
writ arguably the *only* review path, which is precisely Rule 20's
"no other court" element; and (c) experienced counsel and a paid petition.
Those keep this above the pure mandamus floor but nowhere near cert-pool
territory.
