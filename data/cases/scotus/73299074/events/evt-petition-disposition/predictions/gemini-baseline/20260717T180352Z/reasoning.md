### Legal Question
The petition asks the Supreme Court to issue a writ of mandamus compelling the lower courts to remand the case to Texas state court following the Supreme Court's 2024 decision in *DeVillier v. Texas* (601 U.S. 285). The central issue is whether the Fifth Circuit and the federal district court failed to execute the Supreme Court's mandate by retaining jurisdiction over the case rather than returning it to the state court where it was originally filed.

### Governing Standard
A writ of mandamus is an extraordinary remedy. To obtain it, a petitioner must show:
1. No other adequate means exist to attain the desired relief (such as a regular appeal).
2. The right to the issuance of the writ is "clear and indisputable."
3. The issuing court, in its discretion, is satisfied that the writ is appropriate under the circumstances.
The Supreme Court rarely issues mandamus to correct a lower court's procedural or jurisdictional rulings unless there is an egregious and unambiguous defiance of a mandate.

### Reasoning and Probability
The probability of a grant is assessed at **1%**. 

First, the base rate for mandamus petitions at the Supreme Court is near zero—substantially lower than the already-low ~2.5% grant rate for standard discretionary certiorari petitions. 

Second, the substantive legal bar for mandamus is exceptionally high. While the 2024 *DeVillier* decision clarified that the petitioners could pursue their Takings Clause claims via a Texas state law cause of action, it did not explicitly address whether federal subject-matter jurisdiction (such as under the *Grable* doctrine or supplemental jurisdiction) definitively ceased to exist. Because the jurisdictional question on remand is legally complex and subject to debate, the petitioners' right to an immediate remand to state court is likely not "clear and indisputable."

Finally, disagreements over removal jurisdiction and remand orders are typically resolved through the standard appellate process after a final judgment, rather than through an extraordinary writ from the Supreme Court. The Court is highly unlikely to intervene in the procedural mechanics of the lower courts at this stage, resulting in a predicted denial.
