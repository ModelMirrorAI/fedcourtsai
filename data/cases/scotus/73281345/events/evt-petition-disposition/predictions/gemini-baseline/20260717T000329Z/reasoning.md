# Prediction for scotus/73281345: evt-petition-disposition

## Legal Question and Context
The petition asks whether the Fifth Circuit's categorical approach to treating cell phones as instrumentalities of interstate commerce under the Commerce Clause (following *Lopez*) is constitutional, contrasting it with the Tenth Circuit's requirement (in *Chavarria*) of a case-by-case showing of an actual effect on interstate commerce. This presents a direct, cleanly framed circuit split on the constitutional limits of federal regulatory power over modern technology.

## Factual Record and Procedural Posture
The petitioner, Holly Ann Elkins, was convicted in the Fifth Circuit. However, on April 13, 2026, the Court explicitly requested a response. The SG subsequently filed the Brief in Opposition on July 13, 2026, after multiple extensions. The case is now fully briefed and pending distribution for a conference (likely the Long Conference in late September/early October 2026).

## Reasoning
1. **Base Rates:** The overall grant rate for modern paid discretionary-cert petitions is roughly 2.5% to 3.0%. 
2. **The "Response Requested" Signal:** The SG's initial waiver indicates the government did not view the petition as a serious threat for review. However, the Court's subsequent order requesting a response is a highly salient procedural signal. It means at least one Justice (and their clerks) found the petition sufficiently interesting, likely due to the cleanly framed circuit split on a fundamental constitutional issue, to warrant full adversarial briefing. Historically, a requested response elevates the probability of a grant by an order of magnitude (often to the 15-25% range).
3. **The Merits of the Split:** A direct conflict between the Fifth and Tenth Circuits on the application of *Lopez* to cell phones is exactly the type of question the Supreme Court resolves under Rule 10.
4. **Conclusion:** Despite the strong procedural signal and the substantive circuit split, the overwhelming majority of petitions are still denied. The Court may find vehicle problems in the newly filed BIO, or it may simply choose not to intervene at this time. Therefore, the *most likely* disposition remains a denial, but with a significantly elevated probability of a grant compared to the baseline.

I predict a denial, but assign a 25% probability of a grant to reflect the strong positive signal from the Court's request for a response.
