## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether a county may be liable under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 when it contracts with a private company to provide jail healthcare, either because the healthcare duty is nondelegable or because the contractor acquired final policymaking authority. Under *Monell*, a municipality is not vicariously liable merely because its employee or agent committed a constitutional tort. Municipal liability instead requires an injury caused by municipal policy or custom. *Pembaur*, *Praprotnik*, and *Jett* make final policymaking authority a legal, function-specific inquiry informed by state law; discretion alone is insufficient, but an authorized policymaker may delegate final policymaking authority.

## Record and certworthiness

The snapshot dated July 16, 2026 shows a paid petition from a divided, published Fourth Circuit decision. The Court requested a response after the estate initially waived one, a meaningful positive signal. The estate filed its BIO on July 8, while the private medical respondents filed a brief supporting the petition. The docket did not yet show distribution after the BIO, a relist, or a CVSG.

The petition and questions presented characterize the Fourth Circuit as automatically converting a healthcare contractor into a county policymaker and identify asserted disagreement with Sixth Circuit cases and a Fifth Circuit decision. Judge Richardson's dissent gives the petition a clear merits theory: hiring an outside provider, without more, resembles the respondeat-superior liability that *Monell* forbids. Outsourced jail healthcare is common, so a genuine categorical conflict would have recurring importance.

The BIO substantially weakens the grant case. It explains that the Fourth Circuit examined the actual contracts, which assigned Southern Health Partners responsibility for all medical services under its own policies without meaningful county review, and allowed liability only for injuries caused by SHP policies or customs. On that account, the decision applies delegation principles rather than automatic liability for every contractor tort. The BIO also persuasively distinguishes the claimed opposing authorities: the Sixth Circuit cases turned on missing proof of delegation, facial challenges to contracting, or causation, and the Fifth Circuit expressly declined to decide the asserted nondelegable-duty issue. A CourtListener opinion search also surfaced the Seventh Circuit's May 2026 decision in *Lee v. Milwaukee County*, consistent with the BIO's account that the Seventh Circuit rejects recasting policy-or-custom liability as respondeat superior.

Vehicle problems further favor denial. The counties allegedly did not frame their own respondeat-superior theory until rehearing; the case is interlocutory after reversal of summary judgment; SHP will face trial regardless; and causation and immunity issues remain unresolved. The dispute can therefore look like fact-bound contract interpretation rather than a clean circuit conflict. The Court could also wait for a square appellate conflict.
