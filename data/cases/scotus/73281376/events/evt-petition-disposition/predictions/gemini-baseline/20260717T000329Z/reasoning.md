# Reasoning: Davie County v. Swink (25-1091)

## Legal Question and Governing Standard
The petition presents the question of whether a local governmental entity (here, two North Carolina counties) can be held liable under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 for the constitutional torts of a private healthcare provider it hired to provide medical care at its jails. The petitioners argue that holding the counties liable simply for hiring the provider violates the Supreme Court's prohibition on *respondeat superior* liability under *Monell v. Department of Social Services*, and improperly treats the private provider as an automatic "final policymaker" under *Pembaur*, *Praprotnik*, and *Jett*. 

## Facts and Posture
David Ray Gunter died of complications from a blood clot after being detained in the Davie County and Stokes County jails. The counties had contracted with Southern Health Partners, Inc. (SHP) to provide medical care to inmates. The district court granted summary judgment to the counties. However, the Fourth Circuit (in a 2-1 decision) reversed in part, with the majority finding that the counties could be held liable for SHP's policies or customs because the provision of medical care is a non-delegable duty. Judge Richardson dissented, arguing that the majority's approach amounted to "*respondeat superior* liability in poor disguise" and conflicted with Supreme Court precedent by treating the hiring of a contractor as an automatic delegation of final policymaking authority. 

At the Supreme Court, the respondents initially waived their right to respond. On May 8, 2026, the Court requested a response, and the Brief in Opposition was filed on July 8.

## Analysis and Prediction
A Call for Response (CFR) following a waiver indicates that at least one Justice is interested in the petition. Furthermore, the presence of a strong dissent by a prominent conservative judge (Judge Richardson) on a core § 1983/municipal liability issue often signals to the Court's conservative wing that the lower court may have expanded liability improperly. 

However, despite these positive signals, the statistical base rate for cert grants remains overwhelmingly low. The circuit split alleged by the petitioners (pitting the Fourth, Seventh, and Eleventh Circuits against the Fifth and Sixth) is vigorously contested by the respondents, who argue persuasively that the Sixth Circuit's differing outcomes rest on distinct factual records rather than a divergent legal standard. The Court frequently denies petitions where the alleged split is more of a fact-bound application of *Monell* rather than a deep doctrinal divide. 

While the CFR and the Richardson dissent elevate the probability of a grant significantly above the baseline (to approximately 12%), the most likely outcome is still a denial, as the Court may prefer to wait for a cleaner split or may determine that the Fourth Circuit's non-delegable duty rationale is sufficiently tied to specific state-law duties.

**Prediction:** Denied (Probability: 12%)
