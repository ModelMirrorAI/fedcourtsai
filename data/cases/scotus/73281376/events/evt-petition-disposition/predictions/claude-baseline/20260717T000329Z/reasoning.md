# Davie County v. Swink, No. 25-1091 — cert disposition prediction

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.08.**

## The legal question

Davie and Stokes Counties (NC) petition from the Fourth Circuit's 2-1 decision in
*Swink v. Southern Health Partners, Inc.*, 160 F.4th 438 (4th Cir. 2025) (amended
Dec. 2, 2025; rehearing denied Dec. 16, 2025). The panel majority held the counties
may be liable under *Monell* because their contracts with Southern Health Partners
(SHP) delegated "final authority to make decisions about inmates' medical care,"
making SHP a county policymaker whose customs and practices (multi-day medication
delays, no weekend care) a jury could find caused detainee David Gunter's missed
Coumadin doses and subsequent blood clots. The questions presented ask (1) whether
a governmental entity can be held liable under respondeat superior or a
"non-delegable duty" theory for a hired healthcare provider's constitutional torts,
and (2) whether hiring a jail healthcare provider automatically makes the provider
a policymaker under the *Pembaur*/*Praprotnik*/*Jett* framework.

## Signals from the docket (snapshot 2026-07-16)

Grant-positive:

- **Call for response.** All respondents waived; the case was distributed for the
  May 14, 2026 conference; on May 8 the Court requested a response (due June 8,
  extended to July 8). A CFR after waivers means at least one chamber flagged the
  petition — a necessary-but-far-from-sufficient precursor to a paid grant, and the
  single strongest positive signal here.
- **Published dissent below.** Judge Richardson dissented in relevant part,
  characterizing the majority as imposing "respondeat superior liability in a poor
  disguise" — a credentialed validation of the petition's theory.
- **Asserted 3-2 circuit split** (Eleventh/Seventh/Fourth vs. Sixth/Fifth) on a
  recurring fact pattern: most U.S. jails outsource medical care.
- **A co-respondent (SHP) filed a brief in support of the petition** (July 8) —
  respondent-side support for review.
- Paid petition, Fourth Circuit origin (statpack: CA4 modern cert petitions grant
  at ~4.6% vs. ~3.0% overall modern rate).

Grant-negative:

- **The split is soft under inspection.** The BIO (MacArthur Justice Center —
  experienced Supreme Court counsel) shows *Ancata* (11th Cir.) and *King v.
  Kramer* (7th Cir.) expressly disclaim respondeat superior and rest on
  case-specific contractual delegation of final policymaking authority; the Sixth
  Circuit cases the petition cites turned on failure of proof or causation, not a
  contrary rule; and the Fifth Circuit "side" is an unpublished district-court
  ruling the Fifth Circuit expressly declined to reach (*Robinson*, 80 F.4th 704,
  710 n.4). No court of appeals has acknowledged a division. The petition's
  "automatic liability" framing overreads the cases it attacks.
- **Vehicle problems.** The respondeat-superior framing was first raised in the
  rehearing petition (party-presentation problem); the posture is interlocutory
  (reversal of summary judgment, remand for trial); the case proceeds to trial
  against SHP regardless of the outcome; and causation plus the counties'
  immunity defenses remain unresolved below, so the QP may not even be outcome
  determinative for the counties.
- **No en banc traction:** rehearing denied with no judge requesting a poll.
- **No cert-stage amici** on the docket despite the petition's claims of national
  importance for county governments.
- The petition is workmanlike regional-firm work (it even misspells the
  respondent's name in the caption), with a hyperbolic error-correction tone; the
  Court rarely grants to correct a fact-bound contract reading.

## Weighing

The statpack anchor for a modern discretionary cert docket is ~2.5–3% (Term 2025:
2.5%; paid filings run higher than the blended rate). The CFR and the published
dissent move this petition well above that anchor — historically, paid petitions
that draw a CFR grant at roughly 8–12%. But the BIO's no-split showing is
persuasive, the posture is interlocutory, the party-presentation objection is
clean, and *Monell*-delegation petitions of this kind have repeatedly failed to
attract four votes. The realistic ceiling is a relist-and-deny, possibly with a
statement respecting denial. A GVR is implausible (no intervening decision), so
essentially all grant probability is plenary.

Net: **P(grant) ≈ 0.08; predicted disposition: denied.** The likeliest path is
distribution for the late-September 2026 long conference and denial in the first
weeks of October Term 2026.
