## Certiorari and merits assessment

Supreme Court Rule 10 favors review of a genuine lower-court conflict, an important unsettled federal question cleanly decided below, or an exceptional departure from accepted law. The petition does not identify an actual conflict over the questions presented. It asserts that coercive-control laws are spreading, but cites no squarely conflicting appellate holdings. Much of the filing instead contests the evidence, fact findings, and fairness of this particular protection-order proceeding.

The case is an exceptionally poor vehicle for the broad requested ruling:

- The petition says the Washington Court of Appeals affirmed on a domestic-violence rationale after respondent allegedly ceased relying on immigration-related coercive control. That makes the immigration-preemption theory potentially non-dispositive and entangled with disputed facts.
- The federal statutory and bill-of-attainder theories do not appear to have been passed on by the state courts. The petition seeks facial rulings on several federal programs far beyond the judgment under review, creating preservation, standing, redressability, and finality obstacles.
- The VAWA theory rests partly on a possible future self-petition and alleged reputational consequences to the citizen spouse. That is a weak posture for facially invalidating an immigration-benefit framework.
- The petition is pro se, unusually sprawling, and asks the Court to recharacterize a fact-bound family protection proceeding as a criminal adjudication. Those features make a clean merits decision especially unlikely.

The underlying legal theories are also weak. The Sixth Amendment confrontation right and *Crawford* govern criminal prosecutions, not ordinary civil protection-order hearings; civil due process does not automatically require a jury, proof beyond a reasonable doubt, criminal discovery, or criminal evidence rules. Section 2265 itself conditions full faith and credit on jurisdiction and notice plus an opportunity to be heard. The bill-of-attainder theory is a poor fit because the statutes establish generally applicable consequences and rely on judicial or administrative processes rather than legislatively determining the guilt of named persons. The I-864 regulation's permission to withdraw sponsorship does not obviously immunize the surrounding conduct from consideration under neutral state family law. Finally, *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024), recently sustained § 922(g)(8) against a facial Second Amendment challenge in the protection-order setting, making immediate review of this diffuse effort to distinguish and broaden that dispute unlikely absent a developed conflict.

CourtListener research was consistent with that assessment. A narrow search combining § 922(g)(8) and bill-of-attainder terms returned only three results, chiefly *United States v. Emerson*, 270 F.3d 203 (5th Cir. 2001), and *United States v. Coccia*, 598 F.3d 293 (6th Cir. 2010). The *Emerson* record states that the Fifth Circuit reversed a district court's facial Second Amendment and as-applied due-process invalidation. This is not evidence of the split the petition would need.
