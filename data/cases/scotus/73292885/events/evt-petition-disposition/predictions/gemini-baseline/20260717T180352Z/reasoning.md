## Prediction
The petition for a writ of certiorari will be denied.

## Legal Framework & Analysis
The petitioner, acting *pro se*, seeks review of a judgment affirming a state civil order—likely a domestic violence or extreme risk protection order—imposed under a preponderance-of-the-evidence standard. The petitioner argues that such orders carry quasi-criminal consequences (e.g., loss of parental rights, firearms prohibition under state and federal law) and thus should be afforded criminal procedural safeguards under the U.S. Constitution. 

The Supreme Court routinely denies *pro se* petitions challenging the outcome of state domestic relations and family court proceedings. The Court is exceptionally reluctant to intervene in state family law and protective order procedures unless there is a clear, widely recognized split of authority among state supreme courts or federal circuits on a pure question of federal constitutional law. The petition here appears to be a generalized grievance regarding the fairness and constitutionality of civil protective orders and the statutory scheme that amplifies them federally (e.g., VAWA full faith and credit, or 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(8)). 

Furthermore, although the petitioner raises Second Amendment and Due Process claims, the Court recently addressed the constitutionality of disarming individuals subject to domestic violence restraining orders in *United States v. Rahimi*, 144 S. Ct. 1889 (2024), upholding § 922(g)(8) against a facial challenge. A *pro se* petition is an exceedingly unlikely vehicle for the Court to revisit or expand upon that jurisprudence, particularly when reviewing a state court application of a civil protection statute.

## Base Rates & Priors
Paid cert petitions generally face a grant rate of approximately 2-3%. However, for petitions filed by *pro se* litigants challenging state court judgments in family/domestic matters, the historic grant rate is effectively zero. The lack of a CVSG and the absence of clear structural signals (such as multiple relists—this case is merely distributed for the September conference) heavily support a denial. There is no indication that the Court would treat this case differently from the hundreds of similar *pro se* appeals it denies each Term.
