# Prediction: denied

I assign a 10% probability to any grant and predict denial. The principal grant-side alternative is a GVR in light of *Louisiana v. Callais*, rather than plenary review.

## Question and governing law

The petition presents two clusters of issues. First, it asks whether intervening voters and a state legislator have Article III standing to appeal the Section 2 liability ruling and challenge the ensuing remedial map. Second, it asks whether a race-conscious remedial redistricting map triggers strict scrutiny even when race did not predominate in drawing the lines.

Article III requires a concrete, particularized injury fairly traceable to the challenged ruling and redressable by a favorable judgment. An intervenor must establish that standing independently when the original governmental defendants do not appeal. For a racial-gerrymandering claim, the Court's redistricting precedents require the challenger to show that race predominated over traditional districting criteria before strict scrutiny applies.

The Court's April 29, 2026 decision in *Louisiana v. Callais* supplies important intervening law. It held that Section 2 compliance can be a compelling interest only when Section 2, as newly construed, actually requires race-based districting. It updated the *Gingles* framework to require race-neutral illustrative maps that satisfy the State's legitimate objectives, evidence disentangling racial-bloc voting from partisanship, and current evidence supporting a strong inference of intentional discrimination. It also treated an express racial goal in drawing district lines as sufficient to trigger strict scrutiny in that case.

## Record signals

The January 23 petition argues that the Ninth Circuit improperly severed the district court's Section 2 liability decision from the later remedy for traceability purposes. It also argues that any race-conscious redistricting should receive strict scrutiny under *Students for Fair Admissions*. Those are nationally consequential themes, particularly after *Callais*. The docket provides a genuine positive signal: after initial distribution, the Court requested a response, and the paid petition is now fully briefed for the September 28 conference.

The June 2 brief in opposition nevertheless identifies several strong vehicle and merits obstacles:

- Washington and its Secretary of State did not appeal; only permissive intervenors seek review.
- The Ninth Circuit found Trevino's injury traceable to the remedial map rather than the antecedent liability ruling and found Ybarra's claimed candidate injuries speculative or unsupported.
- The court of appeals found the racial-gerrymandering objection likely forfeited because it was not raised during the remedial proceedings.
- The remedial expert drew the proposed maps without racial or political data, while the district court selected Map 3B based on traditional criteria and the nonracial goal of keeping Yakama Nation lands together.
- The petition identifies no circuit split, and the unusual intervenor posture makes both questions poor vehicles for a generally applicable rule.

The BIO also argues, with record citations, that the Section 2 evidence can satisfy the new *Callais* requirements: race-blind remedial alternatives, expert evidence distinguishing race from party, recent discriminatory practices and racial appeals, and evidence of intentional line-drawing. Whether that showing ultimately succeeds is contestable, but it substantially weakens the case for a summary *Callais* remand. The petitioners' broader theory that every race-conscious districting decision triggers strict scrutiny also goes beyond the predominance rule that *Callais* continued to apply.
