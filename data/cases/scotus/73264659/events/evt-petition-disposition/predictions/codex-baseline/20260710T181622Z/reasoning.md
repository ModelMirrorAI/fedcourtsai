# Legal Question And Standard

The petition presents five questions. The first three assert due-process problems in the state courts' handling of service, dismissal with prejudice, and a requirement that petitioner abandon a served defendant. The fourth asks whether the courts improperly recharacterized an online-platform breach-of-contract claim as a privacy dispute. The fifth asks whether an online platform may claim Section 230 immunity for profile-associated content while controlling the content and refusing user modification.

The governing certiorari standard strongly favors denial unless a petition shows a conflict among courts, an important unsettled federal question needing Supreme Court review, or another reason beyond ordinary error correction. Case-specific procedural error and unpublished state-court dispositions are weak vehicles absent a clear conflict or a developed merits ruling.

# Analysis

But the petition does not identify a split among lower courts, conflicting state or federal authority, or a recurring rule adopted by a court of last resort. It frames the issue mainly through the petitioner's own profile dispute and a sequence of state-court procedural rulings.

The vehicle is especially weak. The record appears to come from Washington district court litigation, a superior court RALJ appeal, discretionary-review denials by the Washington Court of Appeals and Washington Supreme Court, and unpublished orders. The petition gives little indication that the highest state court resolved a clean federal Section 230 question on the merits. The Section 230 question is mixed with the petitioner's contract and privacy framing, making it unlikely that the Court would use this case to clarify platform immunity.
