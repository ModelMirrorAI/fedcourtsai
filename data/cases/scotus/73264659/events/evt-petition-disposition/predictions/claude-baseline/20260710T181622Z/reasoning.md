# Fotso v. Needham, et al. (No. 25-7274) — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied.** P(granted) ≈ 0.002. This is a forward-mode (pending)
cell: the petition is distributed for the Conference of September 28, 2026.

## The case

A pro se, in forma pauperis petition for certiorari from the Washington state
courts. Petitioner sued IMDb.com, Inc. and its founder (Col Needham) over
information on her own IMDb profile page that the platform declined to remove.
The King County District Court entered judgment against her; the King County
Superior Court affirmed on RALJ appeal; the Washington Court of Appeals denied
discretionary review; and the Washington Supreme Court denied review (October
3, 2025; rehearing denied January 7, 2026). All decisions below are
unpublished. The petition (10 pages) presents five questions: three
factbound due-process complaints about service-of-process rulings, one about
recharacterization of a contract claim as a privacy claim, and one on the
scope of 47 U.S.C. § 230 immunity where a platform controls user-profile
content while denying the user the ability to remove it.

## Governing standard

Certiorari is discretionary and governed by Rule 10: the Court looks for a
circuit/state-court split on an important federal question, a decision in
conflict with its own precedent, or an issue of national importance presented
in a clean vehicle. Error correction — especially of factbound, unpublished
state-court procedural rulings — is expressly disfavored.

## Why denial is near-certain

1. **Base rates.** The modern discretionary-cert grant rate is a few percent
   of all petitions, and dramatically lower on the IFP/pro se docket. Corpus
   priors from this very docket series (Term-2025 `25-7xxx` IFP petitions
   filed the same week, e.g. 25-7256 through 25-7273) were denied at their
   first conference essentially without exception; the only grant among the
   retrieved neighbors was a counseled immigration case on the paid docket
   (see `retrieval.md`).
2. **No certworthy question as presented.** Questions 1–3 are case-specific
   due-process grievances about Washington's application of its own service
   rules — no split alleged, no published opinion, purely factbound. Question
   4 is a state-law claim-characterization complaint. Question 5 gestures at a
   genuinely live topic (§ 230's scope), but the petition cites no
   authority splits and develops no conflict — it argues the issue is
   "recurring" in two paragraphs.
3. **Terrible vehicle even for the § 230 issue.** The merits judgment comes
   from a small-claims-track district court affirmed on limited RALJ review,
   with every appellate court denying discretionary review in unpublished
   orders; there is no reasoned lower-court opinion on § 230 for the Court to
   review. The Court has repeatedly declined to reach § 230's scope even in
   fully counseled, percolated vehicles (e.g. Gonzalez v. Google (2023),
   resolved without reaching § 230).
4. **No signals of interest.** No amicus support, no response filed by
   respondents' counsel (Davis Wright Tremaine), no CVSG, no relist — the
   petition was distributed on June 18, 2026 for a single (long) conference.
   The overwhelmingly likely path is denial in the orders list following the
   September 28, 2026 conference.

Residual probability (~0.2%) covers only the tail scenarios: a relist plus a
call for a response that revives interest, or a summary GVR — none of which
has any present indication on the docket.
