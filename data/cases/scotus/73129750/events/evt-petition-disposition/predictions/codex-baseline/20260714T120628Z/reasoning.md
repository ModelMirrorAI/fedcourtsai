## Question and governing standard

The underlying dispute asks whether Executive Order 14,160's denial of birthright citizenship to certain children born in the United States violates 8 U.S.C. § 1401(a) and the Citizenship Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The immediate event is not the merits outcome but whether the Court will grant certiorari before judgment.

A grant ordinarily requires four votes. Supreme Court Rule 10 makes certiorari discretionary and favors conflicts or important unsettled federal questions rather than mere error correction. Certiorari before judgment additionally requires the exceptional showing under Rule 11 that the case is of such imperative public importance as to justify bypassing ordinary appellate review.

## Pre-disposition signals

The grant-side signals are unusually strong:

- The Solicitor General seeks review of a nationally consequential constitutional and statutory challenge to a presidential executive order. The order governs citizenship at birth, affects a nationwide class, and generated several parallel challenges and injunctions.
- The Court had already addressed the remedial phase of the same controversy in *Trump v. CASA*; the brief in opposition says that decision contemplated eventual merits review. That recent institutional involvement makes prompt merits review much more plausible than the ordinary cert-before-judgment petition.
- Respondents concede that, if the Court takes the issue, this case has no vehicle problem and argue that review should encompass both this case and the companion *Trump v. Washington* petition. The existence of parallel vehicles lets the Court select or combine a clean route to the merits.
- The petition was distributed for the November 21 conference and then redistributed for the December 5 conference. That apparent single relist is a meaningful positive signal compared with an ordinary petition.

There are substantial denial-side considerations. The lower courts to address the merits were unanimous, so there is no circuit conflict. The provisioned opposition also identifies an independent statutory ground under § 1401(a), argues that *United States v. Wong Kim Ark* already resolves the constitutional issue, and stresses that the government previously declined to seek merits relief. Those points weaken both the need for review and the government's ultimate merits position. Certiorari before judgment is also an extraordinary procedural shortcut.

They do not outweigh the combination of sovereign petitioner, exceptional national importance, the Court's prior engagement with this exact controversy, clean companion vehicles, and the relist. The modern discretionary-cert grant rate in the committed statpack is only about 3.3%, and the one-relist bucket is 7.8%, but this petition is far outside the ordinary pool on institutional importance and likelihood that the Court wants a definitive nationwide answer.

## Votes and uncertainty

I do not predict individual Justices' cert votes. Such votes are not publicly recorded, and later merits alignments in the contaminated snapshot are not a sound proxy for the four votes needed to grant review. The residual 12% denial probability reflects the absence of a split, the independent statutory obstacle, and the exceptional Rule 11 posture.
