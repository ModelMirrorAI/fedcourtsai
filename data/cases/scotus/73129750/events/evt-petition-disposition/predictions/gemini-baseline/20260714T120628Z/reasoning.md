# Reasoning: Cert Petition Disposition

**Legal Question & Context**
This case involves a momentous constitutional question regarding the Citizenship Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Specifically, the case addresses whether children born in the United States to noncitizen parents lacking lawful domicile (e.g., undocumented immigrants or temporary visitors) are "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" and thus entitled to birthright citizenship. President Donald J. Trump issued an Executive Order adopting a restrictive interpretation of the Citizenship Clause, effectively denying birthright citizenship to this group. The First Circuit evidently enjoined this Executive Order, relying on the Supreme Court's seminal precedent in *United States v. Wong Kim Ark*, 169 U.S. 649 (1898), and related statutory provisions (e.g., 8 U.S.C. § 1401(a)).

**Pre-Decision Record Analysis**
The Brief in Opposition (BIO) filed by respondents (Barbara, et al.) vigorously argues that *Wong Kim Ark* settled this question over a century ago by establishing a jus soli rule derived from English common law, which confers citizenship on anyone born within the nation's territory, regardless of their parents' immigration status or domicile. The BIO argues the petition does not warrant review because the constitutional and statutory questions are firmly settled and overruling *Wong Kim Ark* would cause catastrophic disruption to established laws and systems. 

Despite the respondents' strong arguments on the merits, the nature of the dispute virtually guarantees Supreme Court review. The Court rarely allows a nationwide injunction against a significant presidential action to stand without giving it full plenary review. The fact that the Administration explicitly asks the Court to clarify, limit, or overrule a landmark precedent (*Wong Kim Ark*) to resolve a deeply contested national issue provides a textbook reason for granting certiorari (Rule 10). The sheer volume of amicus curiae briefs filed (from law professors, former officials, local governments, and civil rights groups) also underscores the exceptional importance of the case.
