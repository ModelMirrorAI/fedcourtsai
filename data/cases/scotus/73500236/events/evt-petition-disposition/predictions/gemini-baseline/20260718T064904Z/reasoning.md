# Prediction Reasoning

**Background and Legal Question**
The petition in *Colorado Bondshares v. Marin Metropolitan District* challenges a state appellate court's ruling regarding municipal bonds, special district assessments, and the Due Process Clause. The core questions presented ask whether a special district's debt to bondholders can be voided under the Due Process Clause or a theory of unjust enrichment because the landowner supposedly did not benefit from the special assessment, even if the landowner bought the land with full knowledge of the assessment. 

**Governing Standards and Case Facts**
This case comes up from the state court system, specifically the Colorado Court of Appeals (with a discretionary denial by the Colorado Supreme Court). The petition explicitly states that both issues are ones of "first impression," essentially admitting that there is no split among the federal circuit courts or state supreme courts. The respondents (Marin Metropolitan District and Century at Landmark, LLC) both filed explicit waivers of their right to respond in June 2026. 

**Reasoning for Probability**
When a cert petition presents a highly fact-bound, state-law-entangled issue of "first impression" with no circuit split, the Supreme Court is overwhelmingly likely to deny review. The fact that the respondents waived their right to respond strongly confirms this assessment; the respondents evidently view the petition as posing no real threat of a grant and believe that no defensive brief is necessary.

Looking at the baseline statistics (the `metrics/statpack.md`): a modern discretionary-cert petition with 0 relists has a base grant rate of only 0.8%. Adding the negative signals—respondents' waivers of response, the state intermediate court posture, and the lack of a circuit split—the probability of a grant here is exceedingly remote. 

**Conclusion**
I predict a denial. I estimate the probability of a grant to be 0.5% (0.005), reflecting the absolute minimum threshold for random or unforeseen outlier grants while heavily discounting based on the overwhelmingly negative case-specific indicators. I do not predict any votes, as it is highly likely to be a simple, summary denial on the order list.
