# Prediction: denied

I assign a 1.8% probability to any grant and predict denial. This is a forward prediction made from the July 18, 2026 snapshot, the provisioned questions presented and petition, and general pre-disposition legal and base-rate context. No brief in opposition was provisioned; the snapshot shows that both respondents waived response. That is not a merits concession, and the Court can call for a response if the petition draws interest.

## Questions and governing standard

The petition asks (1) whether due process or takings principles permit a special assessment against a purchaser that allegedly knew both of the assessment and the absence of a corresponding benefit, and (2) whether bondholders may obtain restitution from a special district after an underlying repayment obligation is held unenforceable and the borrowed money has been spent.

Certiorari is ordinarily warranted for a square conflict among lower courts, an important and recurring federal question, or a departure from this Court's precedent. Error correction in a fact-specific case, especially from an unpublished state intermediate appellate decision, is disfavored. A state-court judgment also must cleanly present a controlling federal question without an adequate independent state-law ground or vehicle complication.

## Case-specific assessment

Several factors strongly favor denial:

- The petition identifies both questions as issues of first impression, but does not identify a split among federal courts or state courts of last resort. Its central contention is that the Colorado Court of Appeals misconstrued this Court's older cases, which reads primarily as an error-correction request.
- The first question arises from an unusual record: a 2008 bond issuance, earlier litigation recharacterizing a mill levy as a special assessment, and a sophisticated developer's 2016 purchase of the remaining parcel with alleged notice of the levy and the absence of further improvements. That posture makes the case a poor vehicle for a generally applicable constitutional rule.
- The petition's move from the no-benefit rule for special assessments to the *Penn Central* investment-backed-expectations factor is contestable. Notice to a later purchaser does not necessarily eliminate the constitutional limitation the lower court applied, and the petition itself says the lower court treated that limitation as Colorado due-process jurisprudence. This creates both merits uncertainty and a possible state-law/vehicle problem.
- The restitution question is still less certworthy. It depends on the scope of Colorado unjust-enrichment doctrine and on whether old municipal-contract decisions such as *Marsh v. Fulton County* and *Louisiana v. Wood* impose a controlling federal rule in this modern state-law dispute. The petition alleges no conflict and may not present an independently reviewable federal question.
- Both respondents waived response, no supporting amici appear in the provisioned snapshot, and the case was distributed for its first conference without a call for response shown. These are weak but directionally negative docket signals as of the snapshot.

The strongest grant argument is practical importance: if replicated, the ruling could affect pricing and enforceability of special-district bonds, and the petition alleges that many states use similar infrastructure-financing mechanisms. But the asserted nationwide effect is not supported by conflicting decisions or concrete evidence in the provisioned record. The unpublished, Colorado-specific posture substantially limits the case's usefulness for resolving that broader concern.
