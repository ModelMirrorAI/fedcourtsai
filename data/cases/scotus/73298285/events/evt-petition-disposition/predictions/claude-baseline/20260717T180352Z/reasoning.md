# Sigalovskaya v. Braden, No. 25-1250 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.15.**

## The case

Paid petition (Term 2025, docketed May 5, 2026) from the Second Circuit
(No. 23-7625, 149 F.4th 226, decided Aug. 27, 2025; rehearing en banc denied
Dec. 2, 2025). HSI agents entered Sigalovskaya's home without a warrant,
searched it, and — per the complaint — Agent Braden fabricated a confession and
arrested her without probable cause; charges were dropped three weeks later.
The district court (EDNY) dismissed her surviving Bivens false-arrest claim on
the pleadings after *Egbert v. Boule*; a fractured CA2 panel affirmed in a
one-paragraph per curiam with two competing concurrences (Lee: fabrication
makes a new context, plus special factors; Pérez: the 8 C.F.R. § 287.10
grievance channel alone forecloses at step one) and a dissent (Lynch: not a new
context, so the analysis should end in the plaintiff's favor).

The questions presented (from the provisioned QP section) are (1) what makes a
Fourth Amendment claim "meaningfully different" from *Bivens* itself, and
(2) whether an alternative remedy independently forecloses a claim that is not
otherwise meaningfully different — both the subject of well-documented,
petition-cited circuit splits (First/Seventh/Ninth vs. Third/Fourth/Fifth/Eleventh).

## The decisive forward context: Nielsen v. Watanabe

Retrieval (see `retrieval.md`) confirms the Court **granted certiorari in
June 2026 in Nielsen v. Watanabe, No. 25-417** (from the Ninth Circuit's
*Watanabe v. Derr*) for OT2026 — a government-side petition asking whether a
federal prisoner's deliberate-indifference claim may proceed under *Bivens*,
squarely presenting whether alternative remedies (PLRA / BOP ARP) create a
"new context" at step one — the same step-one/alternative-remedies question as
Sigalovskaya's QP2 — plus a "meaningful difference" (injury-severity) question
adjacent to QP1. The Court is already **holding Mohan v. Watkins (No. 25-952)**,
the Seventh Circuit companion, pending *Nielsen*. This all predates the
snapshot and is legitimate forward signal, not leakage; the petition itself
cites the *Watanabe* rehearing-denial opinions.

That reshapes the forecast in three ways:

1. **The Court has picked its vehicle.** Its consistent modern practice in
   Bivens cases (*Hernandez v. Mesa*, *Egbert*, *Goldey v. Fields*, now
   *Nielsen*) is to grant when the lower court *recognized* a remedy — i.e.,
   government/officer petitions — and to deny plaintiff-side petitions raising
   the same splits (*Oliva*, *Byrd*, *Cantu*, *Ahmed*, *Xi*, *Mejia* were all
   denied). Sigalovskaya lost below; a plenary grant would break that pattern.
2. **The likeliest path is a hold.** After the BIO (due Aug. 17), expect
   distribution to the late-September 2026 long conference and then a hold
   pending *Nielsen* (decided by ~June 2027), exactly as with *Mohan*. The CFR
   is fully consistent with teeing the case up for a hold rather than a grant.
3. **Post-*Nielsen* disposition.** If the government wins in *Nielsen*
   (probable, ~80%: *Egbert* already says alternative remedies alone foreclose
   at step two, and *Goldey* was a summary reversal in the same direction),
   the CA2 affirmance is consistent with the new decision → straight denial.
   If Watanabe prevails (~20%) — the Court confines alternative remedies to
   step two — Judge Pérez's ground collapses and a **GVR** becomes the natural
   disposition (~60-70% conditional), though Judge Lee's fabrication-based
   new-context rationale is an independent ground that could still support
   denial.

## Probability

  granted 5.4% in Term 2025, 6.9% in Term 2024; overall modern

- The *Nielsen* grant caps the upside: the realistic grant-side mass is mostly
  **GVR-after-hold**, not plenary review.
