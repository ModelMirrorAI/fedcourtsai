The legal question is whether *Bivens* provides a remedy for a Fourth Amendment claim of false arrest based on fabricated evidence, and whether the availability of an alternative remedy independently forecloses a *Bivens* claim that does not otherwise meaningfully differ from *Bivens* itself.

The governing standard for extending *Bivens* is highly restrictive. In recent years, particularly in *Ziglar v. Abbasi* and *Egbert v. Boule*, the Supreme Court has made it clear that extending *Bivens* to any "new context" is a "disfavored judicial activity." The Court in *Egbert* further held that the existence of an alternative remedial structure, such as an administrative grievance process, is a special factor that independently forecloses a *Bivens* action.

In this case, the petitioner challenges the Second Circuit's affirmation of the district court's dismissal of her *Bivens* claim arising from a false arrest allegedly based on fabricated evidence by Department of Homeland Security agents. The Second Circuit panel was fractured on its reasoning, though it unanimously affirmed the dismissal. Judge Lee found a "new context" due to the fabrication of evidence (as opposed to an unreasonable search), while Judge Pérez found the claim independently foreclosed by DHS's alternative grievance procedures under 8 C.F.R. § 287.10 (applying *Egbert*). Judge Lynch dissented in part, arguing the claim was materially indistinguishable from *Bivens*.

Because the lower court's judgment (dismissing the *Bivens* claim) aligns perfectly with the Supreme Court conservative majority's project of closing the door on implied causes of action, the Court has no incentive to grant certiorari. The Court is highly unlikely to grant cert to reverse the decision and allow the *Bivens* claim to proceed. While the Court did request a response after the Solicitor General initially waived its right to respond, this request is not an indicator of a likely grant in this context. A "Response Requested" is often prompted by a single Justice (likely one of the dissenting liberal Justices) who wishes to review the government's arguments or prepare a dissent from the denial of certiorari. Although a grant to explicitly overrule *Bivens* entirely is possible, it is unlikely given that the lower courts are already effectively nullifying it by aggressively applying *Egbert*. Therefore, the petition is highly likely to be denied.
