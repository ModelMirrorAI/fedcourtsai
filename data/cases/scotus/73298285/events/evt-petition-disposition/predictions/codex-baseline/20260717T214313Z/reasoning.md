# Prediction: denied

I assign a 12% probability to any grant and predict that the petition will be denied. The forecast covers a plenary grant or a GVR on the binary grant axis; denial is the likeliest specific disposition.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether *Bivens* supplies a damages remedy when federal officers allegedly enter a home without a warrant, fabricate a confession, and arrest the resident without probable cause, and whether an alternative administrative remedy can independently foreclose a claim that otherwise falls within the original *Bivens* context. At the certiorari stage, the practical considerations are the asserted circuit conflicts, importance and recurrence, preservation and vehicle quality, and whether four Justices want to resolve the issue now.

The provisioned questions presented and full, untruncated 41-page petition are the principal merits sources. No brief in opposition was provisioned. That is consistent with the snapshot: the Solicitor General initially waived a response; the Court requested one on June 17, 2026; and the response deadline was extended to August 17, 2026. I therefore treat the petition's account as advocacy rather than as a complete adversarial record.

## Grant signals

The allegations place the claim unusually close to the original *Bivens* setting: a warrantless home entry and search, separation from children, and an allegedly unsupported arrest by line federal law-enforcement officers. The petition identifies two developed disagreements among the circuits: how strictly a claim must reproduce *Bivens* to avoid being a new context, and whether a later-created alternative remedy belongs in that threshold analysis. The Second Circuit's one-paragraph affirmance was accompanied by two concurrences resting on different grounds and a partial dissent, which cleanly exposes both questions. The judgment followed a Rule 12(c) dismissal, so the legal issues do not depend on a completed trial record.

Most importantly as a docket signal, the Court requested a response after the government waived one. That indicates interest from at least one Justice and makes the case materially stronger than an ordinary paid petition.

## Denial signals

The Court's modern cases make recognition of a new Bivens context strongly disfavored. The CourtListener text of *Goldey v. Fields*, 606 U.S. 942 (2025), confirms that the Court recently granted and summarily reversed a judgment that had allowed an Eighth Amendment Bivens claim, again stressing that new causes of action are generally Congress's task and that alternative remedies counsel against extension. The First Circuit's *Arias v. Herzon* decision supports the petition's claimed conflict by treating a Fourth Amendment excessive-force claim as within *Bivens* despite a later administrative remedy, but it also shows why the Court can await a cleaner posture: here the government already prevailed under the restrictive approach. Leaving the Second Circuit's judgment in place does not require the Court to approve an expansion of Bivens, and a future government petition from a judgment allowing relief would better match the Court's recent intervention pattern.

The alleged fabrication, the officers' DHS affiliation, and the Inspector General complaint process give the respondent plausible grounds to characterize the claim as meaningfully different or independently barred. No amicus filings appear in the July 17 snapshot, no CVSG has occurred, and the case has not been relisted. A response request is meaningful but substantially weaker than those later-stage signals.
