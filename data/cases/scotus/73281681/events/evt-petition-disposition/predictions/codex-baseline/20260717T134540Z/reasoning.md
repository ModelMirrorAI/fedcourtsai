# Prediction

That is far above the ordinary paid-petition baseline but still below 50%, because a response request is a signal of one or more Justices' interest rather than four votes to grant, and the Court may prefer another vehicle.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether the Takings Clause's protection against unconstitutional land-use permit conditions applies to monetary demands other than payments imposed in lieu of dedicating real property. Under *Nollan* and *Dolan*, a permit exaction must have an essential nexus and rough proportionality to the effects of the proposed land use. *Koontz* applied that framework when the demanded property was money, and *Sheetz* held that legislatively imposed permit conditions are not categorically exempt. The cert-stage dispute is the antecedent question whether this Arts Fund payment is the sort of property demand that can trigger that framework at all.

## Case-specific signals

The provisioned petition and the April 13 questions-presented extract describe a nonrefundable payment exceeding $100 per square foot, tied to permission to convert an artist-restricted loft to ordinary residential use. According to the petition, the fund supports arts programming generally rather than mitigating an effect of a particular conversion. The New York Appellate Division unanimously applied *Nollan* and *Dolan* and invalidated the fee, but the divided New York Court of Appeals reversed because the payment was not demanded in lieu of a real-property transfer. The petition contrasts that categorical rule with the North Carolina Supreme Court's broader reading of *Koontz* in *Anderson Creek* and with decisions from other courts.

The vehicle is strong on the petition's account: final judgment from a state court of last resort, a preserved federal question, a clean legal threshold, and no apparent need for the Court to decide nexus or proportionality itself. The issue is also current. CourtListener research confirmed that the California Court of Appeal's July 2025 opinion on remand in *Sheetz* treated the traffic impact fee as subject to *Nollan/Dolan* analysis and upheld it on the merits, reinforcing both the doctrinal activity and the distinction between coverage and ultimate invalidity.

The docket snapshot supplies substantial selection signals. This is a paid petition with five amicus filings. After the City waived a response and the petition was distributed for the June 11 conference, the Court requested a response; the City then obtained an extension through August 3. A requested response after waiver materially raises the case above the ordinary cert pool. At the snapshot date, however, no brief in opposition, reply, CVSG, relist, or post-response conference history was available. The provisioned document set contains the petition and questions presented but no opposition, so the forecast cannot test the City's vehicle arguments or its account of the split.

The petition itself identifies a pending overlapping petition arising from the *Sheetz* remand. That overlap may draw attention to the issue or produce a hold and eventual GVR, but it also creates vehicle competition and makes a plenary grant in this case less certain. The recent unanimous *Sheetz* decision may also make the Court reluctant to revisit adjacent exactions doctrine immediately unless the conflict is squarely established.
