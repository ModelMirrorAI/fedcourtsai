# Reasoning

## Case Context & Legal Question
The case involves a challenge by SoHo and NoHo loft residents to a New York City zoning regulation. To transition lofts from a restrictive artist-only occupancy designation to standard residential, the City requires residents to pay hundreds of thousands of dollars into a City-administered "Arts Fund" in exchange for a conversion permit. The New York Court of Appeals refused to apply the Takings Clause unconstitutional conditions doctrine (*Nollan* / *Dolan* / *Koontz*) to this monetary demand, holding that the doctrine only applies to monetary exactions imposed "in lieu of a dedication of real property." 

The question presented asks whether the Takings Clause protections for land-use permit applicants encompass monetary demands beyond those imposed in lieu of real property dedications.

## Disposition & Probability
The predicted disposition is **granted**, with a probability of **0.65**.

The conservative supermajority of the Roberts Court has taken a strong, consistent interest in property rights and Takings Clause jurisprudence, particularly concerning unconstitutional conditions (as seen in *Koontz v. St. Johns River Water Management Dist.*, *Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid*, and *Sheetz v. El Dorado County*). The *Sheetz* decision (2024) recently clarified that the doctrine applies to legislative exactions. Taking up this case to clarify that *Koontz* applies to all monetary exactions—and to correct the New York Court of Appeals' narrow reading—aligns perfectly with the Court's recent trajectory.

The docket record signals high salience and a very strong likelihood of a grant:
- **Amici:** Five amicus briefs were filed at the cert stage, including heavyweight conservative and real estate groups (Cato Institute, Manhattan Institute, Advancing American Freedom, and national real estate associations).
- **Counsel:** Petitioners are represented by the Pacific Legal Foundation, a specialized public interest firm with a very high success rate in getting Takings Clause cases granted.
- **Call for Response (CFR):** The City of New York waived its right to respond, but the Court affirmatively requested a response (CFR) on June 2, 2026. A CFR dramatically increases the statistical probability of a grant compared to the baseline.

The combination of a clean, high-profile Takings Clause issue, robust amicus support, elite specialized counsel, and a CFR pushes the grant probability to approximately 65%.
