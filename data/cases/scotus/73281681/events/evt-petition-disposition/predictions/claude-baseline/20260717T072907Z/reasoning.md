# Coalition for Fairness in SoHo and NoHo v. City of New York (No. 25-1187)

**Prediction: denied, but with a well-above-baseline P(grant) = 0.33.**

## The legal question

Whether the Takings Clause protection for land-use permit applicants under
*Nollan v. California Coastal Commission*, 483 U.S. 825 (1987), and *Dolan v.
City of Tigard*, 512 U.S. 374 (1994), as extended to monetary demands by
*Koontz v. St. Johns River Water Management District*, 570 U.S. 595 (2013),
encompasses monetary exactions beyond those imposed **in lieu of** a dedication
of real property.

New York City's 2021 SoHo/NoHo rezoning requires residents of
artist-restricted (JLWQA) lofts to pay a non-refundable "Arts Fund"
contribution of $100+ per square foot — roughly $250,000–$300,000 per home,
~$450 million across all 1,636 units — merely to *apply* for a permit
converting their homes to ordinary residential use. The Appellate Division
unanimously struck the fee under *Nollan*/*Dolan*; the New York Court of
Appeals (Jan. 13, 2026) reversed, holding that a monetary demand triggers the
unconstitutional-conditions doctrine only when imposed in lieu of a
real-property dedication.

## Grant-side signals (from the snapshot and provisioned documents)

1. **Call for response after waiver (June 2, 2026).** The City waived its
   right to respond (May 19); the petition was distributed for the June 11
   conference; the Court then requested a response before that conference
   convened. A CFR means at least one chamber wants the respondent's views —
   the strongest observable pre-conference grant signal on this docket.
   Response now due Aug. 3, 2026, after a granted extension.
2. **Five cert-stage amicus briefs** — Cato Institute; Manhattan Institute
   joined by the National Association of Realtors, NY State Association of
   Realtors, National Association of Home Builders, and National Apartment
   Association; Advancing American Freedom et al.; a brief by NYC Council
   Member Christopher Marte; and one by 44 SoHo/NoHo artists. Broad organized
   support at the petition stage is itself a salience marker.
3. **Elite repeat-player counsel.** Pacific Legal Foundation litigated and won
   *Koontz*, *Knick*, *Cedar Point*, *Pakdel*, *Tyler*, and *Sheetz* — an
   exceptional cert-stage track record in exactly this doctrinal lane.
4. **A credible, outcome-determinative split.** The petition squarely opposes
   *Anderson Creek Partners, L.P. v. County of Hartnett*, 876 S.E.2d 476 (N.C.
   2022) (Koontz covers general monetary demands, not just in-lieu fees),
   against the New York Court of Appeals' categorical in-lieu-only rule, with
   supporting divergence among other state high courts and lower federal
   courts.
5. **Doctrinal timing.** *Sheetz v. County of El Dorado*, 601 U.S. 267 (2024),
   unanimously eliminated the legislative/administrative exaction distinction
   and the concurrences expressly flagged the remaining open questions about
   the scope of *Nollan/Dolan* for fee programs. This petition presents one of
   those flagged questions, in a posture the Court described in *Koontz*
   itself as essential to preventing an "end run" around *Nollan* and *Dolan*.
   The state court's reasoning sits in visible tension with *Koontz*'s text —
   the kind of under-reading of the Court's precedent that attracts review.
6. **Clean-vehicle argument.** Paid petition from a final state high-court
   judgment under 28 U.S.C. § 1257 presenting a pure predicate question of
   law; the petition disclaims any tax/user-fee complication and leaves
   nexus/proportionality application for remand — the same structure as
   *Koontz* and *Sheetz*.

## Deny-side considerations

1. **The base rate is unforgiving.** Modern paid petitions are granted ~5–7%
   of the time (Term 2025 paid est. grant rate 5.4%; Term 2024, 6.9%). Even
   CFR'd, amicus-supported petitions are denied more often than granted.
2. **The Court has repeatedly passed on monetary-exaction follow-ons.**
   Post-*Koontz* petitions raising closely related questions were denied —
   *California Building Industry Assn. v. San Jose* (2016) and *616 Croft
   Ave. v. West Hollywood* (2017) (each with a Thomas writing respecting
   denial), *Cherk v. Marin County* (2020), *Ballinger v. Oakland* (2022) —
   though those pre-dated *Sheetz* and were entangled with the
   legislative-exaction question *Sheetz* has since resolved.
3. **The BIO may surface vehicle problems.** Conversion is formally voluntary
   (current residents may lawfully remain under state law), so the City will
   press voluntariness/ripeness and may argue the record shows no petitioner
   has been made to pay; the Article 78 posture gives it material to work
   with. A facially plausible vehicle objection is the classic way a CFR'd
   petition dies.
4. **The split is young.** The direct state-high-court conflict is essentially
   NY vs. NC with supportive but less square authority elsewhere; the Court
   could let the post-*Sheetz* question percolate another Term or two.

## Weighing

Anchor on the paid-petition grant rate (~5–7%), then apply the large uplift
the observable signals warrant: a CFR (historically roughly an order of
magnitude above the no-response baseline), five amici, a repeat-player
petitioner with a near-perfect recent cert record in this doctrine, a claimed
square split on a question the Court itself flagged as open two Terms ago, and
a facially clean final-judgment vehicle. That combination puts this petition
in the top tier of the cert pool. Against that: the Court's demonstrated
willingness to deny exaction follow-ons, the unfiled BIO (the extension
means the City is mounting a full opposition), and real voluntariness/ripeness
arguments.

I estimate **P(grant, including any GVR) ≈ 0.33**. No intervening decision
suggests a GVR path, so the modal single outcome remains **denied** — but this
is a genuinely close call, and if the BIO fails to land a vehicle objection I
would expect relists and a serious grant look at or shortly after the
late-September 2026 long conference.

## Stakes (big_case_score = 0.55)

If decided, the case would fix the constitutional status of monetary land-use
exactions nationwide — permit and impact fees affect millions of applications
a year — and the SoHo/NoHo scheme itself involves ~$450M demanded from ~1,600
Manhattan homeowners, with organized interest from national real-estate and
homebuilding groups. Significant and closely watched in the
property-rights/land-use world, though not a blockbuster on the order of the
Term's marquee constitutional cases.
