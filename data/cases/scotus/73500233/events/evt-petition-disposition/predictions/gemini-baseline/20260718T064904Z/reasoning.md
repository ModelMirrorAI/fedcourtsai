# Prediction Reasoning: Pitzka v. Wisconsin (25-1333)

**Predicted Disposition:** Denied
**Probability of Grant:** 0.1%

## Legal Analysis and Facts
The petitioner, Mark A. Pitzka, seeks review of a Wisconsin Supreme Court judgment regarding the admissibility of "other acts" evidence under state evidentiary rules and the 5th, 6th, and 14th Amendments. Specifically, the petition asks whether admitting evidence of other acts that are the subject of a pending, unresolved criminal prosecution in another contemporaneous criminal trial violates a defendant's constitutional rights.

Several factors decisively point to a denial of certiorari:
1. **No Circuit Split:** The petition explicitly concedes the absence of a split, stating that "there appears to be no clear legal precedent either approving or disproving of the practice." The Supreme Court rarely grants certiorari simply to address a novel constitutional argument regarding state evidentiary rules without deep percolation and a documented split among lower courts.
2. **State Court Origin:** The case comes from the state court system (Wisconsin). State evidentiary rulings are typically reviewed under highly deferential standards and rarely present the kind of cleanly presented federal question the Court prefers.
3. **Respondent's Waiver:** The respondent, the State of Wisconsin, filed a waiver of its right to respond on June 2, 2026. The Supreme Court almost never grants a petition without first calling for a response (CFR). The fact that the case was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference without a CFR strongly signals that the Court does not intend to grant it.
4. **Base Rates:** The base rate for discretionary cert petitions from state courts is approximately 1.0%. Given the lack of relists (it is scheduled for its first conference), the waiver of response, and the lack of a split, the probability of a grant is far below the baseline.

## Conclusion
This is a routine criminal petition presenting a novel but unpercolated constitutional challenge to a state evidentiary ruling. The combination of no alleged split, a waiver of response, and distribution without a CFR makes a denial a near certainty.
