# Prediction: denied

I estimate a **0.2% probability of any grant** (including a plenary grant or GVR) and predict a denial.

## Question and record

The petition asks whether admitting other-acts evidence that is also the subject of a separate, unresolved prosecution categorically violates the Fifth, Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments. It further argues that the violation is structural error. The provisioned petition says that evidence of a Dodge County arson charge was admitted at Pitzka's Waukesha County arson trial to support motive and identity. The Waukesha jury convicted him, and the Dodge County prosecution was later dismissed.

The snapshot dated July 18, 2026 shows a paid petition, a respondent's waiver, and distribution for the September 28, 2026 conference. It shows no requested response, relist, or CVSG. No brief in opposition was provisioned, so this analysis weighs the petition against the docket posture rather than against merits arguments from the State.

## Certworthiness and vehicle

The petition does not identify a conflict among federal courts or state courts. It instead says there appears to be no clear precedent approving or disapproving the practice and asserts, without demonstrating, that the practice is widespread. That is a poor fit for ordinary certiorari review. The decision below is unpublished and per curiam, and the Wisconsin Supreme Court denied discretionary review.

The vehicle is especially weak. According to the petition, trial counsel did not object to the Dodge County evidence and agreed that it was admissible, so the claim reaches the Court through ineffective-assistance and prejudice issues. The court of appeals applied ordinary other-acts doctrine and did not decide the categorical constitutional question as framed. Those preservation, adequacy-of-the-ruling, and fact-bound prejudice issues would complicate review even if the abstract question interested the Court.

## Governing doctrine and likely disposition

The petition's doctrinal premise faces substantial contrary authority. *Huddleston v. United States*, 485 U.S. 681 (1988), expressly held that a trial judge need not preliminarily find an other act proved by a preponderance; the question is whether the jury could reasonably find that the act occurred and the defendant was the actor. That directly undercuts the petition's assertion that the admissibility inquiry itself necessarily strips the accused of the presumption of innocence.

*Dowling v. United States*, 493 U.S. 342 (1990), allowed Rule 404(b) evidence concerning conduct for which the defendant had already been acquitted and held that neither double jeopardy nor due process categorically barred it. Its due-process test asks whether admission was so extremely unfair that it violated fundamental conceptions of justice, a narrow standard. If even acquitted-conduct evidence is not categorically unconstitutional, the Court is unlikely to create a categorical bar solely because the other charge remains pending. *Spencer v. Texas*, 385 U.S. 554 (1967), likewise reflects the Court's reluctance to constitutionalize state evidentiary rules when limiting instructions and trial-court balancing address prejudice. Admission of evidence also does not itself compel a defendant to testify, making the Fifth Amendment theory an uncertain extension.

There is no apparent intervening authority that would make a GVR more likely than a denial. The petition's reliance on the recent counsel-during-testimony decision in *Villarreal v. Texas* does not resolve the materially different other-acts question.
