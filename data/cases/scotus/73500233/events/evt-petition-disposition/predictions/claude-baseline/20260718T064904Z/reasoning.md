# Pitzka v. Wisconsin, No. 25-1333 — petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) ≈ 0.012.**

## The case

Mark Pitzka was convicted of arson in Waukesha County, Wisconsin, for a 2018
fire at his ex-girlfriend's investment house. Before trial, the prosecution
was allowed to introduce, as "other acts" evidence under Wis. Stat. § 904.04(2),
the allegations underlying a *separate, still-pending* arson prosecution
against him in Dodge County (a garage fire three days after the first). After
the Waukesha conviction, the Dodge County case was dismissed. The Wisconsin
Court of Appeals affirmed in an unpublished per curiam decision (June 25,
2025), applying the ordinary *Sullivan* other-acts test; the Wisconsin Supreme
Court denied review (February 12, 2026).

## The question presented

Whether admitting other-acts evidence that is the subject of a pending,
unresolved criminal prosecution in another contemporaneous trial violates the
Fifth, Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments — the theory being that the
preponderance gateway finding (*Huddleston*/*Dowling*) forces a court to
prejudge the pending charge, and that cross-examination exposure forces the
defendant to choose between testifying in trial one and preserving his silence
and counsel rights in trial two. The petition also asks the Court to hold such
error structural under *Weaver v. Massachusetts*, and leans on the
attorney-consultation discussion in *Villareal v. Texas*, 146 S. Ct. 756
(2026).

## Why this is a near-certain denial

**Base rate.** A modern paid petition faces roughly a 2.5–3% grant rate
(statpack, Terms 2023–2025); petitions at zero relists resolve granted only
~0.8% of the time, and this one has a single distribution — to the
September 28, 2026 long conference, where the deny rate is at its seasonal
peak. State intermediate-court criminal petitions in the corpus's
originating-court table grant at essentially zero.

**The waiver is close to dispositive on timing.** Wisconsin waived its
response on June 2, 2026, and as of the July 18 snapshot the Court has not
called for one. Under the Court's uniform practice it does not grant certiorari
without first requesting a response; a grant from the current posture is
effectively impossible, and no CFR signal exists yet.

**No split.** The petition candidly concedes that "there appears to be no
clear legal precedent either approving or disproving" the practice it
challenges. That is an argument for the question's novelty, but the Court's
dominant cert criterion is conflict among lower courts, and the petition
identifies none — no circuit split, no state-high-court split, not even a
contrary decision.

**Vehicle problems.** (1) Trial counsel did not object to admission of the
Dodge County allegations; the federal claim traveled through Wisconsin's
courts wrapped in an ineffective-assistance framing, raising
preservation/adequate-state-ground doubts. (2) The court of appeals resolved
the case under the state-law *Sullivan* test and "declin[ed] to comment" on
the federal constitutional theory, so there is no reasoned lower-court
decision on the QP to review. (3) The decision below is an unpublished per
curiam. (4) The Dodge County case was dismissed after the Waukesha verdict,
which blunts the live-harm narrative. (5) *Dowling v. United States* already
rejected a due-process challenge to other-acts evidence from an *acquitted*
charge, which cuts against the petitioner a fortiori on the presumption-of-
innocence theory; the Fifth/Sixth Amendment compelled-election theory is more
novel but was never passed on below.

**Nothing on the grant side of the ledger beyond competence.** Paid petition,
capable private counsel (Gimbel Reilly), a timely cite to the Court's fresh
*Villareal* decision — but no amici, no CVSG, no companion case, no
salience. Corpus priors for recent grants (pulled via `fedcourts query`) show
the opposite profile: multiple distributions/relists, marquee counsel, or
high-salience subject matter.

**GVR check.** *Villareal* concerned restrictions on attorney consultation
during a testifying defendant's recess — it does not control the admissibility
question here, and Pitzka did not testify under a restriction order. No
plausible intervening-decision GVR path exists, so P(gvr) is negligible and is
folded into the 0.012.

## Probability

Anchor at the 0-relist bucket (~0.8%), nudge up slightly for a paid,
counsel-drafted petition raising a cleanly framed constitutional question, and
hold well below the paid-petition average for the waived response, the absence
of any split, and the stacked vehicle flaws: **P(grant) ≈ 0.012**, disposition
**denied** — most likely at or shortly after the September 28, 2026 long
conference.

`big_case_score` = 0.12: the QP, if ever granted in some case, would touch
other-acts practice in every state, but this particular case is a single-
defendant state arson appeal with no public profile, no amici, and no
government interest on the petition side.
