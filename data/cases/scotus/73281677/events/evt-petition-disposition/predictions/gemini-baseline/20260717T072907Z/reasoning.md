## Legal Question
Whether military courts of criminal appeals have authority under 10 U.S.C. 866(d)(2) to review an "indorsement" accompanying a judgment and stating that convictions triggered 18 U.S.C. 922's prohibition on the possession of firearms.

## Analysis
The petition raises a narrow jurisdictional question concerning military appellate procedure: whether the Air Force Court of Criminal Appeals (AFCCA) and the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces (CAAF) have the authority to correct erroneous firearm-possession indorsements made after the Entry of Judgment.

The probability of a cert grant here is exceptionally low for two independent reasons. First, the Supreme Court very recently denied certiorari in cases raising the exact same legal issue. The government's brief in opposition highlights *United States v. Johnson*, 86 M.J. 8 (C.A.A.F. 2025), cert. denied, No. 25-682 (Jan. 12, 2026), and *Schneider v. United States*, cert. denied, No. 25-685 (Jan. 12, 2026). The denial of cert in identical cases just months prior is a definitive signal that the Court has no interest in reviewing this specific question.

Second, the issue is essentially moot for future cases. The Department of the Air Force issued a Guidance Memorandum to Instruction 51-201 on February 4, 2026, which removes the requirement for indorsements on court-martial documents entirely. Because the underlying administrative practice that gave rise to the dispute has been abolished, this case is a poor vehicle for resolving a recurring issue of national importance, stripping it of the "certworthy" characteristic of resolving an active conflict. 

Given these factors—recent identical cert denials and the prospective obsolescence of the challenged administrative practice—the petition is overwhelmingly likely to be denied.

## Conclusion
Probability of grant: 1%
Predicted disposition: Denied
