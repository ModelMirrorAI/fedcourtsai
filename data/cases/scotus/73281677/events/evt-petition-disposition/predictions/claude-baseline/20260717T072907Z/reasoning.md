# Myslow v. United States, No. 25-1148 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) ≈ 0.01.**

## The case

Second Lieutenant Parker C. Myslow pleaded guilty at an Air Force general
court-martial to using delta-8 and delta-9 THC (violation of a general
regulation, Art. 92 UCMJ, and wrongful use of a controlled substance,
Art. 112a UCMJ) and was sentenced to dismissal. Under then-operative Air Force
regulations, a judge advocate signed a "First Indorsement" to the Entry of
Judgment indexing him in NICS as subject to 18 U.S.C. § 922's firearm
prohibition. The AFCCA affirmed without relief, citing the CAAF's decision in
*United States v. Johnson*, 86 M.J. 8 (C.A.A.F. 2025), which held that the
indorsement is part of the entry of judgment and therefore outside the CCAs'
Article 66(d)(2), 10 U.S.C. § 866(d)(2), authority to correct post-judgment
processing errors. The CAAF denied review on January 6, 2026.

The question presented: whether military courts of criminal appeals have
authority under § 866(d)(2) to correct an assertedly unconstitutional firearms
ban annotated after entry of judgment.

## Why denial is near-certain

1. **The Court denied this exact question presented twice this Term.** The
   petition's own footnote 1 and the BIO both recount that certiorari was
   denied in *Schneider v. United States*, No. 25-685 (Jan. 12, 2026) — a
   petition consolidating **thirteen** cases raising this QP — and in
   *Dominguez-Garcia*, No. 25-730 (consolidating three more). Cert was also
   denied in *Johnson* itself, No. 25-682 (Jan. 12, 2026). Myslow is a serial
   follow-on from the same Air Force Appellate Defense Division, with no new
   feature distinguishing it from the sixteen-plus cases just turned away.
   Nothing has changed since January except a development that cuts *against*
   review (point 3).

2. **No conflict of authority on the QP.** The CAAF is the only appellate
   court that construes Article 66(d)(2) and the Air Force indorsement
   practice, so there is no split to resolve — only asserted error. The
   petition leans on the broader circuit conflict over § 922(g)(1) as-applied
   challenges (*Range*, *Williams* (6th Cir.) vs. the Second, Fourth, Eighth,
   Ninth, Tenth, and Eleventh Circuits), but concedes that split is not the
   issue presented; and the Court has been denying those petitions too
   (*Vincent v. Bondi*, No. 24-1155, denied Mar. 2, 2026, per the BIO).

3. **The issue is evaporating prospectively.** A February 4, 2026 Air Force
   guidance memorandum removed First Indorsements from courts-martial
   documents. Both sides acknowledge it; the BIO argues it "further
   diminishes any importance of the question presented." A grant would decide
   the reviewability of a document the service no longer produces.

4. **Vehicle and jurisdictional wrinkles.** The CAAF *denied* the petition
   for grant of review; 28 U.S.C. § 1259(3), which petitioner invokes, covers
   cases in which the CAAF *granted* review. The BIO does not press the
   point, but the posture at minimum adds a vehicle question, and petitions
   from CAAF denials are denied essentially without exception. The SG also
   identifies alternative remedies (R.C.M. correction mechanisms, Air Force
   Manual 71-102 expungement, 18 U.S.C. § 925(c) restoration, a stand-alone
   civil suit), further weakening the "no forum" framing.

5. **Docket posture carries no grant signal.** Paid petition, Term 2025;
   BIO filed after a routine one-month extension; reply filed June 15, 2026;
   distributed June 17 for the September 28, 2026 long conference. No relist
   yet (relist-0 petitions grant at ≈0.8% in the corpus statpack), no CVSG,
   no amicus activity visible on the docket.

## Base rate and adjustment

The statpack's modern discretionary-cert base rate is ≈2.5–3% granted
(Term 2025: 2.5%), with paid petitions running above IFP. From that anchor I
adjust sharply downward: an identical-QP denial covering sixteen consolidated
cases in the same Term is about as strong a deny signal as the record can
carry, reinforced by the absence of a split, the mooting regulatory change,
and the jurisdictional posture. A GVR is implausible — there is no intervening
decision to GVR in light of (the recent denials point the other way). I land
at **P(grant) ≈ 0.01, disposition: denied**. The residual probability mostly
covers a hold-and-GVR or grant surprise if the Court unexpectedly takes the
pending companion *Zhong* (No. 25-742) or a § 922(g)(1) split case and holds
this one — which would still most likely end in a post-hold denial.

## Big-case score

0.12. Second Amendment adjacency and a military-justice uniformity theme give
it modest salience, but the question is procedural (which court may review an
indexing memo), the practice has been discontinued, and the Court has shown —
twice — that it does not regard the issue as certworthy. If somehow decided,
the direct impact would be confined to a closed class of Air Force
courts-martial.
