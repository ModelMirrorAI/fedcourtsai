# Prediction: denial

I predict denial, with a 0.8% probability of any grant (including a GVR). The likely disposition is a straightforward denial rather than dismissal or GVR.

## Question and record

The petition asks whether a military court of criminal appeals has authority under Article 66(d)(2), 10 U.S.C. § 866(d)(2), to correct an allegedly unconstitutional firearm prohibition recorded in an Air Force “First Indorsement” after the military judge signed the entry of judgment. Myslow pleaded guilty to repeated use of delta-8 and delta-9 THC, received a dismissal from service, and was marked as subject to a firearm prohibition under 18 U.S.C. § 922. The Air Force Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed without discussing that claim, relying on *United States v. Johnson*, and the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces denied review.

The petition’s best argument is textual and procedural: Article 66(d)(2) permits relief for errors in court-martial processing after judgment is entered, the military judge’s signed entry of judgment appears to be the terminal judicial act, and a different judge advocate later signs the First Indorsement. A separate opinion in *Johnson* agreed that the military courts could not act on the collateral consequence but questioned the majority’s theory of when judgment is entered. The asserted underlying injury—a lifetime firearm disability for nonviolent drug use—also gives the petition constitutional salience.

## Certiorari assessment

The opposition presents unusually strong denial signals:

- The core question concerns the authority of military appellate courts, and the petition identifies no split among courts that can decide that question. The decision below is nonprecedential, while CAAF’s contrary rule is already stated in its published *Johnson* decision.
- The Court recently denied *Johnson* itself and a petition consolidating thirteen cases raising the same question. The petition also acknowledges those denials. Repetition without a material doctrinal change strongly disfavors review.
- The Air Force removed First Indorsements from court-martial documents in February 2026. Legacy consequences remain, but the change sharply reduces the question’s prospective importance and makes the dispute a poor vehicle for broad review.
- Myslow’s Second Amendment theory does not cure the vehicle problem. CAAF did not reach the constitutional merits; the question presented is appellate authority, and the opposition identifies administrative correction, database modification, restoration, and stand-alone civil litigation as alternative routes.
- There is no intervening judgment that would support a GVR, and the provisioned record shows no CVSG or relist signal. The case is merely distributed for its first conference.

Those are broad anchors. The repeated same-question denials and obsolescence of the challenged procedure justify a substantial downward adjustment to 0.8% here.
