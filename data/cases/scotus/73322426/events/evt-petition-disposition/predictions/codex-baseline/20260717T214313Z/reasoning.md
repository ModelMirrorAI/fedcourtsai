# Prediction: denied

I assign a 27% probability to a grant (including any grant-side disposition) and predict denial as the most likely disposition.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks what standard governs a prosecution witness's live testimony by two-way video under the Sixth Amendment's Confrontation Clause. The petition describes three approaches. Most courts apply *Maryland v. Craig*, which permits dispensing with physical face-to-face confrontation when that is necessary to further an important public policy and the procedure otherwise preserves confrontation's protections. Some courts confine *Craig* to its child-witness facts and apply *Crawford v. Washington*'s unavailability and prior-cross-examination framework. The Second Circuit applies its separate exceptional-circumstances/interests-of-justice test from *United States v. Gigante*.

The Montana Supreme Court applied *Craig*. It held that remote testimony was necessary because the 86-year-old witness lived about 450 miles away and needed to remain home to care for his family, and that two-way video preserved the hallmarks of confrontation. The petition says the witness was nevertheless legally available and that Johnson had no prior opportunity to cross-examine him, so the choice between *Craig* and *Crawford* is outcome-determinative.

## Grant signals

The question is recurring and nationally important because two-way video is now easy to deploy in criminal trials. The petition identifies a developed disagreement among state high courts and federal circuits, including decisions expressly recognizing tension between *Craig* and *Crawford*. A CourtListener search for preexisting decisions about two-way-video Confrontation Clause issues returned, among others, the Missouri Supreme Court's *State v. Smith*, the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals' *Haggard*, the Montana Supreme Court's *Mercier*, and Alabama's *Bragg*, corroborating that this is not merely a one-case disagreement.

The vehicle appears clean: final state-court review, a preserved federal constitutional issue, and facts under which the selected legal test changes the answer. The petition is paid and professionally presented. Most importantly, after initial distribution the Court requested Montana's response. That is a meaningful positive signal because at least one Justice or chambers wanted adversarial briefing rather than allowing disposition on a waiver or nonresponse.

## Reasons denial remains more likely

The base rate is demanding. The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 2.49% rate across all resolved petitions that Term. This petition has no relist or CVSG signal as of the July 17 snapshot. A requested response raises the case well above the paid-petition baseline, but most response requests still do not produce four votes to grant.

There are also doctrinal obstacles. *Craig* remains an on-point Supreme Court precedent that *Crawford* did not mention or expressly overrule. Much of the claimed split can be characterized as disagreement over how narrowly lower courts should read binding precedent rather than a conventional conflict over a rule the Court has left wholly open. The Court has previously declined opportunities involving remote testimony, and it may await the State's vehicle objections, a federal-circuit conflict with sharper facts, or a case presenting a more consequential departure from in-person confrontation. The provisioned record contains the petition but no brief in opposition; Montana's response is not due until September 4, so potential waiver, preservation, harmless-error, or record-specific objections cannot yet be assessed.

Balancing the strong response-request, split, importance, and vehicle signals against the low base rate and the Court's ability to leave *Craig* in place, I estimate 27% for any grant-side outcome. If the Court grants, ordinary plenary review is much more plausible than a GVR because there is no identified intervening precedent; nevertheless, denial remains the single most likely disposition.
