# Prediction Reasoning: Johnson v. Montana (25-1273)

## Legal Question & Standard
The petition presents a classic, clean split over the Confrontation Clause of the Sixth Amendment: specifically, whether a prosecution witness may testify by two-way video, and what standard governs that assessment. The petitioner argues that some jurisdictions erroneously follow the "important public policy" exception of *Maryland v. Craig*, 497 U.S. 836 (1990), while others correctly apply the "unavailability and prior cross-examination" mandate of *Crawford v. Washington*, 541 U.S. 36 (2004). 

## Facts & Posture
The petitioner was convicted in Montana state court. At trial, the court permitted a key witness (Eugene Maw, an 86-year-old living in Washington State) to testify via two-way video, citing his age, distance, and family caretaking responsibilities. The Montana Supreme Court affirmed the conviction, expressly applying the *Craig* standard and concluding that *Crawford* does not apply because the witness was technically "available" to testify (just not in person).

## Analysis & Base Rates
This is an exceptionally strong vehicle for a recurring issue that has only gained prominence since the COVID-19 pandemic normalization of remote proceedings. The petition, filed by the UCLA Supreme Court Clinic, thoroughly documents a deep divide among federal circuits and state supreme courts on whether *Craig* survives *Crawford* in the context of two-way video.

A look at the docket confirms that the Court has requested a response (due September 4, 2026, after an extension). A "Response Requested" is a strong pre-grant signal, moving the petition past the "dead list." 

However, the baseline grant rate for all paid cert petitions remains around 3% overall, and even among petitions where a response is requested or filed, the vast majority are still denied. The Court has historically dodged the *Craig* vs. *Crawford* video testimony issue (e.g., denying cert in *Weigand v. United States* in 2023, and earlier in *Wrotten v. New York*). Even though the issue is extremely ripe, the sheer numerical odds strongly disfavor a grant for any individual petition.

## Conclusion
While this petition presents a very strong candidate for certiorari on an important and divided constitutional question, the baseline rate of denial is too high to predict a grant outright. Therefore, the predicted disposition is `denied`, but with a significantly elevated probability of `0.15` (15%) compared to the standard 2-3% base rate, reflecting the deep split, the high-quality lawyering, and the Court's request for a response.
