# Johnson v. Montana, No. 25-1273 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied (P(grant, GVR included) = 0.18).**

## The legal question

The petition asks what standard governs whether the Confrontation Clause
permits a prosecution witness to testify by two-way video: the
public-policy-plus-reliability balancing of *Maryland v. Craig*, 497 U.S. 836
(1990), or the unavailability-plus-prior-cross-examination rule of *Crawford
v. Washington*, 541 U.S. 36 (2004) (with the Second Circuit's *Gigante*
"exceptional circumstances" test as a third outlier). The Montana Supreme
Court applied *Craig* and affirmed Johnson's convictions after an 86-year-old
witness, who had moved 450 miles away and cared for his family, testified by
two-way video over Johnson's Confrontation Clause objection.

## Facts from the snapshot and provisioned documents that drive the call

- **Paid petition, expert counsel.** Docketed May 11, 2026 (Term 2025, case
  type "Paid"). Counsel of record is Stuart Banner of the UCLA School of Law
  Supreme Court Clinic — a repeat-player cert shop that selects vehicles
  deliberately.
- **Call for response.** Montana apparently did not respond by the June 10
  deadline; the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 (long)
  conference on June 24, and on **July 6, 2026 the Court requested a
  response** (CFR), then extended the BIO deadline to September 4, 2026. A
  CFR means at least one chambers wanted the state's answer before acting —
  the strongest pre-conference docket signal available, and it guarantees the
  case will be redistributed after the BIO lands (likely an October/November
  2026 conference).
- **The split is real, mature, and acknowledged.** Michigan (*Jemison*) and
  Missouri (*Smith*) confine *Craig* to its facts and apply *Crawford* to
  adult two-way-video testimony; the Fifth, Sixth, Seventh, and Ninth
  Circuits plus Montana, Ohio, Kentucky, Minnesota, Texas CCA, New Mexico,
  Utah, California, W. Va., and the en banc Eleventh Circuit stay with
  *Craig*; the Second Circuit uses its own *Gigante* standard. Chief Judge
  Sutton's *Cox* concurrence and Justice Sotomayor's statement respecting
  denial in *Wrotten v. New York* (2010) both flag the unresolved tension.
- **Vehicle quality is high.** Direct review of a final state-court criminal
  judgment; the objection was preserved; and the Montana Supreme Court
  expressly found the witness *available* (App. 11a), so *Crawford* vs.
  *Craig* is outcome-determinative — the state cannot satisfy *Crawford*'s
  unavailability or prior-cross prongs. The video accommodation rested on
  witness convenience, presenting the question in its starkest form. The
  petition distinguishes *Weigand* (2023), where the petitioner lost under
  either standard.

## Why denial is still the modal outcome

- **Base rates.** The committed statpack's modern discretionary-cert slice
  puts the overall grant rate at ~2.5–3.3% per recent Term, and **paid**
  petitions in Term 2025 at ~5.4%. Even generous CFR multipliers (roughly
  3–4× in published studies of paid petitions) leave the majority of
  called-for-response cases denied. Relist-count cuts (0 relists → 0.8%
  grant; 2 relists → 34%) show most of the resolution comes later, at
  conference — as of this snapshot the case has not yet been conferenced.
- **The Court has ducked this precise question for 15+ years.** *Wrotten*
  (2010, interlocutory) and *Weigand* (2023, vehicle problem) were denied,
  along with the steady stream of post-COVID remote-testimony petitions the
  petition itself catalogs. Granting requires confronting whether *Craig*
  survives *Crawford* — effectively inviting the overruling of a precedent —
  a step the Court has repeatedly declined despite the acknowledged tension.
- **The BIO is unwritten.** Montana's Solicitor General may raise
  harmless-error or fact-bound objections that degrade the vehicle; no amici
  have yet appeared on the docket.

## Net assessment

The CFR, the clinic counsel, the genuinely split and percolated question, and
the outcome-determinative posture stack this well above the ~5% paid-petition
baseline; the Court's long pattern of denying this exact question and the
unconditioned uncertainty of the coming BIO keep it well below even odds. I
set **P(grant, including any GVR) at 0.18**, with denial as the predicted
disposition. A GVR is implausible here (no intervening decision to remand in
light of), so essentially all of the 0.18 is plenary-grant probability. If
granted, likely interested votes include Gorsuch and Thomas (Confrontation
Clause originalists) and Sotomayor (who flagged the issue in *Wrotten*), but
per-justice cert votes are not observable, so I record no vote predictions.

Timing expectation: BIO September 4, 2026 → redistribution → resolution at an
October–December 2026 conference, consistent with the statpack's ~62-day
median but extended by the CFR cycle.

## Big-case score

0.5 — a grant would set the constitutional standard for remote testimony in
every criminal courtroom in the country and revisit *Craig*; doctrinally
significant and watched by the criminal-law bar, but a low-politics,
low-headline dispute compared to structural or culture-war grants.
