# Prediction

I predict denial, narrowly, with a **47% probability of any grant**. If the Court grants, a plenary grant is much more likely than a GVR because no intervening authority supplies a straightforward remand rule.

## Question and posture

The provisioned questions-presented text asks whether the Supremacy Clause permits Washington to treat federal immigration detainees participating in a work program at a privately operated ICE facility as employees covered by the state minimum wage. The petition frames the case as direct regulation of and discrimination against federal operations, with a separate preemption theory. The snapshot dated July 16, 2026 shows a paid petition from the Ninth Circuit, a first distribution for the May 14 conference, and a May 18 invitation for the Solicitor General to express the views of the United States. No SG brief or disposition appears in that snapshot.

The intergovernmental-immunity standard bars state laws that directly regulate the Federal Government or discriminate against it or those with whom it deals. Modern doctrine nevertheless distinguishes a state law that merely increases a contractor's costs from one that controls or substantially interferes with federal operations. Ordinary certiorari considerations also require a genuine conflict, recurring importance, and a clean vehicle rather than factbound error correction.

## Grant-positive signals

The CVSG is the strongest signal. The committed statpack estimates a 27.1% grant rate among resolved modern CVSG petitions, compared with 5.4% for paid 2025-Term petitions and 3.0% for petitions originating in the Ninth Circuit. This petition deserves an upward adjustment from the CVSG bucket because the United States supported GEO's Supremacy Clause position in the lower courts across three administrations, making a grant recommendation more plausible than in the typical CVSG case.

The petition also has unusually strong conventional markers: seven Ninth Circuit judges opposed denial of rehearing en banc; the federal government says the ruling interferes with a nationwide immigration program; the judgment is approximately $37 million; the Tacoma work program ceased; and experienced Supreme Court counsel presents a recurring question about state regulation of federal contractors. Those considerations make this much closer than an ordinary cert petition.

## Grant-negative signals

The provisioned brief in opposition substantially weakens the claimed split. It explains that the Third Circuit in *CoreCivic v. Governor of New Jersey* expressly aligned its rule with the Ninth Circuit and distinguished this case as a contractor-cost burden rather than substantial interference with federal operations. The Second- and Fourth-Circuit authorities likewise involved state controls over classified federal construction or federal contractor selection, not a generally applicable wage floor. That makes the dispute look like application of a shared standard to a particular contract and factual record.

The record and vehicle also favor denial. GEO's ICE contract required compliance with applicable state labor laws, set $1 per day as a floor rather than an express maximum, and left GEO operational control over the detainee work. The respondents say the evidence showed GEO could remain profitable while paying minimum wage. Washington's highest court construed the state law to apply equally to private state and federal detention contractors, complicating the discrimination theory. The preemption theory has no identified circuit split and the practical-conflict version may not have been preserved below.

Finally, the provisioned BIO and CourtListener's February 25, 2026 opinion in *GEO Group, Inc. v. Menocal* show that the Court recently rejected the broad proposition that the Government transfers sovereign immunity to contractors merely because they do government work. *Menocal* addressed Yearsley and interlocutory appeal rather than intergovernmental immunity, so it does not control this petition. But its near-unanimous reasoning cuts against GEO's broad contractor-steps-into-the-Government's-shoes framing. Importantly, the Court requested the SG's views after *Menocal* and after receiving the BIO, so this is a meaningful negative rather than a reason to collapse the probability to the ordinary base rate.

## Calibration

The CVSG and likely executive-branch support bring the case close to four votes, but the weak conflict, contract-specific facts, preservation concerns, and *Menocal* make denial slightly more likely before the SG files. I therefore assign 47% to any grant and 53% to denial. I do not predict individual cert votes because they are not public and the pending SG recommendation is likely decisive.
