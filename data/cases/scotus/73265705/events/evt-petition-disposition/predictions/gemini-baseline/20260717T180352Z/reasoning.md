# Reasoning: Google LLC v. VirtaMove, Corp., et al.

## Legal Question and Context
This case presents two related questions arising from the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office's (PTO) policy of refusing to institute *inter partes review* (IPR) for patents that have been in force for six years, citing "settled expectations." 
The questions presented are:
1. Whether the PTO lacks statutory authority to deny institution based on "settled expectations" where the patent statutes allow for administrative review at any time during the life of a patent.
2. Whether courts have the power to review a PTO decision denying *inter partes review* on grounds that are contrary to statute.

The second question implicates the reviewability bar under 35 U.S.C. 314(d), and asks the Court to clarify the boundaries of its holdings in *Cuozzo Speed Technologies* and *Thryv*, which barred most review of institution decisions but left open challenges based on constitutional questions or decisions completely outside statutory bounds. 

## Case Facts and Procedural Posture
Google's petition challenges the PTO's denial policy based on "settled expectations." The petition was filed on April 27, 2026. The U.S. PTO, represented by the Solicitor General, initially waived its right to respond. Despite this waiver, the Supreme Court called for a response (CFR) on June 12, 2026. The Solicitor General sought an extension to August 12, 2026, indicating the government intends to actively defend the PTO policy. VirtaMove filed a Brief in Opposition on July 13.

## Salience and Signals
The case displays exceptional salience and highly favorable signals for a cert grant:
*   **Amici Curiae:** Seven amicus briefs were filed at the petition stage supporting Google, including those from Unified Patents, 35 IP Law Professors, the PTAB Bar Association, the Association for Accessible Medicines, and several tech alliances. This broad cross-industry consensus at the cert stage is a massive signal of nationwide importance.
*   **Call for Response (CFR):** The Court's affirmative request for a response following a waiver by the federal government demonstrates that at least one Justice considers the issue worthy of closer inspection.
*   **Base Rates:** A typical paid petition has a grant rate of ~2-3%. However, petitions that secure multiple cert-stage amicus briefs and receive a CFR have historically seen grant rates rise sharply. Given the sweeping administrative law implications of the PTO allegedly inventing a new non-statutory bar to IPRs, the likelihood of Supreme Court intervention is very high.

## Prediction
The combination of an allegedly *ultra vires* agency policy, massive cross-industry cert-stage amicus support, and the Court's affirmative Call for Response strongly indicates that the Court will take up this case to clarify the limits of 35 U.S.C. 314(d).
