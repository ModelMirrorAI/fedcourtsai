# Qualitative Reasoning: Google LLC v. VirtaMove, Corp. (SCOTUS No. 25-1230)

## Executive Summary
This case presents an exceptionally strong candidate for a writ of certiorari at the petition stage. The petitioner, Google LLC, challenges a non-statutory, agency-created "settled expectations" doctrine of the Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB), which acts as a six-year statute of limitations on *inter partes* review (IPR) challenges—a limitation completely absent from the America Invents Act (AIA). 

Given the massive industry and academic support (seven distinct amicus briefs filed at the petition stage), the Supreme Court's active interest (recalling its waiver and requesting a formal response from respondents), and the current administrative law landscape (which, following *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, is deeply skeptical of unauthorized agency discretion), we predict a **granted** petition with a probability of **0.58** (58%).

---

## 1. Questions Presented & Substantive Legal Standards
The questions presented in Google's petition are:
1. **Statutory Authority:** Whether the USPTO lacks statutory authority to deny institution of IPR based on a patent owner's "settled expectations" where the patent statutes expressly allow for administrative review at any point during the term of a patent.
2. **Judicial Review:** Whether federal courts have the power to review a USPTO decision denying IPR on grounds that are contrary to the plain text of the governing statute.

### The "Settled Expectations" Controversy
Under the America Invents Act, Congress established that a third party may petition for IPR to challenge the validity of an issued patent at any point during its active term (subject to specific, enumerated statutory bars such as the one-year litigation bar in 35 U.S.C. § 315(b)). 

However, the PTAB has increasingly relied on an unwritten, self-declared "settled expectations" doctrine (often applied to patents in force for six or more years) to deny IPR petitions. The Board reasons that long-standing patents acquire a presumption of repose that makes review an inefficient use of agency resources. Google argues that this is an ultra vires, non-statutory statute of limitations that directly subverts the congressional design of the AIA, which was meant to allow the weeding out of invalid patents at any point in their life cycle.

### The Judicial Review Dilemma
Under 35 U.S.C. § 314(d), PTAB decisions on whether to institute IPR are "final and nonappealable." While the Supreme Court in *Cuozzo Speed Technologies LLC v. Lee* (2016) and *Thryv, Inc. v. Click-to-Call Technologies, LP* (2020) upheld this bar for ordinary technical or procedural institution decisions, it left a narrow exception for constitutional challenges or claims that the agency acted in clear excess of its statutory jurisdiction (ultra vires acts). Google argues that the Board's creation of a "settled expectations" bar falls squarely within this exception, and that Article III courts must have the power to police such flagrant statutory departures.

## 2. Strong Strategic and Procedural Indicators for a Grant

### A. Response Requested After Initial Waiver (Highly Compelling Signal)
Following the filing of Google's petition on April 27, 2026, the USPTO initially waived its right to file a response on May 22, 2026. Rather than allowing the petition to go to conference unopposed (where it would have almost certainly been denied), the Supreme Court **requested a response** on June 12, 2026. 

A "Response Requested" order is one of the highest-signal indicators at the petition stage. While the baseline grant rate for ordinary petitions is roughly 1.4%, petitions where the Court actively requests a response after a waiver see their grant probability rise dramatically (historically to around 10% to 15%). It indicates that at least one, and typically multiple, Justices are actively tracking the petition and find the questions worthy of serious consideration.

### B. Massive Amicus Support at the Petition Stage
An extraordinary volume of amicus curiae briefs has been filed in support of Google's petition. At the petition stage, seven distinct briefs were submitted on May 29, 2026:
1. **The PTAB Bar Association** (representing patent practitioners)
2. **Unified Patents, LLC** (challenging patent quality)
3. **Association for Accessible Medicines** (generic drug makers relying on IPRs to clear invalid patents)
4. **Askeladden L.L.C.**
5. **35 Intellectual Property Law Professors** (addressing the constitutional and administrative law overreach)
6. **US*Made, Alliance for Automotive Innovation, High Tech Inventors Alliance, CCIA, SIIA, and NRF** (representing major sectors of the U.S. economy)
7. **Association for Competitive Technology** (representing small software and app developers)

This is a massive show of force. Empirical legal research demonstrates that a petition with even a single amicus brief has a vastly superior grant rate than one without. A petition supported by **seven amicus briefs** from highly diverse industries (tech, automotive, retail, pharmaceuticals, academics, and the PTAB bar itself) represents a broad consensus that the USPTO's policy is a recurring, high-stakes drag on the patent system. This amicus pressure elevates the case to the top tier of certworthiness.

### C. The Post-Loper Bright Skepticism of Agency Power
The petition arrives in mid-2026, a period defined by the Supreme Court’s aggressive policing of federal agency overreach. Following *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024), which dismantled *Chevron* deference, the Court has repeatedly signaled its intent to assert independent judicial review over statutory interpretation and to cabin agency power within strict, explicit statutory bounds. 

The PTAB’s "settled expectations" doctrine is a classic administrative law overreach: an agency creating an unwritten, extrastatutory defense to administrative review that directly contradicts a statutory scheme. This is precisely the kind of agency-policing case that the current Court's majority is eager to take.
