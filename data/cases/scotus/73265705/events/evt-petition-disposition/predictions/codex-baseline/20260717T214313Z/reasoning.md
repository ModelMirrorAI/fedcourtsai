# Prediction: denied

I assign a 22% probability to any grant and predict denial. If the Court does grant, a plenary grant is much more likely than a GVR because the record identifies no intervening authority or mootness development that would support vacatur and remand.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the PTO may deny inter partes review based on a patent owner's purportedly settled expectations after a patent has been in force for six years, and whether a court may review such a denial as exceeding statutory authority despite 35 U.S.C. § 314(d) and 5 U.S.C. § 701(a)(2). The certiorari considerations are the usual ones reflected in Rule 10: a square conflict, an important and recurring federal question, or a serious departure from accepted law. Because the judgment arose from denial of mandamus, Google also must overcome the demanding requirement of a clear and indisputable right with no adequate alternative remedy.

## Record signals

The provisioned questions presented and full petition frame a substantial separation-of-powers and patent-administration issue. Google argues that the PTO effectively invented a limitations period Congress omitted, that *Loper Bright* preserves judicial responsibility to police statutory boundaries, and that *Cuozzo*, *SAS Institute*, and *Thryv* leave review available for agency action outside statutory limits. It also alleges disagreement with approaches taken by the D.C., First, and Second Circuits under § 701(a)(2).

The full brief in opposition presents substantial obstacles. VirtaMove argues that § 314(a) limits when the Director may institute review but never commands institution or restricts denial factors; § 314(d) makes the institution determination final and nonappealable; and Google therefore cannot show a clear statutory violation for mandamus. It also identifies an alternative APA notice-and-comment action, says the PTO's order rested on a holistic assessment rather than patent age alone, and disputes the claimed de facto six-year rule with data showing only a small difference in discretionary-denial rates for Google's challenges to older patents. Those points create both vehicle problems and reasons for the Court to await a cleaner case with a fuller lower-court opinion.

Interest signals are unusually strong. The July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition, seven supporting amicus filings from industry groups, patent practitioners, and academics, and a response requested after the petition was initially distributed. The case concerns a newly consequential PTO policy and recurring access to IPR, not merely error correction. Conversely, the docket shows no completed relist or CVSG. The PTO is itself a respondent, initially waived response, and obtained an extension through August 12, so the government's position was not yet before the Court at the prediction date. That unresolved filing is a meaningful source of uncertainty.

## Probability assessment

The committed statpack puts the modern cert grant rate near 3%, the 2025 Term paid-petition rate near 5.4%, and the Federal Circuit modern-cert rate near 3%. Relists sharply increase the historical rate, but this petition had not yet reached that posture. I raise the forecast well above those baselines because of the requested response, numerous amici, national consequences, and the Court's repeated engagement with IPR reviewability. I stop at 22% because the asserted inter-circuit disagreement is not over the same statutory setting, the Federal Circuit's short mandamus disposition offers little merits analysis, an alternative APA vehicle remains possible, and VirtaMove disputes both the factual premise and dispositive role of the settled-expectations factor.

No individual cert votes are predicted. Four votes could plausibly be attracted to the agency-authority question, but the record does not support justice-specific estimates.

The big-case score is 0.78. A merits decision could affect many patent challenges and broader administrative-law doctrine, although the dispute is technical and less publicly salient than the Court's largest constitutional or social-policy cases.

I did not seek or consult this case's disposition.
