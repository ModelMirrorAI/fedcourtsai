# Google LLC v. VirtaMove, Corp., No. 25-1230 — cert disposition prediction

**Prediction: deny, with a substantial minority probability of a grant — P(grant) = 0.30.**

## The case

Google petitions from the Federal Circuit's denial of mandamus (*In re Google
LLC*, No. 2026-111, Jan. 27, 2026, relying on *In re Cambridge Industries*,
Dec. 9, 2025). The PTO denied institution of Google's IPR petitions against a
VirtaMove patent solely on the ground that the patent had "been in force for
more than 14 years, creating strong settled expectations" — an application of
the Acting Director's March 2025 memorandum making "settled expectations" (a
patent's age, presumptively six-plus years) a discretionary-denial factor. The
questions presented are (1) whether the PTO lacks statutory authority to deny
institution on "settled expectations," and (2) whether courts have power to
review an institution denial that rests on grounds contrary to statute.

## Signals from the docket (snapshot 2026-07-17)

Strong grant-side signals, unusually many for a cert-stage docket:

- **Call for a response.** The USPTO waived its response on May 22; the case
  was distributed for the June 25, 2026 conference; on June 12 the Court
  *requested a response* (due July 13, later extended). A CFR after waiver
  means at least one chamber is engaged — historically it multiplies the grant
  probability by an order of magnitude over the whole-docket base rate.
- **Solicitor General engagement.** The SG represents the respondent USPTO and
  obtained an extension to August 12, 2026 to respond. The government's
  defense of its own new policy will be before the Court at the long
  conference; no CVSG is needed because the government is already a party.
- **Seven cert-stage amicus briefs** supporting the petition side of the
  docket (Unified Patents, Askeladden, the PTAB Bar Association, the
  Association for Accessible Medicines, 35 IP law professors, a US*Made /
  auto-tech-retail industry coalition, and ACT | The App Association).
  Cert-stage amicus support at this level is rare and historically correlates
  with grant rates far above baseline.
- **Elite counsel** (Ginger Anders, Munger Tolles & Olson) and a paid petition
  (Term-2025 docket 25-1230, `sJsonCaseType: Paid`).
- **Importance and urgency.** The petition documents a collapse of IPR
  institution (99 of 105 petitions discretionarily denied in late 2025, per
  the petition's sources) and argues the PTO is insulating its denials from
  review by issuing unexplained decisions, so better vehicles may never come.
  Post-*Loper Bright*, the reviewability question (APA § 701(a)(2) and
  35 U.S.C. § 314(d)) is squarely in this Court's current area of interest,
  and the petition alleges a circuit conflict on § 701(a)(2).

Deny-side considerations, which keep this below 50%:

- **Mandamus posture.** The Court reviews mandamus denials only rarely; the
  "clear and indisputable right" standard overlays and muddies both QPs
  (*TC Heartland* shows it is not disqualifying, but it is a tax).
- **Adverse precedent.** *Cuozzo* (institution denial "committed to the Patent
  Office's discretion") and *Thryv* (§ 314(d) bars review even of alleged
  violations of an express statutory limit) give the Court an easy basis to
  conclude the Federal Circuit got it right, and the "shenanigans"/ultra-vires
  reservation is a narrow needle to thread for a *denial* (as opposed to an
  institution in excess of authority).
- **Vehicle problems pressed in the BIO.** The PTO's order recited a
  "holistic" totality assessment (Board-resources language beside settled
  expectations), so whether the challenged factor was dispositive is
  contestable; and the Federal Circuit expressly pointed to an alternative
  vehicle — an APA notice-and-comment challenge in district court — that
  Google has not pursued. The Court may prefer to let that route develop.
- **Track record.** The Court repeatedly denied cert on the analogous
  *Fintiv* discretionary-denial challenges (e.g., after *Apple v. Vidal*),
  despite big-tech petitioners and amicus support.
- **The government's response is not yet in.** If the SG opposes cert (likely,
  defending its own policy), that historically depresses grant odds, though
  the Court does grant over SG opposition in significant separation-of-powers
  and judicial-review cases.

## Base rates and calibration

From the committed statpack (modern discretionary-cert slice): overall grant
rate ≈ 3.1% (367 of ~11,700 resolved); Federal Circuit–originating petitions
≈ 3.0%; relist buckets show 0 relists → 0.8%, 2 relists → 33.6%, 3+ → 21.8%;
CVSG'd petitions → 27.1%. This docket has no relist yet (its first scheduled
conference was mooted by the CFR) and no CVSG, but the CFR-plus-heavy-amicus
profile sits closest to the high-signal buckets (~20–35%) rather than the
whole-docket anchor. A corpus query for recently granted 2020s SCOTUS
petitions confirms the granted set is dominated by exactly this profile:
paid, multi-distribution, high-salience petitions with repeat-player counsel.

Starting from ~20–25% for a paid CFR'd petition, I adjust up for the amicus
wave, the no-percolation/now-or-never posture (Federal Circuit exclusivity
plus unexplained future denials), and the Court's post-*Loper Bright*
appetite for judicial-review questions; I adjust down for the mandamus
posture, *Cuozzo*/*Thryv* headwinds, the holistic-denial and
alternative-APA-vehicle problems, and the *Fintiv*-era denial track record.
That lands at **P(grant, GVR included) ≈ 0.30**. A GVR is implausible here
(no intervening decision to GVR against), so the grant mass is essentially
plenary grant, likely limited to or focused on the reviewability question.
The likeliest single disposition is therefore **denied** (~0.68), with
dismissal/withdrawal residual (~0.02).

Expected timing: the SG's response is due August 12, 2026; distribution for
the late-September 2026 long conference, with a decision on cert (possibly
after relists) in fall 2026.
