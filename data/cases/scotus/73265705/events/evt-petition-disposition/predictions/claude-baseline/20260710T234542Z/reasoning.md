# Google LLC v. VirtaMove, Corp., et al. (No. 25-1230) — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied (modal outcome), P(granted) = 0.35, confidence 0.55.** This is
a `forward`-mode cell: the petition is genuinely pending (response requested, now due
mid-July 2026 with a PTO extension motion submitted today), so the prediction rests on
the pre-decision record only.

## The legal question

Google petitions for certiorari from *In re Google LLC*, No. 2026-111 (Fed. Cir.
Jan. 27, 2026), which denied mandamus relief from the PTO's refusal to institute
inter partes review of a VirtaMove patent. The PTO denied institution solely on the
ground that the patent had "been in force for more than 14 years, creating strong
settled expectations" — an application of the agency's 2025 policy (the March 2025
workload memorandum, the *Dabico* line of decisions, and the October 2025
director-institution memorandum) of presumptively denying IPR institution for patents
more than six years old. The questions presented are (1) whether the PTO lacks
statutory authority to deny institution based on "settled expectations" when the
patent statutes allow administrative review at any time during a patent's life, and
(2) whether courts have power to review an institution denial that is contrary to
statute, notwithstanding 35 U.S.C. § 314(d) and 5 U.S.C. § 701(a)(2). The Federal
Circuit held itself powerless to review the question under its *In re Cambridge
Industries* / *Mylan v. Janssen* line, which reads *Cuozzo* and *Thryv* to bar all
non-constitutional review of institution denials.

## The governing standard

Certiorari is discretionary (Rule 10); the realistic base rate for a paid petition is
a few percent, and the modal disposition is denial. The drivers that move a petition
out of the base-rate pool are: a call for response (CFR) after waiver, cert-stage
amicus support, the systemic importance of the question, vehicle quality, the absence
of further percolation, and the identity/quality of counsel.

## Facts from the snapshot that drive the outcome

Signals pushing the probability far above base rate:

1. **The Court called for a response.** The PTO (through the Solicitor General)
   waived its right to respond on May 22, 2026; the case was distributed for the
   June 25 conference; and on June 12 the Court requested a response (due July 13,
   with a PTO extension motion submitted July 10). A CFR is one of the strongest
   observable cert signals — at least one chambers wants the case briefed — and
   historically multiplies the grant probability roughly an order of magnitude above
   the paid-petition base rate.
2. **Seven cert-stage amicus briefs**, all filed at the petition stage within the
   Rule 15.8 window (Unified Patents, 35 IP law professors, Association for
   Accessible Medicines, PTAB Bar Association, Askeladden, the US*Made coalition of
   six major industry associations, and ACT | The App Association). Petitions with
   4+ cert-stage amici grant at rates dramatically above baseline; this breadth
   (tech, pharma/generics, the patent bar, academia) signals cross-industry systemic
   stakes rather than one litigant's grievance.
3. **Systemic importance.** The petition documents that the PTO's discretionary-denial
   regime has collapsed the IPR institution rate (from ~65% historically to ~37%,
   with a stretch in late 2025 in which 99 of 105 petitions were denied on
   discretionary grounds) and that "settled expectations" is the linchpin. This is a
   plausible claim that the agency has effectively nullified a major congressional
   scheme — exactly the kind of claim the current Court has been receptive to
   post-*Loper Bright*.
4. **No further percolation is possible.** The Federal Circuit has exclusive
   jurisdiction over these appeals and has settled its precedent (*In re Cambridge*,
   *In re SanDisk*); the PTO has moved to unexplained summary denials, so future
   vehicles with a clean, single-ground, patent-age-only denial (as here) are
   disappearing. The vanishing-vehicle argument is credible.
5. **Track record in this space.** The Court has repeatedly granted certiorari on
   IPR-structure questions (*Cuozzo*, *Oil States*, *SAS*, *Thryv*, *Arthrex*),
   showing sustained appetite when the question is systemic rather than
   case-specific. QP2 also maps onto the *Thryv* dissent's (Gorsuch, J.) concerns,
   quoted in the petition.
6. **Elite counsel and petitioner.** Munger, Tolles & Olson (Ginger Anders, a former
   Deputy SG) for Google — a repeat-player petitioner whose petitions are taken
   seriously.

Signals holding the probability below 50%:

1. **The modal outcome for any petition, even a CFR'd one, is still denial.** Most
   called-for-response petitions are denied; the corpus's modern-era resolved SCOTUS
   priors ran ~5% granted (1 of the top-20 2020s-era priors), and the committed
   statpack's resolved-SCOTUS base rate shows granted at ~1.4%.
2. ***Thryv* is a headwind on QP2.** The Court held 7-2 in *Thryv* that § 314(d)
   bars review of questions "closely tied" to institution, and *Cuozzo* itself said
   denial is committed to agency discretion under § 701(a)(2). The Court may view
   the Federal Circuit's rule as a faithful application of its own precedent rather
   than a misreading, and may prefer to let the political branches (a new Director,
   or Congress) fix the policy.
3. **The government will oppose.** The SG initially waived — signaling the government
   saw no cert-worthy question — and as respondent will defend both nonreviewability
   (on strong existing precedent) and the policy. The Court denies most petitions the
   SG opposes, and there is no CVSG dynamic here because the government is already a
   party.
4. **Mandamus posture.** Review of a discretionary mandamus denial is a soft vehicle
   wrinkle. *TC Heartland* shows it is not disqualifying, but it gives a denial vote
   an easy hook, and the underlying infringement litigation continues regardless.
5. **Mootness/percolation-by-policy-change risk.** The "settled expectations" policy
   is an interim-guidance-era construct; a rulemaking (the October 2025 proposal) or
   a change in PTO leadership could alter the landscape, which counsels waiting.

## Weighing

Start from a paid-petition base rate of ~2–4%. The CFR alone moves this to roughly
8–12%. The unusually deep amicus bench, the effective-nullification framing of a
statute the Court has policed five times in a decade, the admitted end of percolation
in the only circuit with jurisdiction, and the Court's post-*Loper Bright* interest
in agency-discretion boundaries together justify a further multiple — this petition
has essentially every observable feature of the grant pool short of a CVSG-following-
recommendation. Against that, *Thryv*'s squarely adverse holding, certain SG
opposition, and the always-dominant denial prior cap the estimate well below even
odds. I land at **P(granted) ≈ 0.35**, which makes **denied** the single most likely
disposition. Within the grant branch, a plain grant (possibly limited to one QP,
most plausibly QP2, reviewability) is far more likely than GVR or any other
disposition; there is no obvious pending merits case to hold this for. Timing note:
with the response now due mid-summer, the petition will likely be considered at the
long conference or shortly after (early OT2026), so the event should resolve in the
fall of 2026.

No prediction of individual votes: cert votes are not disclosed, so `votes` is
omitted.
