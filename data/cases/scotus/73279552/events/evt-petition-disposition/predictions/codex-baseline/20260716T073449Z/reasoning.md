# Prediction

I predict **denial**, with a **29% probability of any grant-side disposition** (including a plenary grant or GVR). Denial remains the likeliest individual disposition, but the procedural posture makes this materially stronger than an ordinary cert petition.

## Questions and governing standard

The petition presents four questions arising from $1,228,700 in civil fines imposed for Calvary Chapel's refusal to enforce Santa Clara County's indoor face-covering requirements from November 2020 through June 2021. It asks whether secular exceptions made the rules non-generally applicable under *Employment Division v. Smith*, whether church autonomy includes a new "liturgical exception," whether *Smith* should be overruled, and whether the fines violate the Excessive Fines Clause.

Under *Tandon v. Newsom* and *Fulton v. City of Philadelphia*, strict scrutiny applies if a rule burdens religious exercise while allowing secular conduct that undermines the government's asserted interest in a comparable way, or if the rule contains a mechanism for individualized exemptions. For the fines claim, *United States v. Bajakajian* asks whether the penalty is grossly disproportionate to the gravity of the offense, with culpability and harm central to the analysis.

## Record and vehicle

The provisioned questions presented and petition argue that exemptions for athletics, dining, face-related services, medical needs, communication, and other activities presented comparable transmission risks. They emphasize that an earlier California appeal invalidated capacity restrictions and associated contempt sanctions, and they characterize the mask rules as government control of worship. The petition also argues that a seven-figure fine is disproportionate where the church acted from sincere religious conviction and respondents could not trace infections to its services.

The provisioned brief in opposition supplies substantial vehicle objections. The judgment below is unpublished and nonprecedential, no split is identified, and the California court framed the dispute as application of settled law to narrow exemptions and a specific factual record. Respondents say the exemptions were constrained by circumstances such as active eating, medical necessity, testing, contact tracing, or temporary face-related services; they also say religious ceremonies such as Communion could involve temporary unmasking. The lower court found no individualized-exemption mechanism and treated indoor services attended by hundreds as not comparable to those activities. Even if strict scrutiny applied, the state trial court regarded masking as a narrowly tailored alternative to closing or capacity-limiting worship.

The alternative questions are also imperfect vehicles. According to the BIO, the proposed liturgical exception was not raised below, creating a preservation and possible jurisdictional problem. The petition offers no developed replacement rule for *Smith*, and the Court has repeatedly avoided overruling it when narrower grounds were available. The Excessive Fines issue is fact-bound: the lower courts relied on intentional noncompliance over many months, prior notices, reported infections among congregants, an outbreak at the associated school, and the church's ability to pay. Those findings make this a poor vehicle for general Eighth Amendment guidance even though the amount is striking.

## Calibration and procedural signal

The committed statpack estimates a 2.5% grant rate for all Term 2025 cert petitions and about 3.0% for petitions without a CVSG. This is a paid petition with extensive amicus participation, which supports some upward movement, but neither feature by itself overcomes the absence of a split and the vehicle defects.

The strongest positive signal is the docket timing in the July 16 snapshot. The case was distributed for the April 24 conference but the snapshot records no disposition almost three months later. That looks more like a hold than an ordinary zero-relist petition. A hold may reflect the related *Smith* question in another pending vehicle; it creates a meaningful chance of a later GVR or other grant-side order. I therefore raise P(any grant) to 29%, while keeping denial modal because a lead case could be resolved narrowly, the Court could conclude this petition would not benefit from that ruling, or the preservation and record-specific defects could independently require denial.

No individual cert votes are predicted. Four votes are required to grant, but the record does not support reliable Justice-by-Justice assignments.

## Significance

The big-case score is 0.78. A decision actually reaching the petition's broader questions could alter the rule governing neutral laws that burden religion, create a new church-autonomy exception, overrule *Smith*, or constrain large civil penalties imposed on religious institutions. The case's COVID-specific facts and vehicle defects reduce its practical breadth, but not the potential stakes if the Court were to reach the merits.
