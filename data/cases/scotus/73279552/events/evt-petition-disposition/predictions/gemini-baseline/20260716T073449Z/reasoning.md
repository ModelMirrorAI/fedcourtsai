# Prediction: Denied

## Legal Question and Context
This case asks whether a state court's imposition of over $1 million in fines against a church for violating COVID-19 capacity and masking restrictions violates the Free Exercise Clause or the Excessive Fines Clause. The petitioners argue that the restrictions contained multiple exemptions, thereby triggering strict scrutiny under *Employment Division v. Smith*, and that the church autonomy doctrine (liturgical exception) protects their worship practices. They also explicitly ask the Court to overrule *Smith* if it does not require strict scrutiny here.

## Analysis
Despite the extremely high level of interest in this case—evidenced by the filing of 7 amicus briefs at the cert stage from prominent religious liberty organizations and states (e.g., Liberty Justice Center, Pacific Justice Institute, Alliance Defending Freedom, National Religious Broadcasters, West Virginia)—the likelihood of a grant remains low for several reasons:

1. **Retrospective Nature (Vehicle Issues):** The COVID-19 pandemic restrictions have long been lifted. While the fines present a live controversy, the underlying regulatory scheme is no longer in place. The Court has generally moved past its "shadow docket" interventions on COVID-19 restrictions, and revisiting them in a plenary merits case in 2026 is less appealing, as it would offer little prospective guidance on active controversies.
2. **Reluctance to Overrule *Smith*:** The Court has been repeatedly asked to overrule *Employment Division v. Smith* (e.g., in *Fulton v. City of Philadelphia*) and has consistently declined to do so, preferring instead to narrow its application. A petition directly asking to overrule *Smith* in a backward-looking COVID-19 context is unlikely to be the vehicle the Court chooses if it ever decides to take that step.
3. **Application of Existing Precedent:** The Court has already established in *Tandon v. Newsom* that COVID-19 restrictions treating secular activities more favorably than religious ones trigger strict scrutiny. If the state court merely misapplied *Tandon*, the Supreme Court might opt for a summary reversal (GVR), but plenary review (a full grant) is less likely for error correction.
4. **Base Rates:** The base rate for a paid discretionary cert petition without a relist or CVSG is exceedingly low (around 0.8% according to the statpack for petitions with 0 relists). While the high number of amici significantly elevates this baseline, the lack of a relist going into the April 24, 2026 conference strongly points toward a denial.

## Conclusion
While the $1 million fine and the substantial amicus support raise the profile of this case significantly (leading to a high `big_case_score`), the retrospective nature of COVID-19 disputes and the lack of a relist suggest the Court will likely deny the petition or, at most, issue a GVR. Therefore, a denial is the most probable outcome.

**Probability:** 8% chance of a grant (or GVR) given the salience, but heavily weighed toward denial.
