# Reasoning

## Legal Question and Stakes
This case involves a major dispute over election law, specifically whether the National Voter Registration Act (NVRA) preempts Arizona's state-specific voter registration form requirements, which mandate documentary proof of citizenship and residence to register for federal elections. Additionally, the case questions whether a related state law (H.B. 2243) requiring database checks to verify citizenship status is intentionally discriminatory. This is an issue of immense national importance given the intense focus on election integrity and federal versus state power in administering elections, particularly with the 2026 midterms looming. 

## Case Posture and Prior SCOTUS Action
The district court issued an injunction against Arizona's proof-of-citizenship and residence requirements. Significantly, the Supreme Court **stayed** the district court's injunction regarding the proof of citizenship requirement pending appeal. Granting such a stay requires the Supreme Court to find a "fair prospect" that it will grant certiorari and a "likelihood" that the Court will reverse the lower court's decision on the merits.

Despite the Supreme Court's intervention and implied view of the merits, a divided Ninth Circuit panel proceeded to affirm the district court's injunction. Over 11 judges dissented from the Ninth Circuit's denial of rehearing en banc, signaling a deep and highly contentious split within the circuit itself.

## Signal Analysis
1. **Prior Stay:** The Supreme Court's prior stay of the district court's injunction makes a cert grant highly probable. The lower court's subsequent decision directly clashes with the Supreme Court's preliminary assessment.
2. **Dissents from Denial of En Banc:** The presence of more than 11 dissenting judges is one of the strongest statistical signals for a cert grant, indicating that the lower court's ruling is highly controversial and widely debated among appellate judges.
3. **Relist:** The petition was distributed for the June 18, 2026 conference and then relisted for the final scheduled conference on June 25, 2026. A relist often indicates that the Justices are taking a closer look, drafting a summary reversal (GVR), or ensuring the case is a suitable vehicle for deciding the issue. Relisted petitions are granted at a significantly higher rate than the baseline (roughly 7.6% vs 2.5%, and given the specific context here, much higher).
4. **Salience:** As an election law dispute originating from a swing state during an election year, the case inherently demands Supreme Court resolution. 

## Conclusion
Given the prior stay, the sheer number of dissents from the denial of rehearing en banc, and the significant national interest in the election law questions presented, this petition is an overwhelming favorite for a grant. The Court may even consider a summary reversal if it views the Ninth Circuit's ruling as directly contradicting its earlier stay rationale, but a plenary grant to settle the NVRA preemption question definitively is the most likely outcome.

**Prediction:** Granted
**Probability:** 85%
