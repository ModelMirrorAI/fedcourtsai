# Prediction for scotus/73281388 (evt-petition-disposition)

## Core Analysis

This is a prediction for a cert petition (paid) in a Fourth Amendment administrative search case out of the First Circuit. 

### The Legal Question and Standard
The petition asks whether Maine's requirement that lobstermen install GPS tracking devices on their private fishing vessels and submit to 24/7 surveillance violates the Fourth Amendment as an unreasonable trespassory search under *United States v. Jones*, and whether courts must evaluate the reasonableness of warrantless administrative searches based on *Jones* trespass protections instead of solely on a business owner's reasonable expectations of privacy (*Burger* test).

The petitioner alleges a circuit split on whether the *Jones* trespass analysis applies to administrative searches of closely regulated industries, citing differences between the First Circuit (this case), the Ninth Circuit, and the Sixth Circuit.

### The Vehicle Problem
Despite the potential interest in the Fourth Amendment implications of constant GPS surveillance in commercial contexts, the case presents a fatal vehicle problem. As the respondent's Brief in Opposition points out, the petitioner never raised the *Jones* trespass argument in the First Circuit. Instead, the petitioner conceded below that the commercial lobster fishery is a "closely regulated industry," and the respondent conceded the regulation authorized a "search." Based on these concessions, the First Circuit applied the *New York v. Burger* test. The word "trespass" did not even appear in the petitioner's First Circuit briefs.

The Supreme Court is a court of review, not of first view. It rarely grants certiorari to decide questions that were not pressed or passed upon below. This profound forfeiture issue makes the case an extremely poor vehicle for resolving any potential circuit split.

### Base Rates and Outcome
The base rate for paid cert petitions is extremely low (a few percent generally, and for the First Circuit specifically, 2.0% for modern discretionary-cert petitions). Furthermore, this case has had zero relists (it has just been fully briefed as of July 6, 2026, and is awaiting distribution for its next conference). With zero relists, the base rate grant probability is only 0.8%. 

When combining the baseline low probability of a grant with the severe waiver issue highlighted in the BIO, the likelihood of a grant is vanishingly small.

## Conclusion
The Supreme Court will deny the petition.
