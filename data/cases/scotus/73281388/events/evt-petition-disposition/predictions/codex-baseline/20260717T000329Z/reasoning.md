## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether requiring a federally permitted lobsterman to keep a GPS device on his licensed vessel, transmitting its position even during personal use, is an unreasonable trespassory search and whether the property-based analysis of *United States v. Jones* must inform reasonableness under *New York v. Burger*.

The search issue itself is conceded. The operative merits standard is therefore *Burger*'s test for a warrantless administrative search in a closely regulated industry: a substantial regulatory interest, necessity to the regulatory scheme, and a regime that supplies a constitutionally adequate substitute for a warrant by giving notice and limiting official discretion. The petitioner argues that *Jones*'s property protections must also shape that reasonableness inquiry; Maine responds that *Jones* identifies when a search occurs and does not modify *Burger* once a search and a closely regulated industry have been conceded.

## Record-specific considerations

The provisioned petition and questions presented describe minute-by-minute vessel-position collection while moving and periodic collection while docked or moored, including when Thompson uses the boat for family trips or emergency transport rather than lobstering. The petition emphasizes the physical placement of the tracker, the duration of monitoring, the proprietary sensitivity of fishing locations, and materially similar tracking rules across Atlantic coastal states. Those facts make the issue important and offer an originalist/property-based argument likely to interest some Justices.

The opposition, however, substantially weakens certworthiness:

- Thompson conceded below that commercial lobstering is closely regulated, while Maine conceded that tracking is a search. That posture leaves application of *Burger*, rather than the more basic *Jones* search inquiry, as the question actually decided.
- Maine says Thompson's appellate briefs never presented a distinct common-law-trespass modification of *Burger*; the First Circuit consequently did not decide that theory. The Court ordinarily avoids questions not pressed or passed upon.
- The asserted split is vulnerable. *Taylor* addressed whether tire chalking was a search and found municipal parking was not a closely regulated industry. *Patel* likewise addressed the existence of a search and rejected the closely regulated premise. *Rush* can be characterized as a fact-specific scope analysis consistent with *Burger*'s third prong. These cases do not cleanly establish competing rules for applying *Jones* within *Burger*.
- The First Circuit's ruling is fact-bound: it relied on the limited position data collected, the lack of regular business hours at sea, and the historical treatment of vessels. The record also states the device collects position rather than audio and that fisheries data is subject to confidentiality protections.

The docket snapshot dated July 16, 2026 shows that Maine initially waived a response, the petition was distributed, and the Court then requested a response, which was filed July 6. That call for a response is a genuine positive signal. There was no CVSG or relist as of the snapshot, although the absence of a relist carries little negative weight because the completed briefing had not yet gone through a later conference cycle.
