# Prediction: denied

I assign a 4% probability to any grant and predict denial. A plenary grant is the only plausible grant-side disposition; the record identifies no intervening judgment that would make a GVR likely.

## Question and governing certiorari considerations

The petition asks whether FHFA's assessment-funded structure violates the Appropriations Clause because Congress imposed no numerical cap or other ascertainable ceiling; whether Congress may direct that the assessments not be construed as government or appropriated funds; and whether the statutory authorization to collect amounts sufficient for the agency's reasonable costs violates the nondelegation doctrine.

Rule 10 considerations strongly disfavor review. The petition identifies no circuit split and presents the case primarily as a conflict with, or unresolved extension of, two very recent Supreme Court decisions: *CFPB v. Community Financial Services Association of America* and *FCC v. Consumers' Research*. The questions are important, but importance alone rarely overcomes the absence of disagreement below and serious vehicle defects.

## Record signals

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition from the Ninth Circuit. FHFA initially waived a response, the Court requested one, and the Solicitor General filed an opposition on July 6. The response request is a genuine positive signal: at least one Justice or the Court wanted adversarial presentation rather than disposing of the petition on the waiver. But the snapshot shows no CVSG, amicus support, relist, or post-response distribution yet.

The petition's questions-presented section and merits argument emphasize that FHFA lacks CFPB's statutory cap, can collect assessments sufficient for reasonable agency expenses, and may maintain a working-capital fund. It argues that the cap was central to *CFPB* and that *Consumers' Research* requires meaningful qualitative limits when Congress uses a sufficiency standard. Those features give the petition some intellectual and institutional significance.

The opposition is substantially stronger on certworthiness. It relies on *CFPB*'s express formulation that a valid appropriation needs an identified source and designated purpose, and on *Consumers' Research*'s acceptance of qualitative rather than numerical constraints. It also identifies four independent obstacles: no circuit conflict; the petition largely relies on positions recently advanced in dissents; an Article III causation/redressability dispute because the enterprises and servicers could enforce the liens independently; and preserved claim-preclusion and issue-preservation arguments. In particular, the Section 4516(f)(2) theory was neither developed nor passed on below. Those defects make this a poor vehicle even if four Justices are interested in agency funding.
