# Reasoning

## The Legal Question
The petition presents three questions asking whether the funding mechanism for the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA), established by 12 U.S.C. § 4516, violates the Appropriations Clause or the nondelegation doctrine. The core of the argument is that, unlike the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) whose funding was recently upheld in *CFPB v. CFSA*, 601 U.S. 416 (2024), the FHFA's funding lacks a statutory cap or sum certain limit.

## The Governing Standard and Precedent
In *CFPB v. CFSA*, the Supreme Court held that the Appropriations Clause requires only that Congress identify a source of public funds and authorize their expenditure for designated purposes. The Ninth Circuit below applied this standard and found that the FHFA's funding mechanism satisfies these requirements by specifying a source (assessments on Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac) and a purpose (reasonable costs and expenses of the Agency).

The Ninth Circuit also rejected the nondelegation challenge, finding that the statutory limitation to an "amount sufficient to provide for reasonable costs" provides an intelligible principle, consistent with numerous broad delegations upheld by the Supreme Court.

## Analysis and Prediction
A grant of certiorari is highly unlikely. The Ninth Circuit faithfully applied the Supreme Court's very recent and directly on-point precedent in *CFSA*. While the petitioners attempt to distinguish *CFSA* by emphasizing the lack of a strict monetary cap in the FHFA statute, the Supreme Court's reasoning in *CFSA* heavily relied on founding-era precedents (like the Customs Service and Post Office) that also featured open-ended or fee-based funding schemes without strict caps. 

Furthermore, there is no circuit split on the post-*CFSA* constitutionality of the FHFA. Although the Court requested a response after the Solicitor General initially waived it, a CVSG was not requested, and a single response request without further signals (like multiple relists) is often just a measure of diligence to confirm the absence of a split or other cert-worthy factors. Given the lack of a split, the straightforward application of a recent Supreme Court precedent, and the baseline low grant rate for paid petitions from the Ninth Circuit (~3%), a denial is the overwhelmingly likely outcome.

**Prediction**: Denied
**Probability**: 4%
