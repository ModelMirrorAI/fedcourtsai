# Prediction: denied

I assign a **0.6% probability of any grant** and predict denial. That probability includes a plenary grant, grant in part, or GVR; none is individually more likely than denial.

## Question and posture

The petition asks whether a civilian has a private right of action to enforce the emergency-use-authorization provision requiring that a recipient be informed of the option to accept or refuse a product, 21 U.S.C. § 360bbb-3(e)(1)(A)(ii)(III), comparable to protections afforded service members under 10 U.S.C. § 1107a. According to the provisioned petition, Providence conditioned the petitioner's hospital privileges on COVID-19 vaccination, she refused, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed dismissal under Rule 12(b)(6).

The prediction uses the May 18, 2026 petition and its extracted questions-presented section, the July 18 docket snapshot, and the document manifest. No brief in opposition was provisioned: the snapshot says Providence waived its right to respond on June 12. The petition was distributed on June 24 for the September 28 conference. As of the snapshot there was no call for a response, CVSG, or relist.

## Certworthiness and merits signal

The petition's central defect is that its asserted conflict does not appear to be a square circuit split on the same cause of action. Its military authorities concern § 1107a and, in several instances described by the petition, claims routed through the Military Pay Act. The petition itself says every court confronting the civilian theory has denied relief. Different treatment under different statutory and remedial settings is weak certworthiness, especially from an unpublished memorandum.

The governing implied-right doctrine also points against review and reversal. *Alexander v. Sandoval* asks whether Congress intended both a private right and a private remedy. Section 337(a)'s direction that FDCA enforcement proceed by and in the name of the United States, reinforced by *Buckman*, is powerful evidence against a privately implied remedy. The petition emphasizes the importance of informed consent and the parallel legislative history of §§ 360bbb-3 and 1107a, but importance does not substitute for rights-creating and remedy-creating statutory text.

Pre-snapshot CourtListener research reinforced that assessment. The published Fifth Circuit decision in *Pearson v. Shriners Hospitals*, 133 F.4th 433 (5th Cir. 2025), held that § 360bbb-3 concerns information supplied in vaccine administration and does not prohibit a private employer from imposing employment consequences; it expressly did not need to decide whether an implied cause of action exists. The published Ninth Circuit decision in *Curtis v. Inslee* (Oct. 6, 2025) held that § 360bbb-3 creates no private right enforceable under § 1983, relying on § 337(a) and the absence of clear congressional intent. Those decisions make the petition look like an application of settled implied-right principles, not an entrenched conflict requiring Supreme Court intervention.

The respondent's waiver is not dispositive, but it is consistent with a petition the Court is unlikely to take. Nor is there an apparent intervening authority that would support a GVR.
