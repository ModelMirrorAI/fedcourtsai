# Prediction: denied

I assign a 3.5% probability to any grant and predict denial. The petition raises an important and recurring Fourth Amendment question, but this case has unusually serious jurisdictional and vehicle defects that make it an improbable vehicle for resolving the conflict.

## Question and governing law

Snapchat's PhotoDNA system identified a 16-second video uploaded from Gasper's account as matching known child sexual abuse material. No person at Snapchat or NCMEC viewed it before a Wisconsin Department of Justice analyst and then a local detective viewed the file without a warrant. The central question is whether the officers exceeded an antecedent private search when human review revealed the video's contents for the first time.

Under *United States v. Jacobsen*, the Fourth Amendment does not restrict a private search, and the government may repeat it without a warrant so long as the government does not exceed its scope. The Wisconsin Supreme Court held that the hash-based match created virtual certainty that viewing the same file would reveal nothing else of significance. The petition argues that *Walter v. United States*, *Riley v. California*, and *Carpenter v. United States* require a warrant because an automated hash comparison reveals less than opening and viewing the file. It also raises reasonable-expectation-of-privacy and good-faith questions that the Wisconsin Supreme Court expressly did not decide.

## Grant signals

There is a concrete, mature conflict. The Wisconsin Supreme Court aligned with the Fifth and Sixth Circuits' treatment of hash-matched files, while the Second and Ninth Circuits require a warrant in materially similar circumstances. Wisconsin's opposition identifies the Fourth Circuit's March 2026 decision in *United States v. Lowers* as joining the Second and Ninth Circuits, which the CourtListener search confirmed was issued on March 10, 2026. The issue is recurring, technologically important, and nationally administered through ESP and NCMEC reporting. A Justice requested Wisconsin's response after its initial waiver, and one amicus brief supports Gasper. Those facts raise the petition above an ordinary paid criminal petition.

## Reasons denial remains overwhelmingly likely

The judgment is interlocutory. Gasper has not been convicted or sentenced, the Wisconsin Supreme Court remanded for further proceedings, and several other suppression theories remain undecided. The opposition's reliance on 28 U.S.C. § 1257(a), *Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn*, and *Florida v. Thomas* is formidable: Gasper can obtain review of all federal issues after final judgment, and an alternative suppression ruling could make this issue unnecessary. The petition does not squarely answer that threshold problem.

The private-search issue also may not affect the evidence underlying the charges. All Wisconsin justices rejected the trial court's sweeping suppression result. Justice Dallet would apply good faith, while Justice Crawford concluded that the unviewed CyberTip independently supplied probable cause for the warrant and therefore would not suppress the ten files recovered from Gasper's phone. In addition, the state argues that Gasper failed to preserve admissible proof of a subjective expectation of privacy. These alternative grounds would complicate merits review and could make a victory largely advisory.

The three questions are broader than the decision below, the petition is imprecisely framed, and its provisioned copy is truncated in the concurring opinions. The complete opposition nonetheless supplies the critical procedural posture and confirms that the private-search question is the only issue the court below decided.
