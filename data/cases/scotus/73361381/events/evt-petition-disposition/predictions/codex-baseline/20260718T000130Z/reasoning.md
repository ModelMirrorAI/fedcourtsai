# Prediction: denied

I assign a **0.6% probability of any grant** and predict denial. This includes the possibility of a plenary grant, a grant in part, or a GVR; none is the likeliest disposition.

## Question and record

The petition asks whether due process permits a state to apply an uncodified transition provision in a session law to shorten the limitations period for an already-accrued claim when that transition rule does not appear beside the codified statute. The provisioned petition says the insurance claim accrued in 2010, Ohio shortened the written-contract limitations period in 2012, and the uncodified transition rule allowed the earlier of the old deadline or eight years from the amendment's effective date. Petitioner filed the contract action in March 2024. The Ohio intermediate appellate court affirmed dismissal, and the Ohio Supreme Court declined discretionary review.

The snapshot dated July 17, 2026 shows a paid petition distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. It shows no relist or CVSG. No brief in opposition was provisioned, so this assessment does not infer any respondent argument beyond the posture described in the petition.

## Certiorari assessment

The Court generally grants certiorari to resolve an important conflict, settle an important federal question, or correct an exceptional departure from accepted law, not merely to review a claimed error in applying state law. Several features weigh heavily against review:

- The petition identifies no split among federal circuits or state courts of last resort. It principally alleges inconsistency in the treatment of the Ohio transition rule by lower Ohio and federal courts, which is a weak vehicle for Supreme Court review.
- The petition concedes that the session law clearly prescribed retroactive application and afforded a constitutionally reasonable period to sue. That removes the usual retroactivity and inadequate-grace-period arguments and leaves the narrower claim that codification itself was necessary for notice.
- The petition's cited authorities substantially weaken that remaining theory. In particular, its discussion of *Texaco, Inc. v. Short* recognizes the ordinary rule that enactment and publication of a law, coupled with a reasonable opportunity to comply, supplies notice. The petition offers no authority establishing that a duly enacted and published session law is constitutionally ineffective merely because the transition provision was not placed in the code.
- The case arises from an individual, pro se contract dispute and a state-specific transition provision. The asserted federal question is discernible, but the record supplies no indication that it is recurring nationally or cleanly preserved in a posture likely to attract four votes.

A GVR is also unlikely because the provisioned record identifies no intervening Supreme Court decision, confession of error, mootness development, or government recommendation that would support vacatur and remand.

## Significance

The pre-decision stakes are modest. A merits ruling could clarify whether due process imposes a codification-specific notice requirement on state transition provisions, but this petition frames that potentially broader issue through one Ohio statute and one private limitations dispute.
