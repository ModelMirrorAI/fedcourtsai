# Reasoning

**Legal Question and Governing Standard**
The central legal question presented by the petition is whether a state (Ohio) violates the Due Process Clause's notice requirement by relying on an uncodified provision in a Senate Bill to apply a statute of limitations retroactively. The governing standard for a grant of certiorari by the Supreme Court (Rule 10) emphasizes compelling reasons, such as a split among United States courts of appeals or state supreme courts on an important question of federal law, or an important federal question that has not been, but should be, settled by the Court. 

**Facts and Context from the Snapshot**
This is a paid petition filed pro se by Naren Chaganti, seeking review of a decision from the Court of Appeals of Ohio, Franklin County (an intermediate state appellate court, rather than the state supreme court). The question is highly specific to Ohio's legislative procedures—specifically the interplay between a codified statute and an uncodified provision in a state Senate Bill.

**Outcome and Reasoning**
The Supreme Court is highly unlikely to grant certiorari in this case. The Court generally does not step in to correct routine, fact-bound, or state-law-heavy matters unless there is a broad, cross-jurisdictional issue of federal constitutional dimension or a clear conflict in precedent. 

Furthermore, petitions filed by pro se litigants, even when the filing fee is paid, are rarely granted due to typical deficiencies in presenting issues of national importance. There is no evidence in the record of a split in authority, and the dispute centers fundamentally on state statutory interpretation.

**Probability and Confidence**
I predict this petition will be denied. The estimated probability of a grant is placed at 0.5%, reflecting the very low base rate for paid petitions generally (~11% for all paid petitions), which is further depressed here by the pro se nature of the filing and the highly fact-bound, state-specific legal question. Confidence in the denial is high.
