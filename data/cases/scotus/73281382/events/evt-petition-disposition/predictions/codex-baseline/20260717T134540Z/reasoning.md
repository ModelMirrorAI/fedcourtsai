# Prediction: denial

I assign a 10% probability to any grant and predict denial. A plenary grant is the realistic grant-side outcome; a GVR is unlikely because the petition relies on established decisions rather than an intervening ruling.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether police conduct a Fourth Amendment search when they enter a home's curtilage for a knock-and-talk with the purpose of gathering incriminating evidence. Under *Florida v. Jardines*, the customary license permits a visitor, including an officer without a warrant, to approach by the ordinary route, knock, wait briefly, and leave if not received. The license is limited by place and purpose: conduct that objectively reveals a purpose to conduct a search falls outside it. *Collins v. Virginia* likewise treats a physical intrusion into curtilage to gather evidence as a search. But *Jardines* also says that approaching to speak with an occupant does not become a search merely because the visitor hopes to discover information.

## Case-specific signals

The provisioned petition, questions presented, brief in opposition, and July 17 snapshot were all available and complete. The officers had two uncorroborated drug tips. Rather than immediately approach the front door, they circled the block, followed a visitor across the yard, stood close behind her as she knocked, held the storm door, and tried to follow her entry. The petition says the 55-second surveillance video objectively shows a search-like purpose and conduct outside ordinary social norms. A divided North Carolina Court of Appeals rejected the suppression claim, a dissent accepted the curtilage argument, and the North Carolina Supreme Court summarily affirmed.

Those facts make the petition stronger than an ordinary fact-bound suppression case. The docket adds selectivity signals: the Court requested a response after North Carolina initially waived, two amicus groups supported review, and the completed briefing was distributed for the September 28 conference. The issue affects a common policing technique, and the compact video record could make the case accessible. Justice Gorsuch's statement joined by Justices Sotomayor and Kagan in *Bovat v. Vermont* also suggests some interest in enforcing *Jardines* in knock-and-talk cases.

The brief in opposition nevertheless identifies substantial barriers. It argues that petitioner did not present the specific officer-purpose theory below, so the Court would be considering it in the first instance. It also persuasively distinguishes the cases offered as evidence of division: several courts considered purpose and differed only in application, while others found a Fourth Amendment violation on a separate spatial ground. The question presented may assume the disputed factual premise that the officers' purpose was to gather incriminating evidence, although the suppression testimony described an intent to speak with an occupant and decide what to do next. These preservation, premise, and split defects give the Court straightforward grounds to deny without resolving the merits. The Court has also repeatedly declined other post-*Jardines* knock-and-talk petitions described in the filings.
