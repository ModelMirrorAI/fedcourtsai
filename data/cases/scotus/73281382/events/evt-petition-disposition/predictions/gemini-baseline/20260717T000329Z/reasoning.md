# Reasoning

## Legal Question and Context
The petition asks whether police conduct a search within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment when they enter the curtilage of a home to conduct a "knock-and-talk" investigation with the subjective purpose of gathering incriminating evidence against the homeowner. This is a claim rooted in *Florida v. Jardines*, 569 U.S. 1 (2013), and *Collins v. Virginia*, 584 U.S. 586 (2018), asking whether the "implied license" to approach a front door is voided if the officer's underlying intent is to investigate.

## Analysis
The case presents an interesting doctrinal question concerning the tension between the Fourth Amendment's generally objective standards (e.g., *Whren*) and the seemingly purpose-based language in *Jardines* ("in order to do nothing but conduct a search"). The petitioner claims lower courts are divided on whether subjective intent matters for curtilage entries. A Call for Response (CFR) and two amicus briefs filed at the petition stage indicate that the Court (or at least some Justices) found the issue significant.

However, the State's Brief in Opposition introduces a devastating vehicle objection: the petitioner allegedly failed to raise the specific officer-purpose argument in the state courts. According to the BIO, the petitioner relied on *Jardines* below but focused on the officers' path (cutting through the side yard) and their waiting behavior, rather than arguing that their subjective purpose invalidated the knock-and-talk. The Supreme Court rarely grants certiorari when a federal claim was not properly preserved and passed upon by the state courts. Given this preservation defect, the Court is highly likely to deny the petition, even if the underlying question warrants review.

## Probability and Conclusion
The base rate for a paid cert petition is around 2.5% to 3.0%. While the CFR and amicus support raise the case's profile, the severe vehicle problem (waiver/preservation) drastically reduces the likelihood of a grant. 

Predicted Disposition: Denied.
Probability of Grant: 4% (0.04).
