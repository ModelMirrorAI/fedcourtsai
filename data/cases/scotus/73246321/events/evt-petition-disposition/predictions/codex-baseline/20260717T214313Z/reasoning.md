# Prediction

## Record and question presented

The provisioned snapshot shows a paid, pro se petition from a private insurance dispute. The Georgia Court of Appeals dismissed Francis's discretionary appeal, the Supreme Court of Georgia denied review, Allstate waived a response, and the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. There is no CVSG or relist as of the snapshot.

The provisioned petition was marked `empty_text: true`. I retrieved the same official Supreme Court PDF identified in the manifest and visually inspected its scan. The petition asks whether strict enforcement of state appellate filing rules against a pro se litigant violates due process or equal protection, and whether dismissal for technical filing errors conflicts with the rule that pro se filings receive liberal construction. Its factual account says the Georgia court dismissed an October 2024 discretionary application as untimely and dismissed a later application in May 2025. The argument occupies roughly one page and cites only *Haines v. Kerner*, 404 U.S. 519 (1972).

## Governing standard and case-specific assessment

Certiorari ordinarily requires a conflict among courts, an important unsettled federal question, or another compelling reason under Supreme Court Rule 10; fact-bound error correction is generally insufficient. The petition identifies no split, no conflicting authority, and no developed lower-court merits opinion. *Haines* concerns liberal construction of a federal pro se complaint and does not by itself establish that a state appellate court must excuse a jurisdictional or mandatory filing deadline. The state timeliness ruling also presents a substantial adequate-and-independent-state-ground obstacle unless the federal challenge to the procedure itself is both properly preserved and meritorious.

The response waiver, short pro se filing, private dispute, and absence of supporting amici or institutional participation reinforce that this is unlikely to attract four votes. A summary GVR is also unlikely because the petition identifies no intervening precedent or other development requiring reconsideration.
