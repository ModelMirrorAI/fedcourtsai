# Reasoning

## Legal Question & Governing Standard
The core question is whether California's ban on ammunition feeding devices capable of holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment and/or the Takings Clause (due to its retrospective dispossession requirement without compensation). The governing standard relies on the Second Amendment framework established in *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association, Inc. v. Bruen*. Under *Bruen*, the government must demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with this Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. 

## Facts from the Snapshot
The case represents a significant challenge to a state gun control measure. Crucially, the petition explicitly notes that the Supreme Court had previously GVR'd (granted, vacated, and remanded) the case in light of *Bruen*, and the Ninth Circuit en banc panel subsequently upheld the ban again. 

The docket reveals a highly unusual procedural history: the petition was filed in August 2025, distributed for a November 21, 2025 conference, and then subsequently relisted over 20 times up to a final "clean-up" conference on June 29, 2026. Along the way, several amici curiae briefs were filed, and supplemental briefs concerning *Benson* were submitted in March and April 2026.

## Reasoning for Probability and Disposition
In the Supreme Court, a case that is relisted over 20 times without being formally held for another case is highly unusual. If the Court merely intended to grant the petition on the merits (which takes 4 votes), it typically would have done so much earlier in the Term. A protracted series of relists often indicates that the Justices are exchanging drafts of opinions. This typically points toward either a summary reversal (a per curiam opinion) or a denial of certiorari accompanied by a robust, multi-page dissent from denial (e.g., from Justices Thomas or Alito).

Given the Court's recent reluctance to take up broad "assault weapon" or magazine bans despite having the 6-3 conservative majority, the sheer length of the relist sequence (stretching the entirety of the 2025-2026 Term) suggests that the Court may ultimately deny certiorari, while several conservative justices have spent the Term drafting a sharp dissent criticizing the Ninth Circuit's application of *Bruen*. Summary reversals usually take less than an entire Term to negotiate, making a denial with a dissent slightly more likely than a grant or summary reversal. 

As such, the predicted disposition is `denied`, but with a substantial probability (0.35) of a grant (or summary reversal).
