# Prediction

I predict that the Court will grant the petition, with a 57% probability of a grant and a 43% probability of a non-grant disposition (principally denial). This is a close forecast. The ordinary certiorari baseline strongly favors denial, but the docket's extraordinary procedural signals and a developing conflict over materially similar magazine bans move this petition just above even odds.

## Questions and governing standard

The petition asks whether California may prohibit possession of ammunition-feeding devices holding more than ten rounds under the Second Amendment and whether requiring owners to surrender, transfer, remove, or permanently alter previously lawful magazines effects a taking without compensation. The petition rests principally on *District of Columbia v. Heller*, *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, and *United States v. Rahimi*: bearable arms and conduct covered by the Second Amendment's text are presumptively protected, and the government must justify a restriction by a sufficiently analogous historical regulatory tradition. The Takings Clause issue asks whether compelled dispossession is a physical taking or instead a permissible exercise of the police power over contraband.

At the certiorari stage, the practical questions are whether four Justices perceive a consequential conflict, an important recurring federal question, and a suitable vehicle. A prediction of a grant is not a prediction that petitioners ultimately win on the merits.

## Record and briefing

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition show a final en banc Ninth Circuit judgment after a full record and an earlier Supreme Court grant-vacate-remand in light of *Bruen*. The Ninth Circuit held both that magazines over ten rounds are unprotected accoutrements rather than arms and, alternatively, that California's ban fits historical traditions involving gunpowder storage and dangerous uses of weapons. Four judges dissented. Petitioners argue that magazines are functional firearm components owned in the hundreds of millions, that the lower courts disagree over whether they are arms and how common use fits the analysis, and that forced dispossession independently violates the Takings Clause.

The provisioned brief in opposition supplies substantial reasons for denial. It emphasizes the Court's recent denials in *Ocean State Tactical*, *Hanson*, and *Harrel*; characterizes much of the asserted disagreement as tentative, assumed, or arising at a preliminary stage; notes other magazine cases still developing below; and argues that California permits modification, sale, or removal rather than appropriating the magazines. Those points make an eventual denial quite plausible. Even so, this case's final judgment and extensive record address vehicle objections that applied to several earlier petitions.

The snapshot supplies the strongest case-specific signal. This paid petition from the Ninth Circuit was first set for a November 21, 2025 conference, rescheduled, and then distributed for conference repeatedly from December 2025 through June 29, 2026. That degree of repeated consideration is far outside the ordinary denied petition and suggests a hold, active drafting, or sustained interest among multiple chambers. It does not identify the eventual action, and an opinion respecting denial remains possible, but it sharply raises the grant probability above the baseline.

Related-case retrieval adds a second important signal without revealing this case's outcome. On March 5, 2026, the District of Columbia Court of Appeals held in *Benson v. United States* that an analogous ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment. The opinion reported that the United States conceded the ban's unconstitutionality. That creates a much cleaner merits disagreement with the Ninth Circuit's decision than existed when the brief in opposition was filed. A March 30 federal district-court opinion reported, however, that en banc proceedings in *Benson* had not concluded. The uncertainty over whether that conflict will survive plausibly explains why this petition remained pending and reduces the weight I give the panel decision.

## Calibration

The committed statpack reports a 4.9% estimated grant rate for resolved modern discretionary-cert petitions in the live/historical slice and a 5.3% grant rate for petitions originating in the Ninth Circuit. Those are the proper starting points, not the forecast's endpoint. This is a paid filing, but the statpack's rendered Term table does not expose a separate paid-case rate, and its relist field is unavailable for this slice. I therefore do not assign a numerical paid or relist multiplier from unavailable data.

The final en banc posture, prior Supreme Court involvement, entrenched lower-court disagreement over a recurring constitutional issue, the post-brief *Benson* development, and more than a Term's worth of repeated conference distributions collectively outweigh the low baseline. Against that are the Court's recent denials of similar petitions, the unsettled status of *Benson*, the possibility that the Court is holding rather than preparing to grant, and the additional complexity introduced by the Takings Clause issue. Balancing those factors produces P(granted) = 0.57.

I do not predict individual certiorari votes. Conference votes are not public, and the record does not support a reliable Justice-by-Justice allocation.
