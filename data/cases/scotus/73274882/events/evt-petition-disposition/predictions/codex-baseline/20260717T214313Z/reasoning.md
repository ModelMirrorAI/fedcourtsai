# Prediction: grant

I estimate a **68% probability of some grant-side disposition** and predict a plenary grant as the most likely label. A GVR in light of intervening Second Amendment authority is a meaningful alternative within that 68%; denial remains the principal alternative overall.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks (1) whether California's ban on possessing ammunition-feeding devices holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment, and (2) whether forcing owners to surrender, transfer, remove, or permanently alter lawfully acquired magazines without compensation violates the Takings Clause. Under *Heller*, *Bruen*, and *Rahimi*, conduct covered by the Second Amendment's text is presumptively protected and the government must identify a relevantly similar historical tradition, assessed through the comparable burden and justification. Certiorari also ordinarily requires an important federal question, a mature conflict, and a suitable final-judgment vehicle.

## Record-specific signals

The provisioned `questions-presented.txt` and petition characterize magazines over ten rounds as integral components of common semiautomatic firearms, owned in the hundreds of millions, and contend that the Ninth Circuit improperly classified them as non-Arms before relying on gunpowder-storage, trap-gun, and carry restrictions. The petition also emphasizes that this case has a final en banc judgment, a full post-*Bruen* record, and an earlier Supreme Court GVR.

The provisioned brief in opposition offers substantial counterweights. It argues that magazines over ten rounds are unnecessary to ordinary firearm operation, that the ban leaves unlimited ten-round magazines and ammunition available, and that historical regulation of especially dangerous weapons and components supplies the relevant tradition. It also argues that prior magazine cases were denied, that other merits cases were still developing, and that the Takings Clause theory conflicts with the traditional power to prohibit dangerous property. The takings question is therefore less likely than the Second Amendment question to drive review.

The docket posture is the strongest predictive fact. The November 2025 initial distribution was followed by a rescheduling and roughly twenty further conference distributions through June 29, 2026, plus supplemental briefing concerning *Benson*. This is not an ordinary unrelisted paid petition. It strongly suggests that at least some Justices are holding the case for related doctrine or litigation, considering a grant or GVR, or preparing a separate writing. Six petitioner-side amicus filings, experienced Supreme Court counsel, the nationwide prevalence of the regulated magazines, and the threat of criminal liability add importance and salience.

Related pre-disposition authority raises the grant signal. In March 2026, the D.C. Court of Appeals held in *Benson v. United States* that an equivalent ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds violated the Second Amendment, producing a concrete disagreement with the Ninth Circuit's final judgment. In July 2026, the Seventh Circuit's merits decision in *Barnett v. Raoul* upheld Illinois restrictions on AR-15s and large-capacity magazines over a dissent, adding a developed federal appellate record even though its result aligns with the Ninth Circuit. In June 2026, *Wolford v. Lopez* applied *Bruen* in a 6–3 decision reversing the Ninth Circuit and stressed nationwide uniformity, the number and acceptance of historical analogues, and close attention to how and why a law burdens armed self-defense. *Wolford* does not itself decide magazine bans, but it makes a grant or a GVR more plausible.

The committed statpack supplies the calibration. Modern paid petitions in the 2025 Term have an estimated 5.36% grant rate; Ninth Circuit petitions are about 3.0%; and petitions with three or more relists are estimated at 21.8%. Those broad buckets understate this petition's exceptional posture: it has far more than three distributions, a final judgment, intervening contrary authority, and a high-profile constitutional question. Still, repeated relists can end in denial accompanied by a statement or dissent, and the Court may prefer to await rehearing or later petitions from the newly developed cases. Those possibilities keep the estimate well below certainty.

## Disposition and significance

A plenary grant on the Second Amendment question is the likeliest single disposition. A GVR based on *Wolford* is plausible but less likely because magazine-capacity bans present a distinct common-use issue and the unusually mature record is suitable for merits review. I do not predict individual votes at the petition stage. The `big_case_score` of 0.90 reflects the potential nationwide effect on state magazine bans and on the methodology governing common arms, not the probability of certiorari.

The petition PDF was provisioned as truncated at 356 pages, but its questions presented and complete cert-stage argument were available; the 29-page brief in opposition was complete. No outcome-revealing material was sought or encountered.
