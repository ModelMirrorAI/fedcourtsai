# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.6% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. The probability includes a plenary grant, grant in part, or GVR; none is the likeliest disposition.

## Question and posture

The petition asks whether the Alabama Supreme Court violated procedural due process by interpreting Ala. Code § 34-3-62 to protect only an attorney's retention of disputed funds, then applying that interpretation in Jerry Blevins's disciplinary case. The provisioned petition says a client fee dispute arose in 2017, the parties obtained a circuit-court judgment in 2018, and the Alabama State Bar later charged Blevins. The disciplinary proceeding found failure to communicate and an excessive fee and imposed a public reprimand and six-month suspension. The Alabama Supreme Court affirmed, reasoning in part that Blevins identified no precedent that could reasonably have led him to understand the statute as blanket immunity from all discipline.

The federal fair-warning doctrine limits unforeseeable judicial enlargement of legal prohibitions. The petition relies principally on *Marks v. United States* and the Alabama decision in *Brooks v. Alabama State Bar*. CourtListener research also identified *Bouie v. City of Columbia*, 378 U.S. 347 (1964), and *Rogers v. Tennessee*, 532 U.S. 451 (2001), as the central Supreme Court decisions concerning an "unexpected and indefensible" judicial construction. But this petition does not identify a conflict among courts over that standard or a settled prior construction of § 34-3-62 contrary to the Alabama court's reading. The statutory text itself focuses on an attorney's retention of compensation from funds in the attorney's hands, which makes the state court's narrower construction foreseeable. The petition therefore looks chiefly like an asserted misapplication of settled due-process principles to a state statute and an individual disciplinary record.

## Certworthiness and vehicle

Several features weigh heavily against review:

- The question is fact-bound and depends on the meaning and application of one Alabama statute.
- The petition alleges no lower-court split and cites no conflicting federal or sister-state authority.
- The claimed federal issue was first developed in Blevins's reply brief in the Alabama Supreme Court, creating a possible preservation or vehicle concern even though that court addressed the argument.
- The disciplinary judgment also involved failure to communicate and charging an excessive fee, while the claimed statutory safe harbor concerns the fee dispute. That mismatch makes broad relief and clean review less likely.
- The petitioner is self-represented, no amici appear in the provisioned record, and the Alabama State Bar waived a response. A waiver is not dispositive, but without a later call for response it is a negative practical signal.
- As of the July 18 snapshot, the petition had only been distributed for its initial September 28 conference. There was no relist or CVSG signal and no identified intervening decision that would support a GVR.

The provisioned snapshot and document manifest show a paid petition with complete extracted petition text and no brief in opposition; the absence of a BIO is explained by the respondent's waiver rather than a document-extraction gap.

## Probability calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 2.5% grant rate across resolved 2025 Term cert petitions and about 5.36% for paid petitions. The broader modern discretionary-cert slice grants about 3.1%. More specifically, petitions with no relist have resolved as grants about 0.8%, compared with 7.6% after one relist and substantially higher rates after repeated relists. Because this petition is still before its first conference, the zero-relist figure is not a final procedural classification, but it supplies the closest present-state calibration. Its absence of a split, recurring federal issue, institutional support, and a clean vehicle warrants a modest downward adjustment to 0.6%.

Recent corpus lookups reinforced only the structural calibration: the returned grant-side cases typically showed multiple conference distributions or prominent recurring questions, while low-salience individual petitions appeared routinely on the denial side. Those rows were not keyword-matched and were not treated as doctrinally similar precedents.

The CourtListener MCP produced two useful general-doctrine searches and then throttled a third search for attorney-discipline decisions. I proceeded from the provisioned petition, the available general precedents, the corpus priors, and the statpack rather than treating that tooling degradation as a blocker.

I do not predict individual Justice votes. At the cert stage, the relevant threshold is four votes, and this record does not support Justice-specific estimates.

The 0.12 big-case score reflects the narrow, individualized stakes. A merits decision could clarify fair notice in professional discipline, but the petition's state-specific statutory setting makes broad legal or public impact unlikely.
