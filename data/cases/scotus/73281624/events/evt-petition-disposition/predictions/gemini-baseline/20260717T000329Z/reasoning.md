# Reasoning

**The Legal Question:**
The petition asks the Court to recognize a "market participant exception" to *Parker* state-action antitrust immunity. Specifically, it questions whether a municipality can invoke *Parker* immunity when it acts as the sole commercial participant in a market while simultaneously using its legislative power to exclude rival private competitors. 

**The Governing Standard:**
Under *Parker v. Brown* and its progeny (e.g., *Town of Hallie*, *City of Columbia v. Omni Outdoor Advertising*), municipalities enjoy state-action immunity from federal antitrust laws if they act pursuant to a "clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed" state policy to displace competition. However, the Court has historically left open the exact boundaries of a "market participant" exception, whereby a state or municipality acting as a commercial participant (rather than a sovereign regulator) might be subject to antitrust scrutiny.

**Facts and Posture:**
The petitioners operate a beach gear rental business (Cherry Grove Beach Gear, LLC). They allege that the City of North Myrtle Beach used its municipal authority to grant itself a monopoly over the beach gear rental market by excluding private rivals, all while acting as a commercial participant. The Fourth Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the City, finding that the City's actions were shielded by *Parker* immunity based on South Carolina statutes delegating beach management authority, essentially concluding that the anti-competitive effect was a foreseeable result of the state's broad delegation of power. 

The docket shows the respondent initially waived its right to respond on April 16, 2026. The Court then distributed the petition for the May 14 conference but explicitly requested a response on May 11, 2026 (a "Call for Response" or CFR). The respondent subsequently filed its Brief in Opposition (BIO) on July 10, 2026, after receiving an extension. 

**Analysis:**
The Call for Response is a strong signal that at least one Justice considers the petition's antitrust question sufficiently important to warrant closer inspection, especially given the unresolved nature of the market participant exception in this Court's jurisprudence (a tension highlighted by cases like *Phoebe Putney* and *Omni*).

However, despite the CFR, the statistical hurdle for a grant remains exceptionally high. The overall base rate for paid cert petitions from the Fourth Circuit is roughly 4.6%. The petition alleges that the Fourth Circuit "dilutes" the clear articulation requirement but frames the issue more as a recurring question of national importance and a need for doctrinal clarification rather than an entrenched, multi-circuit split that strictly commands immediate resolution. Often, the Court waits for a deep and mature circuit split before recognizing a new categorical exception to *Parker* immunity.

Furthermore, this case has not yet survived a conference with the BIO in hand, meaning it has zero relists—the classic leading indicator of an imminent grant. A CFR increases the probability of a grant above the baseline, but the majority of CFRs still result in a denial. 

Given the lack of an entrenched split and the absence of relist signals post-BIO, the most probable disposition is a denial, albeit with an elevated probability reflecting the Court's demonstrated interest.

**Prediction:** Denied.
