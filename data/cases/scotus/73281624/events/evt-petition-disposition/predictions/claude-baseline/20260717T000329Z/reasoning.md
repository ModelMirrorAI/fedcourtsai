# Cherry Grove Beach Gear, LLC v. City of North Myrtle Beach, No. 25-1130 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.07.**

## The legal question

Petitioners, a small beach-equipment rental company and its owners, lost below
on Parker state-action antitrust immunity. The City of North Myrtle Beach
amended its ordinances in June 2022 to prohibit on-beach delivery and setup of
rented beach equipment by anyone other than the City, which itself rents chairs
and umbrellas on the beach and uses the revenue to fund lifeguard services. The
district court (D.S.C.) granted summary judgment on Parker grounds, and a
unanimous published Fourth Circuit panel (Gregory, J., joined by Diaz, C.J.,
and Benjamin, J.) affirmed, holding that S.C. Code §§ 5-7-30 and 5-7-145 —
which expressly authorize coastal municipalities to grant franchises for the
use of public beaches and to grant a contracted beach-safety company "the
exclusive right" to rent beach equipment — clearly articulate a state policy to
displace competition under Town of Hallie / Phoebe Putney, and declining
(per its own Western Star precedent) to adopt a "market participant" exception
to Parker immunity.

The petition presents three overlapping questions: (1) whether the Court should
adopt the market-participant exception where the municipality is the *sole*
market participant; (2) whether the Fourth Circuit's decision erodes the
clear-articulation test; and (3) whether clear articulation should apply with
"heightened rigor" when a municipality legislates competitors out of a market
it serves commercially.

## Why the CFR does not carry this petition to a grant

**The pro-grant case.** The market-participant exception to Parker immunity is
a genuinely open question the Court has twice flagged and reserved — City of
Columbia v. Omni Outdoor Advertising (1991) suggested it "might" exist, and
FTC v. Phoebe Putney (2013) expressly declined to decide it (n.4). The fact
pattern here is unusually clean for that question: the City is, by its own
30(b)(6) witness's admission, a monopolist that amended its ordinance
specifically to eliminate the petitioners' business, and it is the *sole*
provider in the market it closed. The CFR shows someone in the building
noticed. The Court has shown recurring appetite for trimming state-action
immunity (Phoebe Putney and NC Dental were both unanimous or lopsided
FTC-side wins).

**The anti-grant case is stronger.**

1. **No split.** The petition does not allege a circuit conflict on any of its
   three questions, and the BIO plausibly asserts none exists: no court of
   appeals has ever applied a market-participant exception to strip Parker
   immunity (Western Star, and the district-court survey in NFINITYLINK).
   Uniform lower-court refusal to adopt an exception the Supreme Court merely
   hinted at is a weak cert posture without percolation pressure.
2. **Vehicle defects.** The BIO lands several: (a) the Local Government
   Antitrust Act, 15 U.S.C. § 35(a), bars damages against the City, so even a
   win yields injunctive relief at most; (b) the Fourth Circuit held all
   non-antitrust claims abandoned under Rule 28, so the case arrives stripped;
   (c) the "heightened rigor" gloss (QP3) was never raised below —
   unpreserved; (d) the clear-articulation holding rests on an apparently
   unique South Carolina statute that *expressly* contemplates a single
   exclusive beach-equipment franchisee, so the decision below is defensible
   as fact-bound statutory application rather than doctrinal erosion. A Court
   interested in the market-participant question would likely wait for a
   vehicle where clear articulation is not independently so strong — here the
   statute authorizes exactly one rental provider, so the "exception" question
   is nearly outcome-dispositive-proof.
3. **Petition quality.** The petition is roughly ten pages of argument, cites
   no authority beyond the canonical Parker line, contains conspicuous
   drafting errors (including in the questions presented), never quotes the
   controlling statutory text, and was filed by a small local firm without
   Supreme Court experience, with no amicus support. The BIO, by an
   experienced appellate team (Maynard Nexsen — counsel of record is a former
   Fourth Circuit staff attorney/appellate specialist, joined by a former
   Alabama Solicitor General), systematically frames the petition as
   fact-bound error correction. Cert grants on under-developed petitions
   without amici are rare even when the underlying issue is interesting.
4. **No GVR path.** There is no intervening decision of this Court bearing on
   Parker immunity, so a GVR is not a realistic disposition.

## Calibration

From the committed statpack (live/historical slice, denial-reweighted): modern
discretionary-cert petitions are granted ~3% overall; Term 2025 estimated
grant rate 2.5%; CA4-originating modern cert petitions grant at ~4.6%. The
petition is paid (paid petitions grant at several times the IFP rate), and a
CFR after waiver is empirically one of the stronger pre-conference grant
signals — CFR'd paid petitions historically grant at roughly 5–10x the
baseline, but the large majority are still denied, typically after the BIO
deflates the petition. The statpack's relist cut (0 relists → 0.8% grant;
2 relists → 33.6%) confirms that the real information is still ahead of us:
if this petition is relisted repeatedly after the September conference, the
probability would move up sharply, but as of this snapshot there are no
relists.

Starting from a paid-CA4-CFR anchor of roughly 8–12% and discounting for the
absence of any split, the unpreserved third question, the LGAA damages bar,
the abandoned companion claims, the case-specific statutory language, and the
lopsided quality mismatch between petition and BIO, I land at **P(grant) =
0.07**. The likeliest path is denial at or shortly after the long conference,
possibly after a relist if a chambers is writing a statement respecting
denial (a Thomas- or Gorsuch-style separate writing on Parker's scope is the
most plausible non-denial-adjacent outcome short of a grant).

**Disposition: denied. granted = 0, probability = 0.07, confidence = 0.65.**
