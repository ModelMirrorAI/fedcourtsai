# Prediction: denied

I assign a 3% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. A plenary grant is the only plausible grant form; the record identifies no intervening authority or mootness issue that would support a GVR.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether municipal state-action immunity under *Parker* should have a market-participant exception, whether the Fourth Circuit diluted the clear-articulation requirement, and whether courts should apply that requirement with heightened rigor when a municipality legislates away competitors while itself selling the service. Under *Parker*, *Lafayette*, *Hallie*, and *Phoebe Putney*, a municipality receives immunity when its challenged restraint implements a clearly articulated state policy to displace competition; anticompetitive effects can qualify when they are the inherent, logical, or ordinary result of delegated authority. Unlike a private actor, a municipality ordinarily need not establish active state supervision.

At the certiorari stage, the practical question is whether four Justices would see a conflict among courts, an important unresolved federal question, or a serious departure from this Court's precedents, rather than case-specific error. The petition does not identify a circuit split.

## Record-specific assessment

The July 16 snapshot shows a paid Term 2025 petition from a unanimous published Fourth Circuit judgment. The City initially waived a response, the Court distributed the petition for the May 14 conference, and a response was requested on May 11. The City filed a full brief in opposition on July 10. The response request is a genuine positive signal: at least one Justice or chambers thought adversarial briefing worthwhile. The snapshot, however, shows no CVSG, relist, amicus filing, or post-response conference distribution.

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition describe a City ordinance reserving professional beach-equipment setup to City officials after Cherry Grove Beach Gear had offered that service. The petition argues that authorization to grant beach franchises and a conditional statutory authorization for an exclusive private beach-safety contractor did not clearly authorize a City self-monopoly. That framing presents a recognizable antitrust and federalism concern.

The petition is nonetheless a weak vehicle for plenary review. It proposes both a new market-participant exception and an alternative, undefined "heightened rigor" gloss. It identifies no conflicting appellate holding and gives no concrete evidence that the asserted problem is recurring. The case turns heavily on two unusual South Carolina provisions specifically addressing public-beach franchises and exclusive beach-equipment rentals. The petition's additional abandonment discussion is case-specific and does not correspond to a question presented.

The provisioned brief in opposition sharpens the vehicle problems. It argues that no federal appellate court has applied the proposed exception; that the Fourth Circuit followed its close precedent, *Western Star Hospital Authority v. City of Richmond*; that *Hallie* itself afforded immunity while a municipality participated in the market; and that the proposed heightened-rigor theory was not raised below. It also notes that the petition did not seek rehearing, omitted the operative statutory language from its analysis, and abandoned its non-antitrust claims. Those defects give the Court several reasons to deny without reaching the doctrinal issue.
