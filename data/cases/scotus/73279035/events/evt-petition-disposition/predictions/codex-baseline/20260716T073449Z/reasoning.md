# Prediction: denied (34% probability of a grant)

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether ERISA § 502(a)(3), 29 U.S.C. § 1132(a)(3), permits a beneficiary to obtain surcharge as “appropriate equitable relief,” and, if not, whether ERISA preempts state-law claims under a separate rabbi-trust agreement. At certiorari, the central considerations are a developed conflict, national importance, a clean and outcome-determinative vehicle, and whether at least four Justices are likely to want review. This is not a merits forecast.

## Record

The snapshot shows a paid petition from the Sixth Circuit. Regions initially waived a response, but the Court requested one; the case was later distributed for the April 2, 2026 conference, and on April 6 the Court invited the Solicitor General to express the views of the United States. No Solicitor General brief appears in the July 16 snapshot. That CVSG is the strongest grant-side signal.

The petition and questions presented frame a substantial conflict. The petition says six circuits allow surcharge after *CIGNA Corp. v. Amara*, while the Fourth and Sixth Circuits reject it in light of *Mertens* and *Montanile*. It also emphasizes that the judgment is final and that denying both federal surcharge and state contract remedies leaves the petitioners without a remedy. The case concerns alleged losses of retirement benefits after Ruby Tuesday's insolvency and Regions Bank's administration of a rabbi trust.

The brief in opposition identifies serious vehicle problems. The plans are top-hat plans exempt from ERISA's ordinary fiduciary-duty rules; Regions argues that the cases allowing surcharge concern ERISA fiduciaries and therefore would not decide this case differently. It also argues that petitioners may not have identified an ERISA or plan violation at all, an antecedent liability issue the Sixth Circuit did not resolve. The second question lacks a claimed circuit split. Those issues create a meaningful risk of a narrow ruling, avoidance, or denial even if the general surcharge question warrants eventual review.

That decision sharpens the disagreement but its rehearing posture makes the split less settled. The Tenth Circuit's 2025 decision in *Stark v. ## Forecast

I assign a 34% probability to any grant and predict denial as the most likely disposition. The CVSG, acknowledged disagreement, final judgment, and broad remedial significance move the case far above the ordinary cert baseline. The top-hat exemption, unresolved predicate liability, absence of a split on the second question, and unsettled related rehearing posture keep denial more likely than grant. A GVR is unlikely because the record identifies no controlling intervening Supreme Court decision.

The significance score is 0.62: a broad decision could affect ERISA remedies nationwide, but the unusual top-hat-plan and rabbi-trust setting limits how cleanly the case represents ordinary benefit plans.
