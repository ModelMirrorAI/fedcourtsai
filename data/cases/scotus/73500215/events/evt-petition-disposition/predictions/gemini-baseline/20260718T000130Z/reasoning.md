# Reasoning: Gomez v. Saccoccio (No. 25-1314)

## The Legal Question
The petition asks whether a court can find a Fourth Amendment constitutional right "clearly established" for qualified immunity purposes by combining general principles from two factually dissimilar cases (*Nelson* and *Deorle*).

## The Record and Governing Standard
The plaintiff was struck by a less-lethal foam projectile during a curfew. The officer asserted qualified immunity. The Ninth Circuit reversed the district court's grant of qualified immunity to the officer. In doing so, the Ninth Circuit held that "multiple cases can be read together to clearly establish a constitutional violation" and relied on two cases that the petition asserts are factually dissimilar.

The Supreme Court has repeatedly and unanimously reversed the Ninth Circuit (often summarily) for defining clearly established law at too high a level of generality in excessive force cases (e.g., *City of Escondido v. Emmons*, *Kisela v. Hughes*). The Court insists on close factual analogues to overcome qualified immunity.

## Analysis and Probability
While the base rate for cert grants from the Ninth Circuit is low (around 3.0%), this case features a highly salient trigger for the current Court: the Ninth Circuit denying qualified immunity by synthesizing general principles rather than demanding a close factual analogue. 

Furthermore, the docket shows that the respondent waived the right to respond on June 8, 2026, but the Court issued a Call for Response (CFR) on July 14, 2026. A CFR after a waiver is a very strong signal that at least one Justice is closely scrutinizing the petition for a potential grant or summary reversal. The Court rarely grants a petition without first ensuring a response is on record.

Given the Supreme Court's clear and repeated pattern of error-correction regarding the Ninth Circuit's qualified immunity jurisprudence, this case is a prime candidate for a Grant, Vacate, and Remand (GVR) or a summary reversal. The combination of the specific legal error highlighted in the question presented and the explicit CFR significantly elevates the probability of a grant above the baseline. I predict a GVR is the likeliest disposition, reflecting the Court's preference for summary correction rather than plenary review in such fact-bound qualified immunity disputes.

**Predicted Disposition:** gvr
