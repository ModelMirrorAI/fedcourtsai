# Prediction

I predict denial, with a 0.28 probability of any grant (including a GVR or summary disposition).

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the Ninth Circuit could combine general principles from two factually different excessive-force cases, *Nelson v. City of Davis* and *Deorle v. Rutherford*, to hold that Officer Eric Gomez violated clearly established Fourth Amendment law. Under the Court's qualified-immunity cases, existing precedent must put the unlawfulness of the officer's particular conduct beyond debate; especially in excessive-force cases, the right cannot be defined at a high level of generality. Certiorari ordinarily is not granted merely to correct a fact-bound error, but the Court has repeatedly made summary corrections when lower courts deny qualified immunity using overly general precedent.

## Record signals

The provisioned questions presented and petition describe a fast-moving encounter during civil unrest and an emergency curfew. Gomez was helping detain six unsearched vehicle occupants when a group ran toward the officers. After commands to stop and the deployment of pepper balls, Saccoccio climbed a fence into a residential yard. Gomez fired one frangible 40-millimeter OC round from about 100 feet, aiming at a trained target area; it struck Saccoccio's arm and fractured it. The Ninth Circuit nevertheless found both a triable excessive-force violation and clearly established law, reasoning that *Nelson* required distinguishing a peaceful person from a violent crowd and *Deorle* prohibited serious force against someone posing no immediate threat.

The strongest grant signal is procedural: the respondent waived a response, but on July 14 the Court requested one, due August 13. That indicates at least some chambers-level interest. The snapshot is earlier than the long conference and shows no relist or CVSG. No brief in opposition was provisioned because the response had not yet been filed, so the forecast necessarily lacks the respondent's vehicle and merits arguments.

The strongest substantive signal is *Zorn v. Linton*, decided March 23, 2026. There the Court summarily reversed a qualified-immunity denial because a circuit precedent did not establish, at the required specificity, that an officer's use of force against a noncompliant protester was unlawful. The Court emphasized that an officer must be able to read a case involving similar actions and circumstances and know that it proscribes the conduct. *Zorn* postdates the Ninth Circuit's January decision and February rehearing denial here. Together with *Rivas-Villegas v. Cortesluna*, *City of Escondido v. Emmons*, and *Kisela v. Hughes*, it makes the petition's claimed error plausible and creates a realistic path to either a GVR for reconsideration under *Zorn* or another summary reversal.

Denial still has the larger share. The decision below is an unpublished, nonprecedential memorandum from a unanimous panel, with no noted dissent from denial of rehearing. The petition identifies no circuit split and mainly seeks correction of the application of settled doctrine to a particular summary-judgment record. The Court may regard *Nelson* and *Deorle* together as sufficiently specific, may prefer to let factual disputes proceed, or may see no need for another intervention immediately after *Zorn*. The case also has not yet acquired the much stronger relist or CVSG signals.

## Calibration

The committed statpack reports a 5.36% estimated grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, a 3.0% grant rate for modern cert petitions from the Ninth Circuit, 3.0% without a CVSG, and 0.8% with no relist. Those cuts overlap and are not multiplied. The requested response and the close fit with the Court's recent qualified-immunity summary reversals justify a substantial upward adjustment, but not enough to make a grant more likely than denial. Conditional on intervention, a *Zorn*-based GVR and a summary reversal are both plausible; on the overall disposition axis, denial remains the modal result.

The 0.44 big-case score reflects a recurring national issue—qualified immunity in protest policing—but also the narrow, record-specific, nonprecedential posture. A decision would more likely reinforce existing specificity doctrine than announce a major new rule.
