## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(4) requires a court to decide whether a judgment is void when the judgment allegedly resolved a matter outside the pleadings, and whether law of the case may foreclose that inquiry. The petition relies principally on *Reynolds v. Stockton*, 140 U.S. 254 (1891), and describes the alleged mismatch as a jurisdictional defect.

Certiorari requires more than a claimed error. The Court generally looks for a genuine conflict, an important recurring federal question, and a clean vehicle. On the merits of the threshold issue, *United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa*, 559 U.S. 260 (2010), treats Rule 60(b)(4) relief as narrow: a judgment is not void merely because it may be erroneous, and voidness ordinarily requires a jurisdictional defect or a due-process violation amounting to a clear usurpation of power.

## Record and petition

The provisioned petition text is blank because the 37-page filing is a scan without an extractable text layer. I therefore visually read the petition from the exact Supreme Court PDF URL recorded in `documents.json`. The petition says the underlying 2022 complaint concerned the Foleys' asserted property interest in toucans and an alleged denial of process after earlier state litigation. It argues that the district court instead characterized the case as concerning an aviary and applied res judicata to a claim arising from the earlier zoning dispute.

The petition seeks to convert that alleged characterization error into a void-judgment defect. It says the district court denied Rule 60(b)(4) relief because the judgment had already been affirmed, and that the Eleventh Circuit used law of the case to affirm. But the claimed conflict is not persuasive. The other-circuit authorities cited for mandatory relief largely state the uncontroversial proposition that an actually void judgment must be vacated; they do not establish a square conflict over whether this judgment was void or whether law of the case could apply after the original affirmance. The petition itself acknowledges that the question is unlikely to generate an ordinary circuit split.

Vehicle and institutional signals are exceptionally poor. The ruling is unpublished and embedded in fact-specific, pro se litigation over a local zoning dispute. The petition describes roughly eighteen years of related proceedings. Most damagingly, it reports that the Eleventh Circuit awarded attorney's fees and double costs after finding the appeal frivolous. That makes the asserted conflict look like a repackaged disagreement with preclusion and claim characterization, not a clean Rule 60(b)(4) vehicle. The older public Eleventh Circuit opinion in *Foley v. Orange County*, 638 F. App'x 941 (11th Cir. 2016), confirms the long-running and highly case-specific nature of the dispute.

Both sets of respondents waived response. A waiver is not a merits concession, and the absence of a call for response, amicus support, CVSG, or relist as of the snapshot supplies no positive grant signal. The docket had only been distributed for its first conference.
