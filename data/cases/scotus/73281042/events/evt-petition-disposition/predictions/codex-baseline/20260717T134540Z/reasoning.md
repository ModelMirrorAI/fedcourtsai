# Prediction: denial

I estimate a **39% probability of a grant** and predict **denial** as the most likely disposition. “Grant” here includes a plenary grant, summary reversal, or GVR, although the plausible grant-side outcome is principally plenary review. No individual cert votes are predicted because they are not public and the pending Solicitor General brief creates unusually large uncertainty.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the First Amendment overrides antidiscrimination law when challenged discrimination furthers a private association's views about diversity, equity, or inclusion. The immediate dispute concerns eight of forty-nine at-large leadership seats in the voluntary New Jersey State Bar Association. Under Supreme Court Rule 10, review is most plausible if the decision conflicts with Supreme Court precedent or a genuine lower-court split and the case cleanly presents an important recurring federal question. Four Justices must vote to grant.

On the merits, the competing lines are substantial. The petition relies on *Runyon*, *Hishon*, *Roberts*, *Hurley*, and *303 Creative* for the status-versus-message distinction: discriminatory conduct ordinarily receives no First Amendment protection merely because it communicates a view. The Association relies on *Boy Scouts of America v. Dale*: applying antidiscrimination law can violate expressive-association rights when compelled inclusion or altered leadership materially burdens an organization's message. The lower court treated the Association's leadership composition as part of its expression.

## Grant signals

The docket supplies a much stronger signal than an ordinary paid petition. The Court requested a response after the Association initially waived one, received briefs from several nationally prominent amici including eighteen States, distributed the completed briefing for conference, and on June 22 invited the Solicitor General to express the views of the United States. The committed statpack estimates a 3.0% grant rate for modern cert petitions without a CVSG but 27.1% for CVSG petitions; the 2025 Term paid-petition estimate is 5.4%. Thus the CVSG, not the whole-docket rate, is the proper starting point.

The issue is nationally consequential. The petition argues that the decision supplies a First Amendment defense for diversity-conscious programs after *Students for Fair Admissions* and conflicts in principle with the Eleventh Circuit's *Fearless Fund* decision. The question also implicates federal antidiscrimination statutes the United States enforces, making the Solicitor General's participation especially informative. The current Court has shown sustained interest in both compelled expression and status-based classifications. These features justify moving above the raw CVSG prior.

## Denial and vehicle signals

The brief in opposition nevertheless identifies several independent obstacles:

- Article III standing was not litigated in state court, and Saadeh never applied for the challenged seats, including while all seats were open during the injunction. His request for nominal damages still requires a concrete injury.
- The decision is unpublished and nonprecedential, comes from an intermediate state appellate court, and the New Jersey Supreme Court merely denied discretionary review.
- The leadership policy changed after the litigation. The Association says all at-large seats are now reachable through affinity-bar membership open without demographic restrictions, and Saadeh himself belongs to a qualifying association.
- The state appellate court did not decide whether the policy violated New Jersey law. Even reversal on the First Amendment issue could leave an unresolved state-law ground.
- The claimed split is imperfect. *Fearless Fund* concerned a grant contest that the Eleventh Circuit characterized as nonexpressive conduct; the Association's selection of its own leaders fits *Dale* more closely. The Fourth Circuit authority concerns federal-funding conditions. The remaining disputes are mostly pending or settled, favoring percolation.
- The petition apparently did not press *Runyon* below, adding preservation risk.

Those defects make this a poor vehicle even if several Justices want the broader issue resolved. The CVSG may produce a grant recommendation, especially because the United States has a direct institutional interest, but it may instead recommend denial while acknowledging the question's importance. That unresolved brief is the largest source of forecast uncertainty.

## Bottom line

Starting from the 27.1% CVSG prior, I increase for the response request, organized amicus support, ideological salience, national consequences, and a credible tension between *Dale* and antidiscrimination precedents. I then discount for standing, mootness/policy-change concerns, the nonprecedential posture, the unaddressed state-law question, preservation, and the absence of a square split. The result is **P(any grant) = 0.39**, below the threshold for predicting a grant.

The full questions-presented extract, petition, and brief in opposition were available, nonempty, and untruncated. The live corpus-prior query was attempted but the remote corpus endpoint could not be reached, so no case-level priors informed the forecast; calibration instead used the committed statpack and the provisioned filings. CourtListener citation searches confirmed the cited *Dale*, *Roberts*, and *Fearless Fund* authorities but did not add case-specific facts.
