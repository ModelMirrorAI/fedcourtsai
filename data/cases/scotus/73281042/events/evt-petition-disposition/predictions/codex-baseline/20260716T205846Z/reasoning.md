# Prediction

I predict denial, but with a 0.43 probability of a grant. That is far above the ordinary cert-petition baseline because the Court invited the Solicitor General's views on June 22, 2026, after considering the petition at the June 18 conference. A CVSG is a strong sign of genuine interest, and the current federal government's active focus on unlawful DEI programs makes a grant recommendation plausible. The point prediction remains denial because the opposition identifies multiple independent vehicle defects that could lead the Court or the Solicitor General to prefer a cleaner case.

## Question and governing considerations

The petition asks whether the First Amendment overrides antidiscrimination laws when discrimination advances a defendant's views about diversity, equity, or inclusion. The petitioner characterizes the New Jersey State Bar Association's designated leadership seats as status-based discrimination that is conduct rather than speech under *Runyon*, *Hishon*, *Roberts*, and the status/message distinction in *303 Creative*. The Association answers that it is a private, voluntary expressive organization and that its choice of leaders communicates its longstanding diversity message, bringing the case within *Boy Scouts of America v. Dale*.

At the cert stage, the practical question is whether four Justices will view the issue as important and sufficiently divided or unsettled, and whether this record is a suitable vehicle under Rule 10. The merits are relevant chiefly insofar as the state court's reasoning may appear inconsistent with recent antidiscrimination decisions.

## Factors favoring a grant

- The June 22 CVSG is the strongest docket signal. The committed statpack reports a 27.1% historical grant rate for resolved modern cert petitions with a CVSG, compared with 3.0% for petitions without one. The case is also a paid petition; the statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term.
- The petition presents a nationally salient clash between expressive-association doctrine and post-*SFFA* challenges to DEI programs. It is supported by amici including 18 States, Students for Fair Admissions, America First Legal, and the Manhattan Institute.
- The petition plausibly identifies doctrinal tension with *American Alliance for Equal Rights v. Fearless Fund Management*, 103 F.4th 765 (11th Cir. 2024), which rejected a First Amendment defense to a race-restricted grant contest, and with the Fourth Circuit's treatment of antidiscrimination compliance in *National Association of Diversity Officers in Higher Education v. Trump*. A CourtListener MCP lookup confirmed the published Eleventh Circuit decision and citation.
- The Appellate Division deliberately resolved the litigation on federal constitutional grounds, held that *Dale* controlled, and ordered dismissal with prejudice. The question is important to the federal government's enforcement and defense of several antidiscrimination statutes, making the requested government view potentially influential.

## Factors favoring denial

- The decision is unpublished and nonprecedential, comes from an intermediate state court, and leaves no broad binding conflict within New Jersey. The alleged split is imperfect: *Fearless Fund* involved a grant competition found nonexpressive, while this case concerns leadership selection by an organization found expressive.
- Article III standing is a substantial threshold problem on direct review from state court. Saadeh did not apply for the challenged seats, did not allege in the state-court record that he was ready to apply, and did not apply during the period when all seats were open. Nominal damages do not eliminate the need for a concrete, particularized injury.
- The challenged program changed. The opposition says that all eight current at-large seats can be reached through membership in affinity bar associations open regardless of demographic status, and Saadeh is himself a member and former president of a qualifying Muslim lawyers association. That weakens both prospective relief and the petition's framing.
- The Appellate Division did not decide whether the selection program violates the New Jersey Law Against Discrimination. A federal reversal could therefore lead only to a remand for an unresolved state-law issue, reducing the practical value of review.
- The issue has had limited lower-court percolation, and the record is fact-bound under *Dale*'s inquiry into whether compelled inclusion or altered selection would significantly burden the organization's expression.

## Calibration and likely form of disposition

The CVSG and the likely policy interest of the United States justify raising the forecast well above the 27.1% CVSG aggregate. The ideologically salient merits question and organized amicus support also help. I stop below 50% because the standing defect, changed policy, nonprecedential posture, and undecided state-law ground are not cosmetic; together they give the Solicitor General and the Court several ways to recommend or choose denial without resolving the merits.

If the Court acts favorably, a plenary grant is more likely than a GVR because there is no identified intervening controlling decision that would clearly require reconsideration. I therefore predict `denied` as the most likely disposition rather than `gvr` or `granted`.
