# Reasoning

**Case Summary:**
*Rajeh A. Saadeh v. New Jersey State Bar Association* (No. 25-1002) involves a challenge to the NJSBA's policy of reserving specific leadership seats for members of underrepresented demographic groups (a DEI "set-aside" policy). The petitioner argues this violates state antidiscrimination laws. The New Jersey Appellate Division ruled for the NJSBA, holding that the bar association is an "expressive association" under the First Amendment (relying on *Boy Scouts of America v. Dale*) and that its diversity quotas are protected speech. The New Jersey Supreme Court declined to review. 

**Legal Context & Salience:**
This petition arrives in the wake of *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard* (SFFA), where the Supreme Court struck down race-based affirmative action in university admissions. The present case tests whether private organizations can shield demographic quotas from antidiscrimination laws using the First Amendment's expressive association doctrine. This is an extremely high-salience issue for the conservative legal movement, which seeks to dismantle DEI programs across various sectors. The petition has attracted amicus briefs from prominent conservative groups, including SFFA itself, America First Legal Foundation, the Manhattan Institute, and 18 states (led by West Virginia).

**Procedural Posture & Signals:**
The Court has called for the views of the Solicitor General (CVSG). The base rate for cert grants jumps significantly when a CVSG is issued (historically around 27%, compared to ~3% overall, and even higher when the petition aligns with the Court majority's jurisprudential priorities). The CVSG signals that at least one, and likely several, Justices consider the issue legally significant and potentially worthy of review.

Given the conservative supermajority's skepticism of race/gender-conscious policies and their interest in clarifying First Amendment boundaries (as seen in *303 Creative* and *Dale*), the Court is highly likely to take this case. Even if the SG ultimately recommends denying cert, the conservative majority has demonstrated a willingness to grant review over SG opposition when a major ideological priority is at stake. The combination of a CVSG, intense amicus interest, and a core culture-war legal question points strongly toward a grant.

**Prediction:**
I predict the Court will grant the petition. I assign a probability of 75%, reflecting the very strong signals (CVSG + elite amici) and the Court's known appetite for this exact area of law, tempered only by the inherent unpredictability of the cert process and the unknown forthcoming SG recommendation.
