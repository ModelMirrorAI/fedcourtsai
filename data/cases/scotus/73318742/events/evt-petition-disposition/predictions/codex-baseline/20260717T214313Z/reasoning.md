# Prediction: denied

I predict denial of certiorari, with a 0.3% probability of any grant (including a GVR).

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether absolute judicial immunity under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 is limited when a state judge rules against litigants shortly after being served with their federal civil-rights complaint, and whether the due-process recusal principles of *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.* and *Williams v. Pennsylvania* constrain immunity in that setting. It also characterizes campaign-finance disclosure, docket management, and related conduct as nonjudicial functions under *Forrester v. White*.

Under Supreme Court Rule 10, certiorari ordinarily requires a conflict, an important unsettled federal question, or a serious departure from accepted law. The Court generally does not decide questions that were neither pressed nor passed on below, and independent jurisdictional or remedial grounds make a case a poor vehicle. On the underlying doctrine, judicial immunity ordinarily covers acts performed in a judicial capacity unless undertaken in the clear absence of jurisdiction; allegations of bias, bad faith, or corruption do not by themselves defeat it.

## Record signals

The provisioned snapshot shows a paid 2025 Term petition from an unpublished Sixth Circuit judgment, filed May 6, 2026. A brief in opposition was filed, and the case was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. The snapshot shows no CVSG, amici, or relist as of July 17, 2026.

The petition emphasizes a striking alleged sequence: Judge Bunnell was served at chambers with the Wains' § 1983 complaint and three days later disposed of long-pending counterclaims at a hearing the Wains say had been noticed for narrower matters. It argues that the judge's alleged campaign-depository relationship with the foreclosing bank and contributions associated with counsel intensified the due-process concern. If cleanly presented, the relationship between constitutional recusal rules and immunity could have broader doctrinal significance.

The brief in opposition identifies unusually strong obstacles to review. It says the complaint did not allege the post-service conduct, the Wains did not amend to add it, and no lower court considered the petition's *Caperton/Williams* theory. The petition concedes there is no traditional circuit split and supplies no conflicting holding on materially identical facts. The Sixth Circuit also relied on official-capacity/Eleventh Amendment principles, § 1983's limitation on injunctive relief against judicial officers, the absence of acts outside jurisdiction, and the availability of state appellate review. The underlying rulings, recusal decisions, and case management are conventional judicial functions. Treating service of a litigant's suit against a judge as itself disabling the judge would also invite strategic judge-suing, making this an unattractive posture for creating an immunity exception. Several related state proceedings remain unresolved or were held in abeyance at the petitioners' request.

These defects are cumulative: the Court would need to overlook nonpreservation, the absence of a split, an unpublished decision, independent grounds, and a fact-bound pro se record before reaching the proposed constitutional rule. The materials do not suggest an intervening Supreme Court decision that would make a GVR likely.
