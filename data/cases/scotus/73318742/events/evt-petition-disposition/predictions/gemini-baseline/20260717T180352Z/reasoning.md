The Wains, proceeding pro se but having paid the filing fee, petition for certiorari to review the Sixth Circuit's affirmance of the dismissal of their § 1983 lawsuit against two Kentucky state court judges. The Wains sued the judges who ruled against them in a protracted state-court foreclosure action.

The petition attempts to frame a novel constitutional question: whether the Due Process Clause (via *Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.* and *Williams v. Pennsylvania*) limits the scope of absolute judicial immunity when a judge issues a ruling shortly after the litigant serves them with a § 1983 complaint. The petitioners argue that the lawsuit itself creates a personal conflict of interest, thereby making the subsequent ruling a constitutional violation that strips the judge of immunity.

This petition is essentially a collateral attack on a state court judgment by disgruntled litigants. It faces insurmountable hurdles that make a grant of certiorari extraordinarily unlikely:
1.  **Settled Law on Immunity:** It is black-letter law that absolute judicial immunity protects judges from civil liability for acts taken in their judicial capacity, even if alleged to be malicious or corrupt (*Stump v. Sparkman*, *Mireles v. Waco*). Allowing a litigant to strip a judge's immunity merely by filing a lawsuit against them would completely eviscerate the doctrine and allow litigants to unilaterally disqualify judges.
2.  **Eleventh Amendment / Official Capacity:** As the lower courts noted, the Wains sued the judges only in their *official capacities*. This means the claims were properly treated as claims against the state itself, which are barred by the Eleventh Amendment. The petition's focus on individual absolute judicial immunity does not overcome the sovereign immunity bar that independently supported dismissal.
3.  **§ 1983 Bar on Injunctive Relief:** To the extent the Wains sought injunctive relief, § 1983 expressly bars such relief against judicial officers for judicial acts unless a declaratory decree was violated or declaratory relief was unavailable, neither of which was alleged here.
4.  **Error Correction / Unpreserved Issues:** The BIO points out that the specific framing of the conflict-of-interest exception to judicial immunity was not presented to or considered by the lower courts, making it a poor vehicle.

Given the frivolous nature of the underlying legal theory—which is a common tactic among frustrated pro se litigants—and the multiple independent bases for dismissal in the lower courts, this petition presents no circuit split or important federal question requiring the Court's intervention. The base rate for such petitions is functionally zero. The petition will be denied.
