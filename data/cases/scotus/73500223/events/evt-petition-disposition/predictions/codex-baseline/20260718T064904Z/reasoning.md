# Prediction: denied

I assign a 12% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. This is above the ordinary paid-petition baseline because the petition presents a consequential constitutional question with experienced Supreme Court counsel and four amicus filings, but the vehicle and conflict problems are likely dispositive.

## Question and governing standard

The question is whether California violates the Takings Clause or the First Amendment by requiring owners of privately owned shopping centers that are open to the public to permit unwanted expressive activity, notwithstanding *PruneYard Shopping Center v. Robins*, 447 U.S. 74 (1980). Certiorari is most likely when a lower-court conflict or an important, unsettled federal question calls for resolution. A request to overrule controlling precedent can qualify, but the Court ordinarily prefers a clean, final vehicle with developed facts.

The provisioned question-presented text and petition argue that *PruneYard* cannot coexist with the per se physical-takings rule in *Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid*, 594 U.S. 139 (2021), or with modern compelled-speech decisions including *Janus* and *Moody v. NetChoice*. The brief in opposition responds that those later decisions expressly distinguished *PruneYard*: the shopping center was already open to the public, and it was not itself engaged in expressive activity that compelled access would alter.

## Record-specific signals

The snapshot dated July 18, 2026 shows a paid petition from a published California Court of Appeal decision, a response on the merits, a reply, and four supporting amicus briefs. The case was distributed on July 15 for the September 28 conference. There is no CVSG and, at this pre-conference point, no relist. Those features give the petition more institutional support and visibility than a routine filing, but not the strong procedural signal that a relist or CVSG would supply.

The petition's best argument is doctrinal tension. California requires the centers to admit a speaker whom they would exclude, while *Cedar Point* treats a government-created access right as a paradigmatic physical taking. The First Amendment theory also offers the current Court an opportunity to treat forced hosting as an in-kind speech subsidy. The issue has consequences beyond these parties because California and several other states recognize speech rights on some private commercial property.

The brief in opposition identifies three stronger cert-stage obstacles. First, the decision is interlocutory: it reviews a preliminary injunction, leaves reasonable time-place-and-manner rules available, and precedes a final factual record. Second, the petition identifies differences among state constitutional regimes rather than a split over the governing federal rule; the court below simply followed *PruneYard*. Third, the Court itself recently preserved the relevant distinctions. *Cedar Point* described limitations on businesses generally open to the public as different from compelled entry onto closed property, and *Moody* distinguished a shopping center not engaged in its own expression. A grant would therefore require the Court to take a poor vehicle principally to reconsider a precedent it has continued to distinguish rather than treat as displaced.

## Base rates and calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term. Its broader modern-cert cuts estimate 3.0% for petitions without a CVSG and 0.8% for petitions with no relist. The small bucket for petitions from California's Second Appellate District has an 8.3% grant rate, but only twelve resolved cases, so I give it little independent weight. I move above those baselines because of the constitutional stakes, focused presentation, amicus support, and plausible tension with the current Court's property-rights doctrine. I stop at 12% because the absence of a split and final judgment, coupled with recent express preservation of *PruneYard*, makes denial much more likely than review.

No individual cert votes are predicted. The big-case score is 0.76 because a decision overruling or materially narrowing *PruneYard* would affect speech access to private commercial gathering places and the scope of the right to exclude, even though the petition is unlikely to be granted.
