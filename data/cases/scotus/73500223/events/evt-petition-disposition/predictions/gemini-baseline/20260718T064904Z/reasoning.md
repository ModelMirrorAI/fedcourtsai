This case presents a direct challenge to the Court's 1980 decision in *PruneYard Shopping Center v. Robins*, which upheld a California constitutional right of access to private shopping centers for expressive activity against First and Fifth Amendment challenges. Petitioners argue that *PruneYard* is irreconcilable with the Court's recent physical takings jurisprudence (particularly *Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid*) and its compelled-speech jurisprudence (such as *Moody v. NetChoice* and *Janus*).

**Substantive Salience and Prior Base Rates**
The substantive issue is highly salient for the current conservative majority, which has shown a strong interest in strengthening the "right to exclude" as a fundamental property right (*Cedar Point*) and broadly protecting the right against compelled speech. The petition has drawn amicus support from four prominent conservative/libertarian organizations (Cato Institute, Washington Legal Foundation, Goldwater Institute, and Manhattan Institute). This level of organized amicus support at the cert stage significantly elevates the case above the overall 2.5% - 3.0% base grant rate for paid petitions in recent Terms, signaling that the issue is closely watched and high-stakes. If decided, the case would have a massive impact on property rights and state constitutional law (warranting a high `big_case_score`).

**Vehicle and Posture Problems**
Despite the high substantive interest, the case suffers from significant vehicle flaws that make it a poor candidate for Supreme Court review at this time. As the Brief in Opposition highlights, the case arrives in an interlocutory posture—an appeal from a state trial court's ruling on a preliminary injunction. The trial court has not developed a full factual record regarding the specific nature of the "Centers," the extent of the physical invasion, or the precise time, place, and manner restrictions that might be imposed. Under 28 U.S.C. § 1257, the Supreme Court generally requires a "final judgment or decree" from the highest state court. While the *Cox Broadcasting* exceptions can sometimes salvage jurisdiction, the lack of a developed factual record is especially problematic for Takings and First Amendment claims, which are often highly fact-dependent. The Court typically prefers to address major constitutional shifts, like overruling a 40-year-old precedent, on a clean, fully developed record. 

**Conclusion**
While the Court is likely interested in revisiting *PruneYard*, the interlocutory posture and undeveloped factual record make this a poor vehicle. The Court can easily deny certiorari now and wait for the case to reach a final judgment, or wait for a better vehicle. Therefore, the most likely disposition is a denial, though the probability of a grant is elevated relative to the base rate due to the substantive salience and amicus support.
