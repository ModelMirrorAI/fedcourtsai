# Majestic Realty Co. v. Salazar, No. 25-1322 — cert prediction

**Prediction: deny, P(grant) = 0.10.**

## The legal question

The petition asks the Court to overrule *PruneYard Shopping Center v.
Robins*, 447 U.S. 74 (1980), and hold that California violates the Takings
Clause and the First Amendment when it requires private shopping-center
owners to host unwanted expressive activity. Petitioners (represented by
Pacific Legal Foundation) argue *PruneYard* is irreconcilable with the
modern per se physical-takings rule (*Loretto*, *Nollan*, *Horne*, *Cedar
Point Nursery v. Hassid*) and with the compelled-speech/speech-subsidy line
(*Janus*, *Moody v. NetChoice*). Respondent, an individual activist who
sought to leaflet at petitioners' Redlands shopping centers, prevailed in a
published California Court of Appeal decision reversing the denial of a
preliminary injunction; the California Supreme Court denied review on
February 25, 2026.

## Facts from the snapshot and provisioned documents that drive the call

- **Paid petition**, Term 2025 docket (25-1322), docketed May 28, 2026.
- **Four cert-stage amicus briefs** from repeat institutional players (Cato
  Institute et al., Washington Legal Foundation, Goldwater Institute,
  Manhattan Institute) — a genuine, coordinated cert campaign.
- **Top-tier repeat-player counsel**: PLF has recently gotten several
  takings cases granted (*Cedar Point*, *Tyler v. Hennepin County*, *Sheetz
  v. El Dorado County*), and the QP is clean, preserved, and squarely
  presented.
- **Posture**: the decision below reviewed only a **preliminary
  injunction**; merits proceedings continue in the trial court. The BIO
  leads with this (interlocutory posture, undeveloped record, *Hamilton-
  Brown Shoe*), notes the injunction leaves time-place-manner regulation
  open, and stresses that the court below simply applied controlling
  precedent.
- **No split**: the petition candidly frames the case as an
  overrule-*PruneYard* vehicle; it cites state-by-state divergence in
  *state* constitutional law, which is federalism rather than a federal
  conflict, as the BIO points out.
- **Docket state at snapshot**: reply filed July 13, 2026; **distributed
  July 15 for the September 28, 2026 (long) conference** — the first
  distribution, so no relist or CVSG signal exists yet.

## Base rates and priors

From the committed statpack (live/historical slice, denial-reweighted):
modern discretionary cert petitions are granted ~2.5–3.3% per recent Term;
**paid** petitions run ~5.4% (Term 2025) to ~6.9% (Term 2024). Petitions
with **zero relists** grant at ~0.8%; grants overwhelmingly come after one
or more relists (1 relist → 7.6%, 2 → 33.6%, 3+ → 21.8%). No CVSG here
(CVSG cases grant at ~27%, but none was requested). A corpus query for
recently granted 2020s SCOTUS petitions confirms the pattern: grants show
distribution counts of 3–22 — repeated conference distributions — before
the grant order. This petition sits before its first conference, so the
strongest pre-grant signals are unobservable at the snapshot date; the
prediction must price the *chance* those signals later materialize.

## Weighing

Upward from the ~5–7% paid-petition anchor:

1. The amicus support (4 briefs, ideologically coordinated) is the classic
   marker of a serious cert effort and historically correlates with a
   materially elevated grant rate.
2. The current Court has shown sustained appetite for property-rights
   cases, and *Cedar Point* already adopted the doctrinal framework
   (per se physical taking for state-conferred access rights) that makes
   *PruneYard* look anomalous. Some Justices would plausibly welcome the
   question.
3. The facts are clean for petitioners: a total ban on expressive
   activity, an unsympathetic third-party speaker, and a published
   decision resting squarely on *PruneYard*.

Downward:

1. **Vehicle**: an interlocutory state preliminary-injunction ruling with
   an undeveloped record is a textbook reason to deny and await final
   judgment; the federal questions will be fully preserved then.
2. **No conflict**: the Court is being asked purely to overrule, and it
   demands "special justification" plus usually a percolated record or a
   drumbeat of separate opinions. A CourtListener sweep found **no**
   post-2020 SCOTUS separate opinion urging *PruneYard*'s reconsideration
   — *Cedar Point* (2021) and *Moody* (2024) each **distinguished** it on
   the open-to-the-public ground rather than questioning it, which the BIO
   exploits effectively.
3. The respondent's brief supplies a limiting principle the Court can
   credit (public-accommodations tradition; *Yee*; *Heart of Atlanta*),
   making a summary or hasty grant less attractive.
4. Base-rate gravity: even strong paid petitions with amicus support are
   denied far more often than granted absent a split.

The plausible grant path — relist(s) after the long conference, four
Justices willing to take the overruling question on an interlocutory
posture — is real but narrow. I estimate **P(grant, including GVR) ≈
0.10**, with denial the clearly most likely disposition. A GVR is
implausible (no intervening decision to GVR in light of).

## Stakes

`big_case_score` 0.6: if granted and decided, overruling *PruneYard* would
be a major property-rights/First Amendment decision ending state-mandated
speech access at California (and New Jersey, Colorado, Massachusetts)
shopping centers and reshaping ballot-initiative signature gathering — a
significant, closely watched outcome, though affecting a handful of states
rather than a nationwide statute.
