# Prediction

I predict that the petition will be **granted**, with a 0.74 probability of a grant. “Granted” includes a summary grant-vacate-remand disposition rather than only plenary review. I do not predict individual votes because petition dispositions ordinarily do not disclose every Justice's vote and the pre-disposition record does not support a reliable nine-vote allocation.

## Question and governing standard

The respondents' brief frames two questions: whether Alabama's 2023 congressional plan violates Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act and whether Section 2, as applied by the district court, is constitutional. The petition seeks certiorari before judgment from pending Eleventh Circuit appeals. That posture requires an issue of imperative public importance warranting departure from ordinary appellate review; the usual certiorari considerations also favor review of an important, recurring federal question or a conflict requiring resolution.

On the merits underlying the petition, *Thornburg v. Gingles* supplies the vote-dilution framework, and *Allen v. Milligan*, 599 U.S. 1 (2023), recently reaffirmed that framework in litigation concerning Alabama's preceding congressional plan. The provisioned brief in opposition argues that the extensive trial record satisfies all three *Gingles* preconditions and the totality-of-circumstances inquiry, and that Section 2 is valid enforcement legislation under the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments.

## Pre-disposition signals

The ordinary modern cert baseline strongly favors denial: the committed statpack reports an estimated 4.9% grant rate, and its Eleventh Circuit cut reports 6.7% grants among resolved petitions. Several unusually strong case-specific signals justify moving far above that baseline:

- This is a certiorari-before-judgment petition involving congressional redistricting, a constitutional challenge to a central Voting Rights Act provision, and election-administration urgency. Those features make the question nationally important even though the extraordinary posture normally counsels restraint.
- The Court had already reviewed the closely related Alabama dispute in *Allen*. The respondents' brief emphasizes that the district court entered a permanent injunction after an 11-day trial, 23 live witnesses, 28 designated witnesses, stipulated facts, and more than 790 exhibits, and also found intentional discrimination. That developed record makes some form of dispositive Supreme Court action more plausible.
- The petition was initially distributed for the November 21, 2025 conference but remained pending for months. The pre-disposition docket then shows an April 30 motion to expedite, a May 8 emergency stay application, a requested response due May 11, and redistribution for the May 14 conference. That prolonged hold followed by an accelerated emergency sequence is much stronger than an ordinary paid petition and suggests coordination with related litigation or imminent summary action.
- Respondents principally seek denial and summary affirmance in related cases, but expressly state that if the Court declines summary affirmance, they join Alabama's request to grant this petition so the related disputes can be considered together. The absence of categorical opposition to review lowers an important procedural barrier.

Against grant, *Allen* rejected Alabama's effort to remake Section 2 doctrine only recently, and the brief describes fact-intensive findings entitled to substantial deference. Ordinary appellate review also remained incomplete. Those considerations reduce the chance of plenary review, but they are compatible with a summary grant, vacatur, and remand if an intervening related decision changes the governing analysis. The long hold and emergency activity make that summary path more likely than outright denial.
