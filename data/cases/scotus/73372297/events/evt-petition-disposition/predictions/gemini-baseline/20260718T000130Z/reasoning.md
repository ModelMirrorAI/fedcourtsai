# Reasoning

## Analysis of the Case
This is a paid petition for certiorari from a pro se petitioner against the Secretary of Health and Human Services, originating from the Federal Circuit. Such cases generally involve Vaccine Act claims or other HHS administrative appeals. 

The questions presented are drafted in a highly unprofessional, pro se manner, alleging that the case was "illegally hijacked," citing "genetics/health fraud," "attorney/judge collusion," and "targeted evidence suppression/deletion/exclusion." The arguments lack any coherent legal foundation or reference to an important federal question, a circuit split, or a conflict with Supreme Court precedent, which are the primary considerations under Supreme Court Rule 10 for granting certiorari.

The Solicitor General has filed a waiver of the right to respond. A waiver by the respondent—particularly the federal government—is a strong indicator that the petition is viewed as presenting no substantial issues worthy of Supreme Court review. Furthermore, the case has been distributed for conference without any request for a response by the Court, and there are no relists or CVSG indications.

## Base Rates and Prior Indicators
According to the `statpack.md`, the base rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions being granted is roughly 2.5% to 3.0%. For cases where there are no relists, the grant rate drops below 1%. For cases that lack coherent legal arguments and rely on conspiracy allegations against lower courts, the grant probability is effectively zero. 

## Prediction
Given the frivolous nature of the questions presented and the waiver of response by the government, the Supreme Court is certain to deny this petition.
