# Prediction: denied

I predict denial and assign a 0.1% probability to any grant-side disposition, including a plenary grant or GVR.

## Question and posture

The petition arises from a Vaccine Act compensation proceeding. In substance, it asks whether the judgments below may stand despite alleged attorney malpractice, judicial collusion, mishandling of evidence, unlawful acquisition or disclosure of military and veterans' records, and errors in calculating damages. The petition says the Federal Circuit left the judgment in place on a timeliness ground rather than reaching those allegations.

The governing certiorari standard is discretionary and requires a compelling reason, conventionally a developed conflict among courts or an important recurring federal question. The Court ordinarily does not grant review merely to correct fact-bound error, reassess evidence, or resolve disputes about the application of settled law in one case.

## Case-specific signals

The provisioned questions presented and the full 19-page petition are overwhelmingly case-specific. They do not identify a conflict among appellate courts, formulate a narrow rule of federal law, or explain how the asserted privacy and Vaccine Act issues were preserved and decided below. The petition instead disputes the conduct and factual conclusions of the special master, former counsel, the Court of Federal Claims, and the Federal Circuit. That framing creates severe preservation, record, and vehicle problems. Allegations concerning a missed deadline make the vehicle still less suitable because the Court generally will not reach issues that the judgment below did not decide.

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition from the Federal Circuit, distributed for the September 28 conference with no relist yet. The Solicitor General waived a response. A waiver is not a merits concession, and the absence of a call for response is a strong practical signal that the petition has not attracted institutional interest. No brief in opposition was provisioned, so there is no respondent merits argument to weigh.

The committed statpack places the broad modern cert grant rate near 3%, the 2025 Term paid-petition rate at about 5.4%, the Federal Circuit rate at 3.0%, and the zero-relist bucket at 0.8%. This petition warrants a large downward adjustment from even the zero-relist rate: it is pro se, does not present a split, seeks review of intensely record-bound grievances, and appears procedurally barred. A GVR is also implausible because the record identifies no intervening precedent, mootness development, or other remand trigger.

## Forecast

Denial is overwhelmingly the likeliest disposition. I do not predict individual cert votes because the public record supplies no reliable basis to distinguish among Justices, but fewer than four votes to grant is the clear aggregate forecast. The low big-case score reflects the difference between the petitioner's substantial personal stakes and the limited broader significance of the legal vehicle as presented.

CourtListener searches did not locate the specific lower-court disposition, and the final attempted search was briefly throttled. The forecast therefore relies on the provisioned snapshot and petition, the committed base rates, and general certiorari standards rather than an independently retrieved lower-court opinion.
