The issue in this case is whether the United States has an affirmative, money-mandating fiduciary duty under the *Winters* doctrine and 25 C.F.R. § 152.22 to protect an Indian tribe's existing water rights from third-party diversion.

In *Arizona v. Navajo Nation*, 599 U.S. 555 (2023), the Supreme Court recently held that treaties establishing reservations do not require the federal government to take affirmative steps to secure water for a tribe, emphasizing that courts should not infer duties not found in the text of a treaty, statute, or regulation. The Winnemucca Indian Colony attempts to distinguish *Navajo Nation* by arguing that *Navajo Nation* involved a request to secure *new* water infrastructure, whereas the present case involves the government's failure to prevent third-party diversion of *existing* water on the tribe's trust lands.

The Federal Circuit rejected this distinction, noting that 25 C.F.R. § 152.22—a regulation governing the conveyance of trust lands—does not mention water rights or establish any specific governmental duty regarding the diversion of tribal water. Thus, the court found no money-mandating source of law to support the tribe's breach of trust claim under the Tucker Act.
