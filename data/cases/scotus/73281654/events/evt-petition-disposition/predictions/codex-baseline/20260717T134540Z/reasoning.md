## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether the United States may owe Indian Tucker Act damages for failing to prevent third parties from diverting water allegedly appurtenant to tribal trust land. Its proposed source of a specific fiduciary and money-mandating duty is the combination of the *Winters* reserved-water doctrine and 25 C.F.R. § 152.22, which restricts conveyances of tribal trust land.

The principal obstacle is the requirement that a tribe identify a specific duty-imposing statute, treaty, or regulation before common-law trust principles can support damages. As the petition itself recounts, the Federal Circuit held that *Winters* does not independently create a trust obligation and that § 152.22 neither mentions water rights nor imposes a duty relevant to third-party diversion. *Arizona v. Navajo Nation*, 599 U.S. 555 (2023), reinforces the Court's reluctance to infer affirmative water-management duties from the general federal-tribal trust relationship.

## Case-specific assessment

The strongest grant signal is procedural: after the United States waived response, the Court requested one. That indicates genuine chambers interest and materially lifts the case above an ordinary paid petition. The underlying issue also has real stakes for the habitability and value of tribal lands, and the petition offers a facially meaningful distinction between securing new water and protecting existing water from third-party interference.

The petition nevertheless presents several weaknesses:

- It identifies no square circuit split. The second question is essentially an error-correction request, and the three questions substantially overlap.
- Section 152.22 concerns conveyance of land and supplies no express water-management, enforcement, or compensation command. Extending it through *Winters* is difficult to reconcile with the Court's source-of-law methodology.
- The petition's reliance on *Ute Indian Tribe*, 99 F.4th 1353 (Fed. Cir. 2024), is imperfect. That decision allowed existing-infrastructure claims to proceed because a 1906 Act expressly placed the irrigation works and associated title in the Secretary “in trust for the Indians”; it rejected *Winters* and a more general statute as sufficient sources for duties to secure new water. The Winnemucca petition points to no comparably specific trust-management enactment.
- The asserted third-party diversion distinction may narrow *Arizona v. Navajo Nation*, but the claim still asks the government to take affirmative protective action and then pay damages for not doing so. That makes this a poor vehicle for recognizing a broader distinction.
