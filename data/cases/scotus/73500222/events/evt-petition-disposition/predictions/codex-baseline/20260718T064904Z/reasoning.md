# Prediction: denied

I assign a 3.5% probability to any grant and predict denial. This is a forward prediction made from the July 18, 2026 snapshot, the complete 47-page petition and extracted questions presented, the committed corpus statpack, and the pre-petition legal authorities identified through CourtListener. No merits response was provisioned because respondents waived their right to respond.

## Questions and governing law

The petition asks (1) whether a court exercising its discretion under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d)(1) may consider the chilling effect of costs on future civil-rights litigants and (2) whether the Eighth Circuit improperly resolved disputed facts itself rather than remanding. Rule 54(d)(1) presumptively allows costs to the prevailing party, but the district court retains discretion to deny costs for a sufficiently stated reason. The petition invokes the ordinary certiorari considerations: an asserted circuit conflict, recurrence and importance, and departure from settled limits on appellate factfinding.

The first question has the better certworthiness argument. The petition contrasts the Ninth Circuit's decisions in *Stanley v. University of Southern California* and *Association of Mexican-American Educators v. California*, which permit consideration of chilling effects in civil-rights cases, with Sixth and Eighth Circuit decisions declining to give that consideration weight. CourtListener results confirm that the Ninth Circuit formulation has been repeated. They also show, however, that the broader law of cost reduction turns on overlapping considerations such as indigence, relative resources, public importance, and the strength of Rule 54's presumption. That makes the claimed conflict less clean than the petition suggests: courts can reach different results through case-specific exercises of discretion without necessarily disagreeing over a controlling rule.

## Case-specific signals

The equity of the underlying facts favors petitioners. Fourteen indigent, civilly committed plaintiffs face a $366,461.96 joint-and-several cost award after long-running civil-rights litigation. The district court found the litigation nonfrivolous, difficult, important, and potentially chilled by a large award. Those facts make the issue concrete and nationally relevant.

The vehicle is nevertheless weak. The Eighth Circuit relied on the parties' 2013 joint recommendation to split court-appointed expert costs and held that petitioners failed to overcome Rule 54's presumption. The second question therefore depends on whether the panel permissibly applied the record or impermissibly found a disputed fact about ability to pay. It offers a poor basis for resolving the broader first question. The petition also introduces an Excessive Fines theory that was not adjudicated below, which is not a clean alternative ground for review.

Procedural signals are negative. Respondents waived a response, no Supreme Court-stage amicus support is reflected in the snapshot, there has been no CVSG or relist, and the case was distributed for its first conference. The same underlying litigation previously produced several unsuccessful certiorari petitions on other rulings, suggesting limited institutional appetite even though this petition presents a different issue.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 3.3% grant rate for modern petitions from the Eighth Circuit. Petitions with no eventual relist have a much lower 0.8% grant rate, although that statistic cannot be applied mechanically before the first conference because this petition could still be relisted. The asserted circuit disagreement supplies some upward pressure; the waived response, lack of developed Supreme Court briefing, discretionary posture, and fact-bound second question supply stronger downward pressure. On balance, 3.5% is an appropriate probability of any grant. A GVR is especially unlikely because the record identifies no intervening authority or mootness development.

I omit individual vote predictions because a certiorari denial ordinarily does not disclose votes. The 0.38 big-case score reflects a meaningful access-to-courts concern with nationwide application, tempered by the technical costs posture and idiosyncratic expert-fee record.
