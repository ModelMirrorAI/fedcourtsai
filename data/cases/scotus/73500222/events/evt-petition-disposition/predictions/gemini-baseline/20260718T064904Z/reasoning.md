# Reasoning

## The Legal Question
The petition asks the Court to resolve whether a district court awarding costs to a prevailing party under Fed. R. Civ. P. 54(d)(1) may consider the potential "chilling effect" on future civil rights litigants, and whether the Eighth Circuit improperly engaged in fact-finding by relying on a decade-old settlement offer to conclude plaintiffs had the present ability to pay costs.

## The Governing Standard and Base Rates
Certiorari is discretionary. The statpack establishes a modern baseline grant rate for the Eighth Circuit of roughly 3.3%. This is a "Paid" petition but filed by indigent civilly committed patients. Most notably, the respondent waived the right to respond on June 26, 2026. The petition has been distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference with zero relists and no CVSG. A waiver of response is a strong negative signal; the Court almost never grants a petition without either receiving a response or affirmatively calling for one.

## Analysis of the Facts
The petition identifies a circuit split regarding Rule 54(d) costs: the Ninth Circuit (e.g., *Stanley v. USC*, *Association of Mexican-American Educators*) expressly allows district courts to consider the chilling effect on civil rights litigation when denying costs. The Sixth and Eighth Circuits reject any special carve-out or consideration for civil rights plaintiffs. While this is a genuine split on a recurring issue of practical importance for litigants, several factors weigh heavily against a grant:

1. **Fact-Bound Context:** The Eighth Circuit's decision did not rest exclusively on rejecting the "chilling effect" factor. It explicitly held that the district court abused its discretion by failing to consider a highly specific factual factor: the plaintiffs' 2013 offer to split the Rule 706 expert costs. The appellate court found this offer "presumably" showed an ability to pay which the plaintiffs had failed to rebut.
2. **Standard of Review:** Rule 54(d) cost awards are reviewed for abuse of discretion. The Supreme Court is generally reluctant to grant certiorari to micromanage discretionary, fact-bound cost determinations, even when they involve large sums ($366,461.96).
3. **Appellate Fact-Finding Claim:** The second question presented (improper appellate fact-finding) is a routine error-correction claim. The Court rarely grants certiorari simply to correct a court of appeals' alleged misapplication of the *Anderson v. Bessemer City* standard in an idiosyncratic context.
4. **Waiver of Response:** The respondent's waiver of response signals their belief that the petition does not warrant the Court's review and further highlights the unlikelihood of a grant absent a call for response.

## Conclusion
Given the fact-bound nature of the Eighth Circuit's abuse-of-discretion ruling, the routine nature of the error-correction claim, and the respondent's waiver of response, the probability of a grant is extremely low.

**Predicted Disposition:** Denied
**Probability (Grant):** 0.02
