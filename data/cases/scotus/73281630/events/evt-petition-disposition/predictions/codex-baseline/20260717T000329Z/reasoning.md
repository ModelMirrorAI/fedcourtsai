# Prediction: denied

I assign a 27% probability to a grant (including any grant-side disposition) and predict denial as the most likely disposition. This is substantially stronger than an ordinary paid petition, but the asserted split and vehicle are contestable enough that denial remains more likely.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether *Bethel School District No. 403 v. Fraser* permits a public school to censor nondisruptive political speech that is not facially profane or lewd. The students silently wore “Let’s Go Brandon” apparel; the parties agree the expression was political and caused no disruption. Under *Tinker v. Des Moines*, student political expression ordinarily may be restricted only upon actual or reasonably forecast substantial disruption or invasion of others' rights. *Fraser* separately permits schools to regulate lewd, indecent, or offensive student speech without satisfying *Tinker*'s disruption test.

The Sixth Circuit, over Judge Bush's dissent, treated the slogan's concededly vulgar referent as enough for *Fraser*, even though the words on the apparel were sanitized. Petitioners say that conflicts with the Third Circuit's en banc rule in *B.H. ex rel. Hawk* and the Ninth Circuit's approach in *Chandler*, which reserve *Fraser* for plainly or per se vulgar speech. Respondents answer that *B.H.* involved an ambiguously lewd phrase with a nonvulgar breast-cancer message, that *Chandler* did not adopt petitioners' proposed test, and that the slogan here had an undisputed vulgar meaning.

## Grant-positive signals

The provisioned snapshot dated July 16, 2026 shows several unusually strong indicators of serious interest:

- The petition arises from a published 2–1 federal appellate decision, and the dissent expressly described circuit disagreement and called for Supreme Court clarification of *Fraser*.
- After respondents initially waived a response, the Court requested one. That is a meaningful selection signal because at least one Justice or chambers thought adversarial briefing warranted.
- Four petition-stage amicus briefs support review, including First Amendment scholars and speech or education organizations.
- The question is recurring, nationally applicable, and cleanly presented on undisputed facts: political expression, no disruption, and reliance on *Fraser*. The line between a euphemism and its vulgar referent has consequences beyond this slogan.
- The petition presents a plausible legal conflict and a sharp contrast between the majority and dissent over whether school officials receive deference or courts independently identify plainly vulgar speech.

## Grant-negative signals

The brief in opposition identifies substantial certworthiness weaknesses:

- The split may be shallow. The Third Circuit is the clearest contrary authority, but its “I ♥ boobies!” case involved a plausible nonvulgar social message. The Ninth Circuit's “per se” language arose on a motion to dismiss and did not squarely decide how to treat coded speech with an admitted vulgar meaning. The Court may prefer further percolation.
- The slogan makes the case fact-bound and somewhat dated. The Justices could regard the dispute as an application of *Fraser*, rather than a stable conflict over a generally applicable rule.
- Respondents argue qualified immunity would remain on remand. That does not necessarily prevent review because the complaint also sought prospective relief and the students allegedly remained subject to the dress code, but it adds remedial complexity.
- The Sixth Circuit denied rehearing en banc with only the panel dissenter noted, which weakens the claim of acute intracircuit concern.
- As of the snapshot, the petition had not completed a conference or acquired a relist. It was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference, so no positive relist inference is yet available.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, a 3.5% grant rate for modern cert petitions originating in the Sixth Circuit, and only 0.8% for petitions with no relist. Those broad rates understate this petition because they do not condition on a requested response, four supporting amici, a published dissent, and a colorable circuit conflict. I therefore raise the estimate to 27%, while retaining denial as the modal outcome because respondents' no-split distinction is credible and the Court can wait for a cleaner conflict.

The case has a 0.72 big-case score. A merits decision would be significant for student speech nationwide and politically salient, although the narrow slogan-specific posture keeps it below the very highest-stakes constitutional cases.
