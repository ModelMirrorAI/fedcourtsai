# Reasoning: D.A. v. Tri County Area Schools (SCOTUS 25-1143)

## The Legal Question
The petition asks whether *Bethel Sch. Dist. No. 403 v. Fraser*, 478 U.S. 675 (1986), permits public schools to censor non-disruptive political speech that is not "plainly" profane or lewd. Specifically, the case involves students wearing apparel bearing the slogan "Let's Go Brandon," which a divided Sixth Circuit panel held could be banned under *Fraser* because school administrators reasonably understood it as a vulgar chant. Petitioners claim this creates a circuit split with the Third and Ninth Circuits, which require the speech to be plainly profane and lewd before *Fraser* allows its prohibition.

## Context and Signals from the Record
1. **Salience & Amicus Support:** The petition has garnered four amicus briefs at the cert stage (from First Amendment Scholars, The Buckeye Institute, Defending Education, and National Coalition Against Censorship). This indicates significant interest from First Amendment advocates across the political spectrum. The political nature of the slogan ("Let's Go Brandon") ensures high public visibility.
2. **Posture:** The case is a paid petition (No. 25-1143) originating from the Sixth Circuit. 
3. **Supreme Court Dynamics:** The respondents initially waived their right to respond, but the Court subsequently requested a response (May 18, 2026). The request for a response after a waiver is a classic signal that at least one Justice is closely scrutinizing the petition. The respondents are represented by high-profile Supreme Court litigator Lisa Blatt of Williams & Connolly, indicating a robust defense at the cert stage.
4. **Current Status:** The petition is fully briefed and has been distributed for the long conference on September 28, 2026. 

## Base Rates and Probability
The overall grant rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions is low (~3%). However, several factors push this specific petition's probability much higher:
- **Paid Status & Originating Court:** Petitions from the Sixth Circuit have a baseline grant rate of ~3.5%.
- **Response Requested:** The vast majority of granted petitions have a response requested by the Court if one was not initially filed. This clears a major hurdle.
- **Multiple Amici:** Four cert-stage amici significantly boost the statistical likelihood of a grant (historically pushing probabilities into the 15-25% range).
- **Subject Matter:** The Roberts Court has shown consistent interest in student speech issues (e.g., *Mahanoy Area School District v. B.L.*). 

## Conclusion
While the combination of a requested response, a claimed circuit split, four amici, and a high-profile First Amendment issue makes this a very strong candidate for certiorari, the absolute probability of a grant remains below 50% due to the Court's general parsimony in granting review. The respondents likely argue in their BIO that the Sixth Circuit's decision is highly fact-bound or that the circuit split is illusory. Therefore, the most statistically sound prediction is a denial, but with a significantly elevated probability of a grant compared to the baseline.
