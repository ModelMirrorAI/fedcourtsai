# Prediction: denied

I assign a 13% probability to any grant and predict denial. This is materially above the ordinary paid-petition baseline, but denial remains the clear modal disposition.

## Record and legal questions

The July 16 snapshot shows a paid petition from a published Fifth Circuit decision, a divided panel, denied rehearing, and a response request after respondents initially waived. Two oppositions were filed on July 13. The provisioned record includes the petition, its extracted questions presented, and the Henderson County respondents' opposition. It does not include the separate opposition filed by Philip Taft and his practice, so the assessment of respondents' vehicle arguments is incomplete.

The petition presents three related questions arising from the dismissal of a pretrial detainee's Section 1983 claims. It argues that the Fifth Circuit (1) improperly reduced constitutionally required jail mental-health care to protection from violence or suicide, (2) removed the excessiveness or proportionality component of *Bell v. Wolfish*, and (3) improperly rejected an inference of punitive intent at the Rule 12(b)(6) stage. The pleaded conditions are severe: more than five days in a bare, continuously lit suicide-prevention cell without a toilet, running water, ordinary bedding or clothing, with sharply restricted hygiene and drinking water, after Alexander falsely said he was suicidal to escape threats in group housing.

Under *Bell*, a pretrial-detention restriction cannot amount to punishment; absent express punitive intent, its rational connection to a legitimate nonpunitive purpose and whether it is excessive in relation to that purpose matter. At the pleading stage, well-pleaded facts and reasonable inferences favor the plaintiff, while legal conclusions need not be accepted. For certiorari, the practical questions are whether the decision creates a square conflict, resolves an important recurring federal question, and offers a clean vehicle rather than merely an erroneous application of settled law.

## Grant signals

The strongest predictive signal is procedural: after a response waiver and initial distribution, the Court requested a response. That indicates interest from at least one Justice and makes this petition substantially stronger than an unselected paid filing. The Fifth Circuit decision is published and divided, and Judge Dennis's dissent supplies a developed account of why the majority allegedly departed from *Bell* and the ordinary pleading standard. The alleged deprivations are unusually stark, the questions concern recurring treatment of pretrial detainees, and the petition identifies decisions from several circuits that expressly evaluate whether restrictions are excessive in relation to their stated purpose. The posture also leaves open the possibility that some Justices could see the case as suitable for summary correction.

## Denial and vehicle signals

The petition nevertheless asks the Court to take three overlapping, fact-dependent questions at once. The cited circuit decisions apply broadly similar doctrine to different conditions; the record does not establish a clean, acknowledged disagreement over a rule of law. Respondents plausibly characterize the Fifth Circuit's statements about mental-health care as contextual rather than a categorical new rule, and they argue that the panel recited *Bell*'s excessiveness language before applying it adversely to Alexander. Those features make the case look in significant part like claimed misapplication rather than a stable split.

There are also substantial vehicle complications. Alexander admitted that he announced suicidal intent and never alleged that he retracted it; the government has a compelling obligation to prevent detainee suicide. The claims involve multiple groups of defendants with different authority, and respondents dispute whether the complaint attributes alleged threats or punitive intent to identifiable officers. The mental-health-provider claims, municipal-liability theories, conditions claims, and pleading-standard question may not travel together cleanly. No amicus filings or CVSG appear in the snapshot. These weaknesses make four votes for plenary review unlikely even if one or more Justices regard the conditions or panel reasoning as troubling.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025-Term petitions and a 4.8% grant rate for modern petitions originating in the Fifth Circuit; modern petitions with no CVSG are estimated at 3.0%. The response request, published dissent, and severity of the alleged conditions justify raising this case well above those broad rates. The absence of a clean split and the vehicle problems prevent a larger increase. The likely outcome is therefore denial, with a 13% chance of any grant. If the Court does intervene, a summary disposition is plausible, but it is not the most likely overall disposition.

The 0.66 big-case score reflects the stakes if the Court reaches the merits: the case could affect suicide-watch design, basic conditions, and access to mental-health care for pretrial detainees across many facilities. Its procedural complexity and case-specific allegations keep it below the highest-significance tier.

No per-Justice votes are predicted because the pre-conference record does not support reliable Justice-level allocations.
