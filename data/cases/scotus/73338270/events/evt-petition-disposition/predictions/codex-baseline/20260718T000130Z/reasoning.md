# Prediction: denied

I assign an 11% probability to any grant (including a GVR) and predict denial. The categorical call is fairly confident because denial remains overwhelmingly more common and the petition lacks the usual clean split and vehicle characteristics of a likely plenary grant. The probability is nevertheless well above the ordinary paid-petition baseline because the Court requested responses after the respondents initially waived them.

## Question and governing standard

The question presented asks whether due process permits Oregon to condition continued work in a licensed health profession on accepting an allegedly investigational COVID-19 vaccine and, through PREP Act immunity, relinquishing ordinary judicial remedies for resulting injuries. The petition also advances related theories of federal preemption, unconstitutional conditions, bodily autonomy, enforceable property interests in federally funded consent protections, and error under the party-presentation principle.

Certiorari is discretionary. Under Rule 10, the strongest grounds ordinarily are a developed conflict among courts of appeals or an important federal question that requires resolution, rather than case-specific error correction. Four Justices must vote to grant. No individual cert votes are predicted because the pre-conference record does not support Justice-specific estimates.

## Record signals

The provisioned `questions-presented.txt` and 47-page, untruncated `petition.txt` supply the merits framing. No brief in opposition was provisioned: the July 17 snapshot shows that both sets of respondents obtained extensions through August 31, so the adverse presentation is not yet available. The forecast therefore discounts one-sided factual and doctrinal characterizations in the petition.

Positive signals are meaningful:

- This is a paid petition. The Term 2025 statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions, substantially above the all-petition rate.
- After the petition was distributed for the June 18 conference and respondents waived response, the Court requested responses on June 17. That selective step indicates at least some chambers interest and justifies moving materially above the paid-petition prior.
- New Civil Liberties Alliance submitted an amicus brief on July 17. The asserted intersection of bodily autonomy, occupational licensing, federal emergency-use law, and PREP Act immunity is important and potentially recurring.
- The petition identifies several pending petitions presenting related vaccine-mandate issues and suggests consolidated review. A coordinated vehicle could attract more attention than this petition viewed alone.

The negative signals are stronger:

- The Ninth Circuit disposition is unpublished and applies its published decision in *Curtis v. Inslee*. The petition identifies no developed circuit split; it primarily argues that the Ninth Circuit misread statutes and Supreme Court precedents. That is a weak Rule 10 posture unless the Court independently wants the broader issue.
- The petition combines multiple difficult theories. Its assertion that EUA vaccination operated as nonconsensual human-subject research, that federal consent provisions created individually enforceable property rights, and that PREP Act immunity made the employment condition unconstitutional all face substantial doctrinal hurdles. A statutory option to accept or refuse a product does not by itself clearly resolve what employment consequences a state or private employer may impose.
- Vehicle complications remain. PeaceHealth is private, the petition acknowledges unresolved state-action and immunity issues, and the case arrived after Rule 12(b)(6) dismissal. Those issues could prevent a clean decision on the broad question presented.
- There has been no CVSG and no completed merits briefing, conference distribution after briefing, or relist. Corpus examples from recent grants and denials show that repeated distributions occur on both sides and do not resolve the forecast, but this petition has not yet developed that stronger late-stage signal.
- The COVID-19 emergency context is increasingly retrospective, which reduces urgency absent a durable conflict over mandates or emergency-use products.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack places modern discretionary-cert petitions from the Ninth Circuit at about a 3.0% grant rate and Term 2025 paid petitions at about 5.36%. I raise the estimate to 11% for the requested response, amicus participation, and potentially consequential constitutional question, but keep denial dominant because there is no split, the decision is unpublished, the legal theories are diffuse, and the vehicle has threshold complications.

If the Court acts favorably, a plenary grant or a hold for a cleaner companion is more plausible than an immediate GVR; the current record identifies no intervening authority that would support summary vacatur. The most likely disposition is therefore `denied`.

The `big_case_score` of 0.70 reflects significance if decided, not grant likelihood. A merits decision could affect public-health mandates, healthcare employment, federal emergency-use protections, and constitutional bodily-autonomy doctrine nationwide, though the pandemic-specific posture narrows its continuing reach.
