**Legal Question:**
Whether the Eighth Amendment prohibits imposing a sentence of life without parole on a juvenile offender if the sentencing court finds or suggests that the juvenile is capable of change and not permanently incorrigible.

**Governing Standard:**
The Court's decision in *Jones v. Mississippi*, 593 U.S. 98 (2021), held that a sentencer is not required to make a separate factual finding of permanent incorrigibility before sentencing a juvenile to life without parole. However, *Jones* left open whether such a sentence is permissible if the sentencer *does* explicitly find that the juvenile is capable of change (i.e., corrigible). Certiorari is governed by Supreme Court Rule 10, prioritizing splits of authority on important federal questions. Jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1257 requires a final judgment from the state court.

**Facts and Analysis:**
This is a paid petition filed by the State of Utah. The Utah Supreme Court vacated respondent Morris Mullins's juvenile life-without-parole (JLWOP) sentence and remanded for resentencing. The state court reasoned that the trial court's comments at sentencing suggested a finding that Mullins was capable of change, and further, that the trial court misapprehended its obligation to properly consider Mullins's youth.

Despite the State's claim of a 16-8 split of authority on the Eighth Amendment question, the petition suffers from severe jurisdictional and vehicle flaws that make a grant highly unlikely:
1. **Lack of Finality:** The Utah Supreme Court remanded the case for resentencing. Because the sentence is not final, the decision likely falls outside 28 U.S.C. § 1257(a) jurisdiction unless a *Cox Broadcasting* exception applies. As the BIO notes, the State could still appeal if a lesser sentence is imposed on federal constitutional grounds, making interlocutory review unnecessary.
2. **Alternative Ground:** The Utah Supreme Court explicitly based its vacatur on an alternative ground: that the sentencing judge "misapprehended its constitutional obligation to properly consider Mullins's youth." The State's petition challenges only the corrigibility-finding holding, not the alternative youth-consideration holding. Because the judgment of vacatur would stand on this alternative ground regardless of how this Court resolves the question presented, any decision would be an advisory opinion.

Given these independent obstacles to review, the petition is a very poor vehicle to resolve the presented question. The baseline grant rate for paid petitions in recent Terms is approximately 3-5%, and the vehicle problems here push the probability of a grant significantly below that base rate.

**Prediction:**
Denied (Probability: 1.5%)
