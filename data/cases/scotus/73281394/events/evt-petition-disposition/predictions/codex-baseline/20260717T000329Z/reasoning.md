# Prediction: denied

I assign a 4% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. This is a forecast of petition disposition, not a view that the Eighth Amendment question is unimportant.

## Question and governing standard

Utah asks whether a discretionary sentencer may impose life without parole on a juvenile murderer when the sentencer finds, or suggests, that the juvenile is capable of change. The doctrinal tension is real. *Miller v. Alabama* requires consideration of youth before juvenile life without parole; *Montgomery v. Louisiana* described life without parole as disproportionate for crimes reflecting transient immaturity; and *Jones v. Mississippi* held that no finding of permanent incorrigibility is required when the sentencer has discretion and considers youth. Certiorari ordinarily requires an important federal question, a genuine conflict among lower courts, and a clean vehicle within the Court's jurisdiction.

## Record and cert-stage signals

The July 16 snapshot shows a paid state petition, a filed BIO, and initial distribution for the September 28 conference. It shows no CVSG or relist. The question-presented extract and petition describe a 3-2 Utah Supreme Court decision vacating Mullins's sentence after treating a suggestion of possible reform as potentially incompatible with juvenile life without parole. The petition argues that numerous courts read *Jones* to make a discretionary process constitutionally sufficient, while other courts preserve a substantive bar for corrigible juveniles. A divided state high court, a state petitioner, a recurring constitutional doctrine, and disagreement over *Jones* all raise this petition above a routine filing.

The complete BIO substantially weakens those signals. It argues that review is jurisdictionally premature under 28 U.S.C. § 1257 because the Utah court vacated the sentence and resentencing has not occurred. It also disputes the asserted split case by case, contending that only Maryland squarely reached the same precise question; identifies an arguably independent ground based on inadequate consideration of youth that Utah did not challenge; and emphasizes that the pre-*Miller* sentencing judge made only ambiguous comments rather than an express corrigibility finding. Those are serious finality and vehicle problems. The claimed rule also has limited forward reach because Utah and many other jurisdictions no longer permit juvenile life without parole, and Mullins has a consecutive life sentence elsewhere. The absence of supporting state amici, as reported in the BIO, further tempers the petition's claim of nationwide urgency.

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025-Term petitions and a 3.0% grant rate for petitions without a CVSG. Its zero-relist bucket has a 0.8% grant rate, though that figure is only a weak guide before an initial conference because every newly distributed petition begins without a relist. I place this petition above the zero-relist rate because its legal issue and divided decision are substantial, but below the paid baseline because the apparent lack of a final judgment and the independent-ground problem give the Court straightforward reasons to deny without resolving the doctrinal tension.

## Retrieval limits and confidence

CourtListener MCP searches for pre-snapshot opinions confirmed the general decisional landscape identified in the briefs, including *Jones*, *Malvo*, *Felder*, *Cooper*, and the Utah opinion; they did not reveal a cleaner vehicle signal. Two attempted `fedcourts query` lookups failed because the ranged corpus host could not be resolved, so no case-level corpus priors informed the forecast. The provisioned petition is marked truncated in `documents.json`, but its question, factual background, and cert arguments were extractable; the BIO is complete. I therefore rely principally on the provisioned record and committed aggregate base rates.

The predicted disposition is denial. I do not predict individual Justice votes because a cert denial does not disclose them. Confidence of 0.86 reflects the strong base rate and vehicle objections, while allowing for the possibility that four Justices see the post-*Jones* disagreement as warranting immediate review. The 0.68 big-case score reflects meaningful national constitutional stakes if the Court were to decide the merits, discounted for the shrinking population directly affected and the case-specific posture.
