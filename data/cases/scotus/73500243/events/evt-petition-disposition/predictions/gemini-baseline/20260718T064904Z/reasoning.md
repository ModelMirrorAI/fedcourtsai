# Prediction Reasoning: Donna Birks v. Clemente Javier Aguirre-Jarquin

## Procedural Posture and Facts
This is a paid cert petition filed by Donna Birks, a latent print analyst, seeking review of an Eleventh Circuit decision denying her qualified immunity. The underlying case involves a §1983 due process claim by the respondent, Clemente Javier Aguirre-Jarquin, who was exonerated and released after being convicted of murder. He alleges his conviction and subsequent imprisonment were caused by Birks erroneously testifying to a palm print match on the murder weapon. The questions presented ask whether due process liability extends to an analyst's "unknowing and unintentional" submission of inaccurate evidence when the analyst allegedly harbored subjective doubts about the competence of a colleague who verified the match.

## Certiorari Indicators
The overriding signal in this docket is the **waiver of the right to respond**. The respondent, Aguirre-Jarquin, filed a waiver on June 15, 2026. This case has been distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference (the "Long Conference").

When a respondent waives their right to file a brief in opposition, it is overwhelmingly indicative that the petition is not viewed as a serious candidate for certiorari. It signals that the respondent considers the petition meritless or factbound, such that it is not worth the expense to respond unless the Court issues a Call for Response (CFR). Without a CFR, a petition with a waiver is almost universally denied. 

## Base Rates and Corpus Context
The `metrics/statpack.md` provides base-rate context for modern discretionary cert petitions. The overall grant rate for paid petitions is between 2.5% and 3.3%. Petitions with zero relists (like this one on its initial distribution) carry a base grant rate of merely 0.8%. A waiver further pushes the likelihood toward zero. The Eleventh Circuit origin provides no notable bump to the grant rate.

Although the petition raises questions about qualified immunity and forensic evidence, the posture (waiver of response, zero relists) and the factbound nature of the subjective-intent claim make it extremely unlikely the Court will grant cert on this initial distribution.

## Prediction
The likelihood of a grant on the current record without a CFR or relist is exceedingly low. The predicted disposition is "denied" with a probability of 1% (0.01).
