# Prediction: denied

I assign a 7.5% probability to any grant and predict denial. If the Court does act on the petition, a GVR for reconsideration under its recent qualified-immunity precedent appears more plausible than plenary review, but denial remains the most likely disposition.

## Question and posture

The petition arises from an interlocutory qualified-immunity appeal in a Section 1983 action. Respondent Clemente Aguirre-Jarquin alleges that latent-print analyst Donna Birks violated due process by reporting and testifying to an inaccurate palm-print match after deliberately using a verifier whose competence she privately doubted. The Eleventh Circuit reversed the denial of immunity on the Fourth Amendment malicious-prosecution claim but affirmed the denial on the Fourteenth Amendment fabrication claim. It treated causation and materiality as outside the qualified-immunity issue properly before it and held that prior fabrication cases gave fair notice even without a case involving the same verification practice.

The petition presents three overlapping theories: the inaccurate match did not cause the conviction because Aguirre admitted handling the knife; a fabrication claim requires knowing and intentional falsity rather than error or recklessness; and no precedent clearly established that using this particular credentialed verifier violated due process. The provisioned petition was truncated at 171 pages, but its questions presented, merits argument, and relevant lower-court opinion excerpts were available. No brief in opposition was provisioned because respondent waived a response.

## Grant considerations

The best grant-side signal is the Court's recurring insistence that qualified-immunity precedent define the asserted right with factual specificity. A CourtListener search confirmed a line of recent summary decisions including *Zorn v. Linton*, *City of Tahlequah v. Bond*, *Rivas-Villegas v. Cortesluna*, *City of Escondido v. Emmons*, and *Kisela v. Hughes*. The petition argues that *Zorn*, decided after the Eleventh Circuit judgment, undermines the panel's reliance on general fabrication principles. The published decision, denial of immunity, and potentially recurring treatment of forensic evidence give the case some institutional importance. The petition also identifies differences among circuits in their causation/materiality formulations.

Those signals do not establish a clean certworthy split on the dispositive issue. The cited circuits broadly agree that knowing fabrication is actionable; much of the disagreement is about how that settled rule applies to Birks's choice of verifier and about the causal effect of the print evidence. On the respondent-favorable view required at summary judgment, the panel understood the record to permit purposeful evasion of meaningful verification, not merely an innocent mistake. That factual dispute makes the petition a poor vehicle for announcing a general mens-rea rule. The causation question also comes with procedural complications because the panel declined pendent appellate jurisdiction rather than finally resolving it.

The docket signals are weak. This is a paid petition from the Eleventh Circuit, but respondent waived a response, no amicus support or CVSG appears in the snapshot, and the case had only been distributed for its first conference. The waiver is not dispositive because the Court can request a response, but no such request was present by the snapshot date. The five repetitive questions and heavily case-specific presentation further reduce the likelihood of plenary review.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 2.5% grant rate across 2025 Term cert petitions, 5.36% for paid petitions, 4.4% for petitions from the Eleventh Circuit, 3.0% without a CVSG, and 0.8% among petitions with no relist. These cuts overlap and should not be multiplied. Because every pre-conference petition begins without a relist, I place more weight on the paid and circuit baselines. The qualified-immunity specificity issue raises this petition modestly above those baselines, while the factual and vehicle defects keep it well below a strong conflict case. That produces the 7.5% estimate.

The live corpus-query sidecar was unavailable, so no case-level corpus priors were retrieved; the forecast instead uses the provisioned record, committed statpack, and two general CourtListener doctrine searches. No outcome for this petition was sought or encountered.

## Significance

The 0.55 big-case score reflects moderate national stakes. A merits ruling could affect forensic analysts, wrongful-conviction damages suits, fabrication-of-evidence doctrine, and qualified immunity. But the verifier-specific facts and interlocutory posture may allow a narrow disposition, limiting broader significance.
