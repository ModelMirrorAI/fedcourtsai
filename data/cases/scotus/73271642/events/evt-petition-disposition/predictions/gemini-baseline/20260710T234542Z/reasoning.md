## The case

A pro se, in forma pauperis petition for certiorari from the Texas state courts. Petitioner Fabian Aldair Rodriguez-Montes was convicted of possession of a firearm by a felon and drug paraphernalia possession, and was sentenced to sixty-five years on two counts and additional concurrent sentences. The conviction followed a warrantless detention and search at a motel in Cleburne, Texas. Officer Scott-Lee responded to an anonymous tip reporting two Hispanic males in a gold Chrysler 300 offering to sell drugs, but upon arrival found only one passenger, no drug activity, and no driver. Petitioner exited a motel room behind the car, was immediately detained and frisked, revealing a firearm and a meth pipe. 

The trial court denied a motion to suppress. The Texas Court of Appeals, Twelfth District (Tyler) affirmed (2025 Tex. App. LEXIS 2752), and the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals denied rehearing on October 9, 2025 (2025 Tex. Crim. App. LEXIS 564). Both decisions below are unpublished. 

The petition presents four questions:
1. Whether the Fourth Amendment permits law enforcement to detain and frisk based primarily on an uncorroborated anonymous tip and ambiguous, non-criminal conduct.
2. Whether a warrantless search violates the Fourth and Fourteenth Amendments when officers escalate a brief encounter into a hands-on detention and frisk without probable cause or individualized suspicion that the suspect is armed and dangerous.
3. Whether the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment requires a jury instruction when disputed facts concerning the legality of a search are raised at trial (under Texas Code of Criminal Procedure Art. 38.23), and whether a court may deny such an instruction by resolving factual disputes itself.
4. Whether appellate courts may credit officer testimony over objective video evidence, in conflict with *Scott v. Harris* (2007).

## Governing standard

Certiorari is discretionary and governed by Rule 10: the Court looks for a circuit/state-court split on an important federal question, a decision in conflict with its own precedent, or an issue of national importance presented in a clean vehicle. Error correction — especially of factbound, unpublished state-court procedural rulings — is expressly disfavored.

## Why denial is near-certain

1. **Base rates.** The overall SCOTUS cert grant rate is exceptionally low (~1.4% overall of resolved cases, but less than 0.1% on the in forma pauperis/pro se docket). Incarcerated pro se petitions face near-insurmountable hurdles due to lack of counsel, procedural difficulties, and framing.
2. **Factbound error-correction on Fourth Amendment issues.** Questions 1, 2, and 4 are highly fact-intensive, turning on the specific details of a motel-parking-lot encounter (e.g., whether there was "fervent movement" or "testing his grip"). Supreme Court Rule 10 explicitly cautions that certiorari is rarely granted when the asserted error consists of erroneous factual findings or misapplication of a properly stated rule of law. The appellate court's review of the body-camera video and its deference to the trial court's credibility findings is standard error-correction territory.
3. **Absence of a federal question on the jury instruction issue.** Question 3 concerns Texas Code of Criminal Procedure Article 38.23, which is a state statutory exclusionary rule unique to Texas. The U.S. Constitution has never been held to require submission of search-and-seizure suppression questions to a jury. The state court's denial of this instruction is a matter of state statutory law. Under 28 U.S.C. § 1257, the U.S. Supreme Court lacks jurisdiction to review state court interpretations of state law, and attempts to frame this state-law issue as a Fourteenth Amendment Due Process violation (citing *Jackson v. Virginia*) are legally unsupported.
4. **No signals of interest.** No amicus support, no response filed by the State of Texas, and no CVSG (Call for the Views of the Solicitor General). The petition has been distributed for conference on September 28, 2026, without the Court requesting a response from the respondent. Under the Court's rules and practices, a petition cannot be granted without first calling for a response (CFR). The distribution of the petition on waiver without a CFR is a definitive signal that the petition will be denied.

The residual probability (0.1%) represents only the extreme tail possibility of a sudden summary GVR (Grant, Vacate, and Remand) or a post-distribution call for response, neither of which is indicated by the record.
