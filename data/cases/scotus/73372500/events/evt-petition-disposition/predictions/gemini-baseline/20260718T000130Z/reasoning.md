# Reasoning: Citizens Alliance for Government Integrity v. York County

## The Legal Question
The petitioner challenges the standard for bringing a substantive due process claim under the Fourteenth Amendment in land-use disputes, specifically when local executive officials issue permits contrary to zoning board rulings. The petition alleges a circuit split between the Second Circuit ("fundamental procedural irregularity"), the Third Circuit ("shocks the conscience"), and the Eleventh Circuit (which generally forecloses such claims). 

## Fact Pattern & Procedural Posture
The case comes to the Supreme Court from the Supreme Court of South Carolina. The petition was filed in May 2026. On June 18, 2026, the respondents waived their right to respond. On June 24, 2026, the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference (the "Long Conference"). No Call For Response (CFR) has been issued, and the case has not been relisted. 

## Analysis
The procedural posture is highly determinative in predicting the disposition at this stage. It is overwhelmingly rare for the Supreme Court to grant a petition for certiorari when the respondent has waived their right to respond and no CVSG or CFR has been issued prior to the conference distribution.

According to historical priors and base rates:
- **0 Relists**: Paid petitions facing their first conference distribution (0 relists) face an extremely high denial rate (approximately 97.3%). A grant at the Long Conference without a relist is exceedingly rare.
- **Waiver of Response**: A waiver signals that the respondent considers the petition weak enough not to warrant a formal defense. The Court almost never grants outright without first requesting a response from the opposing party. Since no CFR appears on the docket, an outright grant at this distribution is practically negligible.
- **Subject Matter**: While a circuit split is alleged, substantive due process claims in zoning and land-use disputes are heavily fact-bound and historically disfavored by the Court. Such disputes rarely present the kind of clean, nationally significant constitutional question the Court seeks to clarify, further reducing the likelihood that the Court will revive this petition with a CFR.

## Conclusion
Given the combination of 0 relists, the respondents' waiver of their right to file a Brief in Opposition, and the lack of a Call for Response, the base rate overwhelmingly favors denial. The probability of the petition being granted at this stage is less than 1%. Therefore, the predicted disposition is **denied**.
