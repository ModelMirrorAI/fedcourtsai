# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with a 1.2% probability of any grant (including a GVR).

## Question and record

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition ask whether local executive officials' issuance of land-use permits after a board of zoning appeals ruled the proposed use prohibited can constitute arbitrary executive action under the Fourteenth Amendment, and under what standard. The petition describes three approaches: the Second Circuit permits lack of lawful authority or a fundamental procedural irregularity to contribute to substantive-due-process arbitrariness; the Third, Fourth, and several other circuits require conscience-shocking or comparably egregious conduct beyond a state-law violation; and the Eleventh Circuit generally excludes executive deprivations of state-created land-use rights from substantive-due-process protection absent a fundamental right.

The snapshot dated July 17, 2026 shows a paid petition from the Supreme Court of South Carolina, a respondent waiver, and distribution for the September 28, 2026 conference. No brief in opposition was provisioned because respondents waived a response. There is no CVSG or relist in the record.

## Grant analysis

The asserted doctrinal disagreement is plausible and the underlying issue recurs in local land-use administration. The petition also identifies a concrete alleged irregularity: York County officials continued permitting a solar-manufacturing facility after the county board unanimously ruled the use prohibited, without an intervening rezoning or variance. Those points modestly improve the petition over a purely fact-bound zoning dispute.

The vehicle is nevertheless exceptionally weak. The state supreme court issued no merits opinion; it simply declined to entertain an original-jurisdiction action for mandamus, prohibition, and injunctive relief. Parallel state proceedings concerning the zoning ruling remained pending. The unexplained order therefore creates serious questions about an adequate and independent state procedural ground, preservation of the federal theory, finality in practical terms, and the absence of a developed merits record. The petition says its state-court papers used Fourteenth Amendment concepts and cited *Nectow*, but the proceeding principally sought extraordinary state-law relief. The claimed circuit disagreement also did not produce the judgment below, making this case a poor vehicle for resolving it.

A GVR for clarification under the petition's analogy to *Philadelphia Newspapers, Inc. v. Jerome* is possible, but the Court is more likely to treat the state court's discretionary refusal of original jurisdiction as an adequate reason not to intervene. The respondent's waiver and the absence, as of the snapshot, of a Court-requested response are additional negative signals. If any Justice viewed the petition as a serious candidate, a request for a response would be a more likely next step than immediate grant-side disposition.

## Calibration

The committed statpack reports a 3.0% grant rate for modern petitions without a CVSG, 0.8% for petitions with no relist, and 5.36% for paid 2025-Term petitions. The paid status and nonfrivolous alleged split pull upward, while the no-response posture and severe vehicle defects pull sharply downward. A corpus query of recent 2020s grants also showed that the returned grant-side priors commonly had repeated distributions or a CVSG, signals absent here. I therefore assign 1.2% to any grant and make denial the categorical forecast.

I do not predict individual votes because certiorari denials do not disclose a complete vote allocation. The 0.46 big-case score reflects that a clean merits ruling could affect land-use substantive-due-process law nationally, while the specific controversy, parties, and procedural path remain localized and narrow.
