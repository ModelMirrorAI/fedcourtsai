# Citizens Alliance for Government Integrity v. York County (25-1299) — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.01.**

## The case

CAGI, a nonprofit representing upper York County residents, alleges that county
officials kept issuing permits for Silfab Solar's panel-manufacturing conversion
of a Fort Mill warehouse *after* the York County Board of Zoning Appeals ruled
5-0 in May 2024 that the use is prohibited in the Light Industrial district.
CAGI filed an original-jurisdiction petition for mandamus/prohibition/injunction
in the Supreme Court of South Carolina; on December 16, 2025 that court entered
a one-page unexplained order declining to entertain the matter in its original
jurisdiction. The cert petition (filed May 14, 2026, paid, counseled) presents
one question: whether, and under what standard, executive issuance of land-use
permits after a zoning board has ruled the use prohibited can support a
Fourteenth Amendment arbitrary-executive-action (substantive due process)
claim. It alleges a three-way circuit split — Second Circuit (*Cine SK8*:
ultra vires action can itself be constitutionally arbitrary) vs. the
conscience-shocks majority (*United Artists*, 3d Cir., and others) vs. the
Eleventh Circuit's categorical bar (*Hillcrest Property*). Fallback ask:
GVR-style vacatur under *Philadelphia Newspapers v. Jerome* because the state
court's unexplained order may rest on an adequate and independent state ground.

## Why deny is near-certain

**Vehicle defects are dispositive, and they are several and independent:**

1. **Likely adequate and independent state ground / no federal holding below.**
   The order under review is a state supreme court's discretionary refusal to
   entertain an *original* writ proceeding — classic discretionary state
   practice (*Key v. Currie*). No court below ever passed on the federal
   substantive-due-process question. The petition candidly concedes the opacity
   problem and asks for vacatur-and-remand as a fallback; the Court's routine
   answer to "the state court may have declined on state procedural grounds" is
   to deny, not to clean up the record.
2. **The federal question was barely pressed below.** The state-court petition
   sounded in S.C. Code § 6-29-950 mandamus/prohibition; the federal hook is
   "arbitrary and irrational" language and a *Nectow* cite through lower-court
   authority. Fair-presentation is contestable at best.
3. **The split isn't implicated by this posture.** The alleged split is about
   the standard federal circuits apply to §1983 substantive-due-process claims.
   This case arrives from a state extraordinary-writ proceeding in which no
   substantive-due-process claim was adjudicated; granting here would not let
   the Court resolve the circuit conflict on a developed record. A §1983 suit
   remains available to frame the question cleanly.
4. **Ongoing parallel litigation.** The BZA ruling's own appeal is still
   pending in the state circuit court, plus several adjacent state cases — the
   dispute is interlocutory in substance even if the writ proceeding is
   technically final under *Detroit & C. Ry.*

**Docket signals also point to deny.** Respondents waived the right to respond
(June 18, 2026) and, as of the 2026-07-17 snapshot, the Court has not called
for a response — the near-universal precondition of a modern grant. The case
was distributed once, for the September 28, 2026 long conference, where the
grant rate is at its annual low. No amicus support appears on the docket. In
the corpus, recent grants characteristically show multiple distributions
(relists) and/or CVSG; this docket shows neither (relist count 0 bucket:
~0.8% grant).

**On the merits of certworthiness**, the land-use substantive-due-process
split is real but decades old; the Court has repeatedly declined to resolve it
(it denied cert in *Hillcrest* itself). A one-party local zoning fight with an
undeveloped federal record is not the vehicle that finally attracts it.

## Base rate and adjustment

Statpack anchors: modern discretionary-cert petitions grant at ~2.5-3.3% per
recent Term; paid petitions ~5-7%; state-court originators in the live slice
grant rarely; relist-0 petitions grant at ~0.8%. Starting from the paid-petition
rate, the waived response with no CFR, long-conference distribution, state-court
posture, and the stacked vehicle defects push this well below the class
average. A GVR is implausible (no intervening decision; *Jerome*-style vacatur
for clarification of an unexplained discretionary-jurisdiction denial is
vanishingly rare). Dismissal/withdrawal have no visible predicate.

**P(grant incl. GVR) = 0.01; predicted disposition: denied.** Most likely
path: no CFR issues, and the petition is denied on the October 2026 long
conference's order list (early October 2026).
