# Prediction: dismissed

I predict that the petition will be dismissed rather than granted, with a 3% probability of a grant. I do not predict individual votes because a voluntary or mootness-based dismissal would not yield a public merits vote.

## Question and certiorari standard

The petition asks whether an antitrust plaintiff adequately pleads fraudulent concealment, and therefore tolls the Clayton Act's four-year limitations period, by alleging that the defendants maintained an unwritten conspiratorial agreement. The petitioners characterize the Fourth Circuit's divided decision as a square conflict with the Fifth, Sixth, and Ninth Circuits over whether secrecy alone can constitute an affirmative act of concealment.

The governing predictive standard is the Court's discretionary certiorari practice: a square and outcome-determinative circuit conflict, an important recurring federal question, a clean vehicle, and a need for immediate resolution favor review. Interlocutory posture, factual or pleading-bound disputes, alternative grounds, and developments that may moot the controversy weigh against review.

## Record signals

The petition presents several genuine grant signals. It identifies contrary language in decisions such as *Carrier Corp. v. Outokumpu Oyj*, *Rx.com v. Medco Health Solutions, Inc.*, and *Thorman v. American Seafoods Co.*; the judgment below was divided; the alleged rule could affect high-value antitrust class actions; the petition is a paid filing with experienced Supreme Court counsel; and two business amici supported review. Most importantly, after the petition's first distribution, the Court invited the Solicitor General's views on January 12, 2026. That is a strong indication that at least some Justices regarded the issue as potentially certworthy.

The brief in opposition nevertheless identifies substantial obstacles. It argues that every relevant circuit uses a fact-dependent affirmative-acts standard, and that the Fourth Circuit did not hold that an unwritten agreement alone suffices. The complaint allegedly included coded language, private enforcement calls, limited written teaming agreements that created a misleading impression, public compliance statements, and detailed insider accounts. That framing makes the asserted conflict look more like disagreement over the application of a shared standard. The decision is also interlocutory: the Fourth Circuit merely reversed a pleading-stage dismissal and remanded before factual development. Those features make the case a less clean vehicle even if the broader legal question is important.

The decisive procedural signal postdates the merits briefing. The July 13 snapshot shows that on May 18, 2026, petitioners submitted a filing whose linked filename is “Letter re Dismissal.pdf,” and the docket describes the request as holding the petition in abeyance. No Solicitor General brief, renewed distribution, or other merits progression appears afterward. The most natural inference is that an external development, likely a settlement or anticipated dismissal in the underlying litigation, has made the parties expect a non-merits termination but requires time for that development to become final. Because this inference comes directly from the provisioned pre-disposition docket, it does not rely on knowledge of an outcome.

## Probability

The committed statpack's modern discretionary-cert baseline contains 273 grants, 7,840 denials, and 157 dismissals, or roughly a 3.3% grant rate. The case-specific pre-abeyance signals are much stronger: 2025 Term paid petitions have an estimated 5.1% grant rate, Fourth Circuit petitions 5.5%, and CVSG petitions 28.3%. Those cuts are correlated and should not be combined mechanically. The brief in opposition and interlocutory posture reduce the conditional chance of a grant, while the CVSG raises it substantially above the ordinary baseline.

The later dismissal-related abeyance request overwhelms those merits signals. I estimate that the contemplated termination will become effective in the large majority of scenarios. A small residual grant probability remains because the external development could fail, after which the Court could obtain the government's views and resume consideration. On the present record, however, dismissal is much more likely than either denial or grant.
