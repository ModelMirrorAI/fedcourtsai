# General Dynamics Corp. v. Scharpf, No. 25-293 — petition disposition

## The legal question

The petition (docketed September 15, 2025, from the Fourth Circuit, No. 24-1465)
presents a clean, well-framed question: whether antitrust plaintiffs adequately
plead fraudulent concealment — tolling the Clayton Act's four-year statute of
limitations, 15 U.S.C. § 15b — merely by alleging that the defendants maintained
an *unwritten* conspiratorial agreement. The underlying suit is a putative class
action alleging a decades-long no-poach conspiracy among naval shipbuilders and
engineering firms; the Fourth Circuit reversed a limitations dismissal,
petitioners say, in conflict with the Fifth, Sixth, and Ninth Circuits. The BIO
(Gupta Wessler / Cohen Milstein / Hagens Berman) denies the split and defends
the decision as a fact-bound application of settled affirmative-acts pleading.

## Cert-worthiness signals (before the mootness turn)

On the pre-May record this was an unusually strong candidate: an asserted 3–1
circuit split on a recurring, structurally important antitrust limitations
question; elite counsel on both sides (Verrilli/Munger Tolles for petitioners);
cert-stage amici from the U.S. Chamber of Commerce/NAM and the Shipbuilders
Council; a paid petition; and — most tellingly — after a single distribution
(Conference of 1/9/2026) the Court **called for the views of the Solicitor
General on January 12, 2026**. In the corpus statpack, CVSG'd petitions resolve
granted 28.3% / denied 69.6% / dismissed 2.2%, against a ~3% grant rate for
modern paid petitions generally. Standing alone, the CVSG plus the split
quality would have put P(grant) well above the CVSG base rate.

## The decisive development: strategic mootness and a Munsingwear request

On **May 18, 2026**, petitioners' counsel filed a letter (docketed as "Hold
Petition in Abeyance … submitted"; I retrieved and read the letter itself, see
`retrieval.md`) informing the Clerk that:

- Respondent Scharpf **voluntarily dismissed her claims against the General
  Dynamics petitioners** (General Dynamics Corp., Bath Iron Works, Electric
  Boat, and GD Information Technology);
- the **other petitioners have settlements in principle** with the plaintiffs
  (class settlements requiring court approval);
- petitioners ask the Court to **hold the petition in abeyance** until the
  settlements are approved and the case becomes moot, at which point General
  Dynamics — which *did not settle* and says it was deprived of review by the
  plaintiff's unilateral voluntary dismissal — **"will file a suggestion of
  mootness and will request that this Court grant certiorari and vacate the
  judgment below under United States v. Munsingwear, Inc., 340 U.S. 36
  (1950)."**

The SG never filed its CVSG brief; the docket shows nothing after May 18. A
plenary grant-and-decide is now the residual scenario, not the main line. The
disposition question has effectively become: *what order does the Court enter
when the case goes moot?*

## Disposition paths and probabilities

**Path A — the case becomes moot (~85%).** The GD claims are already dismissed
and the remaining defendants have settlements in principle; class-settlement
approval usually completes. GD will then seek Munsingwear vacatur.

- *A1: Munsingwear GVR (~55% of path A).* The standard order — "petition
  granted, judgment vacated, remanded with instructions to dismiss" — is the
  classic remedy where the **prevailing party's unilateral act** moots review
  (Azar v. Garza, 138 S. Ct. 1790 (2018) (per curiam)). Respondent's voluntary
  dismissal of the non-settling GD entities, on its face timed to insulate a
  favorable published Fourth Circuit precedent from review, is the paradigm
  case for vacatur, and the Court has been receptive to that anti-gamesmanship
  rationale. This maps to disposition **granted** (and to granted=1): the writ
  issues, even though there is no merits decision.
- *A2: cert denied (~30% of path A).* Respondent will oppose vacatur under
  U.S. Bancorp Mortgage Co. v. Bonner Mall, 513 U.S. 18 (1994): most
  petitioners *settled*, and settling parties forfeit vacatur; the Court may be
  unwilling to wipe out a published circuit precedent where the mootness is
  settlement-driven for nearly every petitioner and only GD claims the
  unilateral-dismissal equity. Several Justices have voiced growing skepticism
  of routine Munsingwear vacatur. Declining vacatur ordinarily takes the form
  of a simple denial.
- *A3: petition dismissed (~15% of path A).* A Rule 46 stipulated dismissal (if
  GD ultimately settles too) or an order dismissing the petition as moot
  without vacatur.

**Path B — the case revives (~15%).** Settlement approval fails or the
dismissal unravels; the CVSG process resumes. Conditional on revival I put
P(grant) around 45% — above the 28.3% CVSG base rate given the split's quality
and the petition's strength — with the rest mostly denial.

**Aggregate:** P(granted) ≈ 0.85·0.55 + 0.15·0.45 ≈ **0.52**;
P(denied) ≈ 0.33; P(dismissed) ≈ 0.15.

## Bottom line

Modal predicted disposition: **granted** — most likely not a plenary merits
grant but a Munsingwear grant-vacate-remand entered after the settlements are
approved and General Dynamics files its suggestion of mootness. `granted` = 1,
`probability` = 0.52, with modest confidence (0.45): the call between a GVR and
a Bonner Mall-flavored denial is a genuinely contested equitable question, and
either is well within range. I predict no per-judge votes; a Munsingwear order
would issue per curiam.
