# Prediction: denied

I assign a 12% probability to any grant and predict denial. A plenary grant is the plausible grant-side outcome; the record supplies no meaningful basis for a GVR.

## Question and posture

The petition asks whether prejudgment interest is categorically unavailable on noneconomic damages such as pain and suffering and loss of a normal life. The jury awarded Eddie Bolden $25 million for noneconomic harm following more than 22 years of incarceration. The district court added prejudgment interest, and the Seventh Circuit held that such interest is legally available while remanding for allocation between past and future damages.

The provisioned petition and questions-presented text frame the issue as a circuit conflict. The snapshot adds an important cert-stage signal: after the respondent waived a response and the petition was first distributed for the May 28 conference, the Court requested a response. The response is due July 22, after the snapshot date. No brief in opposition was provisioned, so this forecast necessarily evaluates a one-sided merits and vehicle record.

## Certiorari assessment

The petition has genuine positives. It presents a recurring federal remedial question, invokes divergent circuit approaches, arrives in a clean federal case, and has substantial financial consequences for civil-rights defendants and plaintiffs. The requested response indicates interest beyond an ordinary paid petition. An amicus filing by a municipal-lawyers association also supports institutional importance.

The central weakness is that the alleged split is not clean on the categorical question presented. The Fourth Circuit's *Gilliam v. Allen* decision treated prejudgment interest as an abuse of discretion on its record, including concerns about double recovery and an undifferentiated verdict. The Tenth Circuit authority affirmed a district court's discretionary refusal to award interest. The Third Circuit language arose in a Federal Employers' Liability Act setting and was characterized below as dicta. By contrast, the Fifth and Ninth Circuits approved interest on noneconomic injury in non-admiralty federal cases, and several other cited decisions arise in admiralty. These differences in standard of review, statutory context, and treatment of past versus future damages give the Court a strong reason to await a sharper conflict. The unanimous panel, absence of a recorded en banc vote, and the Seventh Circuit's limited remand further reduce urgency.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions and 2.0% for modern cert petitions from the Seventh Circuit. Petitions with no relist have a 0.8% grant rate, while this docket has no CVSG and no completed relist in the snapshot. Those broad cuts understate this petition because a requested response after waiver is a meaningful positive selection signal. I therefore raise the forecast materially above the paid and circuit baselines, but cap it at 12% because the non-square split and discretionary posture remain substantial cert defects. Denial remains much more likely than grant.

The 0.52 big-case score reflects potentially large nationwide effects on federal civil-rights judgments and public budgets, tempered by the narrow prejudgment-interest issue rather than a change to underlying constitutional liability.
