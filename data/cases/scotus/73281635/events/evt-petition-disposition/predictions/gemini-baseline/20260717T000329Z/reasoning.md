# Reasoning

## Question Presented
The petition presents a clear question of federal remedies: whether prejudgment interest is available as a matter of law on awards for noneconomic damages (such as pain and suffering or loss of normal life) under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. 

## Procedural Posture and Signals
The case is in a "forward" mode with a live petition for certiorari. The docket shows significant activity indicating Court interest:
1. **Waiver and Call for Response:** The respondent originally waived the right to file a brief in opposition on May 4, 2026. However, on May 20, 2026, the Court formally requested a response (due June 22, extended to July 22). A Call for Response (CFR) after a waiver is a strong signal that at least one Justice finds the petition worthy of discussion. 
2. **Amicus Support:** The International Municipal Lawyers Association (IMLA) filed an amicus brief at the cert stage, highlighting the issue's salience and recurring nature for local governments nationwide.

## Legal Analysis
The petition explicitly argues a circuit split. The Seventh Circuit (below) affirmed prejudgment interest on noneconomic damages, siding with the Fifth and Ninth Circuits. In contrast, the Third, Fourth, and Tenth Circuits have generally precluded or restricted prejudgment interest on noneconomic damages, reasoning that they are inherently nonpecuniary. While the Seventh Circuit below disputed the maturity of this split, the petitioners make a colorable argument that a 3-3 divide exists on an important federal question. Resolving splits regarding federal damages calculations in civil rights litigation is a classic reason for Supreme Court intervention.

## Conclusion
A pure base rate for a paid cert petition is roughly 1%. However, the combination of a Call for Response after a waiver, amicus support at the cert stage, and a plausible circuit split on a recurring issue of municipal liability significantly elevates the grant probability. Historically, petitions reaching this level of interest—passing the initial pool screening to trigger a CFR—have much higher grant rates, often in the 20-30% range. While a denial remains the most likely single outcome statistically (since CFRs still result in denials more often than not), the probability of a grant is assessed at 25%.
