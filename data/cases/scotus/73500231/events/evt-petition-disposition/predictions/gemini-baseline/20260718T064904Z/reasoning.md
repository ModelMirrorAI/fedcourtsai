# Reasoning: Certiorari Prediction for scotus/73500231

## Legal Question and Context
The petitioner, a pro se individual, seeks certiorari regarding her employment dispute with the United States Department of Veterans Affairs. Based on the questions presented, the petitioner appears to be challenging lower court rulings (the District Court and the D.C. Circuit) that dismissed her employment discrimination/wrongful termination claims on the grounds of *res judicata* (claim preclusion). The petitioner argues that her previous litigation was not "final" under *Parr v. United States* because the courts allegedly did not fully evaluate all material facts or legal arguments, and she claims to have discovered new facts and arguments after the underlying litigation concluded.

- **Originating Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit
- **Docket Activity:** A "Waiver of right of respondent United States, et al. to respond" was filed on June 16, 2026. The petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. Furthermore, an application for an injunction was previously denied by the Chief Justice on July 16, 2026.

## Reasoning
The probability of the Supreme Court granting certiorari in this case is effectively zero. 

1. **Pro Se Petitioner:** Pro se petitions make up a significant portion of the Court's docket but are overwhelmingly denied. They rarely present the type of clean, well-litigated circuit splits or pressing national issues that the Court uses its discretionary docket to resolve.
2. **Fact-Bound and Routine Nature:** The questions presented complain about the specific application of *res judicata* to the petitioner's unique procedural history and the facts of her VA employment. The Court functions to resolve broader issues of federal law, not to correct alleged factual or procedural errors in routine employment discrimination cases. 
3. **Waiver of Response:** The Solicitor General filed a waiver of the right to respond. This is standard practice for the government when responding to petitions it considers entirely frivolous or lacking any chance of being granted. The Court rarely grants a petition without first calling for a response, and it will not do so here given the nature of the claims.
4. **Base Rates:** The base rate for cert grants overall is low (around 2-3% for paid petitions, and even lower for IFP, though this case is marked as "Paid"). For pro se petitions challenging routine lower court procedural dismissals, the rate is practically zero.

Given these factors, there is no reasonable likelihood that this petition will be granted.

Predicted Disposition: **Denied**
