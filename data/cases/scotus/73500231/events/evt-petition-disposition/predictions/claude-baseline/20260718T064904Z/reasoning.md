# Drevaleva v. United States, et al. — No. 25-1328 (evt-petition-disposition)

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.003.**

## The case

Pro se petition for certiorari (petitioner is her own counsel of record) from
the D.C. Circuit (No. 25-5056, decided October 1, 2025; rehearing denied
December 5, 2025), docketed May 29, 2026, distributed for the Conference of
September 28, 2026 — the opening "long conference" of October Term 2026. The
respondent is the United States; the Solicitor General waived the right to
respond on June 16, 2026. A companion application for an injunction (26A37,
seeking mandatory reinstatement to a VA position) was submitted to the Chief
Justice on July 2, 2026 and denied by the Chief Justice, without referral to
the full Court, on July 16, 2026. The filing is paid, not IFP.

## The legal question

The petition presents seven questions, all variations on one theme: whether
res judicata (claim preclusion) barred the petitioner's latest suit over her
2017 termination from a probationary Title 38 hybrid position at the New
Mexico VA Health Care System. The questions assert that the D.C. Circuit's
Ashbourne v. Hansberry, 894 F.3d 298 (D.C. Cir. 2018), conflicts with Lawlor
v. National Screen Service Corp., 349 U.S. 322 (1955), and that a judgment is
not "final" for preclusion purposes under Parr v. United States, 351 U.S. 513
(1956), unless the district court "fully evaluated" the facts and law, or when
the plaintiff later discovers new facts.

## Why this is a near-certain denial

1. **The questions are fact-bound and framed in the first person.** Questions
   5–7 ask whether the lower courts should have evaluated "my Appeal No.
   DE-0752-19-0097-I-1," specific VA handbook provisions, and the petitioner's
   own April–June 2017 appointment. This is error-correction of a
   nonprecedential application of settled preclusion law, not a certworthy
   legal question.
2. **No genuine circuit split.** The asserted conflict rests on a
   misreading of the cited precedents: Lawlor held claims arising *after* a
   prior judgment are not precluded — an uncontroversial rule Ashbourne does
   not touch — and Parr concerned appellate finality of a criminal remand
   order, not preclusion finality. Settled law (e.g., Federated Department
   Stores v. Moitie) rejects the petition's core premise that an
   allegedly erroneous or under-explained dismissal with prejudice loses
   preclusive effect.
3. **The respondent waived response and no response was requested** before
   distribution. A cert grant essentially never happens without the Court
   first calling for a response; distribution on a waiver is the standard
   fast path to denial.
4. **Serial litigant history.** CourtListener shows at least eight prior
   Drevaleva petitions/filings reaching the Supreme Court in 2019–2020 alone
   (Nos. 18-8315, 18-8746, 18-8764, 18-8919, 18-9249, 18-9250, 19-6631,
   19-8012), arising from the same VA and related employment disputes — none
   producing a grant. This is pre-existing history about *other* dockets, not
   this petition's outcome.
5. **The companion injunction application was denied by the Chief Justice
   without referral** — a strong contemporaneous signal that no Justice sees
   plausible merit.
6. **Base rates.** The committed statpack's modern discretionary-cert slice
   puts the Term 2025 grant rate at ~2.5%; petitions with zero relists run
   ~0.8% granted, and pro se petitions (even paid ones) grant far more rarely
   than the counseled average. This petition sits well below even those
   anchors: distributed to the long conference on a waived response, with no
   split, no amici, and no CVSG.

Starting from the ~0.8% zero-relist anchor and adjusting sharply down for the
pro se serial-filer posture, waived response, fact-bound QPs, and the denied
injunction application, I put P(grant) at ~0.3%. A GVR is implausible (no
intervening decision is implicated), and a dismissal (e.g., under Rule 39.8,
which applies to IFP filings) does not fit a paid petition, so the residual
probability mass sits almost entirely on outright denial at or shortly after
the September 28, 2026 conference.
