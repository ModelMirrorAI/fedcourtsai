# Prediction: denied

The forecast is based on the July 18, 2026 snapshot, the complete 50-page petition and extracted questions presented, and the forward-mode context. The United States waived a response; no brief in opposition was provisioned. The docket shows distribution for the September 28, 2026 conference, so no inference can yet be drawn from relisting.

## Question and governing standard

The petition presents eight overlapping questions arising from repeated litigation over petitioner's 2017 removal from a Veterans Affairs position. Its principal theory is that claim preclusion cannot attach unless the prior court fully found the material facts and applicable law. It also argues that newly discovered facts or legal arguments defeat finality, that the MSPB had jurisdiction under personnel rules, and that the district court owed de novo review of discrimination allegations.

Supreme Court Rule 10 makes certiorari principally appropriate for an important federal question, a genuine conflict among lower courts, or a serious departure from settled law—not ordinary error correction. Claim preclusion generally applies when the same parties litigate the same claim to a final, valid judgment on the merits; it covers matters that were or could have been raised. The petition must therefore identify a reviewworthy legal conflict and a suitable vehicle, not merely dispute how prior courts handled the facts.

## Case-specific assessment

The cited precedents do not establish the rule the petition attributes to them:

- *Lawlor v. National Screen Service Corp.*, 349 U.S. 322 (1955), expressly said that a dismissal with prejudice bars a later suit on the same cause of action. It distinguished issue preclusion, for which the absence of findings mattered, and allowed the later suit because it alleged post-judgment conduct, new violations, and additional defendants. The petition instead seeks to revisit the same employment termination through asserted new facts and legal arguments.
- *Parr v. United States*, 351 U.S. 513 (1956), addressed finality for appellate jurisdiction and the policy against piecemeal appeals. It did not require exhaustive factfinding before a judgment can have claim-preclusive effect.
- *Ashbourne v. Hansberry*, 894 F.3d 298 (D.C. Cir. 2018), states the ordinary four-part claim-preclusion test and joined every circuit then to address its Title VII exhaustion issue. That undercuts the petition's asserted conflict rather than demonstrating one.

The remaining MSPB and discrimination questions are entangled with the petitioner's employment status, agency jurisdiction, prior sanctions, and a long sequence of related suits. The petition identifies no square circuit split on those issues. The judgment below is unpublished, the questions are numerous and case-specific, and the petition's readings of its central authorities are materially overbroad. Those features make this a poor vehicle even apart from the merits.

The pre-conference docket signals also point strongly toward denial. The Solicitor General waived a response, no amicus support appears in the snapshot, and the Chief Justice denied the linked injunction application on July 16. A Justice could still request a response, but there is no present sign of institutional interest, a CVSG, an intervening precedent supporting a GVR, or an issue warranting plenary review.
