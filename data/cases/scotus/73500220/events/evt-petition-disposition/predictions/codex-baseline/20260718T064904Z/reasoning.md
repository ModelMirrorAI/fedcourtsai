# Prediction: denied

I assign a 1.8% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. The doctrinal question has some importance, but this petition is an unusually poor vehicle for resolving it.

## Question and record

The question presented is whether the Sixth Amendment right to effective assistance covers advice about a guilty plea's collateral consequence when that consequence is sufficiently intertwined with the conviction and sufficiently severe. The Patels pleaded guilty to health-care fraud. Their admissions and stipulated loss later supported collateral estoppel and a substantial False Claims Act judgment. They contend counsel knew about the pending qui tam action but failed to explain that consequence.

The Third Circuit treated *Padilla v. Kentucky* as a deportation-specific exception and held that counsel need advise only about direct consequences, not collateral ones. The petition argues that this conflicts with the Second Circuit's en banc reasoning in *Farhane v. United States* and with decisions or reasoning from other federal and state courts that look to severity, practical certainty, and connection to the criminal process.

I used the questions-presented extract and the provisioned petition. The petition PDF extract is marked truncated at 240 pages, but it contains the merits petition, the Third Circuit opinion, and part of the appendix. No brief in opposition was provisioned. Instead, the snapshot records that the United States waived response on June 11, 2026. The case was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference and, at the snapshot date, had no relist or CVSG.

## Certiorari assessment

The petition's best feature is a plausible disagreement over how broadly *Padilla* displaced the direct/collateral distinction. *Farhane* rejected a categorical approach for denaturalization leading to deportation, while the Third and Seventh Circuits describe *Padilla* more narrowly. The issue also matters because guilty pleas dominate criminal adjudication and collateral consequences can be severe.

Several defects substantially outweigh that signal:

- The asserted split is not clean on the same consequence. *Farhane* remained tied to denaturalization and deportation, while the cases involving civil commitment, parole, later prosecution, employment, and other consequences turn on different combinations of severity, certainty, and affirmative misadvice. The Third Circuit expressly distinguished *Farhane* on that ground.
- The judgment has an independent nonretroactivity ground. The Third Circuit held that extending *Padilla* to FCA liability would announce a new procedural rule unavailable on collateral review. A ruling for the Patels on the question presented therefore would not itself yield relief.
- The Third Circuit also stated that even under the Patels' proposed severity-and-intertwinement framework, FCA civil liability would not qualify. That alternative conclusion creates a second vehicle obstacle and fact-bound merits dispute.
- The plea agreements and colloquies warned that civil proceedings remained possible. Those facts complicate prejudice and make this record less suitable for announcing a broad constitutional rule.
- The United States waived response, no amici are shown in the provisioned docket, and the Court had not called for a response or requested the Solicitor General's views as of the snapshot. Those are negative preconference signals, although the Court could still call for a response.

The committed statpack places a 2025 Term paid petition's estimated grant rate at about 5.4%, the Third Circuit bucket at 2.9%, and non-CVSG petitions at 3.0%. The zero-relist bucket is lower, but I give it little independent weight here because the snapshot predates the petition's first conference. The real doctrinal issue keeps the estimate above a purely frivolous filing, while the waiver and dual alternative grounds take it below the circuit and paid-petition anchors.

The likely disposition is a straight denial, not a GVR: there is no identified intervening precedent or mootness development that would supply a remand basis.

## Significance

If the Court reached the merits, the case could meaningfully define the Sixth Amendment duties of plea counsel beyond deportation and affect a broad range of collateral sanctions. I therefore rate its potential significance at 0.68, distinct from its very low grant probability. The FCA-specific facts and procedural barriers reduce its likely public salience.
