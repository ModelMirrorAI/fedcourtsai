# Reasoning

## Legal Question
The question presented is whether the Sixth Amendment's right to effective assistance of counsel, as articulated in *Padilla v. Kentucky*, 559 U.S. 356 (2010), requires defense counsel to advise a client that a guilty plea will lead to a civil collateral consequence—specifically, a collateral estoppel judgment in a parallel civil *Qui Tam* (False Claims Act) action.

## Facts from the Snapshot
Petitioners Nita and Kirtish Patel were charged with Medicare fraud. According to the provisioned petition document, they pled guilty and stipulated to a loss amount of approximately $4.8 million. The Patels allege that their attorney failed to advise them that this stipulation would expose them to a collateral estoppel judgment in a pending related *Qui Tam* action. They sought relief based on ineffective assistance of counsel. The Third Circuit affirmed the district court's denial of relief, holding that *Padilla* does not extend to this type of civil collateral consequence.

The case is a paid cert petition. Notably, the docket reflects that the respondent, the United States, waived its right to file a response on June 11, 2026. The petition has been distributed for the September 28, 2026 long conference without any relists.

## Analysis and Probability
The base rate for paid cert petitions is quite low, generally hovering around 2.5% to 3.5%. The respondent's waiver of the right to respond is a highly significant signal: the Solicitor General usually files a Brief in Opposition (BIO) when there is a genuine circuit split or when the issue is of national importance and ripe for review. A waiver signals the government's view that the petition is meritless, that any circuit split is illusory or shallow, or that the case is a poor vehicle. The Supreme Court almost never grants certiorari without at least calling for a response first. Although the Court could call for a response (CVSG or requesting a BIO), the current posture strongly points to a denial.

Substantively, the Supreme Court has consistently circumscribed *Padilla*. *Padilla* recognized that deportation is a uniquely severe and intertwined penalty, "intimately related to the criminal process." Courts of appeals and state courts have broadly resisted expanding *Padilla*'s holding to cover standard civil collateral consequences, such as loss of professional licenses, sex offender registration, or civil liability (like False Claims Act damages). Even if petitioners allege a divergence in how lower courts approach *Padilla*, the specific context of collateral estoppel in a civil suit is highly unlikely to be the vehicle the Court uses to expand the Sixth Amendment.

Given the legal headwinds against extending *Padilla*, the low baseline probability of cert grants, and the decisive signal from the SG's waiver of response, the likelihood of a grant is extremely low. I predict the petition will be denied.
