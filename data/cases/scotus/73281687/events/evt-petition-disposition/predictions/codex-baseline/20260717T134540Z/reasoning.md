# Prediction: denied

I assign a 16% probability to any grant and predict denial. A plenary grant is the plausible grant-side outcome; nothing in the pre-decision record suggests an intervening judgment that would make a GVR likely.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks the Court to overrule *Jones v. United States*, 362 U.S. 257 (1960), and hold that the Fourth Amendment's requirement that probable cause be supported by “Oath or affirmation” permits a warrant only when a witness with firsthand knowledge swears before the issuing magistrate. Under *Jones* and the doctrine culminating in *Illinois v. Gates*, hearsay can establish probable cause when the totality of the circumstances gives the magistrate a substantial basis to credit it.

The petition's theory is that “Oath” was a procedural guarantee at the Founding: an oath exposed a firsthand witness to perjury and allowed the magistrate to assess the actual source. It analogizes *Jones* to the functional precedents displaced in *Crawford v. Washington* and *Ramos v. Louisiana*. Overruling *Jones* would replace informant-reliability analysis with a categorical firsthand-knowledge rule.

## Case-specific signals

The vehicle is facially clean. According to the 52-page petition, Denver sought a warrant for Michael Mendenhall's converted townhouse after an officer relayed an accuser's account by telephone to the affiant detective. The detective neither observed the incident nor spoke to the accuser, so the affidavit was based entirely on double hearsay. The search occurred after midnight, Mendenhall was already in custody, the bat sought by police was seized, and the charges were dropped six days later. Mendenhall brought a municipal-liability claim challenging Denver's policy of allowing hearsay warrant applications; the district court dismissed it and the Tenth Circuit affirmed because *Jones* controlled. Those facts make the constitutional issue dispositive and avoid a factual dispute about whether some firsthand evidence independently supported the warrant.

The docket contains meaningful interest signals. This is a paid petition represented by the Institute for Justice, four amicus briefs were filed, and the Court requested a response after the petition had been distributed for conference. A response request can be made on one Justice's initiative, so it shows genuine attention but not the four votes needed for certiorari. As of the snapshot, Denver's opposition had been submitted but was not among the provisioned documents; I therefore did not infer its arguments or weigh unprovided text.

## Probability assessment

The committed statpack places 2025 Term paid petitions at an estimated 5.36% grant rate and modern petitions from the Tenth Circuit at about 5.0%. The response request, unusually focused facts, capable counsel, multiple amici, and an original-meaning argument suited to several Justices justify a substantial upward adjustment from that baseline.

The obstacles still dominate. The petition identifies no current circuit conflict: every lower court must follow *Jones*, and the requested result depends on overruling a 65-year-old Supreme Court precedent rather than resolving disagreement below. *Jones* also underlies a mature body of warrant law, including *Gates*, and the proposed rule would materially alter routine law-enforcement practice. There is no CVSG and, at this snapshot, no relist after consideration of the opposition. CourtListener research also showed that the Supreme Court search results citing *Jones* in the warrant-hearsay context run through the established *Gates*, *Franks*, *Leon*, and related line, rather than revealing a recent merits development that makes review urgent. These considerations keep denial substantially more likely than grant.

## Stakes

The 0.84 big-case score reflects the potential reach of the issue, not the likelihood of review. A decision adopting the petition's rule could affect warrant applications nationwide, especially those built on confidential informants or layers of officer-to-officer reporting, and could require substantial changes to federal and state warrant procedures.
