# Reasoning

**The Legal Question:** 
Whether Pennsylvania's assertion of jurisdiction over an out-of-state company in a suit brought by an out-of-state plaintiff on claims wholly unrelated to Pennsylvania violates the dormant Commerce Clause.

**The Governing Standard:**
This is a direct follow-up to the Court's decision in *Mallory v. Norfolk S. Ry.*, 600 U.S. 122 (2023). In that case, the Court held that Pennsylvania's corporate registration statute, which extracts consent to general personal jurisdiction as a condition of doing business, does not violate the Due Process Clause. However, the majority explicitly left open Norfolk Southern's alternative argument that the scheme violates the dormant Commerce Clause. 

**The Facts Driving the Outcome:**
1. **Explicit Invitation:** Justice Alito, concurring in part in *Mallory I*, practically invited this exact challenge, writing at length about why such a registration-based jurisdiction scheme likely violates the dormant Commerce Clause by placing an undue burden on interstate commerce. 
2. **Built-in Majority:** Four Justices (Barrett, Roberts, Kagan, Kavanaugh) dissented in *Mallory I*, viewing the jurisdictional scheme as unconstitutional under the Due Process Clause. Combined with Justice Alito's explicitly stated Commerce Clause concerns, there are likely at least five votes to strike down the Pennsylvania scheme on dormant Commerce Clause grounds.
3. **Lower Court Defiance:** On remand, the Pennsylvania courts summarily rejected the Commerce Clause arguments. The Supreme Court often grants certiorari when lower courts brush off explicit constitutional concerns raised and reserved by the Justices.
4. **Amicus Support:** The petition is supported by business and legal groups (e.g., Washington Legal Foundation, Atlantic Legal Foundation) highlighting the national stakes for corporate defendants.

**Reasoning for Probability and Predicted Disposition:**
While the overall base rate for certiorari petitions is very low (around 1-3% for paid petitions without a circuit split), this is not a run-of-the-mill petition. It is a return trip for a case that deeply divided the Court, presenting the precise constitutional question the Court formally reserved and that the deciding fifth vote (Alito) strongly suggested would succeed. The summary rejection by the state court makes this an ideal vehicle to finish resolving the constitutionality of the Pennsylvania statute. 

I predict the Court will grant the petition to resolve the dormant Commerce Clause issue once and for all. Given the clear signals from *Mallory I*, the probability of a grant is exceedingly high.

**Votes:**
I anticipate at least four votes to grant (likely Roberts, Alito, Kavanaugh, Barrett) and potentially more, given the need for doctrinal clarity after leaving the issue unresolved.

**Big Case Score:** 0.85
This is a highly significant case for civil procedure, corporate law, and the limits of state power over interstate businesses, effectively serving as the sequel to a major recent precedent.
