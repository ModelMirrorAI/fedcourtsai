# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.3% probability to any grant and predict denial. This is a paid petition from an unpublished Third Circuit decision, but the paid-case prior is overwhelmed by severe merits and vehicle weaknesses. No individual Justice votes are predicted because the record supplies no Justice-specific signal and cert votes are not public.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the lower courts abused their discretion by refusing a Rule 4(a)(5) extension for a notice of appeal filed two days late, whether courts must balance all of *Pioneer*'s excusable-neglect factors, whether the circuits are divided over that balance, and whether the appeal was timely under Rule 58's separate-document rule.

Supreme Court Rule 10 makes certiorari a discretionary remedy ordinarily reserved for a genuine conflict on an important federal question or a significant departure from accepted law, not correction of a fact-bound misapplication. Under *Pioneer*, excusable neglect is an equitable inquiry considering prejudice, delay, reason for delay and the movant's control, and good faith. Rule 4(a)(5) permits an extension for excusable neglect or good cause, but those standards do not make routine deadline disputes independently certworthy.

## Record-specific assessment

The snapshot dated July 18, 2026 shows a paid petition, filed March 2, 2026 and distributed for the September 28 conference. It shows no relist or CVSG. The provisioned document manifest contains the petition and extracted questions presented but no brief in opposition. The petition says the notice was two days late because the pro se petitioners relied on a paralegal's mistaken view that Rule 6(d) added three days, took a preplanned vacation, and lacked support staff. It also says the delay caused no prejudice and that the lower courts inadequately balanced the *Pioneer* factors.

Those facts make the case an unattractive vehicle. The reason for delay was a mistaken reading of a straightforward filing rule plus voluntary travel, matters ordinarily within a litigant's control. The Third Circuit's affirmance is unpublished and reviewed a discretionary, record-intensive ruling. The petition asserts a circuit split but does not identify conflicting holdings on the same legal rule; it mostly contrasts outcomes from different applications of *Pioneer*. The cited authorities already establish the multi-factor standard, so the petition primarily seeks error correction.

The Rule 58 theory is especially weak. The challenged district-court order disposed of a motion that the petition itself characterizes as arising under Rule 60(b), (c), and (d). Rule 58(a)(5) expressly provides that a separate document is not required for an order disposing of a Rule 60 motion. *United States v. Lawuary*, 669 F.3d 864 (7th Cir. 2012), applies that exception and rejects the claimed 150-day extension in materially analogous timing circumstances. Thus the alternative theory does not supply a clean certworthy issue.

The petition's use of *Ragguette v. Premier Wines & Spirits* is also incomplete. The earlier Third Circuit remand required express consideration of the *Pioneer* factors, but the later precedential decision, 691 F.3d 315 (3d Cir. 2012), held that the reason for delay strongly outweighed the other factors and rejected excusable neglect where counsel's internal failure and travel produced a missed notice. That decision cuts against both the claimed intracircuit inconsistency and the petitioners' application of the standard.
