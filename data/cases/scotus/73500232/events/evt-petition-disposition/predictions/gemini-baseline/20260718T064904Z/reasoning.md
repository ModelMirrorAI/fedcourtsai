# Reasoning: Mazza v. Bank of New York Mellon (25-1331)

**Background and Legal Question**
This is a pro se petition for a writ of certiorari (though filed on the paid docket) seeking review of an unpublished Third Circuit decision. The petitioners, Mark and Lisa Mazza, are involved in a decade-long foreclosure and ejectment dispute with the Bank of New York Mellon. In the underlying decision (No. 24-2794), the Third Circuit affirmed the District Court's denial of their Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4(a)(5) motion to extend the time to file a notice of appeal. The Mazzas filed their notice of appeal two days late, allegedly due to reliance on a paralegal's advice and a preplanned absence.

The petition presents questions regarding whether the courts below abused their discretion by failing to properly apply and balance the equitable standards for "excusable neglect" and "good cause" articulated in *Pioneer Investment Services Co. v. Brunswick Associates Ltd. Partnership*, 507 U.S. 380 (1993). They also allege a circuit split regarding the application of these factors, particularly concerning the weight given to the reason for delay and litigant control, and raise an issue under FRCP 58 (the separate-document requirement).

**Analysis**
Despite the petitioners' attempt to frame the issue as a circuit split over the interpretation of *Pioneer*, the case fundamentally involves a highly fact-bound challenge to a district court's discretionary ruling on a procedural motion. The standard of review for a Rule 4(a)(5) denial is abuse of discretion, which is a deferential standard that heavily insulates the lower court's decision. 

Historically, the Supreme Court rarely grants certiorari to review fact-specific applications of the excusable neglect standard, particularly in unpublished, summary affirmances of procedural defaults. Pro se petitions—even those that are fee-paid—face an extraordinarily high bar for review, and cases arising out of routine foreclosure disputes almost never attract the Court's attention unless they present a clean, novel question of federal law with a deep and acknowledged circuit split. Here, the alleged circuit variations in applying the *Pioneer* factors are generally a feature of the multi-factor balancing test itself, which inherently produces fact-dependent outcomes rather than a sharp methodological split requiring Supreme Court intervention.

**Conclusion**
Given the procedural posture (an unpublished affirmance of an abuse-of-discretion denial of a time extension), the pro se nature of the filing, and the underlying routine foreclosure context, this petition does not present an issue of national significance or a compelling vehicle for clarifying the law. The overall base rate for paid petitions in recent Terms is roughly 2.5–3%, but the characteristics of this specific petition push the probability of a grant significantly below that baseline.

**Prediction**: Denied.
