# Mazza v. Bank of New York Mellon, No. 25-1331 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.003.**

## The case

Mark and Lisa Mazza, pro se, petition from the Third Circuit's unpublished
affirmance (No. 24-2794, May 16, 2025; rehearing denied October 1, 2025) of
the E.D. Pa.'s denial of their Fed. R. App. P. 4(a)(5) motion to extend the
time to appeal. The underlying dispute is a foreclosure/ejectment fight with
BNY Mellon running since 2012. After losing a Rule 60(b)/(c)/(d) motion
attacking the ejectment judgment (denied June 4, 2024), the Mazzas filed
their notice of appeal two days late (July 7, 2024), blaming a paralegal's
bad advice that FRCP 6(d)'s three-day mail rule applied, plus a preplanned
July 4th vacation. Both courts below found no excusable neglect or good
cause under *Pioneer Investment Services Co. v. Brunswick Associates*, 507
U.S. 380 (1993).

The questions presented ask (1)–(2) whether the denial was an abuse of
discretion, (3) whether a circuit split on Rule 4(a)(5)/*Pioneer*
application warrants resolution, and (4) whether FRCP 58's
separate-document rule made the appeal timely because the district court's
combined order triggered the 150-day deemed-entry rule.

## Why this is a near-certain denial

1. **Error correction under abuse-of-discretion review.** QPs 1–2 ask the
   Court to re-weigh the *Pioneer* equities on this record. That is the
   paradigm fact-bound, splitless request the Court denies without comment;
   the petition's own framing ("Suggested Answer: Yes" after each QP)
   underscores its error-correction character.

2. **The asserted circuit split is not substantiated.** The petition's
   split (QP 3) rests on *Deloach v. Standard Insurance Co.*, 224 F. Supp.
   3d 823 — a **district court** decision (E.D. Mo.) cited as the Eighth
   Circuit's standard — and on *Advanced Estimating Systems* (11th Cir.), an
   unpublished 1996 table disposition. Post-*Pioneer* excusable-neglect
   case law is uniformly framed as a totality-of-circumstances inquiry;
   circuits differ in emphasis (some treat "reason for delay" as the key
   factor), but the Court has repeatedly declined to police those
   differences because outcomes are case-specific. Nothing here presents a
   crisp, outcome-determinative rule conflict.

3. **QP 4 is foreclosed by the rule's text.** The appeal was from an order
   denying a Rule 60 motion. FRCP 58(a)(5) expressly **exempts** orders
   disposing of Rule 60(b) motions from the separate-document requirement,
   so the 150-day deemed-entry rule of FRCP 58(c)(2)(B) / FRAP
   4(a)(7)(A)(ii) never triggers. *Brown v. Fifth Third Bank* (7th Cir.
   2013) and *Indrelunas* do not help across that exemption. The
   petition's strongest-sounding argument is thus a vehicle-killing legal
   error, and the courts below treated the appeal as untimely accordingly.

4. **Docket posture is the classic first-conference-denial path.** Paid
   pro se petition; filed March 2, 2026, docketed June 1, 2026; response
   due July 1, 2026 with **no brief in opposition on the docket** (none
   provisioned either); distributed July 15, 2026 for the September 28,
   2026 long conference. Zero relists, no CVSG. The Court essentially never
   grants without first calling for a response, so the realistic ceiling
   before the long conference is already very low.

5. **Base rates.** From the committed statpack (modern discretionary-cert
   slice): 0-relist petitions grant at ~0.8%; Third Circuit petitions at
   ~2.9%; the Term-level grant rate runs 2.5–3.3%. Conditioning on pro se
   drafting, a fact-bound QP, a foreclosed lead legal argument, and no BIO
   called for, this petition sits far below even the 0-relist bucket's
   average. I set P(grant) = 0.003 — a floor acknowledging residual
   uncertainty (e.g., an unexpected relist) rather than any affirmative
   grant signal.

## Disposition split

Denied is the overwhelmingly likely label. Residual mass: a few tenths of a
percent on dismissed (e.g., settlement/withdrawal in the underlying
property dispute), and negligible on GVR — there is no pending or recent
decision of this Court on Rule 4(a)(5)/Pioneer that could trigger one.

No votes predicted: a denial at conference records none.

## Big-case score

0.02. A private foreclosure/ejectment procedural dispute between pro se
homeowners and a bank trustee; even if granted, the question (excusable
neglect for a two-day-late notice of appeal) is narrow and technical with
no public profile.
