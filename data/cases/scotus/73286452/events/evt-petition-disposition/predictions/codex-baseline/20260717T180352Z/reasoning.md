# Prediction

I predict denial, with a 37% probability of any grant-side disposition. That is far above the ordinary certiorari baseline. If the Court does act on the petition, a GVR for reconsideration in light of *Chiles v. Salazar* is more likely than a plenary grant.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether privately created instruction in continuing-medical-education courses required for state licensure is government speech. Under *Shurtleff v. Boston*, history, public perception, and governmental control help identify whether the government is speaking for itself. *Matal v. Tam* cautions against expanding government speech merely because private expression is regulated, and *NIFLA v. Becerra* rejects diminished First Amendment protection for professional speech.

The especially important intervening authority is *Chiles v. Salazar*, decided March 31, 2026. The Court held 8–1 that licensed professionals retain ordinary speech protection and that a state cannot turn speech into conduct by labeling or regulating it. *Chiles* does not directly resolve whether CME instruction is government speech, but its reasoning makes the Ninth Circuit's broad reliance on professional regulation materially vulnerable.

## Case-specific signals

The provisioned questions presented and complete 29-page petition say California requires essentially every CME course, including unrelated technical instruction, to address implicit bias. Petitioners allege private instructors create the content, private entities accredit it, and California ordinarily neither drafts, reviews, nor edits the courses. Those facts make the government-speech label contestable under *Shurtleff* and *Tam*.

The procedural posture is unusually favorable for further review: the Ninth Circuit affirmed dismissal at the pleading stage; Judges VanDyke, Bumatay, and Tung dissented from denial of rehearing en banc; six amicus groups appear on the docket, including a multistate brief; and, after respondents waived a response, the Court requested one. A requested response is meaningful evidence that at least one Justice saw enough in the petition to require adversarial briefing. The response is now due August 12, so no brief in opposition was available in the July 17 provisioned record. I do not treat that ordinary timing circumstance as missing data.

There are substantial reasons not to cross the 50% threshold. The claimed circuit conflict is mostly about how generally framed *Shurtleff* factors are applied; the petition does not identify another circuit deciding the same CME setting. California can also distinguish *Chiles*: the Ninth Circuit characterized qualifying CME as the state's own speech, while *Chiles* addressed a restriction on concededly private therapeutic speech. A request for response requires only one Justice, whereas certiorari requires four, and many response-requested petitions are ultimately denied.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and 3.0% for modern petitions originating in the Ninth Circuit. This petition has no CVSG and has not reached a meaningful post-response relist stage. I raise the probability sharply above those anchors for the response request, multiple amici, en banc dissents, clean pleading-stage vehicle, and the recent 8–1 *Chiles* decision. I stop at 37% because the split is imperfect and the state has not yet supplied its opposition.

No individual vote prediction is offered. Cert-stage votes ordinarily are not disclosed, and a possible GVR would not support a reliable Justice-by-Justice allocation. The 0.78 big-case score measures stakes rather than grant likelihood: a merits decision could affect compelled-speech rules across medical, legal, and other professional continuing-education regimes.
