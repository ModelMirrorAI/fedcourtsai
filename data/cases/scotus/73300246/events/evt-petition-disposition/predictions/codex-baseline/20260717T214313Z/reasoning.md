## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the Fifth Amendment requires compensation when a state agency sells what the owner characterizes as the only feasible access to private property, leaving part of the property landlocked and valueless. The underlying merits theory is that total denial of feasible access is a compensable taking and that a state cannot evade the Takings Clause merely by refusing to recognize a traditional property interest. Certiorari nevertheless depends on the Rule 10 considerations of a genuine lower-court conflict, an important recurring federal question, and a suitable vehicle, not merely plausible error below.

## Record and merits signals

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition frame a potentially important property-rights issue. The petition invokes *Tyler v. Hennepin County*, *Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid*, and older federal and state access cases. It alleges that NDOT removed a bridge during highway construction, permitted use of a replacement access road for years, and later sold that road without reserving an easement.

The provisioned brief in opposition and the appended Nevada Supreme Court order expose serious factual and legal complications. Respondents say the parcel abuts Huffaker Place and that the northwest portion can be reached by constructing a bridge across a drainage ditch. They also say the Harveys acquired the parcel only after the highway project altered access. The state court held that the Trust never owned the sold road, could not acquire a prescriptive easement against state land, and failed to establish the common ownership needed for an easement by necessity or implication. It separately treated the older property and access claims as untimely. Most damagingly as a vehicle matter, the state court noted that the Trust did not preserve below its argument based on NRS 408.533. These state-law, preservation, limitations, and record disputes offer multiple grounds to avoid the broad federal question.

The petition identifies no square conflict on materially equivalent facts. Its principal authorities concern owners with an established access right or government interference with access; here, the existence of the relevant property interest is itself disputed and was resolved adversely under Nevada law. The ruling below is unpublished, unanimous, and highly case-specific. The snapshot also shows no amici, no CVSG, and no relist. CourtListener searches for broader access-takings authority returned scattered state cases and general Supreme Court takings decisions, not a clean Supreme Court precedent or obvious developed conflict that cures these defects.
