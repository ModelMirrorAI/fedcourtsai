# Harvey v. City of Reno, Nevada, et al. — No. 25-1254 (petition disposition)

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.015.**

## The case

The Harvey Trust owns a parcel near I-580 and Huffaker Place in Reno. When
I-580 was built in 1987, a bridge over Dry Creek that had served the parcel's
northwest corner was removed, and the Trust thereafter reached that corner over
an NDOT-owned access road (APN 043-282-03). In 2021 NDOT sold that road parcel
at public auction to Green Acres Storage Partners LLC without reserving an
access easement, and the buyer cut the Trust off. State courts dismissed the
Trust's quiet-title, easement, inverse-condemnation, and due-process claims at
the pleading stage; the Nevada Supreme Court (en banc, unpublished Order of
Affirmance, Sept. 8, 2025) held the quiet-title claims time-barred under NRS
11.080 and — citing Sloat v. Turner — that because the Trust never owned the
access road and could not establish an easement by necessity, prescription, or
implication, it "possessed [no] interest to be taken in the first place."
Rehearing was denied Feb. 6, 2026.

The question presented: whether the Takings Clause requires just compensation
when a state agency sells the only feasible access to private property,
rendering it landlocked and valueless.

## Why the petition has some surface appeal

- The theory rides the Court's genuine recent interest in takings (Knick,
  Cedar Point, Tyler, Sheetz — confirmed via a CourtListener sweep of
  post-2019 SCOTUS takings opinions), and specifically invokes Tyler v.
  Hennepin County's warning that a state cannot "sidestep the Takings Clause
  by disavowing traditional property interests." Framing a state-law
  no-property-interest holding as exactly that sidestep is the petition's best
  card.
- The narrative is sympathetic: a family parcel allegedly rendered worthless
  by the state's own sale of the access it had long tolerated, after the state
  itself removed the original bridge.

## Why it will almost certainly be denied

1. **No split.** The petition concedes the Court "has not unequivocally held"
   its rule and cites no modern conflict — only Nevada precedent against a
   handful of older/intermediate state cases on abutter's rights (Bacich 1943,
   Heath (Colo. App.), Wellswood (Conn.)), which the BIO plausibly
   distinguishes. Rule 10 gives the Court nothing to resolve.
2. **Vehicle problems everywhere.**
   - *Adequate and independent state grounds / antecedent state-law questions*:
     whether an easement arose is state property law, and the quiet-title and
     1987-overtaking claims were dismissed on a state statute of limitations.
   - *Factual dispute on the core premise*: the BIO says the northwest corner
     sits on the same parcel (APN 043-011-40) that abuts the Huffaker Place
     cul-de-sac — the owner could bridge its own drainage ditch — so the
     "landlocked" premise is contested, and the case was decided on a
     12(b)(5) record.
   - *Waiver*: the NRS 408.533 entitlement theory (the Roth hook) was held
     waived below (raised first on appeal), a classic cert-killer for the
     petition's lead statutory argument.
   - *Timing*: petitioner didn't acquire the parcel until 1993, after the
     condemnation and bridge removal, muddying the sympathetic narrative.
3. **No institutional signals.** Unpublished, en banc but unanimous state
   decision; petitioner represented by a Reno solo practitioner, not the
   Supreme Court bar; no amici on the docket as of the 2026-07-17 snapshot;
   three of four respondents waived response (the City of Reno filed a BIO
   voluntarily — there was no call for response); no CVSG; distributed once,
   to the Sept. 28, 2026 long conference, with no relist yet.
4. **Base rates.** The statpack's modern discretionary-cert slice puts the
   overall grant rate at ~2.5–3% per recent Term; the 0-relist bucket grants
   0.8% and this case sits in that bucket as of the snapshot (its first
   conference is still ahead, so I shade up slightly rather than adopting the
   0-relist rate wholesale); the no-CVSG bucket ~3%. Paid filing helps
   relative to IFP, but state-court originators in the corpus grant rarely.

Netting the paid/counseled status against the absence of a split, the waived
lead argument, the contested factual premise, and the state-law posture, I put
P(grant, including GVR) at **1.5%** — near but slightly below the paid-petition
population rate, because this petition is weaker than the average paid petition
on every conventional cert factor. No plausible GVR source exists (no pending
merits case on point), so the disposition call is a plain **denied**.
