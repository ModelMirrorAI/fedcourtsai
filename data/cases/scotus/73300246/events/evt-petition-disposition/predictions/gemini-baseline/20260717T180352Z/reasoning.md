## Reasoning

This case presents a question under the Fifth Amendment's Takings Clause: whether just compensation is required when a state agency sells the only feasible access to private property, thereby rendering it landlocked and valueless. 

While Takings Clause cases can occasionally garner the Court's interest, several factors strongly indicate a denial of certiorari in this instance:
1. **Vehicle Problems/State Law Issues**: The Brief in Opposition (BIO) filed by the respondents heavily focuses on threshold defects. Specifically, the respondents argue that the petitioner's inverse condemnation claims are barred by the applicable statute of limitations. Furthermore, they contend that under state law, the petitioner had no recognizable property interest in the auctioned surplus parcel. The existence of an independent and adequate state law ground (statute of limitations or state property law definitions) makes this case a very poor vehicle to decide a federal constitutional question.
2. **Factual Disputes**: The BIO disputes the premise of the Question Presented, arguing that the petitioner's parcel is not actually landlocked. The Supreme Court rarely grants cert to resolve fact-bound disputes.
3. **Base Rates**: The petition is a paid petition emerging from a state supreme court. Crucially, as of the snapshot date, the petition has 0 relists and has been distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. The historical base rate for grants with 0 relists is approximately 0.8%. 

Given the low base rate combined with significant threshold state law and factual defenses raised in the BIO, the probability of a grant is extremely low. I predict the petition will be denied.
