# Prediction: denied

I assign a 14% probability to any grant and predict denial. This is substantially above the ordinary paid-petition baseline, but the evidence available before the first conference does not make four votes more likely than not.

## Question and governing standard

The petition principally asks whether state anti-SLAPP special-dismissal procedures operate in federal court when their burdens and timing conflict with Federal Rules of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) and 56. It describes a three-way division: the D.C., Second, Fifth, and Eleventh Circuits displace the state procedure; the First Circuit applies it as enacted; and the Ninth Circuit preserves remedies such as fee shifting while replacing conflicting procedural features with federal standards. The second question challenges the Ninth Circuit's limited-purpose-public-figure determination under *Gertz*, *Wolston*, and *Hutchinson*.

At certiorari, the strongest considerations are a square, entrenched circuit conflict, recurring national importance, a clean vehicle, and the absence of obstacles that would prevent resolution. Four votes are required. The Court is much less likely to review a fact-bound claim of misapplied settled law.

## Grant-positive signals

The full 53-page petition provides a serious Rule 10 case on the first question. It identifies published decisions on every side of the asserted conflict and explains why the same Nevada motion would be unavailable in four circuits, untimely under the First Circuit's as-enacted approach, but fee-generating in the Ninth Circuit's hybrid regime. The issue is recurring because many states use burden-shifting anti-SLAPP statutes, and the resulting forum-dependent fee exposure can materially affect speech litigation.

The vehicle has an unusually concrete federal consequence. Respondents filed the Nevada special motion 465 days after service and after discovery, although state law ordinarily supplies a 60-day deadline and discovery stay. The Ninth Circuit nevertheless revived the motion and remanded for mandatory fees. Thus, even though petitioners lost independently on summary judgment, the anti-SLAPP question remains live through the fee award and is plausibly outcome-determinative on that relief.

The Court's January 2026 decision in *Berk v. Choy* strengthens the petition. The provisioned petition relies on *Berk*'s treatment of a state merits-screening requirement as displaced by the Federal Rules and its rejection of rewriting state law to avoid a conflict. CourtListener confirmed that *Berk* held Delaware's affidavit-of-merit requirement inapplicable in federal court and emphasized the straightforward *Shady Grove* framework. The Ninth Circuit's 2025 en banc *Gopher Media* decision also confirms that anti-SLAPP procedure remains an active and contested issue in that circuit.

## Denial-positive signals

The baseline remains powerful. The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 3.0% grant rate for modern cert petitions from the Ninth Circuit. This docket has no relist or CVSG signal in the July 18 snapshot. It was merely distributed for its first conference, and no brief in opposition was provisioned. Only one amicus filing appears on the snapshot. Those are materially weaker institutional signals than typically accompany a likely plenary grant.

The opinion below is unpublished, the underlying dispute is a fact-specific conflict between online coaching businesses, and the case combines a procedural anti-SLAPP issue with a separate defamation/public-figure dispute. Although the fee remand preserves adversity, the independent summary-judgment loss may still make the overall posture look less clean than the petition claims. The second question is especially unlikely to attract review: it principally alleges that the panel performed an insufficiently particularized application of settled public-figure doctrine, and the disputed record facts make it resemble error correction.

There is also vehicle competition. The petition itself identifies a pending petition in *Gopher Media LLC v. Melone*, No. 25-1067, arising from the Ninth Circuit's en banc anti-SLAPP ruling. CourtListener did not return an indexed Supreme Court docket result for that related petition, so I do not infer its status. Its existence nevertheless gives the Court options: select another vehicle, hold this petition, or continue allowing the issue to percolate. The Court has previously declined several anti-SLAPP petitions cited by petitioners, which tempers the force of the longstanding split.

## Bottom line

The circuit conflict, the concrete fee consequence, and *Berk* raise this petition well above the paid-petition base rate. Against that, the lack of a response, relist, or CVSG; the unpublished and mixed posture; the weak second question; and a competing petition make denial the likeliest disposition. I therefore estimate **P(any grant) = 0.14** and select **denied**. A plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR because the principal question requires resolving the circuit conflict rather than merely applying an intervening decision. I do not predict individual Justice votes because the pre-conference record supplies no reliable basis for them.

The significance score of 0.58 reflects moderate-to-high institutional stakes if the Court decided the case: a ruling could affect anti-SLAPP motions and fee exposure across many jurisdictions, though the underlying private dispute is not itself nationally prominent.
