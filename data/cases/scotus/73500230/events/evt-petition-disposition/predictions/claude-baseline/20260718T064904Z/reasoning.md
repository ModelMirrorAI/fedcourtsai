# Wealthy, Inc. v. Cornelia — cert petition disposition (No. 25-1329)

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.07.**

## The case

Petitioners (Wealthy, Inc. and Dale Buczkowski, who run the "Derek Moneyberg"
business-coaching brand) sued YouTube commentator Spencer Cornelia and related
entities in the District of Nevada over five accusatory statements in video
interviews, pleading Nevada defamation claims plus a Lanham Act false-advertising
claim. The district court granted summary judgment for defendants across the
board — holding petitioners limited-purpose public figures with no showing of
actual malice — but denied the Nevada anti-SLAPP special motion on prong one.
The Ninth Circuit (unpublished memorandum) affirmed summary judgment and, in a
February 25, 2026 superseding opinion, reversed the anti-SLAPP denial, reviving
mandatory fee-shifting (respondents have since demanded a $600,000 bond).
Rehearing en banc was refused March 16, 2026.

## The questions presented

1. Whether state anti-SLAPP special-motion statutes are displaced in federal
   court by Rules 12(b)(6) and 56 under *Shady Grove* (the D.C./2d/5th/11th
   position), apply as enacted (1st Cir.), or apply in the Ninth Circuit's
   hybrid form (federal merits standards, state fee-shifting preserved).
2. Whether the panel's one-sentence limited-purpose-public-figure designation
   departed from *Gertz*, *Wolston*, and *Hutchinson*.

## Docket posture (snapshot of 2026-07-18)

Paid petition, docketed May 29, 2026. Response was due June 29, 2026; no brief
in opposition appears on the docket. One petition-stage amicus (Thomas More
Society). Distributed July 15, 2026 for the September 28, 2026 "long
conference." No CVSG, no relists (none possible yet), no call for a response.

## Factors favoring a grant

- QP1 rests on a genuine, mature, well-documented three-way circuit conflict
  the petition papers thoroughly (Abbas; Carbone; Klocke; La Liberte; Godin;
  Newsham/Gopher Media), implicating the anti-SLAPP statutes of roughly 32
  jurisdictions — a classic Rule 10 split.
- Doctrinal moment: *Berk v. Choy* (2026) just reaffirmed a straightforward
  *Shady Grove*/*Sibbach* displacement framework for state procedural overlays,
  and four Ninth Circuit judges urged overruling *Newsham* in the *Gopher
  Media* en banc proceedings. A companion petition on a facet of the same split
  (Gopher Media LLC v. Melone, No. 25-1067, filed March 10, 2026) is pending
  (confirmed still undecided via CourtListener), so the issue is visibly
  percolating.
- Decent vehicle story for QP1: the special motion was *granted*, in a posture
  (465 days after service, post-discovery) the statute as enacted would not
  permit, so all three circuit positions are outcome-determinative at least as
  to fees.

## Factors favoring denial

- Base rates: modern paid cert petitions grant at roughly 5–7% (statpack
  per-Term fee-class rates, OT2023–OT2025); the whole-docket modern cert rate
  is ~2.5–3%. This petition currently carries none of the strong pre-grant
  signals (no relist, no CVSG, no record of a response being called for).
- The Court has faced this exact split repeatedly for over a decade and denied
  every petition presenting it — Ctr. for Med. Progress v. Planned Parenthood
  (18-696), Retzlaff v. Van Dyke (19-1272), Clifford v. Trump (20-602), and
  others the petition itself catalogs while distinguishing. Persistent,
  acknowledged splits on this issue have not moved four Justices.
- Stakes in this case are largely fee-shifting only: the merits dismissal was
  affirmed on independent Rule 56 / actual-malice grounds, so resolving QP1
  would mostly change who pays attorneys' fees — a weaker candidate for plenary
  review than a case where the split flips the judgment on the claims.
- The decision below is an unpublished memorandum disposition (*Berk* shows
  this is not disqualifying, but it is an unfavorable marker on average).
- QP2 is fact-bound error correction: the Court has recently and repeatedly
  declined invitations to police public-figure designations (e.g., the
  *Berisha* denial over Thomas and Gorsuch dissents), and a "vacate for a more
  particularized *Gertz* analysis" ask is the kind of case-specific relief the
  Court rarely supplies.
- No brief in opposition and apparently non-elite Supreme Court counsel; before
  any grant the Court would first call for a response, adding a step at which
  most such petitions die. If the Court wants this issue, it can also choose
  or hold *Gopher Media* (filed earlier, en banc posture) as the vehicle —
  which caps this petition's independent grant odds, though a grant there
  could produce a hold-and-GVR here (folded into the probability).

## Bottom line

Anchoring on the paid-petition base rate (~5–7%) and adjusting: the real,
percolating split and the *Berk* tailwind push up; the fee-only stakes, the
unpublished decision, the fact-bound second QP, the missing BIO, and the
Court's long record of denying precisely this question push down. Net: modestly
above the whole-docket rate but near the paid base rate. **P(grant incl. GVR)
= 0.07; most likely disposition: denied**, most plausibly after a call for a
response and possibly a relist without a grant.
