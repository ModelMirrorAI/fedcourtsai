This case presents a fundamental conflict between two lines of precedent: *Turner v. Safley* (deferring to prison administration) and *United States v. Virginia* (heightened scrutiny for sex classifications) as applied to transgender inmates challenging cross-gender strip searches and housing assignments. The petition points to a deep, 3-3 circuit split on the issue.

At the time the Tenth Circuit affirmed the denial of the motion to dismiss in this case, it relied on its circuit precedent in *Fowler v. Stitt*, holding that policies distinguishing on "transgender status" discriminate based on sex, requiring heightened scrutiny. Subsequently, the Supreme Court issued *United States v. Skrmetti* (145 S. Ct. 1816 (2025)), holding that bans on medical care for transgender minors do not trigger heightened scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause. Immediately following *Skrmetti*, the Court granted, vacated, and remanded (GVR'd) the Tenth Circuit's decision in *Fowler*. 

This petition (docketed October 2025) urges the Court to similarly GVR this case in light of *Skrmetti*, noting the Tenth Circuit's direct reliance on *Fowler*. However, the chronological progression on the docket strongly points towards plenary review rather than a simple GVR. The Court distributed this petition for the December 12, 2025 conference, but then held it for over six months until the June 29, 2026 conference. This aligns precisely with the timeline for *West Virginia v. B.P.J.* (concerning transgender athletes), which the Court had also granted. If the Court believed *Skrmetti* or *B.P.J.* squarely resolved the prison context, it likely would have GVR'd this case at the end of the term in June 2026. Instead, on June 30, 2026—after holding it for the entire term—the Court issued a CVSG (calling for the views of the Solicitor General).

A CVSG after holding a case for an intervening decision signals that the Court recognizes the unique features of the carceral setting (namely, *Turner* deference and the federal government's massive institutional interests in BOP operations) might warrant separate plenary review, rather than relying on lower courts to parse the implications of *Skrmetti* and *B.P.J.* on remand. The SG will almost certainly highlight the federal government's strong interest in resolving the circuit split to establish clear constitutional baselines for prison administration. Given the salience, the acknowledged circuit split, and the Court's deliberate choice to CVSG rather than summarily GVR alongside *Fowler*, I predict a 75% probability of a plenary grant.
