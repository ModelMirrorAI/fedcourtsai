# Prediction

I predict **denial**, with a **38% probability of any grant-side disposition**. If the Court does act on the petition, a GVR in light of *West Virginia v. B. P. J.* and *Little v. Hecox* is more likely than plenary review.

## Record and questions

The July 15 snapshot identifies a paid petition from the Tenth Circuit. The petition was first distributed for the December 12, 2025 conference, distributed again on June 29, 2026, and drew a CVSG on June 30. The provisioned petition and questions-presented text ask (1) whether sex-based prison policies challenged by a transgender inmate receive intermediate scrutiny under *United States v. Virginia* or Turner deference, and (2) whether the Tenth Circuit's treatment of a cross-gender strip-search claim conflicts with *Bell* and *Florence*. The provisioned brief in opposition emphasizes that the case is at the Rule 12(b)(6) stage, petitioners conceded intermediate scrutiny below, the panel expressly reserved the ultimate merits, and the abusive-search claim will continue regardless.

On the alleged facts, Griffith is a transgender woman with gender dysphoria who had developed breasts through hormone treatment but retained male genitalia. The jail housed her in a male unit under a genitalia-based policy and split her intake search between female and male deputies. The complaint further alleges that the male deputy used sexually threatening language, that Griffith was later assaulted in male housing, and that the jail denied requested accommodations. Those allegations make the search question a poor vehicle for a broad categorical ruling and strengthen the opposition's interlocutory-posture objection.

## Legal and certiorari assessment

The governing certiorari considerations favor review when a square, outcome-relevant conflict exists or an important federal question needs resolution. Petitioners identify a serious national question about the interaction of *Turner* deference and intermediate scrutiny, supported by a multi-state amicus brief. But the opposition persuasively identifies vehicle defects: the Turner argument was not passed on because it was not presented, the purported post-*Johnson* circuit conflict is thin, and the Tenth Circuit decided only pleading sufficiency on an undeveloped record.

CourtListener's June 30, 2026 opinion in *West Virginia v. B. P. J.* materially changes the context. A six-Justice majority held that biological-sex classifications in school sports satisfy intermediate scrutiny and that the laws did not classify by transgender status under *Skrmetti*. That reasoning helps petitioners argue that sex-based institutional policies can survive even heightened scrutiny. It does not, however, hold that *Turner* replaces intermediate scrutiny in prisons or decide how a genitalia-only jail policy applies to an individual detainee on these alleged facts. The lower courts can apply the new decision on remand, reducing the need for plenary review while making a GVR plausible.

The CVSG is the strongest grant-side signal. The committed statpack reports a 27.1% grant rate for resolved modern CVSG petitions, compared with 5.4% for paid 2025-Term petitions and 5.0% for modern petitions from the Tenth Circuit. The CVSG's timing—the same day as *B. P. J.*—and the federal government's direct prison-administration interest justify an upward adjustment from the CVSG baseline. Still, a CVSG petition is denied most often, and the waiver, interlocutory posture, and unusually unfavorable search allegations keep denial as the modal result.
