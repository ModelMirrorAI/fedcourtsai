# Roybal v. Griffith, No. 25-442 — cert disposition prediction

**Prediction: P(granted, GVR included) = 0.40; modal disposition: denied.**

## The case

Darlene Griffith, a transgender woman detained pretrial at the El Paso County
(Colorado) Jail, sued under § 1983 after the jail — applying a policy that
assigns housing solely by genitalia — placed her in a male unit, subjected her
to a cross-gender strip search (conducted, per the allegations, in an abusive
and threatening manner by Deputy Mustapick), denied her female clothing and
commissary items, and left her exposed to repeated sexual assault. The district
court dismissed under Rule 12(b)(6). The Tenth Circuit (129 F.4th 790, Feb. 19,
2025) reversed in relevant part, holding Griffith plausibly alleged an equal
protection claim under intermediate scrutiny — a standard petitioners had
conceded applied after the circuit's Fowler v. Stitt decision — and plausibly
alleged Fourth/Fourteenth Amendment claims about the cross-gender search.
Rehearing en banc was denied over dissent (139 F.4th 1183, June 10, 2025).

The sheriff's petition (filed Oct. 7, 2025, paid docket, experienced counsel)
presents two questions: (1) whether a transgender inmate's challenge to
sex-based prison policies gets heightened scrutiny under United States v.
Virginia (as the 8th, 9th, and 10th Circuits hold) or Turner v. Safley
deference (as the 2d, 4th, and D.C. Circuits hold); and (2) whether a rule
against cross-identified-gender strip searches contravenes Bell v. Wolfish and
Florence.

## The docket signals — the heart of this prediction

- Fully briefed (BIO Nov. 10, 2025; reply Nov. 24, 2025); a 23-state amicus
  bloc plus the Arizona Legislature supports the petition.
- Distributed for the 12/12/2025 conference, then **held roughly seven months**
  and redistributed for the 6/29/2026 conference — the classic pattern of a
  hold pending a related merits case. West Virginia v. B.P.J. / Little v.
  Hecox (transgender athletes, equal protection scrutiny) were decided
  **June 30, 2026**.
- On **June 30, 2026 — the same day B.P.J. came down — the Court called for
  the views of the Solicitor General** rather than denying or GVR'ing.

That sequencing is highly informative. B.P.J. (Kavanaugh, J.) held the sports
statutes were sex-based classifications subject to intermediate scrutiny and
**upheld** them as substantially related to safety and competitive fairness.
Had the Court thought B.P.J. disposed of this petition, the cheap moves were a
denial or a GVR at the 6/29 conference. Instead it CVSG'd — signaling that at
least several Justices see a live, distinct question (the prison-specific
Turner-vs.-VMI scrutiny question, which B.P.J. did not address) and want the
government's views, which are unusually pertinent here: the federal government
operates its own sex-segregated prison system, houses by biological sex under
current executive policy, is defending parallel suits, and filed an amicus
brief in the Tenth Circuit's pending Fowler v. Stitt remand (post-Skrmetti).

## Base rates and adjustment

From the committed statpack (modern discretionary-cert, denial-reweighted):

- Whole-population grant rate: ~2.5–3.3% per recent Term; denied ~95%.
- **CVSG bucket: granted 27.1%, denied 71.2%** (n=59 resolved) — the single
  strongest signal cut available, and this case is squarely in it.
- Multiple-distribution petitions (2 relists: granted 33.6%; 3+: 21.8%)
  corroborate that repeated conference attention is a large multiplier.
- Originating circuit (CA10: granted 5.0%) is mildly above baseline.

Published empirical work puts post-CVSG grant rates somewhat higher (roughly a
third to two-fifths), and the SG's eventual recommendation is close to
dispositive in both directions. I start from ~27–35% and adjust:

**Upward:** the CVSG was issued *after* B.P.J. was in hand, so it reflects
continued interest net of the related-case overhang; 23-state amicus support;
undeniable national salience with federal-policy entanglement (an SG
sympathetic to petitioners' merits position is plausible); two independent
questions presented, the strip-search question untouched by the waiver problem.

**Downward (these keep denial modal):**

1. **Vehicle defects are serious.** The case is interlocutory (12(b)(6)
   reversal, no record); petitioners *conceded* intermediate scrutiny below
   and never raised the Turner theory, so QP1 arguably isn't preserved (a
   party-presentation problem the BIO presses hard); the alleged facts of the
   search are egregious, making an unsympathetic vehicle; and the case
   proceeds against Mustapick on the manner-of-search claim regardless.
2. **The split is contestable.** The Tenth Circuit applied a conceded
   standard; the BIO's "no square split" argument has force, and the SG often
   recommends denial on exactly these grounds even while agreeing with the
   petitioner's legal position.
3. **B.P.J. lowers the temperature.** It confirms intermediate scrutiny can be
   satisfied by safety/privacy-justified sex classifications — so petitioners
   may well win on remand under the conceded standard, and the Court can let
   the issue percolate (the Fowler remand, with US amicus participation, is
   pending in the Tenth Circuit).
4. A **GVR** (in light of B.P.J. or of whatever the Tenth Circuit does in
   Fowler) remains a live off-ramp once the SG reports; GVR counts as a grant
   on the binary axis.
