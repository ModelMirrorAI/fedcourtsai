## Questions and governing standards

The first question asks whether Strickland prejudice must be assessed from the cumulative effect of counsel's deficiencies rather than error by error. Under *Strickland v. Washington*, prejudice exists when there is a reasonable probability that, absent counsel's errors, the result would have been different. In a capital sentencing case, *Williams v. Taylor*, *Wong v. Belmontes*, and *Thornell v. Jones* direct a court to consider the total body of mitigating evidence and reweigh it against the aggravating evidence.

The second question asks whether a capital post-conviction court violates due process or the Eighth Amendment by adopting the State's proposed order wholesale without meaningful guidance or substantive revision. The petition relies principally on *Jefferson v. Upton* and the Eleventh Circuit's later treatment of that case; the opposition emphasizes that this Court has criticized verbatim adoption without establishing the broad constitutional rule Lindsey requests.

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid capital petition from the Supreme Court of South Carolina, with a response, reply, and distribution for the September 28 conference. There is no CVSG and, at this pre-conference stage, no relist. Those absences matter because the committed statpack puts the 2025 Term paid-petition grant rate at about 5.4%, while petitions with no CVSG and no relist are materially weaker than selected petitions.

The petition and questions-presented text identify a plausible recurring legal issue. Lindsey argues that the state majority separately rejected the prejudicial force of an inadequate mitigation investigation, mental-health presentation, mercy requests, an omitted contemporaneous witness, and an omitted prison-adaptability expert. Two state justices dissented, stressing the combined mitigation case and the single statutory eligibility aggravator. The petition also collects decisions from the First, Second, Seventh, and Ninth Circuits favoring aggregation and contrasts them with decisions from the Fourth, Sixth, and Eighth Circuits. Experienced Supreme Court counsel and the case's capital posture add some grant signal.

The brief in opposition presents substantial obstacles. It argues that Lindsey did not preserve a distinct cumulative-prejudice claim or obtain a ruling on it; that the state majority considered the new evidence in context, used language covering counsel's errors collectively, and quoted the correct reweighing standard; and that some omitted mitigation would have opened damaging cross-examination concerning jealousy, domestic violence, prison fights, and prior violence. Those points make the case look fact-bound and permit the Court to deny without resolving whether the circuit formulations truly conflict. Recent *Thornell* precedent also gives the Court less reason to take a new case merely to restate totality-of-the-evidence review.

The proposed-order question is an even weaker vehicle. The post-conviction judge initially signed the State's error-filled order, but the state supreme court vacated it, the parties participated on remand, and the judge stated that the findings matched his view after further review. The opposition also notes that Lindsey submitted his own proposed order and that the dissent below did not dispute the resolution of this issue. The cited authorities reflect criticism and different review consequences more clearly than a square split over a categorical constitutional violation.
