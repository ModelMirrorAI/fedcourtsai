## Analysis of Certiorari Likelihood

The petitioner, Marion Alexander Lindsey, seeks review of a South Carolina Supreme Court decision affirming his death sentence and the denial of state post-conviction relief. The petition presents two questions:
1. Whether courts must evaluate the cumulative prejudice of counsel's errors under *Strickland v. Washington* or if they may evaluate each error in isolation.
2. Whether a trial court's wholesale adoption of the State's proposed order in a capital case, with only typographical corrections, violates due process or the Eighth Amendment.

### Legal Merits and Vehicle Considerations
The first question touches on a recognized, simmering tension among lower courts regarding whether *Strickland* prejudice must be assessed cumulatively when multiple deficiencies in counsel's performance are assumed or found. However, the State forcefully argues that the split is illusory, as many courts aggregate only when they find multiple actual deficiencies, rather than assumed ones. Furthermore, the State raises significant vehicle problems: it contends that the petitioner did not fairly present the cumulative-error legal framework as a standalone constitutional requirement to the state supreme court, instead merely arguing the facts of his mitigating evidence.

The second question (adoption of the proposed order) is a heavily fact-bound procedural issue. The state supreme court found that the trial judge independently reviewed the order, left typographical errors in intentionally to prove his involvement during the remand process, and expressly stated that the order reflected his view of the case. Given the deference this Court usually affords state procedural and fact-finding processes, this issue is unlikely to warrant a grant of certiorari. 

### Signals and Base Rates
According to the corpus statpack, the base rate for paid cert petitions in recent Terms is very low (around 2.5-3.0% granted). More importantly, the petition has just been distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference (the "Long Conference") and has no relists. A relist is the classic, almost mandatory signal of the Court's serious consideration for a grant or a written dissent from the denial of certiorari. The absence of a relist at this stage strongly points toward a denial.

### Conclusion
Given the fact-bound nature of the state court's Strickland application, the potential waiver/presentation issues raised by the State, and the lack of any relists signaling the Court's interest, the petition is highly likely to be denied. I assign a 3% probability to a grant, aligning closely with the base rate for paid petitions.
