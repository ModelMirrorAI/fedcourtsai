# Cohen v. Judicial Conduct Board of Pennsylvania, No. 25-1215 — cert prediction

**Prediction: deny. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.10.**

## The case

Mark B. Cohen, a former 42-year Democratic Pennsylvania legislator elected in 2017
to the Philadelphia Court of Common Pleas, was permanently suspended without pay by
Pennsylvania's Court of Judicial Discipline (CJD) — three months before his mandatory
retirement — for 66 Facebook posts the CJD found collectively constituted partisan
political activity, on a public page identifying him as a judge and (initially)
picturing him in his robe. By operation of Pennsylvania law the suspension also
forfeited his pensions. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court unanimously affirmed
(Jan. 21, 2026, 349 A.3d 865), holding that restrictions on a *sitting* judge's
non-campaign speech are reviewed under *Pickering* balancing rather than strict
scrutiny, expressly acknowledging the split of authority and stating it would apply
its balancing test "unless or until the United States Supreme Court resolves this
open question of law."

**Question presented:** whether content-based restrictions on the speech of sitting
judges must satisfy strict scrutiny (petition's framing, from the provisioned
`questions-presented.txt`).

## Grant-side signals

1. **The Court called for a response after a waiver.** A CFR means at least one
   chambers took the petition seriously enough to stop it from dying at the May 21
   conference. Empirically this is the strongest single pre-conference signal
   available here: CFR'd paid petitions grant at several times the paid-petition
   baseline (on the order of ~8–10% vs. the ~2.5–3% modern grant rate in the
   corpus statpack, ~4–5% for paid petitions).
2. **A real, acknowledged, long-percolating split.** The level of scrutiny for
   discipline of sitting judges' extrajudicial speech has divided courts for ~25
   years: strict scrutiny (Fifth Circuit in *Jenevein*; Ohio in *Grendell* (2025);
   Arkansas, Maine, Wyoming, arguably Washington and New York) vs. *Pickering*
   balancing (Seventh Circuit in *Siefert*; Pennsylvania here; California, New
   Mexico, Oregon, Missouri) vs. intermediate formulations (New Jersey, West
   Virginia). The court below expressly flagged the split and invited this Court's
   resolution — a fact pattern the Court sometimes rewards.
3. **Elite counsel on both sides.** A former Assistant to the SG (Eisenstein) for
   petitioner; the fact that the Judicial Conduct Board retained the Stanford
   Clinic (Fletcher, the former Principal Deputy SG, and Fisher) to write the BIO
   itself reveals that respondent perceives genuine grant risk.
4. **Doctrinal salience.** The current Court is speech-protective and has shown
   appetite for policing lower courts' use of *Pickering*-style balancing against
   content-based restrictions; the QP follows directly from *Republican Party of
   Minnesota v. White* (2002) and *Williams-Yulee* (2015), which left the
   sitting-judge question open.

## Deny-side signals

1. **The Court has denied this exact question repeatedly for decades.** The
   petition's own authorities are studded with cert denials in materially similar
   judicial-discipline speech cases: *Broadman* (1999), *Broadbelt* (1997),
   *Schenck* (1994), *Dunleavy* (2004), *Siefert* (2011), *Neely* (2018). Revealed
   preference over 25 years is strong: the Court has let this split percolate
   through many clean-looking vehicles.
2. **Vehicle problems, well-executed in the BIO.**
   - Cohen was *barred from ever running again* (mandatory retirement at 75), so
     *White*'s electorate-information rationale — the engine of the strict-scrutiny
     cases — is absent on these facts.
   - The discipline rested on **six different Code provisions** and varied conduct
     (implied endorsement of a congressional candidate, comments on matters likely
     to be litigated, and abuse of the prestige of office via his robe photo and
     judicial title), inviting fractured, rule-by-rule analysis rather than a clean
     resolution of the scrutiny question.
   - **Outcome-independence:** the CJD found the sanction survives strict scrutiny
     too, and even *Jenevein* (petitioner's best case) *upheld* discipline aimed at
     the "trappings of judicial office" under strict scrutiny. The standard may not
     change the result, which saps the QP's cert-worthiness in this vehicle.
   - The BIO plausibly undermines the Fifth Circuit side of the split via the
     earlier-panel rule (*Scott v. Flowers* (5th Cir. 1990) applied *Pickering* to
     elected judges' speech), and recharacterizes the Arkansas/Ohio cases as ones
     where *Pickering* was never litigated.
3. **Unattractive facts.** A unanimous five-judge CJD panel and a unanimous state
   supreme court; findings of "exceptional" defiance (continued partisan posting
   over his supervising judge's warnings and during the proceedings); a record the
   Court would have to wade through 66 posts to reframe. The equities do not invite
   error-correction sympathy, and the pension forfeiture — the petition's most
   sympathetic fact — is, as the BIO notes, the product of a separate state
   proceeding still on appeal.
4. **No conference-stage momentum yet.** No relists (the corpus statpack's
   0-relist bucket grants at 0.8%, though that cut is post-hoc), no CVSG, no
   amici visible on the provisioned record as of the snapshot.

## Weighing

Anchor on the modern discretionary-cert base rate (~2.5–3%; Term 2025 estimate 2.5%
in `metrics/statpack.md`), adjust up for paid status and materially up for the CFR
(the dominant signal, to roughly 8–12%), and up slightly for the genuinely
acknowledged split and the caliber of counsel. Adjust back down for the Court's
long revealed preference for denying this question, the multiple vehicle defects,
and the unattractive record. The most likely path is a few distributions this fall
followed by a denial — possibly after a relist, and conceivably with a statement
respecting denial from a speech-focused Justice. A GVR is implausible (no
intervening decision; no mootness).

**P(grant) = 0.10; predicted disposition: denied; granted = 0.**

If the docket later shows multiple relists out of the long conference, the
probability should move up sharply (the 2-relist bucket in the statpack grants at
~34%); a single distribution-and-deny is the modal outcome.

## Big-case score

0.5 — a merits decision would set the First Amendment standard governing
extrajudicial speech for the country's 30,000+ state and federal judges, a
significant judicial-administration and speech-doctrine question with real press
interest (judges' social media is a recurring flashpoint), but the underlying
dispute — discipline of one now-retired trial judge — is narrow and the case would
not rank among a Term's headline matters.
