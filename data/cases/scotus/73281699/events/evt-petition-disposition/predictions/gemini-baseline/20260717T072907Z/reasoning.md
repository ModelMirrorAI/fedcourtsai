## Legal Question
Whether content-based restrictions on the non-campaign political speech of sitting judges must satisfy strict scrutiny, or if they are governed by the *Pickering* balancing test for public employees.

## Governing Standard
Certiorari is rarely granted (base rate ~2.5% for paid petitions in recent Terms). The Court looks for deep, mature circuit/state supreme court splits on important questions of federal law, and clean vehicles that cleanly present the legal question without confounding factual or procedural issues.

## Case Specifics
The petitioner, Judge Cohen, was suspended by the Pennsylvania Court of Judicial Discipline (affirmed by the PA Supreme Court) for making dozens of partisan political posts on a Facebook page where he used a photo of himself in his judicial robe. Because he was nearing mandatory retirement, the court held he was not a candidate in an election and thus the strict scrutiny applied in *Williams-Yulee* and *White* for campaign speech did not apply. Instead, applying *Pickering*, it balanced his speech rights against the state's interest in judicial impartiality. The petition alleges a split of authority, pointing to the Fifth Circuit (*Jenevein*) and some state courts as applying strict scrutiny to sitting judges' non-campaign speech.

## Reasoning
This petition is highly likely to be denied. While the First Amendment rights of sitting judges is a significant and recurring constitutional question, this case is a remarkably poor vehicle to resolve the alleged split between *Pickering* and strict scrutiny. 

First, the petitioner prominently featured himself in his judicial robe on the Facebook page where he made the partisan posts. This overt use of the trappings and prestige of his judicial office heavily weights both the *Pickering* balance (favoring the state's interest in preserving the integrity and appearance of impartiality of the judiciary) and the strict scrutiny analysis. Even courts applying strict scrutiny, like the Fifth Circuit in *Jenevein*, recognize a compelling state interest in preventing judges from using their robes or courtrooms to boost personal messages. The Court of Judicial Discipline found he would lose even under strict scrutiny, meaning the choice of standard is unlikely to be outcome-determinative.

Second, the petitioner engaged in extensive, defiant conduct, continuing to post after explicit warnings from his supervisory judge. This factual context makes it an unattractive case for establishing a broad First Amendment precedent protective of judicial speech.

Third, the petitioner was facing imminent mandatory retirement at age 75, making his situation idiosyncratic and distancing the case from the typical scenario of an elected judge who might run again in the future.

Given the substantial vehicle problems and the fact that the petitioner's abuse of the prestige of his office would likely doom his claim under any standard, the Court is very unlikely to grant review, preferring to wait for a cleaner case to address the standard of scrutiny for off-bench judicial speech.
