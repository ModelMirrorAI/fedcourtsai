# Prediction: denial

I predict denial of certiorari, with an 18% probability of any grant. This is well above the ordinary paid-petition rate, but below the threshold at which a grant is the most likely disposition.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether content-based restrictions on a sitting judge's speech must satisfy strict scrutiny. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court instead applied the public-employee balancing framework of *Pickering v. Board of Education* to non-campaign speech by a sitting judge. The certiorari question is principally whether there is a sufficiently developed conflict among federal circuits and state courts of last resort, whether the issue is nationally important, and whether this record cleanly presents it.

The petition relies on *Republican Party of Minnesota v. White* and *Williams-Yulee v. Florida Bar*, which used strict scrutiny for restrictions on judicial-candidate speech. It says the Fifth Circuit and several state high courts apply strict scrutiny to sitting judges while the Seventh Circuit and other state courts use *Pickering* or related balancing. The decision below expressly recognized the disagreement and invited Supreme Court clarification. CourtListener research also confirmed that, after the Pennsylvania judgment, the Ohio Supreme Court applied strict scrutiny and invalidated a judicial anti-endorsement rule in *Disciplinary Counsel v. Rudduck*, 2026-Ohio-1126. That recent, closely related authority makes the conflict more concrete.

## Record-specific signals

The snapshot shows a paid petition docketed April 24, 2026. The respondent initially waived a response, but the Court requested one on May 12; the Judicial Conduct Board filed a full brief in opposition on July 13. A response request is a meaningful positive signal because at least one Justice or chambers saw enough potential interest to require adversarial briefing. There is not yet a relist or CVSG, and the petition had not returned to conference as of the snapshot.

The provisioned petition presents substantial First Amendment stakes. Judge Cohen was disciplined for 66 Facebook posts, and the Pennsylvania courts treated their volume and tone as partisan advocacy. His public page identified him as a judge and at one point used a robed photograph. The petition emphasizes that no individual post was found independently violative, the posts concerned public issues, and the state court applied a lower standard than strict scrutiny.

The provisioned brief in opposition identifies serious grant obstacles:

- The asserted split may be rule- and fact-dependent. Several strict-scrutiny decisions involved campaign speech, religious claims, legislative testimony, or did not consider *Pickering*; respondent also argues an earlier Fifth Circuit decision controls over the petition's cited panel decision.
- This case combines six conduct rules and varied conduct: partisan advocacy, an implied endorsement, comments touching potential cases, and use of judicial title or imagery. That makes it harder to isolate one constitutional rule.
- Cohen could not seek retention because mandatory retirement was imminent, weakening the electoral-speech rationale of *White*.
- The disciplinary court stated that the sanction would survive strict scrutiny because protecting actual and perceived judicial impartiality is compelling and Cohen used the trappings of office. That alternative ground creates a meaningful vehicle problem.
- The challenged suspension lasted only the final three months of Cohen's term, while the pension dispute is proceeding separately.

The recent Ohio decision cuts both ways. It sharpens the doctrinal disagreement and involves a sitting judge's Facebook endorsement on a page identifying him as a judge. But it also shows that a cleaner case focused on a single anti-endorsement rule may reach the Court, while Cohen's broader record permits affirmance regardless of the scrutiny label.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack estimates a 2.5% overall grant rate for 2025 Term petitions and about 5.4% for paid petitions. It also reports only a 0.8% grant rate for petitions with no relist and 3.0% where there has been no CVSG. Those figures are starting points rather than direct matches: the response request, an acknowledged conflict, and the new Ohio authority justify a large upward adjustment. The poor vehicle and alternative holding keep denial substantially more likely.

I therefore assign 18% to any grant and select `denied` as the most likely disposition. A plenary grant, rather than a GVR, would be the plausible grant-side outcome because no intervening Supreme Court decision supplies a new rule for reconsideration. I omit individual votes because cert-stage votes are not public and the record does not support Justice-specific estimates.

The case has a 0.74 big-case score. If decided, it could define the First Amendment scrutiny applicable to sitting judges nationwide and affect judicial-conduct rules across many elected-judiciary states, although its unusual disciplinary facts limit its broader public salience.
