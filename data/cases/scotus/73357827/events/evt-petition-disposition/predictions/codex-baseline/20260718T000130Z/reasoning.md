# Prediction: denied

I assign a 1.2% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. The forecast is based on the July 17, 2026 snapshot, the complete petition and extracted questions presented, the committed corpus statpack, one corpus-prior query, and pre-disposition CourtListener research. No brief in opposition was provisioned: the docket instead records respondents' waiver, and it does not record a call for response, CVSG, or relist as of the snapshot.

## Questions and posture

The petition asks whether HAVA creates rights privately enforceable through 42 U.S.C. § 1983 and whether the complete denial of HAVA's state administrative complaint process is a concrete Article III injury. Petitioners allege that the Wisconsin Elections Commission returned two HAVA complaints against itself without a hearing or merits determination. The Seventh Circuit affirmed dismissal for lack of standing, reasoning that the procedural deprivation lacked concrete harm and that the organizational expenditures were self-inflicted rather than interference with core business activity.

The certiorari standard favors a grant when a clean, outcome-determinative conflict exists on an important recurring federal question. Election administration and the availability of private enforcement are nationally important. The petition also identifies decisions from multiple circuits and says Wisconsin's procedure leaves complainants without a forum. Those considerations add some grant signal.

## Reasons denial is substantially more likely

The claimed split is not clean. The favorable First and Sixth Circuit authorities addressed different HAVA provisions containing more individual-focused language, including provisional-ballot and voter-registration requirements. The petition challenges the administrative-process provision, whose operative commands primarily tell a state what procedures it must establish. The contrary cases likewise arise from varying HAVA provisions and remedial settings. That makes the disagreement look provision-specific rather than a square conflict on the questions presented.

More importantly, *Medina v. Planned Parenthood South Atlantic*, 606 U.S. 357 (2025), holds that Spending Clause provisions support § 1983 enforcement only in the atypical case where Congress clearly and unambiguously uses rights-creating terms with an unmistakable focus on individuals. It describes this as a stringent, demanding test and says language addressing state duties, even when it benefits individuals, is insufficient. HAVA's requirement that a state establish and maintain complaint procedures fits poorly with that rule. The petition's reliance on earlier, more permissive circuit decisions therefore provides little reason for the Court to intervene, and the Seventh Circuit's result is directionally consistent with the current Court's approach.

The standing issue is also an unattractive vehicle. Petitioners seek review of a procedural injury without a separate concrete downstream harm, while *TransUnion* and *FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine* make that theory difficult. The petition bundles § 1983, Article III, Petition Clause, Spending Clause, and organizational-standing theories. Those alternative theories and the case-specific conflict-recusal facts create vehicle complications rather than a clean route to resolving one acknowledged split.

The docket signals are weak. Respondents waived a response, no response was requested before distribution, there is only one amicus brief, and the case had not been relisted or received a CVSG. Distribution for the long conference is ordinary and, by itself, is not affirmative interest.

## Calibration

The statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, but only 2.0% for modern cert petitions from the Seventh Circuit. Its no-relist bucket has a 0.8% grant rate and its no-CVSG bucket a 3.0% rate. The case-specific legal and docket weaknesses justify a forecast near the low end of those reference points. The corpus query's five recent granted priors all had at least three conference distributions, while this petition has only its initial distribution; that comparison is descriptive rather than dispositive.

I do not predict individual votes because cert votes are not public and the record does not support justice-specific estimates. The 0.55 big-case score reflects potentially nationwide consequences for HAVA enforcement if the Court reached the merits, moderated by the narrow procedural posture and Wisconsin-specific mechanism.
