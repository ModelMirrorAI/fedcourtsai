# Reasoning

## Case Context
This is a constitutional challenge brought by the Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA) against the Director of the Oregon Department of Consumer and Business Services. The petition challenges Oregon's HB 4005, a state transparency law requiring pharmaceutical manufacturers to report and justify their drug prices, and directing the state to publish those reports—even if they contain trade secrets—when deemed in the "public interest."

The Ninth Circuit upheld the law, holding that intermediate First Amendment scrutiny applies to government reporting requirements compelling the disclosure of product-specific information and that the law satisfies this scrutiny. The court also rejected the Takings Clause challenge, ruling that entities operating in highly regulated industries categorically lack reasonable investment-backed expectations in their trade secrets.

## Questions Presented
1. Whether a government reporting requirement is subject to intermediate First Amendment scrutiny, and satisfies such scrutiny, so long as it aims to correct "information asymmetries" that are "product-specific."
2. Whether entities that operate in "highly regulated" industries categorically lack reasonable investment-backed expectations in their trade secrets for purposes of the Takings Clause.

## Analysis
This case sits squarely at the intersection of several issues of intense interest to the current Supreme Court majority:
- **First Amendment Compelled Speech:** The Court has increasingly scrutinized compelled disclosures under the First Amendment (e.g., *NIFLA v. Becerra*, *303 Creative*). The petition alleges a circuit conflict regarding the application of intermediate scrutiny to such government-compelled disclosures.
- **Takings Clause & Property Rights:** The Ninth Circuit's sweeping conclusion that a highly regulated industry lacks investment-backed expectations in trade secrets conflicts strongly with the Supreme Court's protective stance on property rights, including its rejection of similar "highly regulated" carve-outs in *Horne v. Department of Agriculture* (raisins) and *Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid*.

The Supreme Court called for the views of the Solicitor General (CVSG) on June 22, 2026. A CVSG is a very strong signal of the Court's interest in potentially granting certiorari. According to the statpack, the average grant rate for a modern discretionary-cert docket petition with a CVSG is 27.1%, up significantly from the baseline ~3% rate. 

However, given the substantive alignment of the petition's questions with the current Court's conservative majority's jurisprudence—protecting property/trade secrets from uncompensated state appropriation and limiting compelled corporate speech—the probability of a grant here is much higher than the average CVSG case. Even if the Biden-Harris administration's Solicitor General recommends a denial (perhaps out of alignment with the state's goal of drug price transparency), the conservative wing of the Court is highly likely to vote to grant certiorari to correct the Ninth Circuit's categorical dismissals of the First Amendment and Takings Clause claims. The Ninth Circuit is frequently reviewed and reversed by this Court on both of these specific constitutional fronts.
