# Prediction

I predict that the petition will not be granted. The most likely recorded disposition is **dismissed**, with `P(granted) = 0.001`.

## Record and question presented

The baseline is the July 10, 2026 snapshot at `data/cases/scotus/73265707/record/snapshots/2026-07-10.json`. I also reviewed the provisioned, untruncated 14-page `record/documents/petition.txt`; no brief in opposition was provisioned.

The petition asks whether the Federal Arbitration Act and Supremacy Clause prevent enforcement of arbitration after litigation-conduct waiver, and whether compelling arbitration without mutual assent and then summarily denying mandamus relief violates due process. It alleges that the employer first invoked an unsigned arbitration document after the petitioner's termination and after litigation began.

## Procedural posture

The strongest predictor is procedural. The Supreme Court denied leave to proceed in forma pauperis on June 29 and gave the pro se petitioner until July 20 to pay the Rule 38(a) docketing fee and submit a Rule 33.1-compliant petition. As of the snapshot, neither step appears on the docket. Failure to comply ordinarily leads to dismissal, and the cost and specialized printing requirements make noncompliance a substantial risk for a pro se litigant who sought IFP status.

Even if the petitioner timely cures those defects, certiorari is very unlikely. The challenged state proceedings are summary denials of mandamus concerning an interlocutory arbitration order. That posture creates serious finality and vehicle problems under 28 U.S.C. § 1257(a), and the absence of a reasoned state-court decision makes it difficult to identify a square holding on either federal question.

## Merits and certworthiness

The petition identifies no conflict among federal courts or state courts. Its waiver theory turns on the sequence and significance of case-specific conduct. *Morgan v. Sundance* rejects an arbitration-specific prejudice rule, but it does not establish that participation in EEOC proceedings or a short litigation delay necessarily waives arbitration. The mutual-assent issue likewise depends on the underlying document and state contract law, making this a poor vehicle for a general FAA rule.

The due-process theory is particularly weak. Summary appellate or mandamus denials do not ordinarily create a constitutional entitlement to a written explanation. *Michigan v. Long* addresses how the Supreme Court treats ambiguity about independent and adequate state grounds; it does not generally require state courts to issue reasoned decisions.

The committed statpack reports a 1.4% grant share among its limited set of resolved SCOTUS petitions, but its promised modern discretionary-cert table is absent, so I treated that number only as a loose check. The combination of a pro se IFP posture, no respondent brief, no developed split, fact-bound claims, and substantial jurisdictional and vehicle defects warrants a probability well below even the already-low ordinary certiorari rate.

## Disposition and votes

I assign dismissal the largest individual probability because the fee and Rule 33.1 cure deadline remains outstanding. Denial would be the likely alternative if the petitioner complies. A dismissal for noncompliance would be administrative, and a later cert denial would not disclose individual votes, so I do not predict justice-by-justice votes.
