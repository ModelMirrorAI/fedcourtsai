# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with a 0.6% probability of any grant (including a GVR).

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether a juror who was a victim of the same type of crime as the charged offense is impliedly biased when she does not unequivocally say she can be fair. The petition grounds the doctrine in the Sixth Amendment right to an impartial jury, this Court's pre-*Smith* decisions, and Justice O'Connor's concurrence in *Smith v. Phillips*, 455 U.S. 209 (1982). In the underlying ineffective-assistance claim, Pearson must also establish deficient performance and prejudice under *Strickland v. Washington*. Because this is federal habeas review of a state conviction, 28 U.S.C. § 2254(d) adds the demanding requirement that the state adjudication contradict or unreasonably apply clearly established Supreme Court law.

## Record and merits signals

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition say that Juror 27 disclosed after voir dire, but before challenges and strikes, that she had been molested as a child and "thought" she could be fair. Counsel did not question or remove her. The petition argues that similar-crime victim status, an equivocal assurance, and delayed private disclosure together establish implied bias. It identifies differing results among several circuits and tension within the Fifth Circuit.

Those facts make the legal issue nonfrivolous, but the petition does not show a crisp rule conflict. It acknowledges that no circuit treats similar-crime victim status as automatically disqualifying; the cited decisions instead turn on combinations of similarity, elapsed time, nondisclosure or dishonesty, ongoing trauma, and assurances of impartiality. Here the juror voluntarily disclosed the experience, and the record contains no details tying her experience to the charged conduct beyond both involving childhood sexual abuse. The Fifth Circuit's unpublished per curiam opinion applied its own closely analogous precedent and treated the case as outside the doctrine's exceptional category.

The vehicle is especially weak. The Court would have to address an unsettled doctrine derived principally from a concurrence through both *Strickland* and AEDPA deference. Even a prospective clarification of implied bias may not establish that the state court unreasonably applied law clearly established when it ruled. The case is also fact-bound, has no noted dissent, and the snapshot shows only an initial distribution for the September 28, 2026 conference. No brief in opposition was provisioned, and the docket snapshot shows no CVSG or relist signal.
