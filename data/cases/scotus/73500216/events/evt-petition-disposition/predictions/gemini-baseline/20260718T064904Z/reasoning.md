# Reasoning

**Legal Question**: Whether a juror who has been the victim of the same type of crime as the charged offense is impliedly biased where she does not unequivocally state that she can be fair and impartial.

**Governing Standard**: Since this is a federal habeas petition from a state conviction (Fifth Circuit, affirming denial of habeas), AEDPA (28 U.S.C. § 2254(d)(1)) applies. Relief can only be granted if the state court unreasonably applied "clearly established Federal law."

**Analysis**: While there is a circuit split on the existence and scope of "implied bias" following Justice O'Connor's concurrence in *Smith v. Phillips*, 455 U.S. 209 (1982), the Supreme Court has never explicitly adopted the implied bias doctrine in a majority opinion that would clearly establish it for AEDPA purposes. Because of this, an AEDPA case is a very poor vehicle for resolving the circuit split, as the petitioner would likely lose on the threshold AEDPA deference standard regardless of the merits of the implied bias doctrine. Furthermore, the docket shows that no response was filed by the respondent (Texas), and the Court distributed the petition for the long conference (9/28/2026) without requesting a response. Under Supreme Court rules and practice, the Court will not grant a petition without a response. Given the AEDPA posture and the lack of a requested response, this petition is highly likely to be denied.
