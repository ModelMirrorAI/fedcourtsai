# Prediction

I predict that the Court will deny certiorari. I assign a 14% probability to any grant and an 86% probability to no grant. If the Court grants, a plenary grant is much more likely than a GVR because there is no identified intervening authority or case-specific development that would support vacatur and remand.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether a claims-administration contract is a “contract … under which the plan is established or operated” that an ERISA plan administrator must disclose on request under 29 U.S.C. § 1024(b)(4). At the certiorari stage, the important considerations are whether the lower courts are genuinely divided on that recurring federal question, whether this case cleanly presents it, and whether four Justices would consider plenary review warranted. The forecast treats the case as pending based on the forward-mode context and the July 16, 2026 snapshot.

## Record and merits signals

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition frame a facially substantial conflict. The Seventh Circuit's *Mondry* decision and the Tenth Circuit's recent, published *M. S. v. Premera Blue Cross* decision required disclosure of claims- or plan-administration agreements. The Ninth Circuit relied on its en banc *Hughes* formulation—documents must inform participants about the plan and benefits—to affirm nondisclosure. The statute expressly names a “contract,” and the agreements allocate claims-administration functions, giving the petition a plausible textual argument. CourtListener research confirmed the published 2024 Tenth Circuit decision and its holding that an administrative services agreement was disclosable. The docket's request for a response after Netflix initially waived one is also a meaningful interest signal.

The vehicle objections nevertheless dominate. The Ninth Circuit disposition is an unpublished memorandum, so it does not establish a binding categorical rule that claims-administration agreements are never disclosable. It applied *Hughes* to these four agreements after the district court made detailed document-specific findings. The lower courts found that the agreements governed Netflix's relationships with service providers, while the separately produced plan documents controlled participant benefits and claims procedures. That makes the petition's broader premise—that the requested contracts governed operation of the plan in the legally relevant sense—contestable on the record.

Netflix also persuasively distinguishes *Mondry* and *Premera*: both arose from actual benefit denials and document requests linked to those denials, while Zavislak's request was abstract; *Premera* was decided on a record that did not contain the complete agreement, whereas this case involved agreement-by-agreement findings reviewed partly for clear error. The Ninth Circuit denied rehearing without a recorded dissent. The snapshot identifies no amicus filings, relist, or invitation for the Solicitor General's views. Finally, ERISA's separate claims-review disclosure mechanism reduces the practical stakes where a participant needs documents relevant to a concrete benefit denial.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025 Term petitions, a 3.0% grant rate for modern petitions from the Ninth Circuit, a 3.0% rate without a CVSG, and only 0.8% before any relist. Those cuts overlap and are not multiplied. The response request, the recent published Tenth Circuit authority, and the facially direct statutory issue justify moving materially above those raw anchors. The unpublished and fact-bound decision, lack of a clean binding conflict, and vehicle distinctions keep the forecast well below an even chance and below the range I would use for a clean, entrenched circuit split.
