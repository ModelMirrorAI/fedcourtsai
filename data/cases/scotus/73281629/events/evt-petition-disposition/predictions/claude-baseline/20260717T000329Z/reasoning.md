# Zavislak v. Netflix, Inc., No. 25-1142 — cert disposition prediction

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.11.**

## The legal question

Whether a claims administration agreement (CAA) — the contract between a plan
sponsor/administrator and a third-party claims administrator — is a "contract
… under which the plan is established or operated" that ERISA § 104(b)(4), 29
U.S.C. § 1024(b)(4), obliges the administrator to furnish on a participant's
written request. The Ninth Circuit (unpublished memorandum, Sept. 24, 2025;
M. Smith, Bumatay, Barker (D.J.)) applied its en banc *Hughes* "information
test" and held Netflix's four CAAs need not be disclosed; rehearing en banc
was denied without recorded dissent, and the Ninth Circuit stayed its mandate
pending certiorari.

## Signals from the provisioned record

**For grant:**

- **Call for response.** Netflix waived; after the petition was distributed
  for the 5/1/2026 conference, the Court requested a response (Apr. 24, 2026).
  A CFR means at least one chambers took an interest, and it is the single
  strongest positive signal on this docket. Paid petitions with a CFR grant at
  roughly an order of magnitude above the paid baseline (mid-teens percent in
  the empirical literature), though the large majority are still denied.
- **A real, recent conflict on the specific document type.** *Mondry* (7th
  Cir. 2009) and *M.S. v. Premera Blue Cross* (10th Cir. 2024, published,
  first impression) squarely hold claims-administration / administrative-
  services agreements disclosable under § 1024(b)(4); the decision below holds
  the opposite for materially similar contracts. The petition also plausibly
  frames a broader 9–1 methodological split (First, Second, Fourth, Fifth,
  Sixth, Seventh, Eighth, Tenth, Eleventh Circuits on a "governing documents"
  text-based test vs. the Ninth's *Hughes* information test), with *Faircloth*
  (4th Cir.) and *Premera* (10th Cir.) expressly criticizing Ninth Circuit
  reasoning.
- **Clean legal question.** Facts undisputed after a paper bench trial; the
  Ninth Circuit affirmed on the single legal ground; reversal would be
  dispositive. Petitioner's anti-percolation point (administrators in the
  other circuits comply rather than litigate, so the split will not develop
  further) is a fair one.
- **Mandate stayed pending certiorari** by the Ninth Circuit (Nov. 7, 2025) —
  a mild signal that the court below saw a substantial question.
- Paid (counseled) petition, timely BIO fight — this is in the seriously
  considered pool, not the IFP noise.

**Against grant:**

- **The decision below is unpublished and nonprecedential.** The BIO's lead
  point is strong: the Ninth Circuit has never held in a published opinion
  that CAAs are outside § 104(b)(4), so a future panel remains free to align
  with *Mondry*/*Premera*. The Court is reluctant to grant on an unpublished
  memorandum, both because the "split" is soft-edged and because it signals
  the court below saw the case as factbound.
- **Factbound vehicle problems.** The ruling rests on document-by-document
  factual findings (reviewed for clear error) that these particular CAAs
  govern only the Netflix–TPA relationship; *Mondry* and *Premera* arose from
  denied benefits claims, while Zavislak's request was untethered to any claim
  — a distinction the BIO exploits and that gives the Court an easy pass.
- **Contested importance.** The BIO's § 503 / full-and-fair-review point
  (claim-denial regulations independently guarantee access to documents relied
  on in a denial) undercuts the practical stakes; the underlying judgment was
  a $765 penalty, vacated.
- **No cert-stage amici.** For a petition claiming the rule "destabilizes 4.1
  million plans," the absence of any amicus support (no Chamber, no ERISA
  plaintiffs' bar, no AARP) as of the July 15 distribution is telling.
- **Asymmetric advocacy.** Respondent's BIO is by Aaron Streett (Baker Botts),
  an experienced Supreme Court advocate; petitioner's counsel is a small
  Bellingham firm without a SCOTUS practice.
- **Base rates.** Statpack modern discretionary-cert baseline: grants are a
  few percent of resolved petitions (Term 2024 ≈ 3.0%, Term 2025 ≈ 2.5%
  estimated; CA9-originating ≈ 3.0%). Zero relists so far (the case simply
  has not reached its conference); the September 28, 2026 "long conference"
  itself grants at a below-average clip.

## Weighing

Anchor: paid, counseled, split-alleging CA9 petition with a CFR — conditional
grant probability in the low-to-mid teens. Adjust down for the unpublished
nonprecedential decision below, the factbound/clear-error posture, the
claim-untethered request distinguishing *Mondry*/*Premera*, the absence of
amici, and a strong BIO; adjust up modestly for the genuinely crisp statutory
question, the published *Premera* conflict from 2024, the anti-percolation
argument, and the mandate stay. A CVSG or a relist string out of the long
conference is plausible (the CFR shows a chambers is engaged), but the modal
path remains denial — likely after the 9/28/2026 conference or a short relist.
No intervening decision makes a GVR path essentially irrelevant here.

**P(grant, incl. GVR) ≈ 0.11 → predicted disposition: denied** (granted = 0).
