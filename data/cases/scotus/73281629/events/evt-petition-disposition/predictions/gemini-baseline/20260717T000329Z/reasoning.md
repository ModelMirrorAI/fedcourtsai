# Reasoning: Mark Zavislak v. Netflix, Inc. (25-1142)

## The Legal Question
The petitioner asks whether a contract governing the administration of benefit claims is a "contract ... under which the plan is established or operated" within the meaning of ERISA § 1024(b)(4), requiring its disclosure upon written request.

## The Record
The petition alleges a 9-1 circuit split, asserting the Ninth Circuit stands alone in applying an "atextual information test" that allows plan administrators to withhold governing contracts if they do not provide individual participants with information about the plan and benefits. The respondent's Brief in Opposition counters that the circuits apply the same substantive test, there is no split over claims administration agreements categorically, and the Ninth Circuit merely affirmed a fact-bound conclusion by the district court after a bench trial that these specific four agreements did not fall within the disclosure obligation. Additionally, the decision below is an unpublished memorandum (No. 24-4156, 9th Cir. 2025). 

## Analysis
While the petition was distributed for conference, Netflix initially waived its right to respond, and the Court subsequently issued a Call For Response (CFR), which indicates the petition garnered some initial interest. However, a CFR does not overcome the significant vehicle problems. The decision below is an unpublished memorandum affirming a factual finding after a bench trial. The Supreme Court typically grants certiorari to resolve clean, legal splits on outcome-determinative issues, often avoiding unpublished dispositions where the lower court did not establish binding circuit precedent. The BIO convincingly frames the split as illusory or, at minimum, heavily fact-dependent. 

## Conclusion
Given the base rates for paid cert petitions (around 2.5-3.0% in recent terms) and the fact that the lower court opinion is unpublished and characterized as fact-bound by the BIO, a grant is highly unlikely despite the CFR. The probability of a grant is low.

**Predicted Disposition:** Denied
**Probability:** 5%
