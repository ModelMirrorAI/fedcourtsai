# City of Cleveland, Ohio v. Pickett (No. 25-677) — petition disposition

**Prediction: dismissed (P(any grant) = 0.07, granted = 0).**

## The case

Cleveland Water places "water liens" (certified to the county auditor) on
properties with water bills ≥ $300 delinquent ≥ 180 days. Respondents — Black
homeowners represented by the NAACP Legal Defense Fund — brought an FHA
disparate-impact class action alleging the race-neutral policy
disproportionately burdens majority-Black neighborhoods (~68% of ~17,000 liens
on majority-Black-neighborhood homes in a 29%-Black county). The district
court certified a Rule 23(b)(3) damages class; the Sixth Circuit affirmed on
Rule 23(f) interlocutory review (140 F.4th 300), holding every class member
has Article III standing because FHA racial-discrimination harm is a
cognizable intangible injury "comparable to traditional harms found in the
Constitution," and denied rehearing en banc.

## The legal question

The petition (Gibson Dunn; Thomas Dupree) presents the question TransUnion
reserved (594 U.S. at 431 n.4) and the Court granted-then-DIG'd in *LabCorp v.
Davis*, 605 U.S. 327 (2025) (Kavanaugh, J., dissenting from the DIG): whether
a damages class may be certified when it contains members who lack any injury
beyond the intangible disparate impact of a race-neutral policy. It also
invokes the *Acheson Hotels* split (statutory violation without downstream
harm). The petition argues a three-way circuit split (five circuits bar
certification with uninjured members, four allow it, the Sixth Circuit now
carving a claim-specific middle path) and that the Sixth Circuit's rule defies
*TransUnion* and *Washington v. Davis*.

## Grant-side signals (from the pre-decision record)

- The Court called for a response on Jan 12, 2026 after respondents waived —
  a CFR is a meaningful step above the ordinary paid petition.
- Three cert-stage amici (Washington Legal Foundation, American Water Works
  Association, Ohio Municipal League/IMLA).
- The Court demonstrably wants to decide this question: it granted LabCorp
  just last Term and lost the vehicle; Justice Kavanaugh dissented from the
  DIG urging resolution.
- Elite counsel on both sides; Rule 23(f) posture cleanly isolates the
  certification issue.

## Deny-side / vehicle problems

The BIO (NAACP LDF) makes a serious vehicle attack that mirrors exactly what
killed LabCorp: the "uninjured members" premise is disputed. The "up to 20%
uninjured" figure comes from expert testimony that one of five *named
plaintiffs* (a 20% "sample") paid no penalties/interest — and even she
allegedly suffered increased mortgage payments. Respondents also assert an
independent, class-wide *tangible* injury: the lien itself is an encumbrance
on title (Connecticut v. Doehr). If every class member is arguably injured,
this case poses the same antecedent-factual-dispute problem that produced the
LabCorp DIG. The interlocutory posture and the Sixth Circuit's express
refusal to decide the class-standing question add first-view concerns.

## The dispositive development: settlement

The docket shows the petition was distributed for the 1/23, 5/14, 5/21, and
5/28/2026 conferences but **rescheduled repeatedly and never acted on**, and
on May 29, 2026 the parties filed a **joint motion to defer consideration of
the petition** (the motion PDF is dated May 7, 2026). Pre-snapshot public
reporting (Cleveland Scene, May 7, 2026) confirms why: Cleveland agreed to a
**$3 million settlement** ($1.8M to the class, $1.2M fees, $14K per named
plaintiff), announced by the City's Law Director in district court, with
details being finalized. The joint deferral motion is the classic
settlement-pending posture; once the class settlement receives Rule 23(e)
approval, the petition is dismissed under Supreme Court Rule 46 (or
withdrawn). A corpus prior fits the pattern: *Elephant Insurance Co. v.
Holmes* (25-1085), a paid class-action petition dismissed in May 2026 without
conference action following settlement below.

## Probability reasoning

Base rates (committed statpack, modern discretionary-cert slice): overall
grant ~2.5–3% per recent Term; CA6-originating ~3.5%; CFR and multiple
distributions would ordinarily lift a paid petition like this well above
baseline (2-relist bucket grants at ~34%). Absent the settlement I would have
put P(grant) near 0.30–0.40 given the CFR, amici, and the Court's demonstrated
appetite for the question, tempered by the LabCorp-style vehicle dispute.

But the settlement dominates the forecast:

- P(settlement completes → Rule 46 dismissal/withdrawal) ≈ 0.80. A joint
  deferral motion plus an on-the-record commitment by the City's Law Director
  means agreement in principle; class settlements at this stage rarely
  collapse. Fairness-hearing rejection or council balking is the residual
  risk.
- P(settlement fails → petition returns to conference) ≈ 0.20, with
  conditional P(grant) ≈ 0.35 (strong signals vs. real vehicle flaws) →
  unconditional P(grant) ≈ 0.07.
- Remainder → denied ≈ 0.13 (includes the Court simply denying once informed
  the dispute is resolving).

**predicted_disposition: dismissed; granted = 0; probability = 0.07.**
