# Prediction: denied

I assign a 38% probability to any grant and predict denial as the most likely disposition. If the Court does intervene, a plenary grant on the police-power exception is substantially more likely than a GVR: no intervening Supreme Court decision identified in the pre-conference record supplies a clear rule for a summary remand.

## Question and governing certiorari considerations

The petition asks whether the Takings Clause categorically excludes property damage caused through the police power, and more specifically whether government must compensate an innocent homeowner when officers intentionally destroy property while trying to apprehend a fugitive. At the certiorari stage, the principal considerations are whether the lower courts are genuinely divided on a recurring federal question, whether this case cleanly presents that conflict, and whether further percolation or vehicle defects counsel denial.

The petition has unusually strong grant signals. The Seventh Circuit treated its precedent as controlling and adhered to a categorical police-power exception. The petition identifies the Tenth and Federal Circuits on that side and the Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Eleventh Circuits as rejecting that categorical rule. The later cases have not produced convergence: the Fifth and Ninth Circuits use a public-necessity framework, the Sixth Circuit uses a search-and-arrest privilege, and the Eleventh Circuit is described as rejecting a necessity exception. The petition also notes that Justices Sotomayor and Gorsuch previously called the issue important and complex when certiorari was denied in *Baker v. City of McKinney*. The Rule 12(b)(6) posture is helpful because the Seventh Circuit expressly declined to conduct the alternative takings analysis once it applied its categorical rule. Multiple amicus briefs and the concurrently filed *Pena* petition further indicate organized interest and give the Court options for addressing the issue.

## Record-specific strengths and weaknesses

The facts alleged in the petition make the constitutional issue concrete and sympathetic. Officers mistakenly traced a fugitive's activity to Amy Hadley's home; her son exited, the door was open, and Hadley told officers the fugitive had no connection to the family. Officers nonetheless fired dozens of tear-gas canisters, broke windows and cameras, and damaged the interior. The fugitive was not there, the house was temporarily uninhabitable, the alleged loss was about $16,000, insurance covered only part of it, and Hadley received no government compensation. Those facts support the petition's contention that the case is about deliberately imposing a public burden on an innocent owner, not merely incidental entry damage.

The brief in opposition identifies meaningful reasons to deny. It argues that the split concerns reasoning rather than outcomes because every circuit would reject compensation for property damage reasonably caused during a lawful search. It emphasizes Hadley's asserted concessions concerning the warrant and Fourth Amendment reasonableness, relies on historical search privileges, and contends that her proposed rule depends on difficult distinctions involving innocence, connection to the suspect, and incidental versus severe damage. The lower court's failure to decide the alternative frameworks is a clean feature for reviewing the categorical exception, but it also means a victory for Hadley may only produce a remand into those unresolved defenses. The Court denied *Baker* despite two Justices' interest, and the existence of several recently developed approaches may lead some Justices to prefer more percolation or a different vehicle.
