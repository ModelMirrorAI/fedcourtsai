# Reasoning for Petition Disposition in scotus/73281642 (Hadley v. City of South Bend)

**Legal Question & Context:**
The petition presents a deeply entrenched circuit split over the interpretation of the Fifth Amendment's Takings Clause: specifically, whether there is a categorical "police-power exception" that exempts the government from paying just compensation when law enforcement officers intentionally destroy an innocent person's property while trying to apprehend a fugitive. 

The Seventh, Tenth (see *Lech v. Jackson*), and Federal Circuits hold that such actions fall under the police power, thus requiring no compensation. In contrast, the Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Eleventh Circuits have rejected a categorical police-power exception for innocent property owners. 

**Facts & Record:**
In this case, the South Bend Police Department raided the home of the petitioner (Amy Hadley) based on a mistaken belief that a murder suspect had accessed her IP address. The suspect was not there and had no connection to the homeowner. The police caused over $16,000 in damage, for which the city refused to compensate her. The Seventh Circuit affirmed the dismissal of her Takings claim under its precedent. The petition was filed in April 2026, and the case has been fully briefed and distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference.

**Analysis & Probability (Grant: 70%):**
While the base rate for a certiorari grant is extremely low (~3.1% for modern discretionary petitions), this petition presents a textbook case for Supreme Court review:
1. **Mature Circuit Split:** The split is acknowledged, deep, and clearly implicated by the facts. 
2. **Prior Interest:** In November 2024, the Supreme Court denied certiorari in a similar case out of the Fifth Circuit (*Baker v. City of McKinney*). In a statement respecting the denial, Justices Sotomayor and Gorsuch explicitly noted the importance of the issue and suggested the Court might simply be waiting for further percolation. 
3. **Clean Vehicle:** The Brief in Opposition raises no procedural hurdles, waiver, or interlocutory issues. It primarily argues the merits of the petitioner's proposed standard.
4. **Amicus Support:** The petition is supported by five distinct amicus briefs, representing a broad ideological spectrum (e.g., Pacific Legal Foundation, property rights institutes, and law enforcement figures), signaling the high salience of the issue.

Given the clear split, the prior signaling from two Justices that they are monitoring this exact issue, and the strong organized backing by the Institute for Justice and amici, this petition is highly likely to be granted.
