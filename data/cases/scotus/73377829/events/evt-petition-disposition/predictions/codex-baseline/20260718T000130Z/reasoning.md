# Prediction: denied

I assign a 16% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. This is well above the ordinary paid-petition baseline because the Court requested a response after the federal respondents waived one, but the petition has unusually serious vehicle and issue-preservation problems and identifies no square conflict.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether the Fifth Amendment permits federal judiciary employees to be confined to the judiciary's internal Employment Dispute Resolution (EDR) system rather than receive protections comparable to Title VII, and whether a circuit EDR plan that falls short of Title VII principles violates the Congressional Accountability Act (CAA) or Judicial Conference policy.

The lower-court materials in the provisioned petition frame the due-process claim around whether the EDR plan created a protected property interest and whether the available Chapter X hearing provided constitutionally adequate process. Ordinarily a claimant must use available procedures unless they are unavailable or patently inadequate. The court of appeals held that the promised Chapter X evidentiary hearing offered discovery, evidence, cross-examination, a neutral judicial decisionmaker, and review, and that Strickland withdrew before using it. Her equal-protection theories also depended on fact-bound findings about deliberate indifference, discriminatory intent, and the reasonableness of officials' response. The CAA provision quoted by the petition directed the Judicial Conference to study judicial-branch coverage and make recommendations; Congress deliberately did not extend Title VII itself to judiciary employees.

## Grant signals

- The strongest signal is procedural: after the federal respondents waived a response, the Court requested one. That indicates at least some chambers-level interest and justifies moving materially above the base rate. The response is now due August 14, 2026, so no brief in opposition was available in the July 17 snapshot.
- The issue is important. The petition says the judiciary employs more than 30,000 people, and a ruling could affect the independence, remedies, and anti-retaliation safeguards available to all of them. The district court and court of appeals acknowledged flaws, delay, confidentiality problems, inadequate training, and structural imperfections in the process used here.
- The case produced a published appellate decision after a six-day bench trial, all Fourth Circuit judges recused, and an outside panel heard the appeal. An amicus filing by women's-lawyer and legal-accountability organizations was docketed before the snapshot. Those features add institutional salience.

## Denial signals

- The petition's reasons-for-granting section does not identify a circuit split. Its cited Second and Ninth Circuit EDR decisions establish background principles but do not appear to create a square conflict over the broad questions presented. The petition principally argues importance and error correction.
- The judgment rests on an extensive, case-specific trial record. Strickland voluntarily left the EDR process before the Chapter X hearing, yet the plan covered former employees and allowed remedies including reinstatement. The court of appeals found no proof that the eventual decisionmaker would be biased, that the hearing was blocked or a sham, or that officials deliberately coerced withdrawal.
- The broad Title VII-exclusion argument was raised for the first time in the appeal from final judgment. The court of appeals expressly declined to reach it, finding no exceptional circumstance. That makes the first question, as broadly drafted, a poor vehicle.
- The second question is also poorly preserved. The current appellate judgment did not decide that the CAA itself creates an enforceable requirement that every EDR plan duplicate Title VII. The statutory language reproduced in the petition is a reporting-and-recommendation directive, while the petition acknowledges that Congress chose not to extend the CAA's employment protections to the judiciary.
- The challenged events arose under the Fourth Circuit's 2013 EDR plan, which the record says was revised in 2020 in response to a new model plan. That weakens the prospective need to review this particular plan and invites disputes over whether the case cleanly presents current practice.
- There has been no relist or CVSG. A response request is significant, but four votes are still required and the government's forthcoming opposition may sharpen the threshold defects.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025-Term petitions, a 4.6% grant rate for modern petitions from the Fourth Circuit, a 0.8% grant rate for petitions with no relist, and a 3.0% rate where no CVSG has occurred. Those cuts are not independent and the statpack has no response-request cut. I therefore use the requested response as a substantial upward adjustment, then discount for the absence of a conflict, the obsolete-plan posture, failure to complete the available process, and issues the court below did not reach. A plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR because no intervening authority or mootness event appears in the record, but denial remains much likelier than either.

The provisioned petition was marked truncated at 388 pages, but it included the questions presented, reasons for granting, relevant merits discussion, and lower-court opinions needed for this forecast. CourtListener opinion searches confirmed the small set of recurring EDR authorities; attempts to fetch their full text were throttled, so I relied on the provisioned petition's treatment of those authorities. The corpus lookups supplied broad modern SCOTUS priors rather than close factual analogues.

## Significance

I score the case 0.78 for significance if decided. It concerns the judiciary's own handling of sex-harassment and discrimination complaints, institutional independence, and remedies for a nationwide workforce. That is consequential and potentially newsworthy even though the cert vehicle is weak.
