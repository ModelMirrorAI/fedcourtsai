# Strickland v. Moritz, No. 25-1303 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.07.**

## The case

Caryn Devins Strickland, a former assistant federal public defender in the
Western District of North Carolina, sued judicial-branch officials (the Chair
of the Judicial Conference Committee on Judicial Resources, the AO Director,
the Fourth Circuit's Chief Judge and Circuit Executive, and the Federal Public
Defender) over the judiciary's handling of her sexual-harassment complaint
through the internal Employment Dispute Resolution (EDR) process. Judiciary
employees are excluded from Title VII; the EDR Plan is the substitute. After a
2022 Fourth Circuit decision let her Fifth Amendment due-process and
equal-protection claims proceed (32 F.4th 311), the district court held a
six-day bench trial and ruled for the defendants (744 F. Supp. 3d 477), and a
panel of out-of-circuit judges (all Fourth Circuit judges recused) affirmed
(149 F.4th 378, Aug. 15, 2025; rehearing en banc denied Dec. 18, 2025).

The questions presented: (1) whether the Fifth Amendment's due-process and
equal-protection guarantees permit the judiciary to route its employees'
harassment and discrimination claims through an internal system lacking an
independent decisionmaker, meaningful procedural safeguards, and adequate
remedies; and (2) whether an EDR Plan that does not comport with Title VII's
fundamental principles violates the Congressional Accountability Act and
Judicial Conference policy.

## Base rates and priors

From the committed statpack (live/historical slice, denial-reweighted): the
modern discretionary-cert grant rate is ~2.5–3.3% overall; **paid** petitions
grant at ~5.4% (Term 2025) / ~6.9% (Term 2024). Relists are the strongest
observable pre-grant signal (0 relists → 0.8% grant; 2 relists → ~34%);
this docket has none yet. Corpus priors on recently granted 2020s petitions
(`fedcourts query`) consistently show multiple conference distributions and
repeat SCOTUS-bar counsel — neither present here yet.

A call for a response after a government waiver historically multiplies the
grant odds several-fold over the paid baseline (roughly into the 8–15% range),
because clerks request BIOs only when a petition clears the first cut. That is
the single strongest factor in this case's favor and is why my probability
sits above the paid base rate.

## Why denial is still much more likely

1. **No circuit split.** The petition's own "reasons for granting" section
   argues importance and recurrence, not a split — and the authority it
   discusses (Dotson v. Griesa, 2d Cir.) *rejected* comparable claims, i.e.,
   the circuits that have spoken are in rough harmony with the decision below.
   Importance-only petitions rarely succeed.
2. **Fact-bound, as-applied posture.** After a full bench trial, both courts
   below found the EDR process — though repeatedly described as "flawed" —
   constitutionally adequate as applied. The 2022 Fourth Circuit decision
   already held the Plan not facially invalid. Reviewing concurrent
   fact-laden holdings is error correction, which the Court avoids.
3. **Acute institutional awkwardness.** The respondents are sitting federal
   judges and judiciary administrators sued in their official capacities; the
   challenged EDR framework flows from the Judicial Conference, which the
   Chief Justice presides over and whose post-2017 workplace-conduct reforms
   he personally initiated (the petition quotes his 2017 Year-End Report).
   Every Fourth Circuit judge was recused below. The Court has historically
   left judiciary-workplace disputes to the Judicial Conference and Congress
   (where the Judiciary Accountability Act has repeatedly stalled), and
   granting would put the Justices in the position of judging their own
   branch's HR system.
4. **Weak second question.** CAA § 1434 required the Judicial Conference to
   *report* to Congress; a claim that an EDR Plan "violates" that statute or
   Judicial Conference policy has no evident private right of action and
   drew no split.
5. **Doctrinal headwinds.** The current Court is unreceptive to expanding
   procedural-due-process property-interest theories or implied remedies
   against federal officials; Davis v. Passman (1979), the petition's best
   analogy, sits in a Bivens line the Court has spent a decade narrowing.

No intervening decision suggests a GVR path, so the grant probability is
essentially plenary-grant probability. A denial with a statement or dissent
from denial (e.g., Justices Sotomayor or Jackson) is a realistic decoration
on the modal outcome.

## Bottom line

Netting the strong CFR signal against the absence of a split, the fact-bound
posture, and the institutional conflict, I put P(grant) at **0.07** — roughly
the paid-petition baseline nudged up for the CFR and amicus interest, then
discounted for the case-specific headwinds. Predicted disposition: **denied**,
most likely in early October 2026 after the long conference.

## Stakes (big_case_score = 0.6)

This is the most prominent judiciary-workplace-harassment litigation in the
country — first of its kind to survive dismissal, national press, GAO and
congressional attention, dozens of institutional amici below. If granted it
would be a blockbuster about the judiciary policing itself; even as a denial
it will be covered. Scored on stakes, not odds.
