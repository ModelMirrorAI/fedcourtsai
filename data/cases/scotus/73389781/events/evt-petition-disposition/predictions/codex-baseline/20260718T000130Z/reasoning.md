# Prediction: denied

I assign a 2.8% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. This is a forward prediction based on the July 17, 2026 snapshot, the petition, its extracted questions presented, the brief in opposition, pre-disposition legal research, and the committed corpus statpack. I did not seek or encounter this case's disposition.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether the Fourteenth Amendment permits incarceration through civil contempt for failure to pay a private property-damage judgment without an adequate inquiry and express finding of present ability to pay. It also challenges placing the burden of showing inability on the debtor and allowing opposing counsel to submit an incarceration order after a missed installment.

The core merits authorities are *Bearden v. Georgia* and *Turner v. Rogers*. They require meaningful consideration of ability to pay before nonpayment results in incarceration; *Turner* identifies notice, an opportunity to provide financial information, and an express ability-to-pay finding as safeguards against erroneous deprivation of liberty. The petition plausibly argues that those protections should apply at least as strongly to collection of a private civil debt as to child-support contempt.

## Record and certworthiness

The snapshot shows a paid petition from the Mississippi Court of Appeals, filed May 1, 2026, opposed June 22, and distributed July 8 for the September 28 conference. It shows no relist, CVSG, or amicus support. The petition says the chancery court imposed six months' incarceration, suspended on a $7,135 payment and later installments, made no express finding of present ability to pay, and permitted respondent's counsel to submit an incarceration order automatically after default. Two Mississippi Supreme Court justices voted to hear the state petition. Those details give the case a real liberty interest and some salience.

The brief in opposition, however, exposes substantial vehicle problems. It says petitioner had counsel and a full evidentiary hearing; the chancery court found that she was able to work and willfully remained unemployed; the appellate court found sufficient evidence of present ability to pay; no incarceration occurred; inability remained a defense before any future commitment; and the federal due-process and equal-protection theories were not adequately preserved in the state courts. These assertions make the dispute turn heavily on the record and on competing characterizations of the state courts' findings.

The asserted conflict is also not clean. The petition relies on decisions involving criminal fines, probation, child support, state constitutional prohibitions, and differing divorce-enforcement regimes. Its federal examples largely apply the common *Bearden*/*Turner* framework rather than establish a square disagreement over a recurring federal rule. A CourtListener search found many lower-court decisions applying ability-to-pay principles in family and contempt settings, but no obvious clean split among state high courts or federal courts on the precise federal question. This looks more like alleged error in applying settled safeguards than a Rule 10 conflict.

## Calibration

The corpus statpack estimates a 3.0% grant rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions in the 2024 Term and 2.5% in the 2025 Term. For 2025 paid petitions, it estimates 5.36%, while petitions ending with no relist have only a 0.8% grant rate and petitions with no CVSG have a 3.0% rate. Because this case is only at its first distribution, the paid-petition rate is the better starting prior; the current absence of enhanced signals and the vehicle defects then move the forecast below that prior. The divided vote in the state high court and the importance of the liberty issue prevent reducing it to the lowest tail.

The most likely outcome is a routine denial. A GVR is less likely than plenary review because there is no identified intervening Supreme Court authority and the dispute depends on what inquiry and findings the state courts already made. I do not predict individual cert votes from this record.

## Significance

The big-case score is 0.67. A merits ruling could materially affect state contempt practice and protections for indigent debtors, but the private divorce/property posture and fact-bound record limit the case's likely national reach.
