# Reasoning

## Legal Question
Whether the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process and Equal Protection Clauses, as interpreted in *Bearden v. Georgia* and *Turner v. Rogers*, prohibit incarcerating an indigent judgment debtor for nonpayment of a private civil money judgment (labeled as "civil contempt") without an express judicial finding of the debtor's present ability to pay.

## Governing Standard
To grant certiorari, the Supreme Court typically looks for a clear conflict among circuit courts or state supreme courts on an important question of federal law, or an area where a state court has decided an important federal question in a way that conflicts with relevant decisions of the Supreme Court. The baseline grant rate for paid petitions (such as this one) is extremely low (under 3%), especially when there have been no relists or a Call for the Views of the Solicitor General (CVSG). 

## Facts Driving Outcome
1. **Procedural Posture & Case Type:** This is a paid petition originating from a Mississippi state court (Court of Appeals of Mississippi) concerning a $21,040.72 civil money judgment. The Mississippi Supreme Court denied certiorari with a divided vote (two Justices voted to grant).
2. **Conflict Allegations:** While the petition alleges a "deeply entrenched conflict among state and federal courts over whether and how civil contempt may be used to collect private money judgments," the Brief in Opposition counters that there is no genuine conflict and that the lower courts simply applied established state-law principles regarding the inability to comply with an order.
3. **No Signals:** The petition is scheduled for the September 28, 2026, conference (the "Long Conference") and has no relists or CVSG signals.

## Conclusion
Given the procedural posture (an appeal from state court applying its own contempt procedures), the lack of a stark, acknowledged circuit split in the questions presented, and the absence of traditional signals of a grant (relists), the Court is highly likely to deny certiorari. The base rate for paid cert petitions without relists is roughly 0.8%, and the specific facts of this case do not significantly elevate the probability of a grant above that baseline. I predict a probability of 0.015 (1.5%) for a grant, resulting in a denial.
