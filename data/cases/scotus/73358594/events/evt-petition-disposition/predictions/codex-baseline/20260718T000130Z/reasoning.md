# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with a 0.7% probability of any grant (including a GVR). The petition presents a plausible claim of adjudicatory error, but not the conflict, clean legal issue, or vehicle that ordinarily attracts certiorari.

## Question and record

The question presented is whether a federal civilian employee who timely premised disclosures on both categories in 5 U.S.C. § 2302(b)(8)(A)—a violation of law, rule, or regulation under clause (i), and gross mismanagement or related wrongdoing under clause (ii)—is entitled to an MSPB decision addressing both. The petition says Bolanos-Reynoso expressly invoked both categories, but the MSPB decided only whether she reasonably believed she was reporting a violation of law, rule, or regulation. The Federal Circuit affirmed without opinion under Rule 36 and denied rehearing.

The petition identifies a real fairness concern. It also supplies facts that make this a difficult vehicle: the 120-page MSPB decision found the petitioner broadly not credible, concluded that her concerns reflected misunderstanding of USDA's budget processes, found no management knowledge linking her OIG contact to the personnel actions, and upheld the agency's performance case. Evidence that a manager later pleaded guilty in connection with a named contractor may corroborate some concerns, but it arose outside the MSPB record and adds a record-remand complication rather than presenting a clean statutory issue.

I used the provisioned questions presented and petition. The document manifest reports that the 194-page petition text is truncated; it nonetheless includes the full petition argument and the relevant portion of the MSPB decision. No BIO was provisioned because the Department of Agriculture waived its right to respond.

## Certiorari considerations

The strongest adverse signal is the absence of a square conflict. The petition principally argues that the MSPB was wrong and that the Federal Circuit should have explained its affirmance. Its cited out-of-circuit decisions are analogies, not conflicting answers to the question presented:

- *Flynn v. SEC*, 877 F.3d 200 (4th Cir. 2017), remanded after the MSPB failed to analyze one of two asserted SEC rules. That supports the petitioner's requested treatment by analogy but does not show a conflict with a contrary precedential Federal Circuit rule.
- *Delgado v. MSPB*, 880 F.3d 913 (7th Cir. 2018), concerned how generously OSC exhaustion allegations should be construed. It does not decide whether an MSPB merits decision must separately analyze both § 2302(b)(8)(A) categories. CourtListener identifies *Delgado* as a Seventh Circuit case, although the petition calls it an Eighth Circuit case.
- A CourtListener search for Supreme Court opinions referring to § 2302(b)(8) returned *Department of Homeland Security v. MacLean* and *Garcetti v. Ceballos*. Neither resolves this claimed omission; *MacLean* dealt with the statute's “prohibited by law” limitation.

The nonprecedential Rule 36 judgment further limits the petition's value as a vehicle for resolving a developed legal disagreement. The question is framed as a procedural entitlement, while the requested relief would require the Court to examine whether the gross-mismanagement theory was preserved, material, and potentially outcome-determinative in a large and fact-intensive record. The subsequent criminal case and the alternative performance/causation grounds create additional obstacles. There is also no intervening Supreme Court decision or comparable development supporting a GVR.

The docket signals are unfavorable. The petition is paid, but the government waived a response; the Court has not called for one, invited the Solicitor General's views, or relisted the case. It is only distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. A waiver is not conclusive, but a case that interests the Court often prompts a call for response before disposition.

## Calibration

The statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 3.0% grant rate for modern cert petitions from the Federal Circuit. Across modern petitions with no CVSG, the grant rate is 3.0%; the resolved zero-relist bucket is 0.8%. Those are starting points rather than mechanical answers, especially because this petition has not yet reached its first conference. The lack of a square split, the summary nonprecedential judgment, the fact-heavy record, and the response waiver move the forecast below those baselines to 0.7%.

I do not predict individual Justice votes. Cert-stage votes are not public, and the record does not support Justice-specific estimates.

The case has moderate institutional stakes because fair treatment of federal whistleblowers affects the federal workforce and reporting of government misconduct. Its current posture and fact-bound framing, however, make it unlikely to become a broadly significant or highly newsworthy merits case; I assign a big-case score of 0.35 independently of the grant forecast.
