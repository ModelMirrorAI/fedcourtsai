## The legal question

Youth 71Five Ministries, a Christian youth-mentoring nonprofit represented by
Alliance Defending Freedom (John Bursch, counsel of record), was excluded from
Oregon's Youth Community Investment Grants program — and had ~$400k in awarded
grants rescinded — after an anonymous complaint that it hires only coreligionists
(all employees sign a statement of faith). A Ninth Circuit motions panel granted
an injunction pending appeal, but the merits panel (153 F.4th 704, amended
Nov. 26, 2025, rehearing denied) held the nondiscrimination rule neutral and
generally applicable under *Smith*, and — most notably — held that the
religious-autonomy (church autonomy) doctrine can be raised only as an
affirmative defense, not as an affirmative §1983 claim against legislative or
executive action. The panel did rule for 71Five in part, holding the rule an
unconstitutional condition on expressive association as applied to
non-grant-funded activities.

The petition presents two questions: (1) whether religious autonomy can be
asserted as an affirmative §1983 claim, and (2) whether conditioning access to a
public grant program on waiving the right to employ coreligionists (including
ministers) violates the First Amendment — the *Trinity Lutheran / Espinoza /
Carson* unconstitutional-conditions line.

## Grant-side signals (from the snapshot and filed documents)

1. **Call for response.** Oregon waived its right to respond (Jan. 12, 2026);
   the Court requested a response four days later (Jan. 16). A CFR is one of the
   strongest observable pre-grant signals — it means at least one chamber is
   interested.
2. **Six cert-stage amicus briefs**, including a multi-state coalition led by
   Montana, the Christian Legal Society, Jewish Coalition for Religious
   Liberty/Young Life (Gibson Dunn), Samaritan's Purse, Heartbeat International,
   and religious-liberty scholars (Profs. Barclay and Garnett). Cert-stage amicus
   volume of this kind sits far out in the salience tail.
3. **Paid petition, elite repeat-player counsel.** ADF/Bursch have an unusually
   strong cert and merits track record before this Court in exactly this space
   (*303 Creative*, *Kennedy*-adjacent litigation, etc.).
4. **Subject-matter fit.** The Roberts Court has repeatedly granted and ruled for
   religious claimants in the funding-conditions line (*Trinity Lutheran*,
   *Espinoza*, *Carson*) and the church-autonomy line (*Hosanna-Tabor*, *Our
   Lady of Guadalupe*), and Justices have flagged the coreligionist-hiring
   question before (Alito's statement in *Seattle's Union Gospel Mission v.
   Woods*, 142 S. Ct. 1094 (2022)). QP1 — whether church autonomy is a shield
   only or also a sword — is a clean, doctrinally consequential question on
   which the Ninth Circuit's own later decision in *Union Gospel Mission of
   Yakima v. Brown*, 162 F.4th 1190 (9th Cir. 2026), sits in visible tension
   with the decision below.
5. **The docket's post-conference silence.** The petition was fully briefed
   (reply Mar. 30) and distributed for the Conference of Apr. 17, 2026. The
   provisioned snapshot — a supremecourt.gov docket JSON generated July 10,
   2026 — shows **no entry after Mar. 30**: no denial, no relist distribution,
   no grant. A paid petition considered at an April conference that is still
   undisposed in mid-July has survived every regular order list **and the
   end-of-Term cleanup**. That pattern is characteristic of a hold (for a
   companion case) or serial consideration, not of a routine denial track.
   Consistent with the hold hypothesis, CourtListener metadata shows the
   companion petition the petition itself cites — *St. Mary Catholic Parish v.
   Roy*, No. 25-581 (10th Cir.; Colorado universal pre-K religious-participation
   conditions), docketed Nov. 17, 2025 — also still pending with no cert grant
   or denial recorded eight months after docketing. Two closely related
   religious-hiring/funding-conditions petitions both sitting past Term-end is
   strong evidence the Court is actively working this cluster rather than
   passively denying it. If the Court grants a lead case (this one, *St. Mary*,
   or a petition from *Union Gospel Mission of Yakima*), the held companions
   most plausibly end in a grant or GVR; a GVR counts as a grant here.

## Deny-side considerations (Oregon's BIO is substantial)

1. **Preservation/vehicle problems on QP1.** The BIO argues, with record cites,
   that 71Five never squarely presented the affirmative-claim question below —
   *Kedroff* appeared only in a reply-brief footnote, *Kreshik* not at all, and
   the out-of-circuit cases only at the rehearing stage. The panel's holding was
   framed around the undeveloped record on a deferential preliminary-injunction
   standard. "Court of review, not first view" (*Moody v. NetChoice*) is a
   genuinely effective deny argument.
2. **Interlocutory posture.** This is a preliminary-injunction appeal; the case
   is ongoing on remand, and the panel expressly left 71Five free to develop its
   comparator evidence and claims there.
3. **Reduced live stakes.** The Ninth Circuit's injunction pending appeal got
   71Five its grant money in full; Oregon has repealed the employment prong of
   the certification rule. What remains live is chiefly damages/qualified
   immunity — mootness-adjacent vehicle rot.
4. **Percolation.** *Union Gospel Mission of Yakima* shows the Ninth Circuit
   itself moving toward the petitioner's position; the Court could let the
   intra-circuit tension resolve or take the cleaner *St. Mary* vehicle and
   dispose of this petition afterward — which is why a meaningful share of my
   grant-family mass is GVR rather than plenary grant, and why denial-after-hold
   (if the lead case resolves on grounds that don't help 71Five) retains real
   probability.

## Base rates and calibration

From the committed statpack (live/historical slice, denial-reweighted): modern
discretionary cert petitions grant at ~2.5–3.3% per recent Term; **paid**
petitions in Term 2025 at ~5.4%. Ninth Circuit origin is unremarkable (~3%).
Relist behavior is the strongest statpack cut: petitions relisted twice grant at
33.6%, 3+ at 21.8%, versus 0.8% for never-relisted. This petition's trajectory —
CFR, then distribution, then three months of silence spanning Term-end — is at
or beyond the deep-relist/hold regime those cuts describe. Layering the CFR
(historically roughly an order-of-magnitude uplift on the paid base rate), the
amicus salience, and the survival past the end-of-Term order lists on top of the
~5% paid anchor, and then discounting for the real vehicle problems and the
companion-case competition, I land at **P(any grant, GVR included) ≈ 0.55**.

## Big-case score

0.6 — if decided on the merits, this would materially extend the
*Trinity Lutheran* line to religious hiring conditions and settle whether church
autonomy supports affirmative claims, affecting faith-based organizations'
participation in grant programs nationwide. High-salience within the
religious-liberty docket, but narrower in public profile than a marquee
constitutional blockbuster.
