# Reasoning

## Record Summary
This is a First Amendment Free Exercise / Religious Autonomy case, filed by Youth 71Five Ministries against the Director of the Oregon Department of Education. After years of receiving public grant funds, the ministry was disqualified because it requires its employees to sign a statement of faith, which violated a new state eligibility rule prohibiting religious "discrimination" in employment. The Ninth Circuit affirmed the denial of an injunction and dismissal of a damages claim, holding that the state's new grant rule is neutral and generally applicable, and that the religious-autonomy doctrine can only be asserted as an affirmative defense, not as an affirmative claim under § 1983. 

The petitioner claims a 5-3 circuit split over the First Amendment right to hire coreligionists and a 3-1 split over waiving religious autonomy rights. At the cert stage, this case has attracted substantial attention: the Court called for a response, and six amicus briefs were filed in support of the petitioner (including one from the State of Montana and several prominent religious liberty organizations).

## Standard and Base Rates
The baseline cert grant rate for the 2025 Term is approximately 2.5%. For petitions out of the Ninth Circuit, the rate is 3.0%. There have been no recorded relists in the snapshot yet (the case was distributed for the April 17, 2026 conference). However, the presence of six amicus briefs—including a multi-state amicus brief—along with a Court-requested response strongly elevates the petition into the highest salience band.

## Analysis
The case squarely presents an unresolved tension in the Court's recent Free Exercise jurisprudence. The Supreme Court has increasingly protected religious entities from being excluded from public benefit programs based on their religious status or exercise (*Trinity Lutheran*, *Espinoza*, *Carson v. Makin*). This case tests whether a state can condition those public funds on the organization's waiver of its right to hire coreligionists, a central tenet of the religious autonomy doctrine (*Our Lady of Guadalupe*, *Fulton*). The Ninth Circuit's holding that the religious autonomy doctrine is merely an affirmative defense and not an affirmative claim under § 1983 also presents a distinct, clean question of federal law that divides the lower courts.

Given the current Court's keen interest in religious liberty and its willingness to review Ninth Circuit decisions on these grounds, the combination of alleged circuit splits, high amicus interest, and the direct implication of recent landmark precedents makes this a prime candidate for a grant.
