# Prediction: denied

I assign a 29% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. The petition is substantially more certworthy than an ordinary paid petition, but its procedural and vehicle defects still outweigh the positive signals.

## Questions and governing considerations

Youth 71Five Ministries asks whether a religious organization may bring an affirmative religious-autonomy claim under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, and whether Oregon may condition access to a public youth-services grant on the organization not limiting employment to coreligionists, including for ministerial roles. The certiorari inquiry turns principally on conflict, importance, preservation, finality, and vehicle quality rather than which party has the better ultimate First Amendment argument.

The provisioned petition argues that the Ninth Circuit uniquely limited religious autonomy to a defense, that other courts protect coreligionist hiring, and that the decision conflicts with *Trinity Lutheran*, *Espinoza*, and *Carson*. Those are important and ideologically salient questions for the current Court. The response, however, persuasively identifies obstacles to review: the ruling arose on a preliminary-injunction record; the first question was not adequately developed below; the claimed conflicts are largely conflicts in principle rather than clean holdings on the same issue; and the Ninth Circuit granted petitioner relief on its expressive-association theory. Oregon also fully funded the grants and repealed the challenged employment rule. The remaining damages theory and possibility of recurrence preserve some controversy, but they do not make this a clean vehicle.

## Case-specific signals

Several docket features materially raise the grant probability above the ordinary baseline. The Court requested a response after an initial waiver, six amicus briefs supported the petition, and petitioner waived the Rule 15.5 waiting period. Most importantly, the petition was distributed for the April 17, 2026 conference and remained unresolved in the July 16 snapshot. That prolonged conference carryover is consistent with repeated consideration or a hold and is a much stronger selection signal than the merits allegations alone.

The committed statpack places modern discretionary-cert grants near 3%, with Ninth Circuit petitions also near 3%. Its denial-reweighted grant rate rises to 21.8% for petitions with three or more relists. The snapshot does not expressly label relists, so I treat the extended carryover as an analogous but not conclusive signal rather than mechanically assigning that bucket.

Related litigation cuts both ways. CourtListener shows that on June 18, 2026, the Ninth Circuit granted rehearing en banc in *Union Gospel Mission of Yakima Washington v. Brown* and vacated the January panel opinion. That eliminates the response's reliance on the panel opinion as a settled answer favorable to religious organizations, modestly strengthening the petitioner's claim that the law is unsettled. But active en banc proceedings also give the Supreme Court a strong reason to hold or deny this imperfect petition while the Ninth Circuit develops the issue in a cleaner posture. The provisioned petition also identifies another pending petition involving religious conditions on public funding, further increasing the possibility that this case is a hold rather than the lead vehicle.

On balance, four Justices could be interested in the underlying religious-liberty issue, and a grant, summary disposition, or later GVR remains plausible. Still, interlocutory posture, inadequate preservation, partial victory below, repeal of the rule, completed grant payments, and ongoing percolation make eventual denial the single likeliest disposition.

No individual Justice votes are predicted because cert-stage votes are not public and the record does not support reliable Justice-by-Justice assignments.
