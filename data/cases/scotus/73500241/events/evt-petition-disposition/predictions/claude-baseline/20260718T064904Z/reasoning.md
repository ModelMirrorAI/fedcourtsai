# Greene v. Kansas Department of Revenue (No. 25-1339) — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.012.**

## The case

Paid petition (Term 2025 numbering stream, docketed June 1, 2026) from the
Kansas state courts. In 2012 the Kansas Department of Revenue assessed a
$460,000 drug tax against the Greenes and levied $62,500 in property; the
Kansas Board of Tax Appeals invalidated the assessment in 2014, but KDOR did
not return the property until October 2023, after the Greenes sued. The Kansas
courts dismissed their Fifth Amendment claim, holding that taxation cannot be a
taking; the Kansas Supreme Court denied review on February 27, 2026.

The question presented: whether retention of property, after the legal
authority for the taking is struck down, itself violates the Fifth Amendment.
The petition pitches a 6–4 circuit split over whether the Fourth or the Fifth
Amendment is the vehicle for challenging continued government retention of
lawfully seized property (Second/Sixth/Seventh/Tenth et al. pointing to due
process vs. Ninth/D.C./Third — *Brewster*, *Asinor*, *Honda Lease* — applying
the Fourth Amendment).

## Why deny is the strong call

**Base rate.** From the committed statpack, modern discretionary-cert petitions
run ~2.5–3.3% granted per recent Term; paid petitions in Term 2025 run ~5.4%
(statpack.json fee-class detail). That is the ceiling this petition starts
from, and everything observable pushes down from it.

**Docket signals are all deny-side.**
- The respondent (a state agency) **waived its response** on June 3, 2026, and
  the case was **distributed directly for the September 28, 2026 long
  conference** with no call for a response. The Court does not grant without a
  response on file; a grant path would first require a CFR, of which there is
  no sign in the snapshot. Statpack relist data show single-distribution,
  never-relisted petitions grant at well under 1%.
- No CVSG, no relists (first distribution pending), no amici noted.
- State-court origin: in the statpack's originating-court cut, state
  intermediate/supreme court petitions grant rarely, and corpus grant priors I
  pulled (e.g., the June 30, 2026 grants) show the opposite profile — multiple
  relists, prominent Supreme Court counsel, federal circuit or state
  high-court origins.

**Vehicle problems.** Even crediting the (genuine) Fourth-vs-Fifth retention
split, this is a poor vehicle for it. The split concerns §1983 retention claims
after lawful seizures; this case arrives as a *takings/illegal-exaction* claim
against a state tax assessment, decided below on the ground that taxation is
not a taking — so the split question is not cleanly presented. The property was
returned in 2023, leaving only a damages theory over the retention period.
There are also state-law and limitations complications lurking in the
procedural history (dismissal affirmed on grounds the petition only glancingly
engages).

**Petition quality.** The 13-page-body petition misattributes circuit
authority to the Supreme Court — it calls *Norman v. United States* (Fed.
Cir.), *Ecco Plains* (10th Cir.), and *Aerolineas Argentinas* (Fed. Cir.)
"explicit rulings of the United States Supreme Court" and asks the Court to
"reaffirm" its illegal-exaction precedent, which is actually Court of Federal
Claims/Federal Circuit Tucker Act doctrine that does not even apply to states.
The question presented conflates takings and due process. Filings of this
quality essentially never attract four votes.

**The one grant-side consideration.** The retention split is real and recently
deepened (*Asinor*, D.C. Cir. 2024; *Honda Lease*, 3d Cir. 2025), and the Court
has been active in adjacent territory (*Culley v. Marshall* (2024) on retention
hearings). If the Court wants the issue, though, it would take a clean §1983
case from a federal circuit — or hold this one for such a case. I found no
pending granted companion (CourtListener's SCOTUS docket coverage returned
nothing for *Asinor*/*Honda Lease* follow-ons), so a hold/GVR path is
speculative; it is folded into the small residual probability.

## Calibration

Start ~5% (paid-petition Term rate), cut sharply for waived response with no
CFR, first-distribution-to-long-conference posture, state-court origin, vehicle
mismatch, and petition quality; add back a sliver for the genuine split and the
long-conference timing (some CFR/relist chance). That lands at **P(grant) ≈
0.012**, disposition **denied**, most likely as an unrelisted denial in the
first October 2026 order lists.
