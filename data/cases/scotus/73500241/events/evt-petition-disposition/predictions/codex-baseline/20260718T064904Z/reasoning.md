# Prediction

I predict denial, with a 1.8% probability of any grant-side disposition. A plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR if the Court takes action, because the record identifies no intervening Supreme Court decision or mootness development that would naturally support vacatur and remand.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether, after the legal authority for an initially valid taking is struck down, the government's continued retention of the property without due process itself violates the Fifth Amendment. The petition presents this as part of a circuit disagreement over whether prolonged retention of lawfully seized property is governed by the Fourth Amendment or instead by Fifth/Fourteenth Amendment due process.

Under the Court's ordinary certiorari criteria, the best grant argument is an acknowledged and recently developed circuit conflict on an important recurring constitutional question. The Court nevertheless normally looks for a square conflict, a cleanly preserved federal issue, and a vehicle that lets it resolve the asserted disagreement without antecedent remedial, immunity, mootness, or pleading problems.

## Record signals

The May 28 petition says the Kansas Department of Revenue assessed $460,000 in 2012 and seized $62,500 in property; the assessment was invalidated in 2014, later efforts to amend it were abated, and state litigation established by 2021 that no assessment remained. The Department allegedly retained the property until October 2023, shortly after petitioners filed this action. The Kansas Court of Appeals affirmed dismissal and the Kansas Supreme Court denied review on February 27, 2026.

There is a real certworthy idea in the petition. It contrasts decisions rejecting a continuing-seizure theory, including *Shaul* and *Lee*, with decisions applying the Fourth Amendment, including *Brewster*, *Asinor*, and the Third Circuit's 2025 *Honda Lease Trust* decision. CourtListener research confirmed that *Asinor* is a published 2024 D.C. Circuit decision and *Honda Lease Trust* a published 2025 Third Circuit decision, so the disagreement is neither wholly stale nor merely hypothetical.

The vehicle and presentation substantially weaken that signal:

- The question asks whether retention violates the Fifth Amendment “in and of itself,” while much of the cited conflict concerns whether the Fourth Amendment continues to apply. Rejecting one Fourth Amendment theory does not necessarily establish a freestanding substantive-due-process or Takings Clause claim.
- The petition moves among procedural due process, substantive due process, the Takings Clause, and federal illegal-exaction doctrine without identifying a precise rule and remedy. Its illegal-exaction authorities largely arise in claims against the federal government and do not by themselves show that the Kansas judgment squarely conflicts with controlling Supreme Court precedent.
- The property was returned before the dismissal proceedings concluded. The petition does not adequately resolve what retrospective relief remains, how immunity affects that relief, or whether the asserted federal theory was cleanly pleaded and preserved. The provisioned record lacks the lower-court opinions, preventing independent confirmation that the federal question was actually decided in the form presented.
- The respondent waived a response, no response was requested as of the snapshot, there is no CVSG, and the case has only its initial distribution for the September 28 conference. Those are weak institutional signals. The short petition also contains substantial editing and doctrinal imprecision, and there is no provisioned brief in opposition to cure or narrow the vehicle issues.
