# Prediction Reasoning

**Legal Question & Governing Standard:**
The petition presents a question regarding whether the continued retention of property (after the legal authority for its initial seizure is invalidated) constitutes a violation of the Fifth Amendment's Takings or Due Process clauses, as opposed to the Fourth Amendment. The petitioners allege a circuit split on the proper constitutional framework (Fourth vs. Fifth Amendment) for claims of prolonged retention of lawfully seized property.

**Facts Driving the Outcome:**
1. **Procedural Posture & Vehicle Issues:** This case arises from state courts (the Kansas Court of Appeals, with review denied by the Kansas Supreme Court). The primary respondent is the Kansas Department of Revenue, a state agency, which introduces substantial sovereign immunity and *Will v. Michigan Dept. of State Police* (States are not "persons" under § 1983) complications for federal constitutional claims seeking damages.
2. **Lack of Respondent Engagement:** The respondents waived their right to respond on June 3, 2026. The Supreme Court functionally never grants a petition without first calling for a response (CFR). While a CFR could theoretically occur at the September Long Conference, the waiver signals that respondents view the petition as posing no real threat of a grant, likely relying on the vehicle flaws and the fact that the property was already returned in 2023 (making this a backward-looking damages dispute).
3. **Undeveloped Record below:** The petition notes that the Kansas Court of Appeals dismissed the Fifth Amendment claim in a "single sentence." The Supreme Court generally prefers vehicles where the lower court has rigorously engaged with the federal constitutional question, which is absent here.

**Prediction & Probability:**
The probability of a grant is extremely low (~1.5%). The baseline grant rate for paid petitions from state courts without a response is close to zero. The vehicle is compromised by the nature of the defendants, the summary dismissal below, and the return of the property. Therefore, the most likely outcome by far is a denial of certiorari.
