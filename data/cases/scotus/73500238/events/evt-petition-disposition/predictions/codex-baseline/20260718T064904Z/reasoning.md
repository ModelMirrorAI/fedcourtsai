# Prediction

I predict that the petition will be **denied**, with a **4% probability of any grant** (including a GVR).

## Record and questions presented

The prediction uses the July 18, 2026 snapshot, the provisioned questions presented, and the complete 47-page petition. No brief in opposition was provisioned; that matches the snapshot's June 9 entry showing that the United States waived its right to respond.

The petition presents two federal criminal-procedure questions. First, it asks whether plain-error review can govern a *Napue* claim when the prosecution allegedly left false testimony uncorrected and then relied on the falsity in closing and rebuttal. Second, it asks whether an appellate court can find harmless a trial court's failure to perform Rule 702 gatekeeping based on the witnesses' credentials and experience. The petition says the Ninth Circuit found Tolbert's account “actually false,” assumed a duty to correct it, and nevertheless rejected relief under plain-error review. It also says the court recognized that portions of two laboratory witnesses' testimony were expert opinion but deemed the admission harmless.

## Certiorari analysis

The governing predictive standard is the Court's discretionary-certiorari practice: a grant ordinarily requires a clean, consequential conflict, an important recurring federal question, and a suitable vehicle. The petition has credible positive features. Its *Napue* theory invokes the recent decision in *Glossip v. Oklahoma*, and prosecutorial use of false testimony is an important constitutional issue. Its Rule 702 theory concerns a frequently used federal rule, cites decisions from several circuits requiring genuine reliability gatekeeping, and points to the 2023 amendment clarifying the proponent's burden. The published lower-court opinion and the prominence of the Theranos prosecution increase the case's visibility.

Those features do not overcome the negative signals. On the first question, the alleged conflict is not clean: the cited cases differ in preservation, procedural posture, who elicited the testimony, and whether the prosecution affirmatively relied on it. The Ninth Circuit also rested on the case-specific conclusion that substantial rights were not affected. *Glossip* supplies petitioner with a forceful merits analogy, but it does not necessarily establish a square conflict over Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 52(b). On the second question, many cited authorities enforce Rule 702 at trial rather than squarely reject the particular appellate harmless-error analysis used below. The asserted intra-circuit conflict is not ordinarily a basis for certiorari, and harmlessness makes the vehicle less attractive. Presenting two fact-intensive trial-error issues also makes the petition look more like error correction than a clean vehicle for resolving either split.

The docket posture is the strongest negative evidence. The United States waived response, the petition was then distributed for the September 28 conference without a requested response, and the snapshot shows no CVSG, relist, amicus filing, or other institutional signal. The Court can request a response later, so this is not dispositive, but grants ordinarily do not occur without the respondent being heard.

## Probability and disposition

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 3.0% grant rate for modern petitions from the Ninth Circuit. The 3.0% no-CVSG rate is also relevant, although these cuts overlap and should not be multiplied. The zero-relist bucket has a lower rate, but I give it little weight because this petition has not yet reached its first conference. Recent corpus examples likewise showed that several grants had multiple conference distributions, while distribution count alone did not distinguish all grants from denials.

I place this petition slightly below the paid-petition baseline because the waiver and lack of a call for response outweigh the colorable split claims. Denial is overwhelmingly the likeliest disposition. I reserve a small portion of the 4% grant probability for a GVR in light of *Glossip*, but a plenary grant or GVR is much less likely than denial.

I do not predict individual cert votes because the pre-conference record provides no justice-specific signal. The 0.70 big-case score measures significance if decided, not grant likelihood: a merits ruling could affect recurring criminal-trial duties nationwide, and the underlying prosecution is unusually prominent.
