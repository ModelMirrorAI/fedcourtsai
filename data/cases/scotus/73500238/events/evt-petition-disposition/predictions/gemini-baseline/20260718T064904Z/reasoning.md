# Prediction Reasoning: Ramesh "Sunny" Balwani v. United States (25-1330)

## The Legal Questions
The petition asks the Court to consider two issues arising from the petitioner's criminal conviction:
1. Whether plain-error review applies to a *Napue* violation (presentation of false testimony) where the prosecution not only failed to correct the testimony but affirmatively exploited it in closing argument.
2. Whether an appellate court can excuse a district court's failure to conduct a Rule 702 gatekeeping analysis on the sole basis that the witnesses possessed sufficient credentials and experience to qualify as experts.

## Analysis
The most determinative factor in assessing this petition's likelihood of a grant is the posture of the respondent. The United States has filed a waiver of its right to respond. While the underlying case (the Theranos fraud trial) generated massive public interest, the legal questions presented in this petition are highly fact-bound and primarily concern standard applications of plain-error review and evidentiary gatekeeping by the lower courts.

According to the pipeline's historical statistics, the overall grant rate for modern paid discretionary-cert petitions is approximately 2.5%. However, that baseline assumes an ordinary adversarial posture where the respondent either files a brief in opposition or is ordered to do so. A waiver by the Solicitor General signals the government's view that the petition raises no significant circuit split and no issue worthy of the Court's review. The Supreme Court almost never grants certiorari on a petition where the respondent has waived without first issuing a Call for Response (CFR). The docket snapshot shows that the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference without any such CFR having been requested.

Absent a CFR, the probability of a grant is vanishingly small. The petition does not raise a clean, purely legal issue of national importance that the Court would feel compelled to take up without government briefing, and the fact-intensive nature of both the *Napue* and Rule 702 claims strongly counsels against review. 

## Conclusion
The combination of a low base grant rate for paid petitions, the highly fact-bound nature of the evidentiary and procedural questions, and the Solicitor General's waiver of response all point decisively to a denial.
