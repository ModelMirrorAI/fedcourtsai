### Summary
The petitioner is challenging a state court's grant of summary judgment in a residential foreclosure action, arguing that the resolution of disputed facts at the summary judgment stage violates the Due Process Clause and the Seventh Amendment.

### Analysis
The probability of a cert grant is assessed at 0.1% for the following reasons:

1. **Seventh Amendment Not Incorporated:** The petitioner's primary argument rests on the Seventh Amendment right to a civil jury trial. However, the Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the Seventh Amendment is not incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment (see *Minneapolis & St. Louis R. Co. v. Bombolis* and *Gasperini v. Center for Humanities, Inc.*). The Court is highly unlikely to use a routine state foreclosure dispute to overturn over a century of precedent on incorporation.
2. **Fact-Bound Error Correction:** The petition essentially asks the Supreme Court to correct what it views as a state appellate court's misapplication of the summary judgment standard. Under Supreme Court Rule 10, a writ of certiorari is rarely granted to review "erroneous factual findings or the misapplication of a properly stated rule of law."
3. **No Split:** The petition does not allege a circuit or state supreme court split on any novel question of federal law, which is the primary criterion for granting certiorari.
4. **Base Rates:** The overall grant rate for modern discretionary petitions is roughly 3.1%. Paid cases have a slightly higher rate than IFP cases, but this case lacks any classic indicators of a grant, such as a CVSG or relists prior to conference distribution.

Given the deeply fact-bound nature of the grievance and the settled law on the Seventh Amendment, this case is an exceedingly poor vehicle for any constitutional holding and is virtually guaranteed to be denied.
