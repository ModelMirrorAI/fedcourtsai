# White v. U.S. Bank N.A. (No. 25-1310) — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.004.**

## The case

A paid cert petition from the Washington state courts. Alvin White lost a
deed-of-trust reformation action in Kitsap County Superior Court on
cross-motions for summary judgment; the Washington Court of Appeals,
Division II, affirmed in an **unpublished** opinion (No. 58849-8-II, May 20,
2025), and the Washington Supreme Court denied discretionary review
(No. 104624-3, January 7, 2026). The petition was docketed May 26, 2026,
with a response due June 25, 2026, and was distributed on July 8 for the
**September 28, 2026 conference** (the "long conference"). The docket lists
no brief in opposition and no relists.

## The questions presented

1. Whether a court may constitutionally resolve disputed factual issues
   bearing on private property rights by weighing evidence at summary
   judgment, without trial.
2. Whether that practice comports with the Due Process Clause, the original
   understanding of the judicial power, and the Seventh Amendment.

The petition's core grievance is that the trial judge, at the summary-judgment
hearing, physically inspected the promissory note and stated on the record
that it "appears to be an original document," which petitioner casts as
impermissible judicial factfinding. The petition leans on Anderson v. Liberty
Lobby's "the judge's function is not himself to weigh the evidence" language
and on Suja Thomas's academic article *Why Summary Judgment Is
Unconstitutional*, 93 Va. L. Rev. 139 (2007).

## Why this is a near-certain denial

- **The ask is to unsettle, not settle.** The petition effectively asks the
  Court to hold summary-judgment practice unconstitutional when it resolves
  contested facts — an argument the Court has had every opportunity to take up
  since the Thomas article appeared two decades ago and has never touched.
  There is no realistic appetite for it.
- **No split.** The petition alleges no conflict among circuits or state
  courts of last resort. Its only "pattern" evidence is a single unpublished
  Washington Court of Appeals decision (Slotke). Under Rule 10 there is
  nothing certworthy: it is a request for error correction in a fact-bound
  state foreclosure dispute.
- **Fatal threshold defect on the Seventh Amendment theory.** The Seventh
  Amendment's civil-jury guarantee has never been incorporated against the
  states (Walker v. Sauvinet; Minneapolis & St. Louis R.R. v. Bombolis). This
  came from a *state* court, so QP2's jury component fails at the door. And
  the action was one for equitable reformation/declaratory relief, where no
  jury right attaches even federally.
- **Weak vehicle regardless.** Unpublished intermediate opinion, discretionary
  review denied below, a substituted-plaintiff securitization-trust dispute,
  and a record where the judge's "finding" was expressly framed as "for
  purposes of today's motion" — i.e., arguably ordinary authentication under
  the summary-judgment standard, not disputed-fact resolution.
- **Docket signals are all baseline.** Paid petition (better than IFP but
  still a low prior), distributed for the long conference with no response
  filed — consistent with a waiver of response and a straight first-conference
  denial. No relist, no CVSG, no amici on the docket.
- **Counsel's track record.** Counsel of record (Scott Stafne) has repeatedly
  pressed these same summary-judgment/judicial-power constitutional theories
  in foreclosure litigation, including prior cert petitions; none has
  attracted a grant. This is background knowledge, not this case's outcome,
  which does not exist yet (forward cell).
