# Prediction

I predict denial, with a 0.4% probability of any grant (including a GVR).

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether a state court may weigh disputed evidence and make factual findings when granting summary judgment in a private-property dispute, and whether doing so violates due process, Article III's conception of judicial power, or the Seventh Amendment.

Certiorari ordinarily requires more than an asserted misapplication of the summary-judgment standard. The strongest indicators would be a developed conflict among courts on an important federal question, a squarely preserved constitutional issue, and a clean vehicle. Summary judgment itself is established procedure; the orthodox rule is that a court decides whether a genuine material factual dispute exists and may not weigh evidence or decide credibility. A case-specific departure from that rule generally presents error correction rather than a certworthy conflict.

## Record and merits signals

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid Term 2025 petition from an unpublished Washington Court of Appeals decision, followed by denial of discretionary review in the Washington Supreme Court. It records only the petition and initial distribution for the September 28 conference. It shows no relist, CVSG, amicus activity, response, or other institutional signal as of the snapshot. No brief in opposition was provisioned, so I make no inference about arguments the respondent might have presented.

The provisioned questions-presented text and full 23-page petition are the principal merits inputs. The petition says that the trial judge inspected an alleged original promissory note during cross-motions for summary judgment, described physical features suggesting authenticity, and made a finding that it appeared original before reforming the deed of trust. That allegation identifies a conventional summary-judgment objection, but the petition does not identify a conflict among federal circuits or state high courts. Its only claimed analogous Washington authority is another intermediate state decision. The requested ruling therefore appears to turn on whether this trial court improperly assessed this record, an unattractive error-correction posture.

The constitutional framing creates additional vehicle problems. Article III governs federal judicial power, not the organization of Washington's courts. The Supreme Court has also continued to state that the Seventh Amendment civil-jury guarantee is not applicable to the states; the CourtListener search surfaced *City of Monterey v. Del Monte Dunes*, 526 U.S. 687 (1999), in response to that proposition. The due-process theory is the only plausible route around those obstacles, but the petition offers no authority establishing that due process constitutionalizes a trial whenever a party labels facts disputed, and no split over that theory. The equitable deed-reformation posture and cross-motions further complicate the jury-right presentation. These problems are evident from the petition itself even without the respondent's position.
