# Fairfield Sentry Ltd. v. Citibank NA London (No. 25-1089) — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied, P(grant incl. GVR) = 0.15.**

## The case

Petitioners are the BVI liquidators of the Fairfield funds — the largest Madoff
feeder funds — seeking to claw back roughly $6 billion in redemption payments
made to investors who cashed out before the Ponzi scheme collapsed. Proceeding
under Chapter 15, they asserted BVI statutory and common-law claims in the
S.D.N.Y. bankruptcy court. The Second Circuit (147 F.4th 136, Aug. 5, 2025)
held that the §546(e) securities safe harbor, applied to Chapter 15 cases
through §561(d), bars *all* of the liquidators' claims — foreign statutory
avoidance claims and foreign common-law claims alike, including constructive-
trust claims against redeemers alleged to have actually known of the fraud.
Rehearing was denied without recorded dissent (Oct. 16, 2025).

The question presented (from the provisioned QP section): whether §546(e)
applies extraterritorially to prevent foreign liquidators from pursuing foreign
statutory and common-law claims that would be cognizable in foreign courts.

## Why the petition is serious

- **Counsel and presentation.** Paul Clement is counsel of record for
  petitioners; Kannon Shanmugam leads for the Citibank respondents, with
  Cleary (HSBC) and Paul, Weiss alongside. Elite counsel on both sides signals
  the bar treats this as a genuine cert candidate, and top-advocate paid
  petitions historically grant at several times the paid base rate.
- **Claimed splits.** The petition asserts (1) a split with the Fifth
  Circuit's *In re Condor Insurance* (601 F.3d 319), which held Chapter 15
  lets foreign representatives wield foreign-law avoidance powers, and (2) a
  split with the Seventh (*Grede*) and Eighth (*Frost*) Circuits on whether
  §546(e) *directly* bars common-law claims rather than operating only on the
  enumerated statutory avoidance powers, invoking *Merit Management*'s reading
  of §546.
- **Extraterritoriality.** The Court has been consistently active in this area
  (*Morrison*, *RJR Nabisco*, *WesternGeco*, *Abitron*, *Yegiazaryan*), and
  the Second Circuit found the presumption rebutted largely via the canon
  against superfluity — a mode of analysis in tension with the Court's
  "clear, affirmative indication" cases.
- **Stakes and percolation dynamics.** ~$6B, the most significant Chapter 15
  litigation ever, and cross-border insolvency is concentrated in the Second
  Circuit, so a clean split on §561(d) may never develop — a consideration
  that sometimes substitutes for percolation. Two cert-stage amicus briefs,
  one from five former S.D.N.Y. bankruptcy judges (Drain, Gerber, Gropper,
  Cyganowski, Clark), support the petition.

## Why denial is still the likelier outcome

- **Base rate.** From the committed statpack, Term 2025 paid petitions grant
  at ~5.4% (denial-reweighted); the modern discretionary-cert anchor overall
  is ~2.5–3%. Everything below is an adjustment off that low anchor.
- **The splits are contestable, and the BIO contests them well.** *Condor*
  never addressed §546(e) or §561(d) — it construed §1521(a)(7) — so the
  extraterritoriality holding below is genuinely the first of its kind, and
  the Court rarely grants on a first-impression circuit ruling. On the
  common-law question, *Frost* and *Grede* also *barred* the common-law
  claims (via implied preemption), so the bottom-line results align; the
  disagreement is about rationale, which the BIO exploits ("the whole is no
  stronger than the sum of its parts").
- **Vehicle problems.** The district court alternatively held the application
  of §561(d) *domestic* at step two of the extraterritoriality framework — an
  antecedent ground the Second Circuit did not need and the petition does not
  engage, giving the Court a reason to see the QP as potentially academic.
  The litigation involves ~400 parties and 300 consolidated adversary
  proceedings; the BVI courts (through the Privy Council) already rejected
  the statutory avoidance claims, lending force to the BIO's forum-shopping
  theme; and the Court denied cert in the parallel Madoff trustee §546(e)
  case in 2015 (*In re BLMIS*, 773 F.3d 411).
- **No pre-grant signals yet.** The petition was distributed on June 17, 2026
  for the September 28, 2026 long conference — no relist, no CVSG, and
  rehearing below was denied without dissent. The classic pre-grant signals
  (relists: 2 relists ≈ 34% grant in the statpack; CVSG ≈ 27%) are absent at
  this snapshot, though the long-conference posture means they simply have
  not had a chance to appear.

## Net assessment

This reads like a strong "petition to watch": elite counsel, real doctrinal
tension with *Merit Management* and the extraterritoriality cases, enormous
stakes, and a plausible no-percolation story — but a contestable split, a
first-impression statute, serious vehicle complexity, and a step-two
alternative ground. A CVSG at or after the long conference is a live
possibility given the comity dimension, which would itself signal elevated
interest. Weighing a ~5% paid-petition anchor up for counsel, stakes,
amici, and the extraterritoriality appetite, and down for the vehicle and
no-split counterarguments, I put P(grant, GVR included) at **0.15** and
predict **denied** as the modal disposition. A GVR path is essentially absent
here (no intervening decision), so the grant mass is plenary.
