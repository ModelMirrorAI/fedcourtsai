# Prediction: denied

I assign a **28% probability of any grant** and predict denial as the most likely disposition. If the Court does intervene, a plenary grant is much more likely than a GVR because no intervening authority or mootness development appears in the pre-decision record.

## Question and governing standard

The question presented is whether independent contractors have the same right as employees to sue for discrimination under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. § 794. The petition argues that Section 504(a)'s broad protection of otherwise qualified individuals extends to contractors and that Section 504(d) imports Title I of the ADA's substantive liability standards, not its employer-employee coverage limit.

At the certiorari stage, the principal considerations are whether the decision creates or deepens a conflict on an important federal question, whether the issue was outcome-determinative and cleanly preserved in a final judgment, and whether the case is a suitable vehicle. Four votes are required to grant review; the likely merits outcome is relevant only insofar as it affects Justices' interest in taking the case.

## Reasons to discount

The most important vehicle risk is that Greer's actual claim is retaliation for advocacy concerning her child's alleged disability discrimination, while the question presented and the principal circuit authorities are framed as independent contractors' right to sue for discrimination. The Arkansas majority distinguished *Flynn* on the ground that it did not involve retaliation and included a footnote assuming without deciding that Section 504's incorporation reaches the relevant claims. The respondent can therefore argue that the asserted split does not squarely resolve the precise retaliation theory in this case. The judgment also addresses several unrelated Section 1983 and state-law theories, adding procedural complexity even though the Section 504 holding is separately identifiable.

The committed statpack supplies a strong low prior: the estimated 2025 Term grant rate is 2.5% across modern cert petitions and 5.36% for paid petitions. The no-CVSG and zero-relist buckets have grant rates of 3.0% and 0.8%, respectively. Those buckets do not capture the unusually favorable response-request and acknowledged-split signals, so I adjust sharply upward rather than applying them mechanically. Still, no response brief has yet tested the petition's characterization of the split or the vehicle, and the Court denied review when the issue previously arrived in the interlocutory *Fleming* petition.

Balancing the mature conflict, clean final judgment, and response request against the retaliation-specific vehicle objection and the Court's very low cert base rate yields 0.28. Denial remains the single most likely outcome, but this petition is substantially stronger than an ordinary paid filing.
