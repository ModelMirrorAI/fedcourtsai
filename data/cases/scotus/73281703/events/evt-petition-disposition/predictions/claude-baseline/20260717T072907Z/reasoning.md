# Greer v. Benton School District, No. 25-1197 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: P(grant, GVR included) = 0.25; likeliest single disposition: denied.**

## The legal question

Whether independent contractors have the same right as employees to sue for
discrimination under section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. § 794.
The Arkansas Supreme Court (2026 Ark. 53, Mar. 12, 2026) held that
§ 794(d)'s incorporation of ADA Title I "standards" imports Title I's
employee-status threshold, so a non-employee cannot bring a § 504
employment-retaliation claim — following the Eighth Circuit (*Wojewski*, 450
F.3d 338) and splitting from the Fifth (*Flynn*, 812 F.3d 422), Ninth
(*Fleming*, 587 F.3d 938), and D.C. (*Redd*, 232 F.3d 933) Circuits, with the
Tenth (*Schrader*) and Sixth (*Hiler*) Circuits strongly implied on each side.

## Facts driving the forecast (from the snapshot and provisioned petition)

Grant-side signals — this is a far stronger petition than the base rate:

1. **A genuine, mature, acknowledged split.** The petition documents a 3–2
   split on the precise question (plus two implied circuits and the state
   supreme court below), and — importantly — the respondent *conceded the
   split in its briefing below* ("there is a split among the circuits as to
   the applicability of § 504 to independent contractors"). Splits on the
   meaning of a federal statute between federal circuits and a state court of
   last resort are core Rule 10 territory.
2. **The Court called for a response.** The docket shows the respondent
   waived, the petition was distributed for the 6/18/2026 conference, and on
   June 11, 2026 a response was requested (now due Aug. 12 after an
   extension). A CFR means at least one chambers took the petition seriously —
   historically one of the strongest observable pre-grant signals, moving a
   paid petition from the ~5% paid base rate into roughly the 10–20% range on
   its own.
3. **Expert counsel and a paid petition.** The UCLA School of Law Supreme
   Court Clinic (Stuart Banner, counsel of record) filed the petition; clinic
   petitions are pre-selected for certworthiness and professionally framed.
4. **Vehicle strengths.** Final judgment (unlike *Fleming*, denied in 2010 in
   an interlocutory posture), a threshold holding that was outcome-dispositive
   (summary judgment and a jury award were reversed solely on the
   employee-status ground), and undisputed facts.

Deny-side considerations — why the probability still sits well below 0.5:

1. **The brutal base rate.** Modern discretionary cert petitions grant at
   ~2.5–3% overall (statpack, OT2023–OT2025); paid petitions at ~5.4%
   (OT2025 paid fee class). Even strong CFR'd split petitions are more often
   denied than granted: the Court calls for responses in far more cases than
   it grants, and many conceded splits are left to percolate.
2. **Vehicle wrinkles the BIO will press.** Petitioner's claim is a
   *retaliation* claim (for advocacy on behalf of her disabled son), not a
   direct disability-discrimination claim by a disabled contractor — layering
   an associational/retaliation posture on top of the coverage question. She
   was also a Kelly Services staffing-agency employee rather than a classic
   independent contractor, which lets the respondent argue the question
   presented is not cleanly posed. Damages are modest ($3,500 compensatory +
   $3,500 punitive), which does not bar cert but does nothing for it.
3. **The Court has passed once before** on this exact conflict (*Yuma
   Anesthesia v. Fleming*, No. 09-1146, cert. denied 2010), and the low volume
   of intervening petitions suggests the issue, while recurring, is not
   producing heavy litigation pressure.
4. **No relists yet and no BIO yet.** The strongest observable signals
   (post-BIO distribution and relists) have not had a chance to accrue; as of
   the snapshot the case simply awaits a response due Aug. 12, 2026, pointing
   to a long-conference-or-later disposition in OT2026.

## Synthesis

Anchor: paid-petition rate ~5%. The CFR alone moves the estimate to roughly
10–20%. The conceded, well-percolated split with a state court of last resort
on the shallow side, clinic counsel, and a final-judgment vehicle push toward
the upper end and beyond; the retaliation/staffing-agency wrinkles, the small
stakes, and the Court's 2010 denial on the same conflict pull back. I land at
**P(grant) = 0.25**. A GVR is essentially off the table (no intervening
Supreme Court decision on point), so the grant mass is plenary; the likeliest
single disposition remains **denied** (~0.72), with dismissal/withdrawal
residual (~0.03).
