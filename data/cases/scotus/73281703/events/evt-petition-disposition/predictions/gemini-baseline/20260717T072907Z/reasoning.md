# Reasoning: Brandi Greer v. Benton School District

## Legal Question
Whether independent contractors have the same right as employees to sue for discrimination under section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. § 794.

## Case Facts and Posture
This is a paid petition (Case 25-1197) arising from the Supreme Court of Arkansas. The petitioner, represented by the UCLA Supreme Court Clinic, alleges a deep circuit split on whether Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act extends anti-discrimination protections to independent contractors or only to employees. 

## Analysis of Signals
- **Circuit Split**: The petition alleges a split, and an MCP CourtListener search confirms it. For example, the Fifth Circuit has held that independent contractors may sue under Section 504 (*Flynn v. Distinctive Home Care, Inc.*, 812 F.3d 422 (5th Cir. 2016)), while the Eighth Circuit has held they may not (*Wojewski v. Rapid City Reg'l Hosp.*, 450 F.3d 338 (8th Cir. 2006)).
- **Response Requested**: The Supreme Court requested a response on June 11, 2026, indicating active interest from at least one Justice.
- **Relists/CVSG**: The case has not yet gone to conference (the BIO deadline was extended to August 12), so there are no relists or CVSG signals yet.
- **Base Rates**: The baseline grant rate for paid petitions is approximately 3.0%. The presence of a genuine circuit split and an affirmative request for a response significantly elevates this probability. 

## Conclusion
While the vast majority of petitions are denied, this case possesses the hallmarks of a cert-worthy petition: a clean, acknowledged circuit split on a question of federal statutory interpretation, capable clinic counsel, and an affirmative signal of interest from the Court (Response Requested). Because the Brief in Opposition has not yet been filed, we cannot assess any vehicle arguments the respondent might raise. Despite the strong signals for a grant, the absolute probability of a grant remains below 50% due to the structural base rate of denials. I estimate a 35% probability of a grant.
