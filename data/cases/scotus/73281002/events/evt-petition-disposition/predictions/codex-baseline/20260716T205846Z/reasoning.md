# Prediction

I predict **denial**, with a **0.42 probability of any grant**. The grant probability includes a plenary grant, a grant in part, or a GVR (including a possible mootness vacatur); denial remains the single likeliest disposition.

## Question and governing considerations

The petition asks how constitutional challenges to neutral mail-ballot rules should be reviewed. It presents three related questions: whether a nondiscriminatory rule imposing only the usual burdens of voting is constitutional without further balancing; whether *McDonald v. Board of Election Commissioners* limits review of a mail-voting rule to rational basis when in-person voting remains available; and, under *Anderson-Burdick*, whether a minimally burdensome rule receives rational-basis review and whether burden means the cost of compliance or the consequence of noncompliance.

At the certiorari stage, the main considerations are not simply which side has the better merits argument, but whether the case presents a developed and outcome-relevant conflict, an important recurring federal question, and a clean vehicle. The underlying merits framework weighs the burden on voters against the State's asserted election-administration interests, with the degree of scrutiny disputed here.

## Grant signals

The docket has unusually strong indicators of serious Court interest. This is a paid petition from a published Third Circuit decision invalidating a state election rule. Rehearing was denied by a closely divided 7–6 court. The petition identifies conflicts over the application of *McDonald*, the scrutiny applied to minimally burdensome election rules, and whether burden is measured by compliance cost or noncompliance consequences. CourtListener searches confirmed that the competing lower-court lines are real: the Seventh Circuit's mail-voting cases apply the *McDonald* rational-basis approach, while the Ninth Circuit's *Arizona Democratic Party v. Hobbs* addresses the competing burden formulation.

The procedural history is more predictive than the petition's advocacy. The Court initially distributed the petition, requested responses, received full adversarial briefing, distributed it for the June 25 conference, and on June 29 invited the Solicitor General to express the views of the United States. The CVSG is a major positive signal. The related Commonwealth petition, No. 25-967, reinforces that the controversy is institutionally significant rather than a private dispute. The subject is nationally important, and the petition's position is plausibly attractive to Justices who favor clearer limits on open-ended election-law balancing.

## Reasons to expect denial or delay

The vehicle is not clean enough to make grant more likely than not before the Solicitor General speaks. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court's intervening *Center for Coalfield Justice* ruling requires notice and preserves a provisional-ballot opportunity for at least some voters, altering a premise of the panel's burden analysis. The provisioned brief in opposition explains that ballots received or reviewed near or after the poll-closing deadline may still leave no practical cure, so the factual significance of that change is itself contested.

More importantly, separate Pennsylvania constitutional litigation concerning the date requirement may eliminate or narrow the live federal controversy. The petition acknowledges that an adverse state-law ruling could lead to a mootness GVR rather than plenary review. The three questions are also broader than necessary to resolve this particular ballot-envelope rule, giving the Court options to wait for a cleaner case, deny after the Solicitor General's recommendation, or dispose of the case procedurally. A CVSG demonstrates interest but does not itself imply four votes to grant.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025 Term petitions and a 2.9% rate for modern petitions originating in the Third Circuit. Those are weak unconditional anchors. The more relevant cut is the CVSG bucket: 27.1% granted among 59 resolved modern CVSG petitions, with 71.2% denied and 1.7% dismissed. These cuts overlap and should not be multiplied.

I move above the CVSG anchor because this case combines a published invalidation of state law, a narrow en banc vote, identifiable inter-circuit disagreement, a companion sovereign petition, extensive amici participation, and substantial national stakes. I stop at 0.42 because the Solicitor General's position is not yet known and the state-law/cure developments create unusually concrete vehicle and mootness risks. The forecast is therefore close enough that a favorable Solicitor General brief would materially change it, but denial is presently the modal outcome.

No individual certiorari votes are predicted. The public record supports an institutional-interest inference, not reliable Justice-by-Justice attribution.
