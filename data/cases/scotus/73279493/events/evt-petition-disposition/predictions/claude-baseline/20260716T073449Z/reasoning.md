# Watson v. Bradsher, No. 25-651 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.08.**

## The case

Stanley Watson, a former DeKalb County commissioner, lost his wallet at a
nightclub in 2012, genuinely (but mistakenly) believed two women had stolen
it, and had them detained by off-duty police. A Georgia jury returned a
general verdict of $150,500 for slander, false imprisonment, and battery.
In Watson's Chapter 7 case the bankruptcy court held the slander debt
dischargeable — his genuine belief negated intent to speak falsely — but the
false-imprisonment debt nondischargeable under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6) because
he intended the confinement itself. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed in a
published opinion, In re Watson, 147 F.4th 1330 (11th Cir. Aug. 4, 2025),
holding the "injury" was the confinement (not its unlawfulness) and implying
malice from the objective excessiveness of Watson's conduct.

## The questions presented

1. Whether "injury" in § 523(a)(6) means mere harm (detriment in fact) or
   requires intent to invade a legally protected interest, such that a
   genuine-but-mistaken belief of legal justification negates willfulness.
2. Whether a false-imprisonment debt is nondischargeable where the debtor
   subjectively believed the confinement lawful, or whether courts may imply
   malice from objective "excessiveness."

## Merits of the cert case

**For a grant:**

- **The split is real.** Since Kawaauhau v. Geiger, 523 U.S. 57 (1998), the
  circuits have genuinely divided on § 523(a)(6)'s intent standard: the
  Sixth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits require subjective intent to injure (or
  subjective knowledge of substantial certainty — Markowitz, Su, Moore),
  while the Fifth and Eleventh accept objective substantial certainty or
  implied malice from objectively wrongful/excessive conduct (Miller, Kane).
  The petition characterizes it fairly.
- **CFR.** The Court called for a response on February 2, 2026, after the
  petition had been distributed for the February 20 conference — respondents
  had evidently waived. A call for a response means someone in the building
  looked twice; CFR'd paid petitions grant at several times the paid base
  rate.
- **Clean-vehicle argument.** The bankruptcy court made an undisturbed
  factual finding that Watson "genuinely believed" the theft occurred, and
  the bifurcated result — the same mental state discharged the slander debt
  but not the false-imprisonment debt — isolates the definitional question
  crisply. The petition's strongest card.
- **A fresh hook.** Medical Marijuana, Inc. v. Horn, 604 U.S. 593 (2025),
  discussed "injury" vs. "harm" in civil RICO and said context determines
  the choice; the petition argues courts will wrongly import Horn's broad
  reading into bankruptcy. Recent Court attention to the word makes the
  question more salient than it was five years ago.
- **Professional presentation.** Paid petition, big-firm counsel (Nelson
  Mullins), published circuit opinion (147 F.4th), extension granted by
  Justice Thomas.

**Against a grant:**

- **The Court has tolerated this split for 25+ years.** The
  subjective/objective § 523(a)(6) divide has been presented in many
  petitions since Geiger (Berge itself, the Su line, others) and the Court
  has passed every time. A mature, repeatedly-denied split is weak evidence
  of certworthiness absent something new; Horn is a hook but not a
  intervening change in § 523 law.
- **Unattractive facts and modest stakes.** A former county commissioner who
  had two women arrested over a wallet he later found in his own car, with a
  $150,500 judgment. The equities run against the "honest but unfortunate
  debtor" framing, and the dollar stakes are small. The Court's recent
  bankruptcy grants (Bartenwerfer, Lac du Flambeau, Horn itself) had cleaner
  presentations or institutional respondents.
- **No visible amicus support** at the cert stage as of the snapshot — the
  usual accompaniment of a granted "circuit split" petition.
- **Latent vehicle wrinkle.** The underlying state judgment was a general
  verdict that did not allocate among slander, false imprisonment, and
  battery; the allocation litigation on remand adds procedural residue the
  petition glosses over.
- **QP1's framing is somewhat academic.** The injury-vs-harm Restatement
  distinction is a fresh gloss the courts of appeals have not squarely
  divided over in those terms; the entrenched split (QP2's territory) is
  about subjective vs. objective intent, which the Court has already
  declined to police.
