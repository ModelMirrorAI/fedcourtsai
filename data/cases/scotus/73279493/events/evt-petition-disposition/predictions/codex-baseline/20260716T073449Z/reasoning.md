## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether a debt for false imprisonment is a debt for a “willful and malicious injury” under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6) when the debtor deliberately caused confinement but genuinely and mistakenly believed the confinement was justified. It also asks whether “injury” means factual harm or the invasion of a legally protected interest. Under *Kawaauhau v. Geiger*, § 523(a)(6) reaches acts done with actual intent to cause injury, not merely intentional acts that happen to cause injury; negligent or reckless injury is insufficient.

The provisioned petition and its questions-presented extract supply the legal and factual record used here. The petition reports that Watson believed two women had taken his wallet, pressed off-duty police to detain them, and found the wallet in his car the next day. A state jury returned a general verdict on slander, false imprisonment, and battery. In the later bankruptcy litigation, the slander portion was held dischargeable because Watson believed his accusation, while the false-imprisonment portion was held nondischargeable because he intended the confinement and his conduct was found wrongful and excessive. The Eleventh Circuit’s published opinion affirmed.

## Certworthiness

There is a real doctrinal disagreement in the lower courts over formulations of § 523(a)(6). The petition identifies decisions using subjective-intent language and contrasts them with the Fifth Circuit’s objective-substantial-certainty formulation. CourtListener research also confirmed that the Sixth Circuit in *MarketGraphics Research Group v. Berge*, 953 F.3d 907 (6th Cir. 2020), described a circuit disagreement over unitary and two-pronged tests and required subjective intent to injure. Those features, a published federal appellate opinion, and the Court’s request for a response are positive grant signals.

The vehicle is nevertheless weak for resolving the asserted split. The judgment rests on the intentional confinement itself, not on a merely reckless accidental consequence. Even *Berge* explains that subjective intent can be inferred where the knowing act is itself the injury. The petition therefore does not establish that the Sixth, Ninth, or Tenth Circuit would discharge this particular false-imprisonment debt; its outcome-determinative split assertion is largely inferential. The Eleventh Circuit separately analyzed willfulness and malice, while the petition’s cited split partly concerns whether those are separate elements. The fact-specific findings of repeated threats, pressure on police, and objectively excessive conduct also offer alternative grounds and make the case a poor vehicle for a clean definition of “injury.” The general state-court verdict and the bankruptcy court’s later allocation of damages add procedural clutter. No dissent below, CVSG, relist, amicus support, or respondent’s merits presentation appears in the provisioned inputs.
