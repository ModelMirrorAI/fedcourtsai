# Reasoning

**Legal Question & Standard:**
The petition presents a question regarding the interpretation of 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(6), which excepts from a bankruptcy discharge any debt for a "willful and malicious injury." Specifically, the petition asks whether the term "injury" denotes mere factual harm (an objective standard) or requires the intent to invade a legally protected interest (a subjective standard). The Supreme Court previously held in *Kawaauhau v. Geiger*, 523 U.S. 57 (1998), that the exception covers only acts done with the actual intent to cause injury. The circuits are split on how to apply *Geiger* when a debtor intentionally commits an act causing harm but genuinely believes the act is legally justified. The Fifth and Eleventh Circuits apply an objective standard (or imply malice from "excessive" conduct), whereas the Sixth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits apply a stricter subjective standard requiring intent to cause a legal injury.

**Facts Driving the Outcome:**
The petitioner, Watson, a former county commissioner, became intoxicated at a bar, falsely accused two women of stealing his wallet, and used his political influence to pressure police into detaining them. A state court jury returned a general verdict against him for slander, false imprisonment, and battery. In his subsequent bankruptcy proceedings, the bankruptcy court found that Watson "genuinely believed" the women had stolen his wallet. Applying this finding, the lower courts ruled that his debt for slander was dischargeable (because he did not subjectively intend to speak falsely), but his debt for false imprisonment was nondischargeable because his conduct was objectively "excessive" and he intended the confinement itself, even if he believed it was lawful. This bifurcated result based on identical conduct perfectly isolates the legal question and confirms the depth of the circuit split.

**Reasoning for Probability (P(Grant) = 0.15):**
The petition is a very strong candidate for certiorari on the merits of the legal question: it presents a deep, acknowledged, and outcome-determinative circuit split on a recurring issue of federal bankruptcy law. Furthermore, the lower court's bifurcated ruling makes it a legally clean vehicle, isolating the statutory interpretation question. The docket also shows a "Response Requested" entry, which significantly elevates the base rate probability of a grant above the typical ~2.5-3.3% rate for paid petitions.

However, the Supreme Court frequently denies otherwise meritorious petitions if they present "bad facts" that make them poor vehicles for establishing a broad rule. The petitioner is highly unsympathetic: an intoxicated public official who abused his position to baselessly harass and detain two innocent women. The Court may be reluctant to grant certiorari to a case where ruling in favor of the petitioner would discharge a debt stemming from such egregious conduct, fearing that "bad facts make bad law." The Court might prefer to resolve this circuit split in a case involving a more typical and sympathetic "honest but unfortunate debtor" (e.g., a well-meaning shopkeeper making an honest mistake). Because of these severe vehicle problems, the petition is highly likely to be denied, despite the compelling legal issue.
