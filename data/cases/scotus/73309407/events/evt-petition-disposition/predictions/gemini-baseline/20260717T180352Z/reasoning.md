# Prediction: Denied

## The Legal Question
The petitioner challenges a state court decision on two grounds:
1. Whether he had "actual notice" of the acceptable level of THC in his bloodstream when operating a motor vehicle on the specific night in question.
2. Whether the case is "capable of repetition yet evading judicial review."

## Governing Standard
The Supreme Court grants certiorari for "compelling reasons," primarily to resolve splits of authority among federal courts of appeals or state courts of last resort, or to decide an important question of federal law that has not been, but should be, settled by the Court (Sup. Ct. R. 10). The Court rarely grants certiorari simply to correct alleged factual errors or misapplication of properly stated rules of law.

## Analysis
The petition raises issues that are highly fact-bound and primarily concern state law. The first question specifically asks whether the petitioner had actual notice "on the night in question," indicating a fact-specific inquiry rather than a broad legal principle or a split in authority. Furthermore, the second question suggests the underlying case might be moot and the petitioner is seeking an exception, which adds an additional procedural hurdle that makes the case a poor vehicle for Supreme Court review.

Additionally, the procedural posture strongly suggests a denial:
- The state of Michigan did not file a brief in opposition, and the case was distributed for conference without one. It is standard practice for the Court to call for a response if it is seriously considering granting a petition.
- The base rate for cert grants in paid petitions without a circuit split is exceptionally low (around 1-3%).

## Conclusion
Given the fact-bound nature of the questions presented, the absence of a response from the state, and the lack of any apparent circuit split, this petition is overwhelmingly likely to be denied. I assess the probability of a grant at 1%.
