# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.05% probability to any grant, including a plenary grant or GVR, and predict denial. I do not predict individual votes: the likely result is simply that the petition does not attract the four votes required for certiorari.

## Question and record

The petition asks whether Ricky Patterson had constitutionally adequate notice of an acceptable blood-THC level when he drove and whether the dispute is capable of repetition yet evading review. It says Patterson, charged after a motorcycle collision, had 4.6 ng/mL active THC, passed the responding trooper's field-impairment tests, and nevertheless was bound over after a preliminary examination under Michigan's impaired-driving law. The Michigan appellate courts denied interlocutory review. These facts come from the complete, 32-page provisioned petition and its extracted questions presented; no brief in opposition was provisioned (`record/documents/documents.json`).

The petition's strongest merits point is that blood THC does not map cleanly onto impairment and that Michigan authority requires an actual effect on driving rather than mere consumption. That makes the underlying evidentiary dispute nonfrivolous. But the federal question is poorly framed. A functional prohibition on driving "under the influence" can give notice without specifying a numerical blood threshold, while disagreement over whether this record establishes impairment is primarily fact-bound. The requested judicial adoption of an 8.2 ng/mL formula also resembles policymaking and is not supported by a developed conflict among lower courts. The petition cites no split over a federal constitutional rule.

## Threshold and vehicle problems

The decisive weakness is finality. The petition expressly seeks review of denials of interlocutory leave during an ongoing state criminal prosecution, not a final judgment after trial and direct appeal. Section 1257 ordinarily permits review only after a final judgment from the state's highest court. The Court's finality cases allow narrow exceptions, but nothing in the petition shows that later review would be unavailable or that the federal issue must be decided immediately. CourtListener research surfaced *Jefferson v. City of Tarrant*, 522 U.S. 75 (1997), describing a still-pending state matter as brought to the Court too soon, and *Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn*, 420 U.S. 469 (1975), the leading source for the limited exceptions. Patterson can preserve a federal due-process argument through trial and ordinary appellate review.

The second question does not repair that defect. "Capable of repetition yet evading review" is a mootness exception, but this prosecution is not moot and the claimed issue need not evade post-judgment review. *United States v. Sanchez-Gomez*, 584 U.S. 381 (2018), confirms the narrowness of that exception in criminal proceedings.

Other certworthiness signals are absent as of the snapshot. The docket shows only the petition and distribution for the September 28 conference, with no response, amicus support, CVSG, or relist. The petition arises from a preliminary probable-cause ruling, turns heavily on its own accident and toxicology record, and asks the Court both to interpret Michigan law and create a nationwide scientific threshold.

## Calibration

The committed corpus statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025-Term petitions, but only 0.8% for petitions with no relist; the no-CVSG bucket is 3.0%. This petition is materially weaker than those broad buckets because of the apparent jurisdictional defect, lack of a split, and poor vehicle posture. Those factors justify reducing the forecast to 0.05%. A GVR is also unlikely because the record identifies no intervening controlling decision or other natural remand trigger.

The `big_case_score` of 0.62 is separate from grant likelihood. A Supreme Court holding that due process requires a nationwide numerical THC threshold could materially affect impaired-driving regimes in marijuana-legal jurisdictions, but the case itself is an interlocutory, record-specific vehicle.
