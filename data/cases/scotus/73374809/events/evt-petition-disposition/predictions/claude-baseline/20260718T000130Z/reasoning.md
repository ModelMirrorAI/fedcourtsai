# scotus/73374809 — Matsumura v. Court of Appeal of California, First Appellate District, Division Five, et al. (No. 25-1301)

**Prediction: denied. P(grant) = 0.002.**

## The case

Kenneth Matsumura, proceeding pro se (paid docket), petitions for certiorari
from the Supreme Court of California's November 13, 2024 denial of review
(S287041). The underlying dispute is the revocation of his California medical
license by the Medical Board of California, upheld by the San Francisco
Superior Court; the California Court of Appeal rejected his appeal on the
procedural ground that the lower-court record was not provided (the petition
says a clerk refused his USB drive), and the state supreme court denied review
without comment. The named respondents are the state courts themselves, with
the Medical Board as a real party in interest.

The petition was docketed May 22, 2026, the response was due June 22, 2026
(the docket shows no brief in opposition — a state-court respondent
predictably files none), and on July 8, 2026 it was distributed for the
September 28, 2026 conference — the first distribution, zero relists, no CVSG.

## The questions presented

The QP section (provisioned `questions-presented.txt`) does not state a
certworthy legal question. It recites Article III and the Commerce Clause,
asserts an unsupported proposition that the Commerce Clause guarantees a
physician licensed in any state an "automatic right" to practice on Indian
reservations, and then asks whether the Supreme Court "has the right to
intervene" when a state regulatory action blocks a "lifesaving medical
therapy" that could save Medicare money. These are rhetorical, fact-bound
grievances, not questions on which lower courts are divided.

## Why this is a near-certain denial

1. **No circuit split, no unsettled federal question.** The petition (17
   pages, read in full) cites no conflicting decisions of any court; its only
   authorities are a California statute (BPC § 2234.1) and constitutional
   text. The Reasons for Granting section argues the equities of the
   petitioner's cancer therapy and the finances of Indian reservations, not
   any legal conflict.
2. **Vehicle defects abound.** The state judgment rests on an independent and
   adequate state procedural ground (failure to provide the appellate
   record), which ordinarily forecloses review under 28 U.S.C. § 1257. The
   petition names the state courts as respondents — courts are not proper
   adverse parties to their own judgments. No federal question appears to have
   been pressed or passed on below.
3. **Fact-bound administrative dispute.** At bottom this asks error-correction
   of a state licensing proceeding — testimony weight, record-keeping
   findings, fee reasonableness — territory the Court does not enter.
4. **Base rates.** Modern discretionary-cert petitions deny at ~95%
   (statpack, per-Term rows: 2.5–3.3% grant). Petitions at zero relists deny
   at 97.3% with a 0.8% grant rate; this one awaits its first (long)
   conference. Petitions originating from the California state courts in the
   corpus run 83–100% denied with grants at 0–8% (Supreme Court of California
   bucket: 0 grants in 19). Pro se petitions — even paid ones — are granted
   at rates far below the overall average; effectively never without a
   QP a court could take.
5. **No countervailing signal.** No CVSG, no amici indicated on the docket, no
   relists, no response filed, no companion case.

Stacking a paid pro se filing, a defective QP, an adequate-state-ground
posture, and improper respondents onto the ~0.8% zero-relist baseline, the
honest estimate sits well below the base rate. I set P(grant, including GVR)
at 0.002. A GVR is implausible (no intervening decision is implicated);
dismissal is unlikely for a paid, docketed petition, so the modal and
near-certain disposition is a straight denial — most likely in the
October 2026 order list following the September 28 long conference.
