# Reasoning

## The Legal Question and Standard
The petitioner, acting pro se, challenges an action of the Medical Board of California (and subsequent rejections by California state courts) related to a cancer therapy he developed and his practice of medicine. The petition attempts to frame the issue as a violation of Article III and the Commerce Clause of the US Constitution, asserting that the state regulatory action deprives citizens of other states and Indian tribes of lifesaving medical therapy and costs the federal government Medicare money.

For the Supreme Court to grant certiorari, the petition typically must present a compelling question of federal law, a circuit split, or a conflict with Supreme Court precedent. State disciplinary or regulatory matters are almost exclusively the domain of state courts, barring a significant and well-pled constitutional violation.

## Case Specifics and Analysis
The petitioner is a physician whose alternative cancer therapy and clinic operations appear to have faced scrutiny or restriction by the California Medical Board. The California Supreme Court denied his petition for review without comment, which is the standard disposition for such matters in state courts.

The petition presented to the US Supreme Court is highly idiosyncratic and fact-bound. It does not present a cognizable legal question of national importance, nor does it identify any split among lower courts. Instead, it is an attempt to use broad constitutional provisions (Article III and the Commerce Clause) to relitigate a personal grievance against a state medical board. Pro se petitions of this nature, especially those lacking a focused legal question or lower court division, are routinely denied by the Court.

## Conclusion
Given the idiosyncratic nature of the petition, the lack of a clear federal question of national importance, and the pro se status of the petitioner raising a personal grievance against a state regulatory body, the probability of a grant is essentially zero. The predicted disposition is a denial of certiorari.
