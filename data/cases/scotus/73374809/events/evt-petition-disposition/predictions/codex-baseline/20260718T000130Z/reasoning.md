# Prediction: denied

I estimate a **0.1% probability of any grant** and predict a routine denial of certiorari. There is no meaningful indication that a GVR or a grant in part is more likely than plenary review.

## Question and posture

The petition asks, in broad terms, whether Article III and the Commerce Clause permit the Supreme Court or federal government to intervene when state medical discipline allegedly deprives out-of-state citizens or members of an Indian tribe of a lifesaving therapy and increases federal Medicare costs. The requested relief is review and reversal of the petitioner's California medical-license revocation.

The provisioned snapshot (`record/snapshots/2026-07-17.json`) identifies a paid petition from the Supreme Court of California, docketed May 22, 2026 and distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. It shows no relist, CVSG, response, or other signal of institutional interest. The petition is pro se. The snapshot and `record/documents/documents.json` provide the petition and extracted questions presented, but no brief in opposition.

The petition itself (`record/documents/petition.txt` and `record/documents/questions-presented.txt`) says the California Court of Appeal declined the appeal because the lower-court record was not supplied in an acceptable form and that the California Supreme Court denied review without comment. It does not supply a reasoned lower-court opinion deciding the asserted federal questions.

## Governing considerations

Certiorari ordinarily requires a compelling reason under Supreme Court Rule 10, such as a developed conflict among appellate courts or an important federal question actually decided below. The Court is not principally an error-correction forum. Review of a state judgment also requires a suitable final judgment and a properly presented federal question; an unresolved state procedural ground or failure to preserve the issue is a serious vehicle problem.

The petition identifies no conflict among courts, no contrary controlling decision, and no concrete federal rule adopted below. Article III defines federal judicial power but does not itself compel the Court to take a particular dispute. The Indian Commerce Clause gives Congress regulatory authority; the petition does not develop why it creates the petitioner's asserted personal right to retain a California license or why it displaces the state disciplinary judgment here.

The vehicle is especially weak. The petition principally disputes the medical board's factfinding, expert evidence, recordkeeping conclusions, and assessment of fees. Those are case-specific matters. Its account indicates that appellate review failed on a record-submission issue, creating a likely preservation or independent-state-ground obstacle. The petition also says tribal healthcare autonomy would permit practice without a valid state license but argues that revocation makes opening other tribal clinics practically harder. That weakens the claimed causal link between the state judgment and the broad federal questions.

Two corpus queries supplied recent granted and denied SCOTUS priors. The granted examples generally had developed federal appellate records, represented parties, and repeated distributions; even well-developed and salient represented petitions appeared among the denials. Those coarse priors are not close factual analogues but confirm that this petition lacks the features commonly visible in grants.

CourtListener searches for Supreme Court authority specifically combining physician licensing, reservations, and state licensing returned no directly relevant result. I do not infer from that search miss that no relevant doctrine exists; the petition's failure to identify and frame such authority is itself the predictive point.

No individual votes are predicted. A denial does not disclose the Justices' votes, and the record offers no sound basis to attribute a grant vote to any Justice.

## Significance

The underlying claims concern cancer treatment, tribal economic autonomy, interstate licensing, and potentially large public expenditures. A clean, precedential resolution of those subjects could matter nationally. This petition, however, is an individualized professional-discipline dispute with an undeveloped federal theory and a poor procedural vehicle, so I assign a modest **0.25 big-case score**, distinct from the much lower grant probability.
