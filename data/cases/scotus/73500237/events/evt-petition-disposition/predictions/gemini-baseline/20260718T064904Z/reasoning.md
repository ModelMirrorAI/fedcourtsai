# Reasoning

## Case Summary
This case involves a Rehabilitation Act claim by a federal employee (Petitioner Stephanie Redding against the Secretary of Homeland Security). The Questions Presented allege a failure to accommodate and a failure to engage in a good-faith interactive process under 29 C.F.R. § 1630.2(o)(3). Specifically, Petitioner claims the government concealed that accepting a reassignment would eliminate a pending disability-retirement benefit and closed the accommodation process prematurely. The petition also alleges that the Fourth Circuit's decision conflicts with *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Sch.*, 605 U.S. 335 (2025), and a multi-circuit consensus.

## Legal and Procedural Analysis
The most decisive factor in predicting the disposition of this petition is the procedural posture: the Solicitor General, representing the federal respondents, filed a waiver of the right to respond on June 17, 2026. The petition was subsequently distributed for the September 28, 2026 Long Conference.

The Supreme Court rarely, if ever, grants a petition for certiorari without first hearing from the respondent. While the Court could relist the petition and call for a response (CFR) if a Justice finds the petition's claims of a circuit split or conflict with recent precedent compelling, a waiver by the SG strongly signals the government's view that the petition lacks merit or presents a poor vehicle for reviewing the question.

Even with an alleged circuit split, the overall base rate for paid cert petitions is extremely low (around 2.5% to 3.0% for recent Terms). Cert petitions originating from the Fourth Circuit have a historical grant rate of about 4.6%. The lack of any pre-conference request for a response further depresses the probability of a grant.

## Prediction
Given the base rates for paid petitions and the strong negative signal from the Solicitor General's waiver of response, the petition is highly likely to be denied. If the Court had serious interest, it would likely have requested a response prior to the long conference or will do so out of the conference, but the ultimate disposition remains overwhelmingly likely to be a denial.

**Predicted Disposition:** denied
**Probability:** 0.02
