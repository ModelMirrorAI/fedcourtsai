# Prediction: denied

I assign a 1.2% probability to any grant and predict a denial of certiorari. This is a forward prediction made from the July 18, 2026 snapshot, the provisioned questions presented and petition, historical corpus context, and pre-disposition legal research. I did not seek or encounter this petition's disposition.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the Rehabilitation Act's reasonable-accommodation framework requires a good-faith interactive process when a federal employer allegedly concealed that accepting a reassignment would end a pending disability-retirement claim and closed the process before the employee completed training. It also asserts that the Fourth Circuit conflicts with other circuits and with *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, and analogizes the employee's acceptance to involuntary consent under *Bumper v. North Carolina*.

Certiorari ordinarily requires an important, outcome-determinative federal question and a clean conflict among courts of appeals. The Court is unlikely to grant merely to correct a fact-bound application of settled accommodation principles, particularly when an independent ground supports the judgment.

## Record signals

The provisioned petition supplies the only merits briefing; the snapshot records that the Solicitor General waived a response on June 17. The case is a paid petition from a published, unanimous Fourth Circuit decision and is distributed for the September 28 conference. Those features give it somewhat more credibility than an ordinary filing, and the asserted disagreement over interactive-process doctrine is a genuine potential cert vehicle in the abstract.

The vehicle is nevertheless poor:

- The Fourth Circuit held both that Redding was not a qualified individual for her desired Regional Coordinator position and, alternatively, that TSA reasonably accommodated her through reassignment. Even a ruling favorable to Redding on process obligations would leave the threshold qualified-individual ground in the way.
- The claimed split is not cleanly outcome-determinative. *Strife v. Aldine Independent School District*, 138 F.4th 237 (5th Cir. 2025), analyzed unjustified delay as a failure to make a reasonable accommodation. It repeated that bad-faith participation violates the ADA when it leads to a failure reasonably to accommodate; it did not establish a free-standing process tort whenever the ultimate accommodation was reasonable. That formulation can coexist with the Fourth Circuit's result more readily than the petition suggests.
- *A.J.T.* rejected a heightened intent standard for schoolchildren's ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims. It did not decide the elements or remedies for an employment interactive-process claim, so the asserted direct conflict is attenuated.
- The concealment and premature-closure allegations are fact-intensive and intertwined with events after reassignment. The court of appeals emphasized that review was confined to the complaint, and its opinion characterizes the claim litigated below as seeking permanent retention in the Regional Coordinator role. That mismatch creates preservation and record problems.
- The proposed *Bumper* consent analogy imports Fourth Amendment search doctrine into an employment-accommodation dispute and is unlikely to attract four votes.
- The government's response waiver is a strong practical negative signal. There is no CVSG, call for a response, relist, noted dissent, or competing merits brief in the provisioned record.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 2.5% grant rate across 2025 Term cert petitions and about 5.4% for paid petitions. Fourth Circuit petitions have an estimated 4.6% grant rate, but petitions with no relist and those without a CVSG are materially lower (about 0.8% and 3.0%, respectively). This petition's published opinion and facial split theory keep it above a near-zero nuisance-filing estimate, while the independent ground, weak doctrinal conflict, fact-bound posture, and response waiver push it below the paid-petition baseline. A plenary grant is much more plausible than a GVR if the Court acts, but denial remains by far the likeliest disposition.

No individual cert votes are predicted because the Justices' conference votes are not public and the record does not support Justice-specific estimates.
