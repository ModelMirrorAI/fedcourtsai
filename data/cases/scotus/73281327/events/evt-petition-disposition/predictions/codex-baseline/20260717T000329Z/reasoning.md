# Prediction

I predict a **grant, vacate, and remand (GVR)**, with a **0.62 probability of any grant-side disposition**. This is not a forecast that the Court will hear plenary argument in Pileggi. It is a forecast that the Court will continue holding this petition for *Salazar v. Paramount Global*, No. 25-459, and, more likely than not, later vacate and remand in light of the decision there. If *Salazar* is affirmed, denial is the clear alternative.

## Question and posture

The Video Privacy Protection Act (VPPA) defines a consumer to include a “subscriber of goods or services from a video tape service provider.” The question is whether the subscriber must obtain an audiovisual good or service, or whether a subscription to any good or service from an entity that qualifies as a video tape service provider is enough. Nicole Pileggi alleges that she subscribed to a Washington Examiner newsletter and that the publisher disclosed her website video-viewing history and Facebook identifier through a tracking pixel.

The procedural signal dominates the ordinary certiorari base rate. The provisioned snapshot shows a paid petition from the D.C. Circuit, filed February 27, 2026; an opposition filed April 3; and distribution for the May 14 conference. It shows no later disposition as of July 16. More importantly, the petition says the Court granted *Salazar* on January 26, 2026 to decide the same question and expressly requests a hold followed, if appropriate, by a GVR. The opposition agrees that this petition raises the same question in the same posture, agrees it should be held, and says the eventual result should be denial if Paramount wins or GVR if Salazar wins. That agreement, coupled with the petition's survival past its first listed conference, makes a hold highly likely.

Under the Court's usual companion-case practice, a GVR is appropriate when an intervening merits decision may affect the judgment below and the lower court should reconsider. A GVR would still count as a grant on the binary prediction axis. The D.C. Circuit could then address respondent's preserved alternative grounds, including whether the publisher is a video tape service provider, whether a free newsletter creates subscriber status, and whether the alleged disclosure was knowing. Those alternatives make plenary review in this case less attractive, but they do not materially undercut the GVR route requested by both parties.

## Conditional merits forecast

The 0.62 probability is principally the estimated chance that *Salazar* produces a plaintiff-favorable ruling, discounted slightly for an unusual disposition or a decision that does not cleanly disturb the judgment here.

Pileggi and Salazar have substantial textual arguments. “Goods or services” is not expressly modified by “audiovisual,” while the adjacent definition of personally identifiable information expressly says “specific video materials or services.” The Second and Seventh Circuits adopted that reading, as did Judge Bloomekatz's dissent in the Sixth Circuit. The Seventh Circuit's agreement is meaningful because its reading is straightforwardly textual: provider status and the particular good supplied are separate concepts.

The contrary argument is nonetheless serious and keeps the estimate close. The Sixth and D.C. Circuits read the definition in the context of a statute focused throughout on video transactions. The D.C. Circuit also relied on the statute's substantial liquidated and punitive remedies, administrability concerns, and the potentially sweeping conversion of the VPPA into a general internet-privacy regime. The current Court has sometimes used whole-statute context to narrow facially broad language. Those considerations create a realistic affirmance-and-denial path.

On balance, the clean unmodified text, meaningful variation elsewhere in the same subsection, a developed circuit split, and the usual reversal tendency in a granted case put a *Salazar* reversal modestly above even. Conditional on reversal, a GVR here is overwhelmingly more likely than a separate plenary grant. Conditional on affirmance, denial is overwhelmingly likely.
