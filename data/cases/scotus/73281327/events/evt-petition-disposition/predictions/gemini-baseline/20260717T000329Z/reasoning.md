# Reasoning: Pileggi v. Washington Newspaper Publishing Co.

## The Legal Question
The case asks whether the Video Privacy Protection Act (VPPA) requires a plaintiff to purchase, rent, or subscribe to an *audio-visual* good or service to qualify as a "consumer," or whether subscribing to *any* good or service (such as a free email newsletter) from a video tape service provider suffices. 

## Case Posture and Key Facts
Petitioner Nicole Pileggi subscribed to a free email newsletter from the Washington Examiner and watched videos on its website. She alleged the Washington Examiner violated the VPPA by sharing her video-viewing history with Facebook via a tracking pixel. The D.C. Circuit affirmed the dismissal of her claim, holding she was not a statutory "consumer" because her newsletter subscription had no connection to the website's audio-visual content.

In her cert petition, Pileggi notes that the Supreme Court has already granted certiorari in *Salazar v. Paramount Global* (No. 25-459) to resolve a circuit split on this exact question. She requests her petition be held pending the decision in *Salazar*. Respondent Washington Newspaper Publishing agrees that the petition should be held for *Salazar* and makes that the entirety of its Brief in Opposition.

## Analysis and Predicted Outcome
Because both parties agree this petition presents the exact question already pending before the Court in *Salazar*, the Court is near-certain to hold the petition. 

The ultimate disposition of a held petition depends on the outcome of the lead case. Historically, the Supreme Court reverses the lower court in roughly 60% to 70% of the cases it accepts for merits review. Applying that base rate to *Salazar*, the petitioner in *Salazar* is favored to win, which would establish that the Sixth Circuit—and by extension the D.C. Circuit here—construed "consumer" too narrowly. 

If the Court reverses in *Salazar*, the mechanical disposition for *Pileggi* will be a Grant, Vacate, and Remand (GVR) so the D.C. Circuit can reconsider its judgment in light of the new precedent (and address the respondent's alternative grounds for dismissal). Thus, the most likely outcome for this petition is a GVR, with the probability tracking the petitioner's overall likelihood of success in *Salazar* (approximately 65%).
