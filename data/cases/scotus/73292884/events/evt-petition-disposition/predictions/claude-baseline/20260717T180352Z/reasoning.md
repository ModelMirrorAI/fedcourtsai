# Rio Grande Foundation v. Toulouse Oliver, No. 25-1248 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.15.**

## The case

Rio Grande Foundation (RGF), a New Mexico 501(c)(3) issue-advocacy nonprofit
represented by the Liberty Justice Center, challenges the New Mexico Campaign
Reporting Act's donor-disclosure requirements as applied to "independent
expenditures" defined to include ads that merely *mention* a candidate or
ballot question within 30/60 days of an election (N.M. Stat. Ann.
§ 1-19-26(Q)(3)(c)) — speech the parties agree is neither express advocacy nor
its functional equivalent (RGF's legislative "Freedom Index" scorecard). The
district court upheld the law; a divided Tenth Circuit panel affirmed (154
F.4th 1213), with Judge Hartz concurring only under compulsion of precedent and
Judge Eid dissenting on AFPF-tailoring grounds; rehearing en banc was denied
over an Eid dissent joined by Judge Tymkovich (162 F.4th 1251).

The three questions presented all reduce to whether *Buckley*'s
express-advocacy / "major purpose" limits (or strict scrutiny outright)
constrain campaign-finance donor disclosure applied to pure issue advocacy, and
whether *Citizens United*'s brief disclosure holding (558 U.S. at 369) extends
beyond the functional equivalent of express advocacy.

## Docket signals (from the 2026-07-17 snapshot)

Positive for grant:

- **Call for response.** Respondent waived (May 15, 2026); after distribution
  for the June 4 conference the Court **requested a response** (May 26, 2026),
  later extended to July 27, 2026. A CFR requires at least one chambers'
  interest and is the classic pre-grant screen: the large majority of granted
  paid petitions passed through one, though most CFR'd petitions are still
  denied (conditional grant rates in the empirical literature run roughly
  8–20%, versus ~5% for the paid class overall and near zero for waived
  petitions with no CFR).
- **Four cert-stage amicus briefs** (Americans for Prosperity Foundation +
  Manhattan Institute; National Taxpayers Union Foundation + People United for
  Privacy; Advancing American Freedom; Randy Elf) — organized, ideologically
  aligned support signaling salience.
- **Two dissenting circuit judges** below, with the panel dissent expressly
  grounded in *AFPF v. Bonta*'s tailoring analysis, plus a reluctant
  concurrence — a strong "vehicle prepared by a circuit judge" signal.
- **Doctrinal fit with this Court's direction.** The 6-Justice majority that
  decided *AFPF* (2021) tightened exacting scrutiny for compelled donor
  disclosure; Thomas has repeatedly urged revisiting disclosure doctrine
  (e.g., dissent from denial in *Delaware Strong Families v. Denn* (2016));
  Gorsuch (whose confirmation-hearing quote the petition deploys, and who is
  the Tenth Circuit's circuit Justice) granted the filing extension. The
  petition squarely tees up the acknowledged tension between *Buckley*'s
  express-advocacy limiting construction and *Citizens United*'s
  four-sentence extension of disclosure to electioneering communications.
- Paid petition, competent institutional counsel, clean stipulated record
  (no dispute that the speech is not express advocacy), final judgment.

Negative for grant:

- **No circuit split is pressed.** The petition argues error and importance.
  The courts of appeals have fairly uniformly *upheld*
  electioneering-communication-style disclosure applied to issue speech after
  *Citizens United* — CA1 in *Gaspee Project v. Mederos*, 13 F.4th 79 (2021),
  CA9 in *No on E v. Chiu*, CA10 here and in *Wyoming Gun Owners v. Gray* —
  so the Court would be granting to extend/revisit doctrine, not to resolve
  disagreement.
- **This exact line of petitions has been repeatedly denied.** From training
  knowledge (disclosed, pre-dating this petition, and corroborated by the
  uniform circuit case law retrieved above): cert was denied in *Delaware
  Strong Families* (2016, over dissent), *Independence Institute v. FEC*
  (2017, summarily affirmed), *Gaspee Project* (2022), and *No on E* (~2024)
  — the Court has so far declined every invitation to extend *AFPF* from
  charitable-donor disclosure to campaign-finance electioneering disclosure,
  where *Citizens United*'s 8-1 disclosure holding stands in the way.
- Disclosure (as opposed to spending limits) is the one campaign-finance
  device this Court has consistently blessed; granting QP3 (strict scrutiny)
  would be a major doctrinal move the Court can avoid by denying.
- A single state's statute with idiosyncratic thresholds and an opt-out
  provision gives the Court an easy "poor vehicle / splitless error
  correction" out; the CFR itself is also consistent with a chambers merely
  ensuring an adversarial record before denying.
