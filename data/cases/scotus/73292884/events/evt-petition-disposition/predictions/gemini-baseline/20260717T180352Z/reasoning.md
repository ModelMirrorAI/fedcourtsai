# Reasoning

## The Legal Question
The case presents First Amendment challenges to New Mexico's campaign finance disclosure law as applied to an issue-advocacy organization (a 501(c)(3) nonprofit) that engaged in "electioneering communications" (referencing candidates close to an election) but did not engage in express advocacy. The petition asks whether New Mexico's informational interest satisfies exacting scrutiny to justify compelling donor disclosure for such organizations, and whether applying the disclosure regime without requiring donor earmarking constitutes narrow tailoring under *Buckley v. Valeo* and *AFPF v. Bonta*. Finally, the petition asks whether strict scrutiny should apply instead of exacting scrutiny to disclosure requirements targeting pure political speech.

## The Record and Posture
The Tenth Circuit upheld the disclosure regime over a divided panel. Judge Eid dissented, arguing the law fails the narrow tailoring analysis of exacting scrutiny by unnecessarily burdening core political speech and ignoring retaliation concerns. Judge Hartz concurred in the judgment, stating he was bound by controlling precedent but wrote separately to express that the disclosure requirements are unjustified and harmful to the public interest. 

The petition was docketed on May 4, 2026. After the state respondent initially waived its right to respond, the Supreme Court issued a Call for Response (CFR) on May 26, 2026, indicating active interest from at least one Justice. Four cert-stage amicus briefs were filed by prominent conservative and libertarian groups, including the Americans for Prosperity Foundation, the National Taxpayers Union Foundation, and Advancing American Freedom, marking this as a highly salient case for the conservative legal movement.

## Probability and Predicted Outcome
Although the overall grant rate for paid petitions is low (around 5.3% per the 2025 Term statpack), several strong signals suggest a significantly elevated likelihood of a grant:
1. **Call for Response (CFR):** The Court's request for a response after a waiver is a classic signal of interest, raising the base rate of a grant substantially.
2. **High Salience (Amicus Support):** The presence of four cert-stage amicus briefs from prominent organizations underscores the importance of the issue to a key constituency of the current Court's conservative majority.
3. **Panel Division:** The Tenth Circuit panel was fractured, featuring a strong dissent from Judge Eid and a reluctant concurrence from Judge Hartz criticizing the binding precedent. 
4. **Subject Matter:** The Roberts Court has shown a sustained interest in First Amendment challenges to campaign finance and donor disclosure laws (e.g., *Citizens United*, *AFPF v. Bonta*). This petition provides a clear vehicle to extend *AFPF*'s narrow tailoring requirement to electioneering communications or to narrow the scope of the informational interest recognized in *Citizens United*.

Given the alignment of a fractured lower court opinion, high coordinated amicus support, a CFR, and a subject matter squarely within the conservative supermajority's jurisprudential priorities, the probability of a grant is very high compared to the base rate.
