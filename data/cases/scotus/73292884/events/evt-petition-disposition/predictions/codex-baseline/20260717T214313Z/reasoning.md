# Prediction: denied

I assign a **14% probability of any grant** and predict denial. If the Court grants, the likely form is plenary review rather than a GVR: the petition asks the Court to define the First Amendment rules governing donor disclosure, and the record identifies no intervening judgment that would supply an obvious basis for vacatur and remand.

## Question and governing law

The petition challenges New Mexico's disclosure regime as applied to an issue-advocacy organization whose proposed legislative scorecard names candidates but concededly does not contain express advocacy or its functional equivalent. Its three questions ask whether the state's informational interest reaches that speech, whether exacting scrutiny permits disclosure of general-fund donors without an earmarking limitation, and whether strict scrutiny should instead apply.

Under *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta*, exacting scrutiny requires a substantial relation to a sufficiently important interest and narrow tailoring, though not the least restrictive means. *Citizens United v. FEC* recognizes an informational interest in knowing who speaks about a candidate shortly before an election and applies exacting scrutiny to disclosure. The petition argues that *Citizens United* should not extend beyond express advocacy or its functional equivalent and that *Bonta* makes New Mexico's opt-out regime insufficiently tailored.

## Case-specific signals

The provisioned petition and questions presented supply real grant-positive signals. The case concerns core political speech and compelled association, subjects on which the Court has recently enforced meaningful limits. The Tenth Circuit panel divided; Judge Hartz wrote separately, Judge Eid dissented, and Judges Eid and Tymkovich dissented from denial of rehearing en banc. The Supreme Court docket snapshot also shows that a response was requested after an initial waiver and that four amicus groups or groupings filed in support. Those are stronger indicators of interest than an ordinary paid petition.

The countervailing considerations are stronger. The petition does not identify a conflict among courts of appeals. The related authorities surfaced in legal research—including *Human Life of Washington v. Brumsickle*, *The Gaspee Project v. Mederos*, and the Tenth Circuit's own *Independence Institute v. Williams*—reflect substantial precedent accepting disclosure for election-related communications beyond express advocacy. The Tenth Circuit majority relied on the timing, monetary thresholds, political-purpose definition, and donor opt-out to find a sufficient fit with the state's informational interest. The petition therefore principally asks the Court to narrow or distinguish existing doctrine rather than resolve a mature split.

The vehicle also has weaknesses. This is a facial challenge. The lower-court opinion records that Rio Grande Foundation conceded exacting scrutiny at oral argument and preserved strict scrutiny only for further review, weakening the third question. It also notes tension in the donor-harm evidence: the organization's president asserted likely donor attrition but testified that donors had not actually said they would stop donating if their information became public. No brief in opposition was provisioned because the requested response remained due on July 27, 2026, so the respondent's cert-stage vehicle and merits arguments are not yet available.

## Calibration

The committed statpack places 2025-Term paid petitions at about a 5.4% grant rate and modern petitions from the Tenth Circuit at about 5.0%. This petition deserves an upward adjustment for the requested response, supportive amici, divided panel, and en banc dissent. I stop at 14% because there is no asserted circuit split, no relist or CVSG, and the lower-court judgment rests on established disclosure precedents and several case-specific tailoring features. Denial remains substantially more likely than plenary review.

The **0.72 big-case score** reflects the potential breadth of a merits ruling: it could affect state campaign-finance disclosure laws and donor privacy for issue-advocacy organizations nationwide. That significance assessment is separate from the lower probability that the Court selects this particular vehicle.
