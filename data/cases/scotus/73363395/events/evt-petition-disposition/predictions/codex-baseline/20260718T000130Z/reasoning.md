# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.2% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari.

## Record and question

The July 17, 2026 snapshot identifies a paid petition from a Florida wrongful-death action. The trial court entered summary judgment for Uber and affiliated entities; Florida's Third District Court of Appeal affirmed, and the Florida Supreme Court declined jurisdiction. The petition's two questions ask whether the Seventh Amendment and the Fourteenth Amendment require the case to proceed to a jury so jurors can weigh the evidence and draw adverse inferences against Uber.

I used the complete, untruncated 58-page petition and its extracted questions presented, as recorded in `record/documents/documents.json`. The petition emphasizes evidence about the driver's multiple phones, uncertainty about whether he was logged into Uber's app, Uber's recordkeeping and control, and the petitioners' expert opinion. No brief in opposition was provisioned because the snapshot records that respondents waived a response on June 10. The petition was distributed on June 17 for the September 28 conference; as of the snapshot there was no CVSG, call for a response, conference relist, or other affirmative interest signal. The May 18 grant shown on the docket concerns the separate motion to file a supplemental appendix under seal, not the certiorari petition.

## Governing standard and merits

Supreme Court Rule 10 makes certiorari discretionary and ordinarily calls for a conflict among courts, a departure from accepted judicial practice, or an important unsettled federal question. Four votes are required to grant. Error correction in a record-specific application of summary-judgment law is normally insufficient.

The petition is an exceptionally weak vehicle under that standard:

- It identifies no split or recurring disagreement among courts. Its questions are framed around these named defendants and whether this particular evidentiary record should reach a jury.
- The central Seventh Amendment theory is foreclosed at a basic level. *Minneapolis & St. Louis Railroad v. Bombolis*, 241 U.S. 211 (1916), states that the Seventh Amendment governs federal proceedings and does not regulate state-court jury trials. The petition does not cite or confront that rule.
- The Fourteenth Amendment question merely repackages the assertion that the state court weighed evidence incorrectly. The petition supplies no developed, independent due-process rule that would constitutionalize an ordinary state summary-judgment dispute.
- Even accepting the petition's account of disputed phone and app evidence, the requested relief would require close review of a large case-specific record. That poor vehicle posture is compounded by the absence of a developed lower-court federal analysis in the petition.

The fatal accident and the involvement of Uber give the litigation some public salience. A broad ruling changing the federal constitutional treatment of civil juries in state courts would be important, which supports a 0.44 big-case score, but the petition's actual framing is narrow and its doctrinal premise is deeply unfavorable.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 3.0% grant rate for modern petitions with no CVSG and 0.8% for petitions with no relist. The 2025 Term paid-petition estimate is higher at about 5.4%, but this petition lacks the conflict, legal uncertainty, clean vehicle, response, and observable Court-interest signals that characterize plausible grants. The direct conflict with settled Seventh Amendment doctrine warrants a forecast materially below the unrelisted-petition rate. I see no intervening authority or mootness issue suggesting a GVR, so denial is much more likely than any other disposition.
