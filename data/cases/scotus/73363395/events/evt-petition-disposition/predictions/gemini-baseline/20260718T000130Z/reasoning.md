# Reasoning

## The Legal Question and Standard
The petitioner challenges a lower state court ruling involving a wrongful death civil trial against Uber Technologies, Inc. and its subsidiaries. The petition raises two questions presented: whether a jury trial should proceed under the Seventh Amendment and Fourteenth Amendment to allow the jury to "draw adverse inferences from the record evidence" against the defendants. The standard for a writ of certiorari relies on whether the petition presents a compelling, broad legal question of national importance, such as a split of authority among circuit courts or a departure from the accepted and usual course of judicial proceedings.

## Analysis of the Facts
The petition arises from a Florida state appellate court (District Court of Appeal of Florida, Third District) decision. The core complaint is highly fact-bound, focusing on specific adverse inferences at trial. There is no evidence of a circuit split, nor does the petition raise a novel question of federal law that warrants Supreme Court review. The issues framed are poorly articulated and primarily concern state-level tort proceedings.

Furthermore, Uber (the respondent) filed a waiver of its right to respond. A waiver of response is a strong, well-established signal that the respondent does not view the petition as posing a credible threat of being granted. Petitions from non-specialist counsel that lack a response and present deeply fact-bound state tort issues are overwhelmingly denied.

## Prediction and Probability
Given the lack of a circuit split, the fact-bound nature of the petition, its origin in a state appellate court, and the waiver of response by the respondent, the likelihood of a grant is near zero. Based on historical base rates for cert petitions (which hover around ~2-3% for paid petitions, and even lower for state court tort claims with no response), I assign a probability of 0.001 (0.1%) to the petition being granted. The predicted disposition is "denied".
