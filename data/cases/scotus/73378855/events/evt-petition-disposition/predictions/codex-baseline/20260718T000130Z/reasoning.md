# Prediction: denied

I predict denial and assign a 0.3% probability to any grant, including a GVR.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether due process permits a state supreme court to affirm a punitive-damages award without a written opinion or constitutional analysis, and whether the proceedings as a whole denied meaningful review. The relevant merits principles are the constitutional guideposts for punitive damages in *BMW of North America, Inc. v. Gore* and *State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. v. Campbell*, de novo appellate review under *Cooper Industries, Inc. v. Leatherman Tool Group, Inc.*, and the requirement of meaningful judicial review discussed in *Honda Motor Co. v. Oberg*. At the certiorari stage, however, the Court ordinarily looks for a conflict, an important recurring federal question, or a serious departure from settled law rather than fact-bound error correction; four Justices must vote to grant.

## Record

The 2026-07-17 snapshot shows a paid petition from the Supreme Court of Alabama, distributed on July 8 for the September 28, 2026 conference. It shows no relist, CVSG, amicus filing, or other selection signal.

The provisioned petition and questions presented characterize the $800,000 punitive award as having received no meaningful constitutional review. The petition says the award accompanied $200,000 in jury-awarded compensatory damages and a prior $160,696.46 compensatory award, and emphasizes the Alabama Supreme Court's affirmance without opinion.

The provisioned brief in opposition materially weakens that framing. Its appended remittitur-hearing transcript shows that the trial court heard the constitutional argument, addressed reprehensibility, and treated the relevant ratio as approximately 2.2:1 after including both compensatory components. The judge stated that the award was not excessive and was within *BMW*. The opposition also invokes *TXO Production Corp. v. Alliance Resources Corp.*, where the Court declined to treat a trial judge's failure to articulate written reasons after an adequate post-verdict hearing as a constitutional violation. Alabama's cited no-opinion rule represents that the state supreme court reviewed the record and found no legal error.

## Grant analysis

The petition identifies no split among state high courts or federal courts and cites no Supreme Court holding requiring a written appellate opinion in these circumstances. Its proposed rule therefore appears to seek an extension of existing precedent, while *TXO* supplies a close adverse analogy. The factual premise is also disputed by the hearing transcript: this is not a record with literally no constitutional consideration. Those features make the case a poor vehicle for announcing a broad rule about silent affirmances.

The underlying award does not independently create a strong summary-reversal or GVR candidate. On the opposition's record, the punitive-to-compensatory ratio is about 2.2:1, the conduct involved taking entrusted funds under false pretenses, and the trial court expressly considered reprehensibility and proportionality. There is no identified intervening decision that would support a GVR.

The committed statpack supplies a 2.5% estimated overall grant rate for Term 2025 cert petitions and a 5.36% paid-petition rate, but the more case-specific no-relist bucket is 0.8%. This petition's lack of a conflict, absence of institutional support, adverse vehicle facts, and close adverse precedent justify a forecast below that already-low bucket. The most likely disposition is a routine denial at or soon after the first conference.

No individual votes are predicted because certiorari votes are not public and the record does not support Justice-specific estimates.

## Significance

If the Court granted and required state appellate courts to provide written constitutional analysis whenever punitive damages are challenged, the ruling could affect appellate procedure beyond this dispute. But the case itself is a private monetary controversy, the proposed question is constrained by a disputed record, and there are no evident indicators of broad public or institutional attention. I therefore assign a 0.30 big-case score, independently of the much lower grant probability.
