# Wilson v. Watts, No. 25-1304 — cert prediction

**Prediction: deny. P(grant) = 0.005.**

## The case

Robert T. Wilson, Jr. — a disbarred Alabama attorney proceeding pro se — was
found liable for converting trust funds entrusted to him. A Walker County jury
awarded $200,000 in compensatory (mental-anguish) damages and $800,000 in
punitive damages on top of a $160,696.46 partial summary judgment for the
missing trust corpus. The trial court denied remittitur, new-trial, and JMOL
motions; the Supreme Court of Alabama affirmed without opinion under Ala. R.
App. P. 53(a)(1) and (a)(2)(F). The petition (filed at the 90-day deadline,
docketed May 21, 2026) presents two questions: (1) whether due process permits
a state court of last resort to affirm a Gore/State Farm/Cooper Industries
excessiveness challenge without opinion, and (2) whether due process is
violated where post-trial motions raising constitutional objections were
denied without articulated analysis.

## Why this petition fails

1. **Controlling precedent runs directly against it.** TXO Production Corp. v.
   Alliance Resources Corp., 509 U.S. 443, 464–65 (1993) (plurality), held that
   a trial judge's failure to articulate reasons for denying remittitur of a
   punitive award is not a constitutional violation where an adequate hearing
   was held. The BIO leads with exactly this. Neither Gore, State Farm, nor
   Cooper Industries requires a *written opinion* from an appellate court
   conducting de novo review; petitioner cites no authority mandating one, and
   summary affirmance practices exist in many state appellate systems. A grant
   here would constitutionalize opinion-writing — a step the Court has shown no
   appetite for.

2. **The factual premise is contested and weak.** The BIO appends the
   remittitur-hearing transcript showing the trial judge *did* engage the Gore
   guideposts on the record — reprehensibility ("he took their money... under
   false pretenses"), ratio ("well within the range"), and an express
   conclusion ("It's well within BMW"). This is not the silent rubber-stamp the
   petition portrays. The "denial of meaningful judicial review" framing of QP2
   collapses on the record.

3. **No split, no recurring-issue showing.** The petition alleges no conflict
   among state high courts or federal circuits on whether summary affirmance of
   a preserved punitive-damages challenge violates due process. It is
   error-correction dressed as a procedural question.

4. **The underlying award is unremarkable.** Counting all compensatory harm
   ($360,696), the ratio is 2.2:1 — comfortably single-digit under State Farm.
   Even petitioner's preferred 7.8:1 framing (computed against only part of the
   compensatory award) would not make this a vehicle: the reprehensibility
   finding (a fiduciary converting a beneficiary's inheritance) is at the high
   end, where State Farm tolerates larger ratios.

5. **Every grant marker is absent.** Pro se petitioner (paid docket, but
   self-represented — the petition is 9 pages of argument with typographical
   OCR-level errors); no amici; no notable counsel; respondent's BIO filed by a
   regional firm; distributed once, for the September 28, 2026 long conference
   (0 relists as of the snapshot); no CVSG. Vehicle problems abound (contested
   record about what review occurred; TXO on point).
