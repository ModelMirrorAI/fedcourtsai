# Prediction: Denied

## Legal Question and Governing Standard
The legal question is whether absolute judicial immunity protects a state trial judge from a 42 U.S.C. § 1983 suit when the judge allegedly "directs" and "supervises" police officers in conducting a warrantless home arrest of a litigant to enforce a contempt order. Absolute judicial immunity protects judges for actions taken in their judicial capacity, provided they do not act in the "clear absence of all jurisdiction" (*Stump v. Sparkman*). The Supreme Court has applied a functional approach (*Forrester v. White*), denying immunity when a judge acts as an "adjunct law enforcement officer" by personally participating in a search or arrest (*Lo-Ji Sales, Inc. v. New York*).

## Facts and Posture
This case arises from a local landlord-tenant discovery dispute in Pennsylvania state court. According to the petition, Respondent Judge Philip Ignelzi held Petitioner (attorney Walter Bernard) in contempt and issued an order that led sheriff's deputies to go to Petitioner's home and arrest him. The Petitioner alleges the judge "directed" the arrest and used law enforcement threats to secure his appearance, characterizing this as non-judicial executive action akin to *Lo-Ji Sales* and decisions from the Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Circuits (e.g., *Gibson v. Goldston*, *Rockett v. Eighmy*).

The District Court dismissed the complaint, finding the issuance of a civil contempt order to be a core judicial act. In an unpublished decision, the Third Circuit affirmed, holding primarily that Petitioner forfeited his executive-function argument by not properly raising it below, and alternatively that the judge had authority to manage the contempt proceedings. 

On July 7, 2026, the respondent waived the right to respond. The case is distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference.

## Reasoning
The probability of a cert grant is exceptionally low (~0.5%).
1. **Waiver of Response**: The respondent's waiver of the right to respond is a strong signal that the case does not warrant the Court's review. The Court rarely grants a petition without first calling for a response.
2. **Poor Vehicle**: The Third Circuit's decision is unpublished and relies on an independent procedural ground (forfeiture of the executive-function argument below). The Supreme Court typically avoids vehicles tainted by procedural default because it prevents cleanly reaching the merits of the federal question.
3. **Fact-Bound Application**: Despite Petitioner's attempt to frame this as a 4-1 circuit split regarding *Lo-Ji Sales*, the facts alleged here do not match the core of the cases in the purported split. In cases where immunity was denied, the judge *personally* left the bench and physically participated in law enforcement activities (e.g., executing a search warrant at a bookstore or personally escorting minors to jail). Here, the judge issued an order from the bench that was executed by deputies. The dispute is thus a highly fact-bound disagreement over whether the judge's enforcement of a contempt order crossed the line into executive action, rather than a pure legal question dividing the circuits.
4. **Base Rates**: The overall base rate for paid petitions in recent Terms is around 3-5%, but that drops to near zero for cases without a response and with vehicle defects.
