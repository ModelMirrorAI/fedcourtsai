# Prediction

I predict that the petition will be **denied**, with a 2.2% probability of any grant (including a plenary grant, summary reversal, or GVR).

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether absolute judicial immunity bars a 42 U.S.C. § 1983 claim against a state judge alleged to have personally directed and supervised police officers conducting a warrantless home arrest. Under the petition's authorities, immunity turns on function: judicial acts generally receive absolute immunity, while executive or law-enforcement acts do not. At the certiorari stage, the principal considerations are whether the decision below creates a genuine and consequential conflict, whether this case cleanly presents the issue, and whether the judgment is sufficiently important to warrant review.

## Record and merits signals

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition from an unpublished Third Circuit decision, a respondent's waiver of the right to respond, and distribution for the September 28, 2026 conference. It shows no CVSG, call for a response, or relist. The provisioned questions-presented text and full petition were available and not truncated; no brief in opposition was provisioned.

The petition identifies a facially important line between adjudication and police work. It relies principally on *Lo-Ji Sales*, *Forrester*, *Gibson*, *Rockett*, *King*, and *Harper*, and frames the result below as a four-to-one circuit conflict. That framing supplies some grant signal because the issue concerns the availability of a federal civil-rights remedy for warrantless home arrests.

The vehicle is nonetheless weak. The Third Circuit treated the nonjudicial-function theory as forfeited and affirmed in a nonprecedential opinion. The petition acknowledges that the district court understood petitioner not to dispute that the acts were taken in a judicial capacity. Those procedural features make the case a poor vehicle for resolving a broad split.

There are also serious factual distinctions within the asserted conflict. In *Gibson*, the judge personally participated in a home search; in *Rockett*, the judge personally walked children to jail and placed them in cells. The allegations here are that Judge Ignelzi directed sheriff's deputies to bring a litigant before him in connection with an ongoing matter and then conducted a hearing. That conduct is closer to a judge's courtroom-management and enforcement role than the hands-on searches or jail operations described in the cited cases. The respondent's waiver is not dispositive—the Court can call for a response—but it is another negative signal at this stage.
