# Prediction for scotus/73281702: evt-petition-disposition

## Legal Question
Does the Texas continuous-sexual-abuse statute, which allows a jury to convict without unanimously agreeing on which specific acts of sexual abuse the defendant committed, violate the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments under *Ramos v. Louisiana*?

## Analysis
The petitioner, John David Trice, was convicted under Texas Penal Code § 21.02(d), which explicitly provides that "members of the jury are not required to agree on which specific acts of sexual abuse occurred," as long as they agree that a continuous course of conduct took place. Trice argues this violates the jury unanimity requirement incorporated against the states in *Ramos v. Louisiana*, 590 U.S. 83 (2020). 

The case is currently at the petition stage. It is a paid petition where the respondent (Texas) initially waived its right to respond. However, the Court subsequently requested a response, indicating that at least one Justice found the petition's *Ramos* argument intriguing enough to warrant a closer look.

Despite the call for response (CFR), a grant remains unlikely. The Supreme Court has historically distinguished between the elements of a crime (which require unanimity) and the means of committing it (which do not, per *Schad v. Arizona* and *Mathis v. United States*). State legislatures generally have broad leeway to define a continuous course of conduct as a single element. Furthermore, the petition itself concedes that several other states have enacted similar statutes but fails to allege a deep or mature split of authority among state supreme courts or federal circuits regarding the constitutionality of these specific continuous-abuse statutes post-*Ramos*. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals denied discretionary review without issuing an opinion on the merits that would tee this up perfectly for SCOTUS review. 

Absent a clear split, the Court is likely to let state courts continue percolating the application of *Ramos* to continuous offense statutes. The CFR elevates the grant probability above the base rate for paid petitions, but the most probable outcome remains a denial. 

## Conclusion
Probability of grant: 4%
Predicted disposition: denied
