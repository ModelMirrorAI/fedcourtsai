# Prediction: denied

I assign a 14% probability to any grant and predict denial. If the Court does grant, the likely form is plenary review rather than a GVR because the petition presents a clean constitutional question and identifies no intervening authority that would warrant remand.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether Texas Penal Code § 21.02(d) violates the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments by allowing jurors to convict for continuous sexual abuse without agreeing on which specific predicate acts occurred. The legal boundary is between elements, on which the jury must agree, and alternative means or underlying facts, on which unanimity is generally unnecessary. *Ramos v. Louisiana* establishes that the Sixth Amendment's unanimity rule applies to state convictions. *Richardson v. United States* required agreement on the predicate violations of the federal continuing-criminal-enterprise statute as a matter of statutory interpretation, partly to avoid constitutional difficulty, while also recognizing the special proof problems presented by child-abuse course-of-conduct statutes.

At the certiorari stage, the principal considerations are whether the decision conflicts with another court of last resort or Supreme Court precedent, whether the issue is important and recurring, and whether this case is a clean vehicle.

## Record and vehicle

The April 15 petition and its questions-presented extract show that Trice received a 40-year sentence on the continuous-abuse count. The published Texas appellate opinion treated the individual abusive acts as means of committing the course-of-conduct element and upheld § 21.02(d). The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals refused discretionary review. The issue was preserved and decided, and invalidating Count One would matter notwithstanding the concurrent 10-to-20-year sentences on the other counts.

The July 17 snapshot supplies a meaningful positive signal: Texas initially waived a response, the petition was distributed for the May 28 conference, and the Court requested a response on May 26. Texas's response is now due July 24, so no brief in opposition was available in the provisioned documents. A requested response indicates attention from at least one Justice and lifts this petition materially above an ordinary paid filing. It does not itself imply the four votes needed for certiorari, and the case has not yet produced the stronger signal of a relist.

## Factors against review

The petition's central weakness is the absence of a developed conflict. It identifies a uniform line of Texas intermediate-appellate decisions rejecting the claim, not a split. CourtListener research also located *State v. Young*, 150 Haw. 365, 502 P.3d 45 (Haw. Ct. App. 2021), which upheld a materially similar Hawaii statute after *Ramos*, as well as pre-*Ramos* decisions from Arizona and Wisconsin taking the same elements-versus-means view. The lower court's analysis is also a plausible application of existing doctrine: all jurors must find the requisite number of acts during the statutory period, even if they identify different acts. *Ramos* dealt directly with nonunanimous jury votes, not disagreement over alternative means, and *Richardson* did not constitutionalize its construction of a different federal statute.

The issue is recurring and the statute's express nonagreement provision makes the constitutional question substantial. Several states use comparable statutes, and the requested response suggests at least preliminary interest. Still, the lack of contrary authority, the Court's usual reluctance to take an error-correction case from an intermediate state court, and the low certiorari base rate outweigh those considerations.
