# Prediction: denied

I assign a 21% probability to any grant and predict denial. This is substantially above the ordinary paid-petition prior, but it remains below 50% because the asserted split is contestable and the case is interlocutory.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether a plaintiff that refused to buy an allegedly price-fixed product has antitrust standing to recover downstream losses caused by that refusal. The Second Circuit held at the pleading stage that DirecTV plausibly alleged antitrust injury and efficient-enforcer status: the alleged conspiracy reduced output through station blackouts, the lost subscribers and profits flowed from that reduction, and the parties' prior course of dealing supplied a benchmark. Judge Sullivan dissented, reasoning that DirecTV's nonpurchase, subscriber decisions, and calculation of net lost profits created the indirect and speculative chain disfavored by *Associated General Contractors*.

Certiorari turns principally on the usual Rule 10 considerations: a genuine conflict among courts of appeals, recurring national importance, and a clean vehicle, rather than whether the Second Circuit merely misapplied settled law.

## Case-specific signals

Several features materially favor review. The docket snapshot shows that DirecTV initially waived a response, but the Court requested one after the petition was distributed for the June 25 conference. That call for a response is a meaningful expression of interest, although it does not show the four votes required to grant. The panel divided, and the petition identifies real tension with the Ninth Circuit's *City of Oakland* decision and the Tenth Circuit's *Montreal Trading* decision over priced-out nonpurchasers. The issue governs access to treble damages across industries, and the lower-court opinion notes that the United States participated as an antitrust amicus below. The case is a paid petition from the Second Circuit, whose denial-reweighted historical grant rate in the committed statpack is about 5.4%.

The principal weakness is that the conflict may not be square. *Montreal Trading* itself contemplated that a regular course of dealing could make a nonpurchaser's damages less speculative, and the Second Circuit relied on that feature. Respondent can characterize the disagreement with *City of Oakland* as an application of the multifactor antitrust-standing test to different allegations rather than a categorical rule conflict. The petition arises from reversal of a motion to dismiss and remand for further proceedings, giving the Court a conventional reason to await a final judgment and more percolation. The current record also has no CVSG, no relist, and no brief in opposition: the response is not due until August 21, 2026, so the provisioned materials necessarily present only petitioners' account.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack reports a 5.4% estimated grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, about 5.4% for modern petitions originating in the Second Circuit, and a much lower rate for petitions that ultimately have no relist. I raise the forecast to 21% because of the requested response, divided panel, federal-law issue, and plausible circuit tension. I stop there because the split and vehicle objections are serious and the missing opposition prevents testing petitioners' framing. If the Court does not deny, a plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR because no intervening authority or mootness issue appears in the record.

No individual cert votes are predicted; cert-stage votes ordinarily are not disclosed, and the present record does not support justice-specific assignments.

The big-case score is 0.67. A merits decision could affect the scope of private antitrust damages and bargaining disputes well beyond broadcasting, though the issue is a technical standing doctrine rather than a case with unusually broad public visibility.
