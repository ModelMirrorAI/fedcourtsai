# Prediction: denied

I estimate a 9% probability of any grant-side disposition and predict denial.

## Question and record

The petition asks whether Chicago effects an uncompensated taking when it impounds and disposes of vehicles registered to an owner with unpaid traffic judgments, keeps any sale proceeds without crediting the debt or returning a surplus, and may reach vehicles unrelated to the violations. The petition presents the practice as debt enforcement governed by *Tyler v. Hennepin County*: historically, a creditor could seize enough property to satisfy a judgment but had to return the overplus. The brief in opposition instead characterizes the ordinance as a graduated punitive forfeiture mechanism under *Bennis v. Michigan* and emphasizes the ordinance's repeated notice, hearing, payment-plan, redemption, and claim procedures.

The factual equities favor review. According to the petition, O'Donnell lost a vehicle over several minor parking judgments, while Goree's vehicle was disposed of because a co-signer owed ticket debt associated with another vehicle. The petition also alleges that Chicago neither offsets the underlying debt nor refunds surplus proceeds. The fresh *Pung v. Isabella County* decision reaffirms that a government conducting a tax sale must return proceeds above the debt, and its separate opinions show continuing interest in the historical rule against selling more property than necessary. That doctrine does not, however, resolve whether a law-enforcement forfeiture is outside the Takings Clause.

## Certiorari assessment

Several signals lift this petition above the ordinary docket baseline. It is a paid petition, the Court requested a response after both respondents initially waived, the issue has broad consequences for municipal forfeiture and low-income vehicle owners, and contemporaneous petitions in *Hadley* and *Pena* concern the related police-power exception to takings liability.

The vehicle problems are more important. Petitioners pleaded only a facial Takings Clause challenge. As the opposition explains, many applications of the ordinance do not produce surplus proceeds at all: a vehicle may be released, retained without sale, or sold for less than enforcement and storage costs. Those valid or materially different applications make it difficult to satisfy the demanding facial standard and prevent a clean decision about petitioners' strongest, fact-specific surplus theory. The petition also does not identify a square circuit conflict. The D.C. Circuit's *Tate* decision supports Chicago, and the other recent police-power cases concern destruction of property during law enforcement rather than this debt-linked vehicle regime. *Bennis* supplies a plausible basis for the judgment below, while due-process and Excessive Fines theories that might fit the facts were not presented.

The 2025 Term statpack estimates a 5.4% grant rate for paid petitions. The response request and substantive importance justify moving upward, but the Seventh Circuit/no-relist/no-CVSG cuts and the procedural defects pull strongly toward denial. There had been no relist as of the snapshot; the September 28 conference was the petition's first scheduled conference after briefing. If the Court takes up the broader police-power question through a cleaner companion case, a hold followed by a GVR is possible, but denial remains by far the most likely disposition here.
