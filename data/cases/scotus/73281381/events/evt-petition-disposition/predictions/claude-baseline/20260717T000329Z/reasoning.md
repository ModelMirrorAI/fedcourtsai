# O'Donnell v. City of Chicago, No. 25-1098 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.13.**

## The legal question

Petitioners challenge Chicago's vehicle impound-and-sale ordinance (MCC
§ 9-100-120): once an owner has two-to-three unpaid parking-ticket judgments,
the City may boot, tow, impound, and ultimately dispose of *every* vehicle
registered to the owner — including vehicles never involved in any infraction —
keeping all sale proceeds without offsetting the debt or returning any surplus.
The questions presented ask (1) whether this practice is an unconstitutional
taking and (2) whether the Takings Clause places *any* limit on confiscation
under the police power. The Seventh Circuit (163 F.4th 411, Dec. 22, 2025),
applying its own recent decision in *Hadley v. City of South Bend*, 154 F.4th
549 (7th Cir. 2025), held the scheme is a punitive exercise of law-enforcement
authority under *Bennis v. Michigan*, so the Takings Clause "doesn't apply,"
and *Tyler v. Hennepin County*'s surplus-retention principle does not reach
police-power enforcement.

## Signals from the record

**Positive (grant-side):**

- **Call for a response.** Both respondents waived (Mar. 31 / Apr. 16); the
  case was distributed for the May 14 conference; on April 30 the Court
  **requested a response** (due June 1, extended to July 1). A CFR requires at
  least one Justice's active interest and historically multiplies the paid-case
  grant rate several-fold. This is the strongest single docket signal.
- **Doctrinal momentum.** *Tyler* (2023, unanimous) held that keeping the
  surplus from a tax-debt sale is a taking; *Timbs* (2019) incorporated the
  Excessive Fines Clause; per the BIO, the Court decided *Pung v. Isabella
  County* (No. 25-95) on June 23, 2026, continuing its work in the
  fines/forfeiture space. Two Justices' statement respecting denial in *Baker
  v. City of McKinney* (145 S. Ct. 11 (2024)) flagged the police-power/takings
  intersection as an issue awaiting percolation.
- **A pending cluster.** Companion petitions squarely raising the police-power
  exception are pending: *Hadley* (No. 25-1158, same CA7 precedent this
  decision rests on) and *Pena v. City of Los Angeles* (No. 25-1163, CA9).
  CourtListener confirms both remain undecided. All are queued toward the
  OT2026 long conference; this petition was distributed for the
  **September 28, 2026 conference** on July 15. If the Court grants *Hadley*
  or *Pena* and rules for the property owner, this case is a natural hold →
  GVR (a GVR counts as a grant on the binary axis here).
- **Sympathetic, press-covered facts** (cars sold over a few hundred dollars
  of meter tickets; Goree's car taken for a co-signor's debt on a different
  vehicle; ProPublica bankruptcy-boom coverage), and a paid petition.

**Negative (deny-side):**

- **Facial-challenge-only posture.** Petitioners pleaded exclusively a facial
  Takings claim. Under *Salerno*/*Keystone*, the Court would have to find no
  valid application of the ordinance's "mere enactment" — and the BIO shows
  many applications (vehicles redeemed, held without sale, or sold for scrap
  below storage costs) with no surplus at all. This is a genuine vehicle
  defect, and the petition never engages the facial standard.
- **No circuit split — conceded.** Petitioners admit the courts of appeals
  that have decided the question (CA7 here, CADC in *Tate*) agree; they argue
  the *agreement* justifies review, which rarely persuades.
- **Weak petition craft.** The petition does not cite or grapple with
  *Hadley*, the very precedent the decision below applies; the BIO catalogs
  record-citation errors. Counsel are not Supreme Court specialists.
- **Better vehicles exist.** If the Court wants the police-power/takings
  question, *Hadley* (as-applied, innocent-owner, destroyed home) and *Pena*
  are cleaner. The likeliest role for this petition is hold-and-see, with
  outright denial the modal end state.
- **The Court passed once already** in *Baker* (2024), preferring percolation,
  and the respondents plausibly argue Eighth Amendment/due process — not
  takings — are the doctrinal homes for excessive-punishment limits (*Austin*,
  *Timbs*, *Pung*).

## Base rates and calibration

From the committed statpack (live/historical slice, denial-reweighted): modern
discretionary-cert petitions grant ~2.5–3.3% per recent Term; **paid**
petitions grant 5.4% (Term 2025), 6.9% (2024), 8.0% (2023); CA7-originating
petitions run below average (2.0% granted); zero-relist petitions grant 0.8%,
but this petition has not yet reached a decisional conference — its first
distribution produced the CFR rather than a denial, and the 9/28 long-conference
distribution is not yet a relist.

Starting from the paid-petition base (~6%), the CFR roughly doubles-to-triples
the odds (empirically CFR'd paid cases land near 8–12%). The pending
*Hadley*/*Pena* cluster adds a real hold→GVR path: I estimate P(the Court
grants one of the companion petitions) ≈ 25%, and conditional on a
petitioner-favorable merits outcome there, a GVR here is likely — contributing
perhaps 5–8 points. Against that, the facial-only posture and conceded absence
of a split materially cap the odds of a *plenary* grant of this particular
petition (I'd put that alone at ~3–4%). Netting these paths:

**P(grant, including GVR) ≈ 0.13; predicted disposition: denied** (modal
outcome ~85%: denial, most plausibly after being held for *Hadley*/*Pena* or
after one or more relists starting from the September 28 long conference).
