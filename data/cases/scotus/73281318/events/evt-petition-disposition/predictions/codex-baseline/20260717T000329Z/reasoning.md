# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with a 1.8% probability of any grant (including a GVR).

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether police may make a warrantless home entry under the Fourth Amendment's emergency-aid exception when they observed no struggle and had no report that anyone was injured or that an emergency existed. Under *Brigham City v. Stuart*, officers may enter when the circumstances give them an objectively reasonable basis to believe an occupant is seriously injured or imminently threatened. *Case v. Montana*, decided before this petition was filed, reaffirmed the objective, totality-of-the-circumstances inquiry and rejected a requirement of probable cause to believe aid is needed.

The provisioned petition and appendix show that police responded to a noise complaint at 2:31 a.m. They found extremely loud music, illuminated rooms, a barking dog, the petitioner's vehicle in the driveway, and an interior door ajar. A neighbor who knew the petitioner believed he was home, described a drug problem, and officers confirmed a narcotics conviction. Repeated knocking and telephone calls produced no response for 76 minutes. The officers entered and found the petitioner asleep. The trial court suppressed; the Illinois Appellate Court reversed, concluding that the objective circumstances supplied reasonable grounds for an emergency-aid entry.

## Certworthiness and vehicle

The brief in opposition identifies two powerful denial grounds. First, the state appellate decision is interlocutory: the prosecution has not reached trial or final judgment, and the petition offers no convincing basis for treating the suppression ruling as final under 28 U.S.C. § 1257. Acquittal or another suppression ruling could eliminate the federal question; a conviction would permit later review.

Second, the petition expressly challenges only the application of settled law to this record. It identifies no split and no recurring legal disagreement. The facts cut both ways, but the Illinois court applied an objective totality test and acknowledged the absence of direct evidence of injury. That is a poor Rule 10 vehicle for plenary review, especially immediately after *Case* clarified the governing standard.

A GVR is also unlikely. Although the opinion below used "approximating probable cause" language, *Case* rejected that more demanding gloss. Applying *Case* would therefore not plausibly improve the petitioner's position, and Illinois's court repeatedly relied on the objective reasonable-basis formulation. The interlocutory posture supplies an additional reason not to GVR.
