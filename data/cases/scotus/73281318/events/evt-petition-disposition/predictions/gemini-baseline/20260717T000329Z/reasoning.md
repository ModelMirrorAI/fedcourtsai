# Reasoning

## Legal Question and Standard
The petitioner challenges the Illinois Appellate Court's application of the "emergency aid" exception to the Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement, citing *Brigham City v. Stuart*, 547 U.S. 398 (2006), and *Case v. Montana*, 607 U.S. 107 (2026). The petitioner argues the police lacked an objectively reasonable basis to enter his home without a warrant because they did not observe a "fracas" or receive specific information that someone was hurt. The standard for a cert grant generally requires a circuit split on an important question of federal law, which is absent here.

## Facts from the Snapshot and Record
This case is a state criminal prosecution where the petitioner is charged with narcotics offenses following a warrantless search. The trial court suppressed the evidence, but the Illinois Appellate Court reversed the suppression order on an interlocutory appeal and remanded for further proceedings.

The State initially waived its right to respond, but the Court requested a response. The State's Brief in Opposition points out two fatal flaws for the petition:
1. **Jurisdiction:** Under 28 U.S.C. § 1257, the Supreme Court has jurisdiction over state court judgments only when they are "[f]inal judgments or decrees." Because the appellate court's decision was interlocutory (remanding for trial), and none of the *Cox Broadcasting* exceptions apply (e.g., an acquittal at trial would moot the federal question), the Court lacks jurisdiction.
2. **Error Correction without Split:** The petitioner explicitly admits he is not challenging the rule of law but its fact-bound application. The Supreme Court "rarely grants certiorari to review the misapplication of a properly stated rule of law" (Rule 10). Furthermore, the recent decision in *Case v. Montana* fully reaffirmed the *Brigham City* framework that the lower court applied.

## Probability and Conclusion
The base rate for modern cert petitions is roughly 3.1% (367 grants out of ~11,700 resolved petitions). While the Court's request for a response after a waiver indicates at least one Justice's interest in the case, the combination of an interlocutory posture from a state court (a strict jurisdictional bar) and the petition being a fact-bound error correction request makes a grant highly unlikely. The probability of a grant is placed at 0.02 to reflect the tiny baseline chance that the Court finds a way to overlook the interlocutory posture to GVR in light of *Case v. Montana*, though the BIO persuasively argues a GVR is unwarranted because the lower court applied a standard *more* favorable to the petitioner than required by *Case*.

**Predicted Disposition:** denied
**Probability:** 0.02
