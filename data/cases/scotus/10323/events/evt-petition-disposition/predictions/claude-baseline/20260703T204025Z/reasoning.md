# Auflick v. Wainwright — petition disposition (scotus/10323)

## The question

Whether the Supreme Court granted or denied the petition in *Auflick v.
Wainwright*, docketed as "979, Misc" on the Court's miscellaneous docket.

## What the snapshot shows

The snapshot (`data/cases/scotus/10323/record/snapshots/2026-07-03.json`) is
sparse: no docket entries, no filing or termination dates, no lower-court
information, and no party or attorney detail. The load-bearing facts are:

1. **Court and docket number.** A SCOTUS case numbered "979, Misc". The
   separate miscellaneous docket — used for in forma pauperis petitions,
   almost all of them pro se prisoner filings — existed only until the Court
   unified its docket after October Term 1970. So this is a pre-1971 IFP
   petition, most plausibly from the 1960s.
2. **The respondent.** "Wainwright" is Louie L. Wainwright, who headed
   Florida's corrections system from 1962 to 1987 and was the nominal
   respondent in thousands of Florida state-prisoner habeas and
   post-conviction petitions. A pro se prisoner case caption of the form
   *Prisoner v. Wainwright* on the miscellaneous docket is the classic
   profile of a Florida prisoner's petition for certiorari (or habeas)
   seeking review of a conviction or of denied state/federal collateral
   relief.

## Governing standard and base rates

Certiorari is discretionary (Sup. Ct. R. 10); review is reserved for
conflicts and questions of national importance, which pro se prisoner
petitions almost never present in a cert-worthy posture. Historically the
miscellaneous docket was disposed of overwhelmingly by one-line denials:
the IFP grant rate through the 1960s was on the order of 1–3%, far below
even the low paid-docket rate. The corpus's resolved-SCOTUS base rates point
the same direction (granted ≈ 1.4% of resolved SCOTUS cases; denied and
dismissed together dominate cert-stage outcomes), though the labeled set is
small and skewed toward merits decisions coded "other".

## The one countervailing scenario

The era and caption place this case near the *Gideon v. Wainwright* (1963)
aftermath, when the Court granted, vacated, and remanded dozens of Florida
prisoner miscellaneous-docket cases (e.g. *Pickelsimer v. Wainwright*, 375
U.S. 2 (1963)) for reconsideration in light of *Gideon*. If *Auflick* was an
OT1962–63 right-to-counsel case, a GVR — which would be recorded as a grant —
is a live possibility. But that wave touched one Term and only the subset of
Florida petitions squarely presenting a counsel claim; across the whole
1962–1970 window that "979, Misc" could sit in, the overwhelming majority of
*v. Wainwright* miscellaneous petitions were simply denied. The corpus
contains several comparable Misc-docket *v. Wainwright* priors (e.g. 522
Misc, 780 Misc, 126 Misc, 6 Misc) but carries no disposition labels or
opinion text for them, so they sharpen the profile without changing the
base-rate anchor.
