# Prediction: denied

I estimate a 40% probability that the Court grants the petition and a 60% probability that it denies it. The predicted disposition is therefore **denied**. I do not predict individual votes: certiorari votes are not public, and the record does not support justice-by-justice assignments.

## Question and governing standard

The question is whether a general motion for judgment of acquittal under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 29 preserves de novo appellate review of evidentiary sufficiency, or whether a defendant must identify each particular evidentiary deficiency to avoid plain-error review.

At the certiorari stage, the relevant considerations are those reflected in Supreme Court Rule 10: a square conflict among federal courts of appeals, the recurring importance of the federal question, a clean vehicle, and whether the issue warrants the Court's discretionary review. Four votes are required to grant certiorari. The prediction concerns only the petition's disposition, not which preservation rule is correct.

## Factors favoring a grant

The petition presents a substantial and apparently mature circuit conflict. It describes the First, Second, Sixth, Seventh, Ninth, Tenth, and D.C. Circuits as treating a general Rule 29 motion as sufficient, while the Third, Fifth, Eighth, and Eleventh Circuits require greater specificity. The Third Circuit expressly confronted the competing rule, declined to follow the contrary circuits, and made its preservation holding the threshold for applying plain-error review. That is materially stronger than a petition alleging an implicit or fact-bound disagreement.

The issue is recurring and exclusively federal. Rule 29 motions are routine in federal criminal trials, and the asserted conflict changes the steps counsel must take to preserve sufficiency review. The petition also identifies a practical trap: in some circuits specificity is necessary, while in others listing some grounds may forfeit unlisted grounds.

The procedural posture is comparatively clean. Trial counsel said, "I move for judgment of acquittal on Rule 29(a). I waive argument"; the district court denied the motion; and the Third Circuit squarely held that the motion did not preserve the later sufficiency arguments. The petition reports that the government itself invited the Third Circuit to resolve the open preservation question. The docket snapshot adds meaningful signs of institutional interest: the United States initially waived a response, the Court requested one on June 9, 2026, and two amicus briefs—one from the National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers and another from former judges—had been filed. The government's response was due August 10 after an extension.

## Factors favoring denial

The discretionary-certiorari base rate remains very low, so even a credible split does not make a grant more likely than denial without stronger confirmation from the respondent. The committed statpack's modern discretionary-cert cut was unavailable, and its small set of resolved SCOTUS cases is not a reliable substitute; I therefore used only the general low-cert-rate principle rather than its blended percentages.

There is also a consequential vehicle concern. The Third Circuit described the trial evidence as comfortably supporting all convictions and the petition's appendix reflects extensive evidence of forged documents, false representations, and diverted investor funds. Even under de novo sufficiency review—which remains deferential to a jury verdict—Abrams may lose. The Court could see little practical difference between the standards in this record, reducing the value of using this case to settle the conflict.

The trial motion was not merely concise; counsel expressly waived argument. That stark formulation may make the case less representative of ordinary preservation disputes and may strengthen the government's contention that the trial court received no notice of any particular deficiency. The Court could also regard specific Rule 29 practice as manageable advice for defense counsel or as a matter better addressed through the rulemaking process.

Finally, the petition identifies another pending petition, *Tovar v. United States*, raising a similar issue. A parallel vehicle confirms that the issue is live, but it also creates alternatives to directly granting this petition, including selecting another case, holding one petition, or waiting for further development. At the snapshot date, no brief in opposition or other response from the United States was provisioned, so the government's current position on certiorari—and any vehicle objection—was unknown. That missing adversarial presentation is the largest source of uncertainty in the 40% estimate.
