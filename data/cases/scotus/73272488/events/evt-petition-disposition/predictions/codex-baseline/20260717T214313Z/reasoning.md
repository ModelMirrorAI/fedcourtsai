# Prediction: denied

I assign a **41% probability of any grant** and predict denial. This is far above the ordinary cert-petition baseline, but still below even odds because the Court has not yet received the requested government response and the record presents a meaningful vehicle/remedy concern.

## Question and governing standard

The question is whether a general motion for judgment of acquittal under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 29 preserves de novo appellate review of all sufficiency arguments, or whether the defendant must identify each evidentiary deficiency to avoid plain-error review. Certiorari turns principally on the Rule 10 considerations: a square and mature conflict among courts of appeals, recurring importance, and a clean vehicle. The correctness of the Third Circuit's rule matters, but error correction alone would not justify review.

## Grant signals

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition describe an unusually developed conflict. According to the petition, the First, Second, Sixth, Seventh, Ninth, Tenth, and D.C. Circuits permit a general Rule 29 motion to preserve sufficiency challenges, while the Third, Eighth, Eleventh, and, in practice, Fifth Circuits require specificity. The Third Circuit expressly declined to follow the contrary rule and made the preservation question a threshold holding. The issue is recurring across federal criminal trials and produces different preservation strategies in different circuits.

The vehicle has several strengths. Abrams made a bare Rule 29 motion and waived argument; the Third Circuit squarely held that it did not preserve his later sufficiency arguments and applied plain-error review. The issue is legal, fully briefed below, and not dependent on a disputed account of what trial counsel said. The petition also says the government acknowledged the split in its brief opposing certiorari in *Tovar v. United States* but identified vehicle problems there, making Abrams a proposed cleaner replacement. Two amicus briefs support review. Most importantly, after the United States waived response, the Court requested one. That is a meaningful interest signal and substantially raises the case above the base rate.

## Denial and vehicle risks

The requested government response is not yet in the provisioned record; it is due August 10, 2026. The Solicitor General may dispute the split's depth, defend the Third Circuit's reading of Rules 29 and 47, favor Rules Committee consideration, or identify a vehicle defect not apparent from the petition. There has also been no relist: the only distribution preceded the response request, so the strong historical relist signal is absent at this stage.

The most important vehicle concern appears in the petition's appended Third Circuit opinion. Although the court selected plain-error review, it also described the trial evidence as amply supporting every challenged fraud and aggravated-identity-theft conviction. De novo sufficiency review remains highly deferential to the jury, and the opinion's factual analysis suggests Abrams may lose under either standard. That gives the government a serious argument that changing the preservation rule would not change the judgment, weakening the case as a vehicle despite the square legal holding.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, a 2.9% grant rate for modern petitions from the Third Circuit, and a 0.8% grant rate for petitions with no relist. Those broad rates do not capture a called-for response or a conceded multi-circuit split, so I adjust sharply upward. But a response request is not a grant, and the unresolved merits-response and harmlessness/remedy risks keep denial slightly more likely. I therefore place the case at 41% for any grant, with a plenary grant much more plausible than a GVR if the Court acts favorably.
