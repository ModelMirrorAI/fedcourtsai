# Prediction Reasoning

**Issue Presented:**
The petition presents a clean question of federal criminal procedure: Whether a defendant's general motion for a judgment of acquittal under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 29 preserves de novo appellate review of the sufficiency of the evidence, or whether the defendant must independently articulate each specific deficiency to avoid plain-error review.

**Legal and Factual Context:**
This issue implicates a deep, mature, and acknowledged split among the federal circuits (often cited as an 8-4 split). The majority of circuits (1st, 2nd, 4th, 6th, 7th, 9th, 10th, and D.C. Circuits) hold that a general Rule 29 motion preserves all sufficiency challenges for de novo review. A minority of circuits, including the Third Circuit below (along with the 5th, 8th, and 11th Circuits), hold that specific articulation is required, and failure to do so results in forfeiture and the much more demanding "plain error" standard on appeal. 

The record reflects a paid petition filed by high-profile Supreme Court practitioners (Debevoise & Plimpton). The presence of two cert-stage amicus briefs (from the National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers and a group of Former Judges) underscores the practical importance of the issue to the defense bar and the judiciary, highlighting the "specific motion trap" created by the minority rule.

**Procedural Posture and Signals:**
The United States originally waived its right to respond on May 21, 2026. However, on June 9, 2026, the Court requested a response (CFR). A CFR after an initial waiver is one of the strongest signals of the Court's interest in a petition. It indicates that at least one Justice (and likely a clerk pool) identified the petition as a strong candidate for certiorari and required the government's views before bringing it to conference.

**Probability:**
While the overall base rate for paid cert petitions is quite low (around 2.5% to 3.3%), the combination of an entrenched and acknowledged circuit split, cert-stage amicus support, elite counsel, and a Call for Response (CFR) following a government waiver elevates the probability substantially. These features are the hallmarks of a petition that the Court grants to resolve federal procedural uniformity. Consequently, the probability of a grant is assessed at 75%.

**Votes and Big Case Score:**
The "big case score" is assessed at 0.4. While resolving a Rule 29 circuit split is highly significant for federal criminal practitioners and ensures uniform application of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure, it is a technical legal issue that is unlikely to garner widespread public attention or mainstream media salience.
