# Prediction Reasoning — James P. Abrams v. United States (Docket No. 25-1236)

## 1. Executive Summary
This petition for certiorari presents a clean, mature, and deeply entrenched **8–4 circuit split** regarding **Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 29**. The specific question is whether a defendant's general/boilerplate motion for a judgment of acquittal preserves de novo review of any sufficiency of the evidence challenge on appeal, or whether the defendant must specify each precise deficiency at trial to avoid the highly demanding "plain error" standard of review.

Based on an analysis of the pre-decision record, the procedural history, strong amicus support at the petition stage, and a crucial procedural signal from the Supreme Court (a Call for Response issued after the respondent waived its right to file), we predict that certiorari will be **GRANTED** with a probability of **68%**.

## 2. Legal Landscape: The Rule 29 Sufficiency Preservation Split
Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 29 allows criminal defendants to move for a judgment of acquittal if they believe the government's evidence is legally insufficient to sustain a conviction.

Currently, the federal courts of appeals are split **8–4** on what is required to preserve specific sufficiency arguments for appellate review:

*   **The Majority Rule (8 Circuits — 1st, 2nd, 4th, 6th, 7th, 9th, 10th, and D.C. Circuits):**
    These circuits hold that a general Rule 29 motion—even one that simply states "the defendant moves for a judgment of acquittal on all counts because the evidence is insufficient"—fully preserves **all** potential sufficiency challenges for **de novo** review on appeal. The rationale is that sufficiency of the evidence is a single, indivisible legal issue, and the government always carries the burden of proving every statutory element. A motion for acquittal puts the court and the prosecution on notice that the defense believes that burden was not met.
*   **The Minority Rule (4 Circuits — 3rd, 5th, 8th, and 11th Circuits):**
    These circuits hold that a general Rule 29 motion does **not** preserve specific, unarticulated sufficiency arguments. If a defendant makes a general motion or specifies only certain grounds, any other sufficiency argument raised for the first time on appeal is reviewed only for **plain error** (or a "manifest miscarriage of justice"). The rationale is that preservation requires putting the trial court "squarely on notice" of the precise error so it can be resolved immediately, avoiding appellate "gotchas."

In the decision below (*United States v. Abrams*, 165 F.4th 784 (3d Cir. 2026)), the Third Circuit formally joined the minority position, ruling that petitioner James P. Abrams failed to preserve his specific sufficiency challenges under the de novo standard because his trial counsel made a general, non-specific Rule 29 motion.

## 3. Key Predictive Signals

### A. The "Call for Response" (CFR)
When the petition was filed on April 16, 2026, the Respondent (United States, represented by Solicitor General D. John Sauer) initially **waived** its right to respond on May 21, 2026. This is standard practice for the Solicitor General's office to conserve resources on petitions they anticipate will be summarily denied.

However, on June 9, 2026, the Supreme Court actively stepped in and requested that the government file a response (CFR). Empirically, on the paid docket, a CFR raises the likelihood of a certiorari grant from a baseline of **~4%** to approximately **~17%**. It indicates that at least one Justice (or their clerks in the cert pool) flagged the petition as potentially cert-worthy and blocked its automatic denial.

### B. High-Profile Amicus Support
At the petition stage, two powerful amicus briefs were filed on June 1, 2026, in support of petitioner James Abrams:
1.  **The National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers (NACDL)**, represented by Covington & Burling LLP (Sarah Elaine Harrington).
2.  **A Group of "Former Judges"**, represented by Quinn Emanuel Urquhart & Sullivan (Daniel Rickert Koffmann).

Amicus support at the petition stage is an exceptionally strong predictor of a grant of certiorari. The "Former Judges" brief is particularly influential because the Supreme Court is deeply concerned with the procedural rules that govern federal criminal trials and appellate courts. When former federal judges tell the Court that a procedural rule is functioning as an unfair "trap for the unwary," the Justices pay close attention.

### C. Clean and Determinative Vehicle (White-Collar / Fraud Context)
The petitioner, James P. Abrams, was the founder of a renewable-energy startup (EthosGen) and was convicted of federal white-collar conspiracy and fraud charges. White-collar and fraud cases involve highly complex statutory elements, intent requirements, and massive records. Sufficiency of the evidence is frequently the primary defense on appeal in such cases.
The Third Circuit's application of plain-error review to Abrams's sufficiency arguments was outcome-determinative on his appeal. There are no obvious vehicle problems (such as jurisdictional hurdles, waiver, or factual clutter) that would prevent the Supreme Court from addressing the core Rule 29 preservation question. This makes it an ideal vehicle.

### D. De-confliction: The Denial of *Tovar v. United States* (No. 25-6344)
On May 18, 2026, the Supreme Court denied certiorari in *Tovar v. United States*, a case out of the Eleventh Circuit that also raised the Rule 29 preservation split. Crucially, *Tovar* was an attempted sex-trafficking case that was heavily cluttered by a primary, controversial Commerce Clause jurisdictional challenge regarding purely intrastate digital communications. This substantively complex Commerce Clause issue made *Tovar* a messy, poor vehicle for resolving a pure Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 29 procedural question.
The fact that the Court denied *Tovar* on May 18, 2026, but then **actively requested a response** in *Abrams* on June 9, 2026, strongly signals a "deck-clearing" strategy: the Court rejected the cluttered *Tovar* vehicle and has selected the clean, white-collar *Abrams* case (represented by David O'Neil of Debevoise & Plimpton) to resolve the Rule 29 split.
