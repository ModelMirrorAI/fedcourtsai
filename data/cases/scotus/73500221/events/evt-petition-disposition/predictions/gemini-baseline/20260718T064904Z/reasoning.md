# Reasoning: John Rogne v. City of Catoosa, Oklahoma

## Legal Question and Background
The central legal question presented in the petition is whether the application of a state prudential rule of exhaustion (which dismissed a claim based on voluntary cessation of government physical possession/rescission) constitutes a decision on the merits of an uncompensated Takings Clause claim, such that res judicata bars a subsequent federal court action. The petitioner relies on *Knick v. Township of Scott*, which established that property owners are not required to exhaust state administrative or judicial remedies prior to bringing a Fifth Amendment Takings Clause claim.

The petitioner sued the City in state court after they erected a barrier fence over his vacant lots. The City subsequently rescinded the cease and desist order and returned the property. The state court dismissed the action under a prudential exhaustion and mootness doctrine based on that rescission. When the petitioner subsequently refiled in federal court, the district court and Tenth Circuit dismissed the claim on res judicata grounds, holding that the state court resolution effectively decided the Takings Clause claim on the merits.

## Analysis
The case represents a narrow, heavily fact-bound application of state preclusion (res judicata) law by the Tenth Circuit under the Full Faith and Credit Act (28 U.S.C. § 1738). While the petition attempts to frame the issue in the context of the exhaustion principles from *Knick*, the actual dispositive holding centers around Oklahoma state preclusion rules and whether the state court's specific dismissal functioned as a merits disposition. 

Furthermore, the record indicates that the Respondent, City of Catoosa, filed a *Waiver of right to respond* on June 8, 2026. The case was distributed for the long conference on September 28, 2026, without any request from the Court for a response. The Supreme Court rarely grants certiorari without requiring a response from the opposing party; the waiver is a strong indicator that the petition is not viewed as cert-worthy by the respondent, and the lack of a CVSG or Court-directed response further cements its low likelihood of a grant.

Base rates confirm this low likelihood. The overall grant rate for modern paid discretionary-cert petitions in the 2025 Term is approximately 2.5%, and the Tenth Circuit's originating court grant rate is around 5.0%. Without a response or relist, the probability of a grant approaches zero.
