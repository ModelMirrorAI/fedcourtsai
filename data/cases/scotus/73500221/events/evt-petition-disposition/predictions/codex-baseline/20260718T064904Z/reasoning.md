# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.6% probability to any grant and predict denial. The most plausible grant-side disposition would be plenary review, not a GVR: the petition identifies no intervening decision that would support vacatur and remand.

## Question and record

The petition asks whether a state court's use of exhaustion and the City's voluntary rescission to reject John Rogne's inverse-condemnation claim was a decision “on the merits,” such that Rogne could not invoke Oklahoma's savings statute to refile in federal court. Rogne alleges that Catoosa physically occupied his vacant lots with a barrier fence for six years, prevented dirt stockpiling, and never paid compensation. He relies chiefly on *Knick v. Township of Scott*, *First English Evangelical Lutheran Church v. County of Los Angeles*, and *Cedar Point Nursery v. Hassid* for the propositions that a taking is complete without payment, temporary physical occupations can require compensation, and later rescission does not extinguish the accrued compensation claim.

The provisioned snapshot, dated July 18, 2026, shows a paid petition filed May 18, a respondent waiver, and distribution for the September 28 conference. No brief in opposition was filed or provisioned. The analysis therefore treats the petition's factual description as an allegation, not an adjudicated fact.

## Certworthiness and vehicle

The underlying Takings Clause premise has some doctrinal force: ending an alleged physical occupation would not ordinarily erase a claim for compensation for the occupation's duration. But the decision presented for review is separated from that clean question by a long and case-specific procedural history. According to the petition itself, the Oklahoma appellate court said “as a matter of law” that no taking occurred after Rogne obtained relief through the administrative process, and the Tenth Circuit characterized that ruling as a merits resolution. Whether that characterization permits use of Oklahoma's savings statute is heavily dependent on the precise state judgments and state law.

The petition does not identify a conflict among federal courts of appeals or state courts on a recurring federal question. Instead, it principally alleges that the Tenth Circuit mischaracterized one Oklahoma judgment. The Court rarely grants review merely to correct a fact-bound error, especially where the asserted federal issue is entangled with claim preclusion, the meaning of a state savings statute, the adequacy and timing of administrative proceedings, and disputes over what property occupation occurred. The petition also appears to arise from an unpublished order and judgment and offers no developed alternative respondent account. Those are weak vehicles for clarifying *Knick* or *First English*.

The docket signals are likewise adverse. The City waived a response, and as of the snapshot the petition had only an initial distribution, with no relist or CVSG. A response waiver is not dispositive because the Court can request a response, but the absence of a call for response before initial distribution is a strong negative signal for an ordinary paid petition. No amici or nationally significant institutional parties appear in the provisioned record.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, but only a 0.8% grant rate across modern petitions with zero relists. Tenth Circuit petitions are granted about 5.0% overall, while petitions without a CVSG are granted about 3.0%. Those broad cuts are not independent and should not be multiplied. Here, the petition's individual defects and weak docket signals justify moving below the zero-relist rate to 0.6%.

The stakes are meaningful to Rogne but comparatively narrow: compensation for one local land-use dispute, filtered through preclusion and Oklahoma savings-law questions.
