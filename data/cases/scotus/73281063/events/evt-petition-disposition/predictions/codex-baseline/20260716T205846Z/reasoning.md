# Prediction: denied

I assign a 24% probability to a grant-side disposition (including a plenary grant, grant in part, or GVR) and predict denial. This is far above the ordinary cert-petition base rate, but the strongest reasons for review attach more cleanly to a linked petition than to this docket.

## Questions and governing standards

Arizona asks whether an organization has Article III standing when no identified member is shown likely to suffer an imminent injury, and whether the Ninth Circuit exceeded clear-error review by reweighing the district court's finding that Arizona did not enact H.B. 2243 with discriminatory purpose. The standing question invokes *Summers v. Earth Island Institute* and the requirement of concrete, imminent injury to at least one member. The merits question turns on deferential review of the district court's *Arlington Heights* analysis, including the presumption of legislative good faith emphasized in *Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee* and related cases.

## Record signals favoring review

The provisioned petition and questions presented identify a substantial standing disagreement: the Ninth Circuit allowed representational standing based on unidentified members who “may be” affected, while the petition describes seven circuits as requiring identification of an injured member. The case reached judgment after a ten-day bench trial, so the alleged evidentiary deficiency is more pronounced than at the pleading stage. On discriminatory intent, the district court considered the *Arlington Heights* factors and found no discriminatory purpose; the panel vacated that finding over Judge Bumatay's dissent. Eleven judges dissented from denial of rehearing en banc.

The snapshot adds unusually strong procedural signals. This is a paid state petition from the Ninth Circuit, four amicus briefs were filed across the linked matters, the case was distributed for the June 18 conference and redistributed for June 25, and the litigation is linked to dockets 25-1017 and 25-1019. These facts raise the petition well above the approximately 5.4% Term 2025 paid-petition grant rate in `metrics/statpack.json`; the committed statpack reports a 7.6% grant rate after one relist and a 3.0% grant rate for modern cert petitions from the Ninth Circuit.

## Reasons denial remains likelier

The United States' provisioned brief is the strongest docket-specific counterweight. It agrees that the Ninth Circuit's discriminatory-intent analysis was wrong and supports review of two statutory questions in linked docket 25-1017, but it expressly recommends denying this petition. The brief characterizes the intent issue as fact-bound and interlocutory because the district court may reaffirm its original finding on remand. It also argues that the standing question is a poor vehicle: the absence of imminent injury may permit a narrow resolution without deciding whether an organization must always name a member. Those objections give the Court a clean path to grant the linked lead petition while denying this one.

The second question also asks the Court to police clear-error review in a dense factual record, a form of error correction the Court generally avoids, especially before final judgment. The first question is more certworthy, but the combination of an unresolved remand, alternative direct-standing theories, and the Solicitor General's vehicle objection materially reduces the likelihood of four votes for this docket.

## Bottom line

The relist, en banc dissents, election-law stakes, and plausible standing split make a grant or grant-side hold/GVR a serious possibility. Nevertheless, the linked lead petition offers cleaner statutory questions and carries the United States' affirmative support, whereas this petition carries an explicit recommendation to deny and substantial interlocutory and vehicle problems. I therefore predict denial with 24% probability of any grant-side disposition. I do not predict individual Justice votes because the record does not support reliable vote-level assignments at the cert stage.
