# Prediction: denied

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether prejudice from a jury's exposure to extra-record information turns on whether the material detrimentally influenced deliberations, or instead on whether the verdict would have been different without it. At the cert stage, the controlling practical standard is the Rule of Four, informed principally by a square and mature lower-court conflict, the importance and recurrence of the federal question, and a clean vehicle. The merits framework invoked by the petition comes from *Mattox*, *Remmer*, *Smith*, and *Olano*: outside influence is presumptively serious, but the Court has not clearly specified whether detrimental influence on deliberations alone establishes prejudice or whether outcome-level harmlessness remains relevant.

## Record-specific grant signals

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition frame a genuine-looking doctrinal disagreement. The petition identifies the First, Second, and Ninth Circuits as focusing on whether extraneous material affected the deliberative process, while the Third and Tenth Circuits—and the Sixth Circuit below—permit the strength of the admitted evidence to defeat prejudice. CourtListener searches independently surfaced the principal cases on both sides, including *Dutkel*, *Henley*, *Morrison*, *Lloyd*, and *Scull*. This makes the asserted conflict more concrete than a petition resting only on differing verbal formulations.

The vehicle is comparatively clean. According to the complete merits portion of the provisioned petition, the district court found that at least one juror reviewed an unadmitted transcript and likely that jurors heard the unredacted recording; the Sixth Circuit accepted the serious error and acknowledged that the material plausibly undermined Maund's defense. The petition says the Sixth Circuit nevertheless reversed solely because it believed the verdict would not have differed. The case is a direct federal criminal proceeding, with no habeas deference problem, and the issue was preserved. Those features increase the chance of review.

The underlying jury-integrity question is recurring and constitutionally important. The CourtListener Supreme Court search also suggests that the Court has addressed this precise doctrinal area only intermittently; that long gap modestly supports review if four Justices regard the split as consequential.

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 3.5% grant rate for modern petitions from the Sixth Circuit. The current docket shows no relist and no CVSG. The statpack's zero-relist bucket has only a 0.8% resolved grant rate, although that cut is especially provisional here because the petition has not yet reached its first scheduled conference.

Second, the United States waived a response on June 8. A call for response could still occur before the September 28 conference, but no response or brief in opposition was present in the July 18 snapshot. The lack of adversarial briefing means the petition's characterization of the split and vehicle has not yet been tested, and the present absence of a requested response is a negative signal.

Third, the alleged split may be less outcome-determinative than the petition contends. Courts on both sides use harmlessness, presumptions, and context-sensitive inquiries, and the distinction between an effect on deliberations and an effect on the verdict can blur in application. The Sixth Circuit characterized the unadmitted material as one or two sentences that only mildly undermined a defense theory, which gives the Court a fact-bound reason to deny even if the broader issue is real. The petition also asks the Court to disentangle prejudice, burden allocation, structural error, and appellate review; that surrounding complexity may make another vehicle preferable.

Finally, the corpus query for recent 2020s grants returned a set dominated by petitions with three or more conference distributions, whereas this petition has only its initial distribution. That comparison is not subject-matter matched, but it reinforces the absence of a developed positive docket signal at this snapshot.

Balancing a well-presented split and clean federal vehicle against the low base rate, response waiver, lack of relist/CVSG, and possible fact-boundness, I place the petition slightly below the paid-petition base rate at 4.5%. The most likely disposition is a straightforward denial, not a GVR: there is no identified intervening authority or mootness event that would support vacatur and remand.
