# Robinhood Markets, Inc. v. Sodha, No. 25-944 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied is still the modal outcome, but P(grant) = 0.35 — far above
the baseline for a paid petition — because the Court has already CVSG'd the
case.**

## The legal question

Robinhood, its officers and directors, and its IPO underwriters seek review of
a divided Ninth Circuit panel decision (Aug. 29, 2025; rehearing en banc denied
Oct. 8, 2025) that reversed dismissal of a Securities Act class action arising
from Robinhood's July 2021 IPO. Two questions presented (from the petition):

1. Whether an issuer violates **Section 11(a)'s misleading-omissions prong** by
   failing to disclose interim (intra-quarter) financial data that investors may
   consider material, without any inquiry into whether the omission rendered an
   affirmative statement misleading.
2. Whether an issuer violates **Item 303 of Regulation S-K** — and thus
   Section 11(a)'s required-statements prong — by failing to disclose and
   quantify interim financial data before quarterly reporting deadlines.

This is the natural sequel to *Macquarie Infrastructure Corp. v. Moab Partners*
(2024), where the Court held pure omissions are not actionable under Rule
10b-5(b) and expressly noted that Section 11(a) is textually different. The
petition claims the Ninth Circuit collapsed materiality into misleadingness
(conflicting with the First Circuit's *Shaw* "extreme departure" rule and with
other circuits' text-first approach) and read Item 303's "trend" language more
broadly than the Fifth and Eleventh Circuits.

## Weighing grant vs. deny

**For a grant.** The CVSG shows at least several Justices take the questions
seriously. The case sits squarely in the Court's recent line of securities
disclosure cases (*Macquarie*, *Omnicare*, *Slack v. Pirani* — the last also a
Ninth Circuit Section 11 reversal), the split allegations are colorable (First
Circuit's *Shaw* test; Fifth/Eleventh Circuit limits on Item 303), the panel
below was divided, and the stakes for IPO disclosure practice are substantial
and well-amplified by the amici.

**For a denial.** The respondents' BIO makes two arguments with real bite:

1. **Posture.** The Ninth Circuit reversed a Rule 12(b)(6) dismissal and
   remanded for application of (what it characterized as) settled materiality
   standards — an interlocutory posture with no factual record and no holding
   that liability exists. The Court generally awaits final judgment.
2. **The NVIDIA/Facebook DIG pattern.** In OT2024 the Court dismissed as
   improvidently granted two pleading-stage securities cases (*NVIDIA v.
   E. Ohman*, *Facebook v. Amalgamated Bank*) after concluding the cert-stage
   framing dissolved into factbound application of agreed standards. The BIO
   argues — plausibly — that the Ninth Circuit did not adopt the bright-line
   rules the petition attributes to it, and that petitioners themselves do not
   ask the Court to adopt the First Circuit's "extreme departure" test. That
   recent institutional burn makes the Court demonstrably cautious in exactly
   this genre.
3. **An administrative off-ramp.** The SEC has opened a comprehensive review
   of Regulation S-K (per the BIO, a Jan. 13, 2026 Atkins statement), giving
   the SG an easy basis to recommend denial on QP2 as premature.

**The CVSG mechanics.** The disposition now runs through the SG's brief, which
will likely not arrive until late 2026 or spring 2027. Historically the Court
follows the SG's cert recommendation in the large majority of CVSG cases, and
the SG recommends denial more often than grant. Here the SG's call is genuinely
uncertain: the government's *Macquarie* position and the current SEC's
deregulatory posture cut toward sympathy with petitioners on the merits, but
the interlocutory posture and the pending Reg S-K rulemaking give a
conventional basis to recommend deny (or deny QP2, grant QP1). A GVR is not a
live path — there is no intervening decision to GVR against.

## Bottom line

Anchor on the corpus CVSG base rate (~27% grant), then adjust modestly upward
for the unusually strong grant-side cluster (divided panel, colorable
post-*Macquarie* splits, elite counsel, Chamber/SIFMA/Grundfest amici, a
recurring and important question the Court has repeatedly engaged) and
modestly back down for the interlocutory posture and the Court's fresh DIG
scars in pleading-stage securities cases. That lands at **P(grant) ≈ 0.35**,
with **denied** remaining the single most likely disposition. No per-Justice
votes predicted at the cert stage.
