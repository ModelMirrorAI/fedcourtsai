# Prediction

I predict **denial**, with a **36% probability of any grant**. That is far above the ordinary cert-petition rate but still leaves denial as the single most likely disposition.

## Question and governing standard

The petition presents two related securities-disclosure questions. First, it asks whether Section 11(a)'s misleading-omissions prong can require disclosure of material interim financial data without a separate inquiry into whether the omission made an affirmative statement misleading. Second, it asks whether Item 303 can require disclosure and reasonably practicable quantification of interim data before the normal reporting deadline.

The cert inquiry is whether the case presents an important recurring federal question, a genuine and outcome-determinative conflict among the circuits, and a clean vehicle for resolving it. The merits backdrop is *Macquarie Infrastructure Corp. v. Moab Partners*, which distinguishes actionable half-truths from pure omissions under closely related securities-law language.

## Record-specific signals

The strongest grant signal is procedural: after the petition was distributed for the May 28 conference, the Court invited the Solicitor General's views on June 1. In the committed statpack, modern CVSG petitions have a 27.1% grant rate among 59 resolved cases, compared with a 5.36% estimated grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions. The CVSG therefore moves this case well above its ordinary paid-petition and Ninth Circuit baselines. Three cert-stage amicus briefs from securities and business interests, the recent *Macquarie* decision, a divided 100-plus-page Ninth Circuit opinion, and the nationwide importance of IPO and periodic-reporting rules add weight.

The petition also has a plausible conflict theory. Robinhood characterizes the Ninth Circuit as collapsing materiality and misleadingness despite *Macquarie*, departing from the Third, Fifth, Tenth, and Eleventh Circuits and rejecting the First Circuit's treatment of intra-quarter data. On Item 303, it identifies tension with the Fifth and Eleventh Circuits over persistence and with decisions applying more definite temporal limits. Robinhood further argues that its offering documents warned that exceptional first-quarter performance would not continue, forecast lower third-quarter revenue, and accurately estimated second-quarter revenue.

The opposition supplies substantial reasons not to grant. It characterizes the decision as a remand applying ordinary materiality principles to alleged half-truths: Robinhood disclosed selected positive second-quarter information while omitting contemporaneous declines. The Ninth Circuit expressly left materiality, the existence and persistence of any Item 303 trend, knowledge, and practicable quantification for the district court. That interlocutory, pleading-stage posture makes the asserted conflicts less clean and creates a vehicle risk resembling the securities cases the Court recently dismissed as improvidently granted. The opposition also persuasively disputes whether any circuit has adopted the categorical rules described in the petition. An ongoing SEC review of Regulation S-K could make agency views especially important and could favor leaving refinement to the agency.

## Calibration and disposition

The statpack's 27.1% CVSG rate is the most relevant empirical anchor. I adjust upward because the Court recently addressed analogous omission language, the petition has sophisticated amici, and the alleged disagreement affects a large securities-law jurisdiction. I stop at 36% because the lower court did not finally impose liability, both questions may reduce to application of accepted standards, and the Solicitor General's still-unknown position may recommend denial or agency-led resolution. A plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR because there is no identified intervening ruling that would itself require reconsideration; denial remains the modal outcome.

The **big-case score is 0.78**. If decided, the case could materially affect IPO drafting, interim financial disclosures, Item 303 compliance, and the settlement leverage of Section 11 suits nationwide. Robinhood's prominence and the claimed multibillion-dollar stakes add public salience, although the technical securities-law framing keeps it below the highest-profile constitutional cases.
