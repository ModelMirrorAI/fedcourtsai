# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.15% probability of a grant and predict denial. The petition presents recognizable constitutional themes, but it is a poor vehicle for resolving either asserted question and does not show a clean, outcome-determinative conflict warranting certiorari.

## Questions and governing standards

The petition raises three issues: whether ineffective-assistance prejudice may be assessed cumulatively; whether the differing results of two trials establish prejudice under *Strickland v. Washington* or *Brecht v. Abrahamson*; and whether refusing to poll every juror after the foreperson reports a deadlock impermissibly shifts the prosecution's burden to establish manifest necessity for a mistrial.

Certiorari is discretionary and ordinarily requires a square conflict, an important recurring federal question, or a serious departure from governing law. This case also arrives through federal habeas review after the district court denied relief and a certificate of appealability, and the Eighth Circuit denied a certificate in an unpublished disposition. The habeas posture adds the limits of 28 U.S.C. § 2254(d): relief generally must rest on clearly established Supreme Court law, not merely a preferable synthesis of lower-court decisions. A cert grant requires four votes; no Justice-specific votes are predicted because cert votes are not publicly attributable here.

## Record-specific analysis

The complete 17-page pro se petition states that Price's first five-day trial ended in a mistrial after eight hours of deliberation. The judge questioned only the foreperson, accepted the foreperson's statement that the jury was hopelessly deadlocked, and declined Price's request to poll the entire panel. A second jury convicted Price of aiding and abetting robbery and first-degree assault. According to the petition, counsel at the second trial did not use several prior inconsistencies and a failed photo identification to impeach the principal eyewitness as thoroughly as counsel had at the first trial. State courts rejected the asserted deficiencies individually; the federal district court denied habeas relief and a certificate of appealability; and the Eighth Circuit denied a certificate.

The first question has some certworthiness in the abstract because the petition collects decisions using differing language about cumulative-error review. But it does not establish a clean split on the same legal proposition. Courts often distinguish between considering the combined prejudicial effect of multiple proven counsel deficiencies within *Strickland*'s totality-of-the-evidence inquiry and aggregating claims that were not constitutional errors at all. The cited authorities appear to span those different formulations, several are unpublished, and the unpublished denial of a certificate of appealability is an unusually weak vehicle for announcing a general rule.

The second question is predominantly factual and rests on an inference the Court is unlikely to accept as dispositive. A hung first jury is not equivalent to an acquittal and does not establish that the second trial would probably have ended differently absent counsel's alleged omissions. The two trials necessarily involved different deliberations, and the petition does not supply a developed record isolating counsel's impeachment choices as the cause of the different outcomes. *Brecht*'s substantial-and-injurious-effect test for trial error also does not turn the first mistrial into automatic proof of *Strickland* prejudice.

The double-jeopardy question is likewise factbound. *Arizona v. Washington* requires manifest necessity and affords substantial latitude to a trial judge assessing a deadlock; the petition identifies no Supreme Court rule categorically requiring an individual poll of all jurors. Its own discussion acknowledges authority that a defendant has no right to such a poll. Decisions criticizing reliance on a foreperson in particular circumstances do not clearly establish the proposed mandatory procedure, especially on deferential habeas review.

The docket snapshot adds negative procedural signals: this is an in-forma-pauperis prisoner petition, no brief in opposition was provisioned, and the docket shows ordinary distribution for conference rather than an order requesting a response. Those signals are not dispositive, but they reinforce the already very low base rate.
