# Webb v. Trombley, No. 25-1346 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.02.**

## The legal question

Whether the second sentence of 42 U.S.C. § 1997e(d)(2) (PLRA) — "If the award
of attorney's fees is not greater than 150 percent of the judgment, the excess
shall be paid by the defendant" — prohibits fee awards greater than 150% of
the monetary judgment. Every court of appeals to reach the question (the
Second Circuit in *Shepherd v. Goord*, and per the summary order below, the
First, Third through Eleventh Circuits as well) has read the provision as a
150% fee cap. Webb, who won a $15,000 excessive-force verdict and had his
$206,977.50 fee request cut to $22,500, argues on textualist grounds that the
cap is a judicial invention.

## Why denial is heavily favored

1. **No circuit split on the question presented.** The petition's core ask is
   to overturn a *unanimous* consensus of eleven circuits. The Court grants
   review overwhelmingly to resolve splits; it rarely grants to declare every
   circuit wrong, absent strong intervening signals (a statutory-interpretation
   sea change, repeated separate opinions flagging the issue, or SG
   involvement), none of which are present. The splits the petition does
   identify (Sixth vs. Ninth Circuit on whether the cap reaches appellate
   fees, *Riley v. Kurtz* vs. *Woods v. Carey*; a Tenth Circuit vs.
   district-court disagreement on pre-incarceration conduct) are *downstream
   of* the cap's existence and are **not presented by this case**, which
   involves only trial-level fees — this is not a vehicle for those questions,
   and a circuit-versus-district-court disagreement is not a cert-worthy split
   at all.
2. **The Court has left this regime alone for nearly 30 years.** The fee cap
   has been applied since *Boivin* (1st Cir. 2000) and *Walker* (6th Cir.
   2001); the Court has repeatedly declined to review it, and in *Murphy v.
   Smith* (2018) it construed the *first* sentence of § 1997e(d)(2) (where
   there was a real split) while treating the surrounding fee-limitation
   scheme as settled background. A CourtListener search surfaces no SCOTUS
   opinion or pending companion docket engaging the 150%-cap question.
3. **Waived response, one distribution, long conference.** Petitions decided
   at the long conference without a call for a response are denied at very
   high rates. The realistic grant path here requires a call for a response,
   then repeated relists — none of which has happened as of the July 18, 2026
   snapshot. Corpus base rates (see below): grant rate ~2.5–3% for modern
   petitions overall, ~0.8% for petitions never relisted; recent granted
   petitions in the corpus show multiple distributions and specialist counsel.
4. **Modest countervailing signals.** The petition is paid and counseled, the
   vehicle is clean (a single pure question of statutory interpretation,
   squarely passed on below), the textualist argument is colorable and cites
   the Court's recent statutory-interpretation decisions, and the *Riley*/*Woods*
   split plus sustained academic criticism give the issue some salience. These
   justify a probability above the never-relisted floor, and leave room for a
   call for a response after the long conference, but they do not approach the
   profile of a granted case.

## Probability

Anchor: modern discretionary-cert grant rate ~2.5–3% (statpack, Terms
2023–2025); Second Circuit petitions ~5.4%; zero-relist petitions ~0.8%; no
CVSG ~3%. Adjusting down for the waived response, the unanimous circuit
consensus against the petitioner, and the unpublished summary order below,
and up slightly for paid/counseled status and vehicle quality:
**P(grant, including GVR) ≈ 0.02**. GVR is essentially unavailable here — no
intervening decision bears on the question — so the predicted disposition if
not an outright denial would be a plenary grant, and denial is the modal
outcome by a wide margin. Most likely near-term docket event short of denial
is a request for a response, which would still leave denial the likely
endpoint.

Predicted disposition: **denied** (granted = 0).
