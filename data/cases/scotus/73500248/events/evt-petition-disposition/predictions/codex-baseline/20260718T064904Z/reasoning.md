# Prediction: denied

I assign a 2.5% probability to any grant and predict denial. The petition is a paid, forward-mode petition from the Second Circuit, presently distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. I do not predict a GVR because the record identifies no intervening authority, mootness issue, or other reason for summary vacatur.

## Question and governing standard

The question is whether the second sentence of 42 U.S.C. § 1997e(d)(2) prohibits a prevailing prisoner from receiving attorney's fees greater than 150% of the monetary judgment. The petition argues that the sentence addresses only awards that are not greater than 150% and does not cap larger awards. The Second Circuit treated its precedent as binding and affirmed a reduction of requested fees from $206,977.50 to $22,500, or 150% of a $15,000 civil-rights judgment.

At the certiorari stage, the practical question is whether four Justices are likely to see a clean, important conflict or a sufficiently consequential federal-law error warranting review. The correctness of the petition's textual argument matters, but vehicle quality, conflict, recurrence, importance, and the case's procedural signals are more predictive of a grant.

## Grant signals

The vehicle is comparatively clean. The question is legal, preserved, and outcome-determinative; both courts below expressly relied on the fee-cap construction. The petition also makes a plausible textualist argument based on the conditional word “if,” Congress's use of express prohibitory language in neighboring subsections, and the Court's insistence in *Murphy v. Smith* on attending to the words Congress chose in the same provision. The issue recurs nationally and can materially affect whether counsel will accept meritorious prisoner civil-rights cases, especially those producing low damages.

The general base-rate cuts modestly favor this petition over the average filing. The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025 Term petitions and a 5.4% rate for modern petitions originating in the Second Circuit, compared with roughly 3.1% across resolved modern discretionary-cert petitions.

## Denial signals

The central defect is the absence of a conflict on the question presented. The petition's own appendix says that every numbered circuit to address § 1997e(d)(2) has adopted the 150% cap. Its asserted splits concern downstream applications—appellate fees, pre-incarceration conduct, and mixed injunctive relief—not whether the cap exists. Reversing a longstanding, nationwide consensus without a direct split is possible but uncommon.

Other signals reinforce the low prior. The Second Circuit disposition is an unpublished summary order. Respondents waived a response, and the July 18 snapshot shows neither a Court-requested response nor a CVSG. The case has not yet produced a relist signal; the statpack's resolved zero-relist bucket has only a 0.8% grant rate, although that figure deserves limited weight before the first scheduled conference. The petition is also asking the Court to revisit an interpretation followed for about twenty-five years, and its proposed reading must overcome the conventional inference that the second sentence functions as a cap rather than merely guaranteeing payment of smaller awards. The petition's proposed alternative functions for the sentence are creative but not obviously compelled.

Balancing the clean vehicle and non-frivolous textual argument against the lack of a direct split and the weak docket signals, I place the case below the paid and Second Circuit baselines but above a purely frivolous filing. Denial is substantially more likely than grant, dismissal, or another disposition.
