## Qualitative Analysis

### Legal Question and Governing Standard
The legal question presented is whether the second sentence of the Prison Litigation Reform Act's (PLRA) attorney’s fees provision, 42 U.S.C. § 1997e(d)(2), prohibits awards of attorney’s fees that are greater than 150% of the judgment. The provision states: "If the award of attorney’s fees is not greater than 150 percent of the judgment, the excess shall be paid by the defendant." The petitioner challenges the "entrenched" and uniform interpretation across multiple courts of appeals that this language imposes a strict cap on the total fee award.

### Facts from the Snapshot
The petitioner, Michael Webb, won a civil rights judgment and was awarded attorney's fees, which the Second Circuit apparently capped according to its precedent (*Shepherd v. Goord*, 662 F.3d 603 (2d Cir. 2011)). Webb filed a petition for certiorari on May 29, 2026. This is a paid case, meaning the petitioner is represented by paid counsel (Brian David Ginsberg). On June 24, 2026, the respondents (Edmund Trombley, et al., represented by the New York Solicitor General) filed a waiver of their right to respond. The case was distributed for the September 28, 2026, long conference.

### Reasoning Behind Probability and Predicted Disposition
1. **Circuit Consensus vs. Splits:** The petition openly acknowledges that its argument rests on an "atextual" interpretation that is "entrenched" across multiple federal courts of appeals. The petition claims that this uniform misinterpretation has spawned "downstream" circuit splits on secondary issues (e.g., whether the cap applies to appellate fees). However, because the primary question presented asks the Supreme Court to overturn a unanimous, decades-old interpretation of a 1996 statute, the Court is exceptionally unlikely to grant the petition. If Congress disagreed with the courts' unanimous consensus that § 1997e(d)(2) caps fees at 150% of the judgment, it has had ample opportunity to amend the PLRA.
2. **Waiver of Response:** A waiver of the right to respond is a strong signal from the respondent that the petition is not cert-worthy and is highly unlikely to be granted. While the Court will sometimes call for a response (CFR) if a petition raises a genuine, fresh circuit split, the Court is unlikely to do so here because the primary issue involves overturning an entrenched consensus.
3. **Base Rates:** The overall base rate for a paid petition to be granted is roughly 2.5% to 3.3%. For a petition with 0 relists, the grant rate drops to roughly 0.8%. Taking into account the uniform circuit consensus against the petitioner's primary argument and the respondent's waiver, the likelihood of a grant (or even a CFR leading to a grant) is depressed below the standard base rate.

### Conclusion
The Supreme Court will almost certainly deny the petition summarily.
**Probability of Grant:** 1.5% (0.015)
**Predicted Disposition:** Denied
