# Prediction: denied

I estimate a **7% probability of any grant** and predict denial of certiorari.

## Question and governing framework

New York bars sales to minors of dietary supplements represented for weight loss or muscle building. The petition asks whether the Second Circuit diluted the third and fourth prongs of *Central Hudson*: first, by accepting studies, anecdotes, and common sense without evidence that the statute's particular marketing-based classification will materially alleviate harm; and second, by deferring to the legislature despite a proposed ingredients-based alternative.

At the certiorari stage, the important considerations are whether the decision creates a genuine and outcome-relevant conflict, whether the issue warrants nationwide resolution, and whether this case cleanly presents it. Four votes are required to grant. The Court ordinarily does not use certiorari to correct a fact-bound application of a settled test, and interlocutory posture is a substantial vehicle concern.

## Record signals

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition from the Second Circuit, four supporting amicus briefs, a filed BIO and reply, and distribution for the September 28 conference. It shows no relist or CVSG. The provisioned questions presented and petition make a plausible doctrinal pitch: the panel called marketing an indicator of risk, relied partly on common sense, and said it must defer to the legislature's reasonable judgment. The petition argues that other circuits demand more evidence and more serious consideration of non-speech alternatives. The issue has significance beyond supplements because speech or labeling is often used to identify regulated products.

The BIO substantially weakens the grant case. It characterizes the supposed split as different applications of the same settled standard and identifies scientific studies, anecdotes, and product-safety evidence in the preliminary-injunction record. It also says the statute considers ingredients and retail placement as well as labeling and marketing. Most importantly, the judgment reviewed is an interlocutory affirmance of the denial of a preliminary injunction in ongoing litigation. The lower courts also relied independently on delay, irreparable harm, and the public interest. Even a petitioner victory on the questions presented therefore might not change the denial of preliminary relief, and a fuller merits record could sharpen or eliminate the evidentiary disputes. There is no noted panel dissent or en banc dissent.

The provisioned petition text is marked truncated, but its questions, statement, certworthiness argument, and conclusion are present; the truncation falls in the appended lower-court material and does not obscure the petition's principal pitch. The BIO is complete.

## Calibration and conclusion

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025 Term petitions and 5.4% for modern petitions originating in the Second Circuit. The four amicus filings, constitutional subject, and asserted multi-circuit disagreement justify moving above that ordinary baseline. The absence of a relist or CVSG, the respondent's strong factual-distinction answer, the preliminary-injunction posture, independent equitable grounds, and ongoing proceedings pull sharply the other way. On balance, the case has a credible but distinctly nonstandard path to review, producing a 7% forecast rather than a high-signal grant prediction.

I do not predict individual votes: the record supports at most a tentative inference that some Justices interested in stronger protection for commercial speech could favor review, but not a reliable Justice-by-Justice allocation.
