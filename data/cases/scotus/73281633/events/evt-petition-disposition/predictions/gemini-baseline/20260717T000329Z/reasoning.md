# Prediction: Denied

## The Legal Question
The petitioner, Council for Responsible Nutrition (CRN), challenges a New York law (N.Y. Gen. Bus. Law § 391-oo) that bans the sale of dietary supplements marketed for "weight loss" or "muscle building" to minors. CRN argues the law is a content-based restriction on commercial speech that fails intermediate scrutiny under *Central Hudson Gas & Elec. Corp. v. Pub. Serv. Comm’n of N.Y.* The questions presented ask whether the Second Circuit erred in its application of *Central Hudson*'s third prong (by allegedly not requiring empirical evidence that the speech restriction will ameliorate harm) and fourth prong (by allegedly deferring to the legislature rather than requiring consideration of less-intrusive alternatives).

## The Record and Arguments
The Second Circuit affirmed the district court's denial of a preliminary injunction, holding that New York satisfied *Central Hudson*. CRN's petition alleges a split among the circuits on the evidentiary burden required for the third prong (citing the 4th, 5th, 6th, 9th, and 10th Circuits as requiring "exacting and supportive data" versus the 1st and 2nd Circuits relying on "common sense") and the deference owed under the fourth prong. The petition is supported by four amicus briefs at the cert stage, including from the Manhattan Institute and the Taxpayers Protection Alliance Foundation, indicating significant interest from commercial speech and retail advocacy groups.

The State of New York's Brief in Opposition counters that the Second Circuit's decision was "record-bound," relying on "scientific studies, anecdotal evidence, and common sense," rather than common sense alone. The State argues that the purported circuit split is illusory and merely reflects different courts applying the established *Central Hudson* standard to varying factual records. Furthermore, the State argues the case is a poor vehicle for revisiting the *Central Hudson* framework.

## Reasoning and Base Rates
The historical base rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions from the Second Circuit is approximately 5.4% (above the baseline 3.1% average). The presence of four amicus briefs at the cert stage significantly elevates the probability of a grant, as it signals to the Court that the issue has national importance and stakes beyond the immediate parties. The case also touches on a recurring tension in First Amendment jurisprudence regarding commercial speech and regulations aimed at protecting minors.

However, the Court often denies cert in cases where the alleged circuit split amounts to a disagreement over the application of an established test to specific facts. Because New York plausibly argues that the Second Circuit did rely on evidentiary data (not just "common sense"), the Court may view this case as a dispute over the sufficiency of the specific record rather than a clean split on the legal standard itself. The lack of a CVSG also tempers the grant probability. Consequently, while the amicus support pushes the probability above the base rate, a denial remains the most likely outcome.
