# Sanders v. City of Long Beach, No. 25-1235 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.002.**

## The case

Leslie Sanders, a Long Beach homeowner proceeding pro se, sued the City of Long
Beach over flood damage to his and neighbors' homes from a February 2019 storm,
alleging the City had constructive notice (a 2005 stormwater report) that Pump
13 was a dangerous condition and failed to repair it or warn residents
(Cal. Gov. Code § 835 premises liability, negligence, nuisance). After a bench
trial, Judge Kim found for the City. The California Court of Appeal (Second
Appellate District, Div. Seven) affirmed in an unpublished opinion
(Aug. 18, 2025; rehearing denied Sept. 3, 2025), and the California Supreme
Court denied review (Nov. 19, 2025). The petition was filed Feb. 9, 2026,
docketed Apr. 30, 2026, and — with no brief in opposition on the docket — was
distributed on June 17, 2026 for the Conference of September 28, 2026 (the
"long conference").

## The question presented and the governing standard

The QP is a single run-on paragraph asserting that the trial judge (1) excluded
the plaintiffs' impeachment evidence (depositions, the 2005 report) on
custodianship/foundation grounds, (2) admitted the City witness's allegedly
perjured and hearsay-based testimony, (3) displayed bias, and (4) departed from
"uniformity" of federal and state law — framed as violations of the Fifth,
Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments.

Certiorari is governed by Rule 10: the Court looks for conflicts among circuits
or state courts of last resort on an important federal question, or a decision
in conflict with this Court's precedent — and expressly disfavors petitions
asserting "erroneous factual findings or the misapplication of a properly
stated rule of law." Every factor cuts against this petition:

- **Fact-bound error correction.** The grievances are individual evidentiary
  rulings in a state civil bench trial — the paradigm of what Rule 10 excludes.
- **The federal hooks are largely miscast.** The Sixth Amendment's
  Confrontation Clause applies to criminal prosecutions, not civil trials; the
  Federal Rules of Evidence and Civil Procedure (FRE 613, 802, 901; FRCP 26,
  32) do not govern California state courts; Fifth Amendment due process
  constrains federal, not state, action. The only colorable claim is
  Fourteenth Amendment due process, presented as garden-variety trial error —
  and state evidentiary rulings essentially never make a federal due-process
  question the Court would take.
- **No conflict.** The cited authorities (Harris v. New York, United States v.
  Havens, AT&T Mobility v. Concepcion) are not in tension with the decision
  below; no split is alleged beyond a generalized "uniformity" appeal.
- **Poor vehicle.** Unpublished intermediate state-court opinion; discretionary
  review denied without comment below; the appellate court found the claims
  unsupported by record citations, and independent state-law grounds
  (California evidence and government-claims law) underlie the judgment.

## Docket signals

- **Pro se, paid petition** (fee class "Paid", petitioner is his own counsel of
  record). Pro se petitions — paid or IFP — are granted at rates well below
  even the low overall paid rate; grants essentially require a call for a
  response and re-distribution, neither present here.
- **No brief in opposition.** The response was due June 1, 2026; none appears,
  and the case was distributed anyway — the City evidently waived. The Court
  does not grant without first calling for a response (CFR), and no CFR issued
  before distribution.
- **Distributed once, for the September 28, 2026 long conference** — the
  posture in which the summer's accumulated petitions are denied en masse in
  the first October order list. Zero relists, no CVSG, no amici.

Starting from the ~2.5% modern anchor and adjusting sharply down for: pro se
presentation, no BIO and no CFR, zero relists, unpublished state
intermediate-court origin, no split, miscast federal claims, and a single
distribution to the long conference — I put P(grant, including GVR) at ~0.2%.
There is no plausible GVR hook (no intervening decision of this Court bears on
the claims), and dismissal is unlikely for a paid, timely petition, so
"denied" takes essentially all remaining mass.
