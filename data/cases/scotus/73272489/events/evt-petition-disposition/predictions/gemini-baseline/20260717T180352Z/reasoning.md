# Reasoning

## Legal Question and Standard
The petitioner, Leslie Sanders, acting pro se, filed a civil lawsuit against the City of Long Beach for premises liability, public nuisance, and negligence following flooding that damaged his home in 2019. The petition for certiorari challenges the state trial court's evidentiary rulings, specifically the exclusion of a 2005 Stormwater Report and the denial of the ability to use prior depositions and interrogatories to impeach a key witness for the City. The petitioner frames these state law evidentiary and procedural issues as violations of the 5th, 6th, and 14th Amendments (Due Process, Equal Protection, and the Confrontation Clause). 

The governing standard for granting certiorari requires compelling reasons, such as a circuit split on an important question of federal law, a state court deciding a federal question in a way that conflicts with another state court of last resort or a federal court of appeals, or a state court significantly departing from the accepted and usual course of judicial proceedings. 

## Facts Driving the Outcome
1. **Pro Se Petitioner with Fact-Bound State Law Claims:** The petition fundamentally asks the Supreme Court to review state court rulings on the admissibility of evidence (hearsay, authentication, impeachment) and the application of California tort law (Gov't Code 835) in a municipal liability case. The Supreme Court does not sit as a court of errors to review state court evidentiary rulings or the application of state tort law.
2. **Lack of a Valid Constitutional Claim:** While the petitioner invokes the 5th, 6th, and 14th Amendments, the arguments reflect a misunderstanding of constitutional law as applied to civil trials in state courts. The Confrontation Clause of the 6th Amendment applies to criminal prosecutions, not civil tort suits. Disagreements with a judge's discretionary evidentiary rulings do not constitute a Due Process or Equal Protection violation warranting Supreme Court review.
3. **No Split of Authority:** The petition does not allege any split between federal circuits or state supreme courts on any federal question. It merely asserts that the lower courts ruled incorrectly and out of step with general principles of "uniformity" in federal rules of evidence (which do not govern state court proceedings).
4. **Procedural Posture:** The case was denied review en banc by the California Supreme Court. The petition docket shows only a single distribution for conference (0 relists), which heavily correlates with a denial (the baseline grant rate for 0 relists is less than 1%).

## Probability and Conclusion
Cases of this nature—pro se petitions seeking error correction of state court evidentiary and tort liability decisions without any genuine federal question or split of authority—are summarily denied. The probability of a grant is exceedingly low, estimated at 0.1%. Therefore, the predicted disposition is denied.
