## 2. Background and Procedural History

### A. The Incident and Claims
The case arises from a major rainstorm and subsequent flooding on February 2, 2019, which caused severe property damage at the Orangewood Village Townhomes in Long Beach, California. Leslie Sanders and other residents filed a lawsuit in Los Angeles County Superior Court (Case No. 19STCV43406) against the City of Long Beach, alleging that the city was negligent in maintaining its stormwater infrastructure (specifically Pump Station 13) under California Government Code § 835 (liability of public entities for dangerous conditions of public property). The plaintiffs argued that the city was aware of a dangerous condition and negligently failed to implement backup generators to maintain power at the pump station during severe weather.

### B. State Court Outcomes
1. **Trial Court:** Following a bench trial, Judge Mark C. Kim of the Los Angeles County Superior Court entered a judgment in favor of the City of Long Beach in September 2023. The court found that the city lacked actual or constructive knowledge of a dangerous condition, had performed reasonable, regular maintenance, and had acted reasonably given its budgetary and infrastructure constraints.
2. **California Court of Appeal:** Sanders appealed *in pro. per.* to the Second Appellate District (Division Seven, Case No. B334226). On August 18, 2025, the Court of Appeal issued an unpublished opinion affirming the trial court's judgment.
3. **California Supreme Court:** Sanders's petition for review en banc (Case No. S293138) was denied on November 19, 2025.

### C. Petition to the U.S. Supreme Court
Sanders filed a petition for a writ of certiorari postmarked February 9, 2026, which was docketed as No. 25-1235 on April 30, 2026. The respondent (City of Long Beach) did not file a brief in opposition (or filed a waiver of its right to respond), and on June 17, 2026, the Clerk distributed the petition for the Court's conference on September 28, 2026, without requesting a response.

## 3. Analysis of the Questions Presented and Substantive Merits

In his petition, Sanders raises several arguments attempting to frame state-court trial and evidentiary rulings as constitutional violations under the Fifth, Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments:

### A. Evidentiary and Witness Impeachment Claims
Sanders argues that the trial judge erred by:
* Excluding key historical evidence (specifically a 2005 Storm Water Report) that allegedly proved the city's long-standing knowledge of dangerous conditions.
* Denying the plaintiffs' ability to impeach a city witness with inconsistent statements or prior deposition testimony.
* Limiting cross-examination and confrontation of an individual who provided hearsay statements not under oath.

### B. Constitutional Framing Failures
Sanders's attempts to elevate these evidentiary disputes to the federal constitutional level are legally deficient:
1. **The Sixth Amendment's Confrontation Clause** ("In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right ... to be confronted with the witnesses against him") applies **exclusively to criminal prosecutions**. It has no application to a civil property damage lawsuit.
2. **The Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause** applies strictly to the actions of the federal government, not to state courts or municipal entities.
3. **The Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause** does govern state civil trials. However, the admission and exclusion of evidence are matters of state evidentiary law, over which trial courts possess broad discretion. An evidentiary ruling or credibility determination in a civil trial does not rise to a federal constitutional violation unless it renders the entire trial fundamentally unfair, which is not supported by the record. Sanders had a full trial, a direct appeal, and sought supreme court review at the state level. Disagreement with factual and credibility findings does not implicate a viable due process claim.

### C. Pure State-Law Dispute and Rule 10 Considerations
Under **Supreme Court Rule 10**, certiorari is granted only for "compelling reasons," such as resolving a conflict between federal courts of appeals or state supreme courts on an important question of federal law.
* **No Federal Question:** The underlying claim is based on California Government Code § 835, which is a state-law statutory tort. State-court interpretations and applications of state statutes do not present federal questions. The Supreme Court has no jurisdiction to review a state court's interpretation of its own state's laws.
* **No Split of Authority:** There is no division of opinion among federal circuits or state supreme courts on any legal question presented in this case.
* **Fact-Bound Nature of the Petition:** Rule 10 explicitly states that certiorari is "rarely granted when the asserted error consists of erroneous factual findings or the misapplication of a properly stated rule of law." Sanders's entire petition consists of a request to review the trial judge's factual findings and discretionary evidentiary rulings.
* **Unpublished Lower Court Decision:** The California Court of Appeal's decision was unpublished under California Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(a). The Supreme Court almost never grants certiorari to review unpublished state decisions, as they carry no precedential weight and have no impact on the uniformity of federal law.

## 4. Key Procedural and Statistical Indicators

The procedural posture of this docket provides conclusive evidence that the petition will be denied:

1. **Pro Se Status:** The petitioner is representing himself *pro se* and seeking to represent other property owners *pro se* (which is also procedurally improper for non-attorney litigants). The Supreme Court's grant rate for *pro se* petitions is exceptionally low (typically less than 0.1%).
2. **No Response Requested:** Under established Supreme Court practice, the Court will **never grant** a petition for a writ of certiorari without first calling for a response from the respondent. If a respondent waives the right to file an opposition (as is common for frivolous or pro se petitions) and the Court distributes the petition for conference without requesting a response, it is a definitive signal that the petition will be denied.
