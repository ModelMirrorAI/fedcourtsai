# Prediction: denied

I assign a **0.15% probability of any grant** (including a plenary grant, grant in part, or GVR) and predict denial. I do not predict individual votes: certiorari denials ordinarily disclose no vote, and the record supplies no basis for justice-specific estimates.

## Question and posture

Leslie Sanders, proceeding pro se, seeks review of an unpublished decision of the California Court of Appeal arising from a civil action over 2019 flooding in Long Beach. The petition says the trial judge excluded a 2005 storm-water report, depositions, and other material offered to establish the City's notice of a dangerous condition and to impeach a City witness. It characterizes the rulings and the judge's credibility findings as bias and violations of the Fifth, Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments, federal evidence and civil-procedure rules, and federal criminal statutes.

The July 17, 2026 snapshot identifies the filing as a paid 2025-Term petition, docketed April 30, 2026, and distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. It shows no relist and no CVSG. The provisioned document inventory contains the complete 24-page petition and its questions-presented extract, but no brief in opposition.

## Governing considerations

Certiorari is discretionary and ordinarily requires a conflict among courts, an important unsettled federal question, or a serious departure from accepted law—not merely alleged error in one case. Review of a state judgment under 28 U.S.C. § 1257 also requires a properly presented federal issue and a vehicle in which the Court can resolve it without becoming entangled in independent state-law grounds or record disputes.

The petition does not identify a genuine split. Its core complaints concern the admissibility, authentication, and weight of evidence in a particular California bench trial. Federal Rules of Evidence and Civil Procedure do not themselves govern a California state-court trial; the Sixth Amendment's confrontation guarantee applies to criminal prosecutions; and the Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause constrains federal rather than state action. A Fourteenth Amendment due-process theory is stated, but the petition does not articulate a broadly applicable standard that requires clarification or show that the fact-bound evidentiary rulings rise to a federal constitutional violation.

Vehicle problems are substantial. The intermediate decision is unpublished, the petition repeatedly disputes witness credibility and the trial record, several cited authorities or propositions are described imprecisely, and the asserted constitutional theories are interwoven with California tort and evidence law. The petition also asks the Court to correct the result rather than resolve a clean, recurring legal question. Nothing in the provisioned record suggests an intervening decision that would support a GVR.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term, but the more case-specific signal is much lower: modern petitions with no relist have a 0.8% grant rate, while petitions without a CVSG have a 3.0% grant rate. Those broad rates include professionally presented cases with clean conflicts and national importance. The defects here justify a large downward adjustment below the zero-relist baseline.

Two bounded corpus queries sampled recent 2020s SCOTUS grants and denials. They were only weakly similar, but the grants generally reflected institutional counsel, nationally recurring questions, or multiple distributions; the sample did not supply a reason to raise this petition above its base-rate and vehicle assessment. Two general CourtListener searches returned background Supreme Court opinions rather than a close analogue. No search sought or revealed this case's disposition or later docket history.

The local property damage matters greatly to the litigants, but the petition's stakes and likely effects are geographically and doctrinally narrow. I therefore assign a `big_case_score` of 0.08, independently of the grant forecast.
