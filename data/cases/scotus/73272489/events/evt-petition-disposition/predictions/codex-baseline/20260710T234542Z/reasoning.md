# Prediction: denied

I predict denial of certiorari, with a 0.1% probability of a grant.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether asserted exclusion of evidence and limits on impeachment in a California civil trial violated the Fifth, Sixth, and Fourteenth Amendments, and whether alleged judicial bias and departures from federal and state evidentiary rules warrant review.

Certiorari is discretionary. The Court ordinarily looks for a developed conflict among appellate courts, a state court's conflict with controlling Supreme Court precedent, or an important and recurring federal question. It rarely grants review merely to correct fact-bound evidentiary error or reweigh credibility in one case.

## Record-specific assessment

The July 10, 2026 snapshot identifies No. 25-1235 as a paid petition from an unpublished decision of the California Court of Appeal, Second Appellate District. The California Supreme Court denied review, and the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. The docket reflects no response, amicus participation, or other signal of institutional interest.

The provisioned questions-presented text and the full, untruncated 24-page petition frame the dispute around evidence concerning a 2005 stormwater report, testimony about Pump 13, witness credibility, hearsay, and alleged trial-court bias. Those are highly record-dependent matters. The petition asserts broad "uniformity" concerns but does not identify a concrete split among federal circuits or state courts on a clean federal rule. It also repeatedly relies on the Federal Rules of Evidence and Federal Rules of Civil Procedure to challenge rulings in a California state civil trial, although those federal procedural rules do not govern that trial. Its Sixth Amendment confrontation theory is especially weak because the Confrontation Clause governs criminal prosecutions, while this was a civil negligence/public-entity-liability action. The Fifth Amendment does not itself regulate state action; any viable procedural claim would have to run through the Fourteenth Amendment and show a fundamentally unfair proceeding, not merely an adverse evidentiary ruling.

The petition is pro se, the decision below is unpublished, the proposed questions are lengthy and case-specific, and the requested review would require revisiting disputed facts, admissibility, and credibility. Even accepting the petitioner's account, the case presents a poor vehicle for resolving a recurring federal question. No provisioned brief in opposition was available, but the absence of a response does not materially improve the petition because the Court may deny without calling for one.
