# Prediction

I predict denial, but only narrowly: **46% probability of any grant** and 54% probability of denial or another non-grant disposition. If the Court grants, a plenary grant is much more likely than a GVR because the petition presents a live statutory-scope question and identifies no intervening decision that would support summary remand.

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether the Second Hickenlooper Amendment, 22 U.S.C. § 2370(e)(2), displaces the federal act-of-state doctrine for a damages claim arising from a foreign taking when neither the taken property nor traceable property ever entered the United States. The act-of-state doctrine ordinarily prevents a U.S. court from declaring invalid a recognized foreign sovereign's official act within its territory. The Amendment removes that restraint in a case involving a claim of title or another right to property based upon or traced through a confiscation that violates international law. The cert-stage question is not who has the better merits reading, but whether the asserted conflict, importance, and vehicle justify four votes for review.

## Record signals

The May 1 petition and its extracted questions presented were available in full. No brief in opposition had been filed by the July 17 snapshot: respondent initially waived a response, the Court requested one, and the response deadline was extended to August 7. A requested response is meaningful evidence that at least one Justice or the Court is examining the petition, but it does not itself establish four votes.

The case has unusually strong certworthiness signals. The D.C. Circuit expressly rejected a domestic-nexus requirement that the petition says the Second and Fifth Circuits apply; the petition also identifies consistent decisions from the New York and Texas high courts. The issue is a clean question of federal statutory scope, was decided below, and appears outcome-determinative because the parties treated the act-of-state defense as defeating the remaining expropriation claim if the Amendment does not apply. Foreign-sovereign immunity and foreign-relations consequences increase the institutional importance. The Court has already reviewed an earlier stage of this litigation, and the petition connects the question to the Court's recent discussion of the Amendment in *Republic of Hungary v. Simon*.

There are substantial reasons to remain below 50%. The principal federal conflict rests on Second and Fifth Circuit decisions from 1981 and 1982, reducing the appearance of a pressing, recurring conflict. Respondent may dispute whether those rulings squarely control damages claims, emphasize that the statutory text contains no express geographic limitation, and raise the interlocutory posture and pendent appellate jurisdiction as vehicle concerns. The Court may also prefer the Solicitor General's views before taking a foreign-relations case. At this snapshot there is no relist or CVSG, and the opposition that could expose record or vehicle defects is unavailable.

## Calibration

The committed statpack places the 2025 Term paid-petition grant rate near 5.4%, the modern D.C. Circuit cert-petition grant rate near 11.8%, and no-CVSG petitions near 3.0%. Those broad rates are only starting points. I adjust sharply upward for the requested response, acknowledged multi-circuit disagreement, clean statutory question, prior Supreme Court involvement, and foreign-relations importance. I stop at 46% because of the old conflict, absent opposition, lack of a relist or CVSG, and ordinary cert-denial dominance.

The 0.68 big-case score reflects potentially broad consequences for challenges to foreign expropriations and U.S. foreign relations. The question is important but legally specialized, so I do not score it as a top-tier nationally salient merits case.
