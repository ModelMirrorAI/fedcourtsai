This case presents a classic vehicle for a certiorari grant: an acknowledged, mature circuit split on a question of federal statutory interpretation with significant foreign-policy implications.

**1. The Legal Question and Standard**
The petition asks whether the Second Hickenlooper Amendment to the Foreign Assistance Act (22 U.S.C. § 2370(e)(2))—which creates an exception to the act-of-state doctrine for claims based on a confiscation in violation of international law—requires that the expropriated property (or its proceeds) be present in the United States, and whether it applies to claims for damages rather than just claims for title. The D.C. Circuit answered no to the domestic-nexus requirement and yes to the damages question, allowing Helmerich & Payne's expropriation claim against PDVSA to proceed despite the property remaining in Venezuela.

**2. Circuit Split and Lower Court Decision**
The D.C. Circuit expressly rejected the interpretations of the Second Circuit (*Banco Nacional de Cuba v. First Nat'l City Bank of N.Y.*, 431 F.2d 394 (1970)) and the Fifth Circuit (*Compania de Gas de Nuevo Laredo, S.A. v. Entex, Inc.*, 686 F.2d 322 (1982)), which had read a domestic-nexus requirement into the Amendment based on its legislative history. The D.C. Circuit employed a textualist analysis to depart from this precedent, noting the text's lack of any domestic-nexus language and the "lessened significance of legislative history" since those earlier decisions. This creates a deep and acknowledged split among the courts of appeals on a question of significant practical importance.

**3. Indicators of Supreme Court Interest**
The Court's actions to date signal strong interest. After the respondent waived its right to respond, the Court affirmatively requested a response (CFR) on June 8, 2026. A CFR following a waiver is a classic indicator that at least one Justice sees the petition as a potential candidate for a grant.

**4. Stakes and Significance**
The stakes in this case are exceptionally high. Expanding the Second Hickenlooper Amendment to cases without a domestic nexus effectively opens U.S. courts to a wide array of foreign expropriation claims, a matter that heavily impacts foreign relations and international comity. The Executive Branch's views will likely be highly relevant here, making a Call for the Views of the Solicitor General (CVSG) a strong possibility before a final disposition. However, whether the Court grants the petition outright or issues a CVSG first, the ultimate disposition of the petition strongly leans toward a grant.

Given the explicit circuit split, the foreign affairs implications, the high stakes for the parties, and the Court's affirmative request for a response, the petition is highly likely to be granted.
