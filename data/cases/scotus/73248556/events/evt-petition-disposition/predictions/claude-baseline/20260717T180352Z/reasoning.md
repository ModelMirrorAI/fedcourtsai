# United States v. Hembree, No. 25-1219 — petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.04.**

## The legal question and posture

The Solicitor General petitions from the Fifth Circuit's judgment (165 F.4th
909, Jan. 27, 2026) holding 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) — the felon-in-possession
ban — unconstitutional under the Second Amendment *as applied* to Charles
Hembree, whose sole predicate felony is a 2018 Mississippi conviction for
simple possession of methamphetamine. Applying its *Diaz*/*Kimble*
felony-by-felony framework, the panel (Higginson, joined by Willett and
Engelhardt, with a Willett concurrence) held the government's two proffered
historical analogues (severe Founding-era punishment of contraband offenses;
a tradition of disarming "dangerous" persons) insufficient for a
simple-possession predicate, drawing on *United States v. Connelly*, 117
F.4th 269 (5th Cir. 2024) (§ 922(g)(3)).

The critical feature of the petition is its ask. The government does **not**
seek plenary review and does not even argue the decision below is wrong. It
asks only that the Court **hold** the petition pending *United States v.
Hemani*, No. 24-1234 (the § 922(g)(3) case argued March 2, 2026), and then,
"if appropriate," **GVR** for reconsideration in light of *Hemani*.

## What happened after the petition was filed

The petition was filed April 24, 2026, before *Hemani* came down. Everything
that has happened since (all of it predating the July 17, 2026 snapshot —
this is a forward cell, so these are legitimate pre-outcome signals, not
leakage) cuts against any form of grant:

1. **The government lost *Hemani*.** On June 18, 2026 the Court (Gorsuch,
   J.) **affirmed** the Fifth Circuit: "we cannot say [the government] has
   carried its conceded burden of showing its prosecution of Mr. Hemani
   complies with the Second Amendment. The judgment of the Fifth Circuit is
   affirmed." (Verified from the opinion text via CourtListener.) The Court
   expressly reserved § 922(g)(1): "We do not address 18 U.S.C.
   § 922(g)(1)'s provision disarming individuals convicted of felonies
   (often including drug-related ones)." A GVR requires a "reasonable
   probability" that the intervening decision would change the lower court's
   analysis (*Lawrence v. Chater*, the petition's own standard). *Hemani*
   affirmed the very *Connelly* reasoning the Fifth Circuit drew on below,
   so it *reinforces* the judgment the government wants vacated. The
   petition's requested relief has no remaining foothold.

2. **The Court denied the government's parallel hold-and-GVR petitions.**
   Verified in the corpus: *United States v. Mitchell*, No. 25-935, **cert
   denied June 29, 2026** — eleven days *after* *Hemani*, in the closest
   possible companion (Fifth Circuit voided a § 922(g)(1) conviction whose
   predicate was itself a § 922(g)(3) conviction) — and *United States v.
   Cockerham*, No. 25-1029, **cert denied June 8, 2026** (predicate:
   failure to pay child support). The BIO also cites the denial in *United
   States v. Doucet*, No. 25-1001 (Apr. 27, 2026; predicate: attempted
   marijuana cultivation), which I could not independently verify but which
   is a specific, checkable representation by a federal defender in a filed
   brief and consistent with the verified pattern. Hembree's petition is
   materially identical to these; if anything it is the weakest of the set,
   since a simple-possession predicate is even further from the
   dangerousness rationale the government pressed in *Hemani* and lost.

3. **The Court has repeatedly declined the underlying § 922(g)(1) split.**
   Denials in *Diaz* (June 23, 2025), *Duarte* (Jan. 20, 2026), and
   *Vincent v. Bondi* (Mar. 2, 2026), per the BIO's citations; Justice
   Jackson's *Hemani* concurrence acknowledges the circuit inconsistency
   (comparing *Cockerham* (CA5) with *Duarte* (CA9) and *Jackson* (CA8))
   without the Court taking it up. The split is real and will likely be
   resolved eventually, but the Court is visibly letting it percolate, and
   this petition — where the government asks only for a hold, not plenary
   review, and the QP is expressly as-applied — is a poor vehicle the
   Court's own recent denials say it does not want.

Residual grant mass (~4%): a small chance the Court relists and finds some

recasting the petition after *Hemani*. Dismissal/withdrawal paths look
unlikely (nothing on the docket suggests them).

**Bottom line:** denial, most likely on the October 5–6, 2026 order list
following the September 28 long conference. P(grant incl. GVR) = 0.04;
predicted disposition: **denied**; confidence 0.85.
