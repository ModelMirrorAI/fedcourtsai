# Prediction: denied

I assign an 8% probability to any grant-side disposition (principally a GVR) and predict denial.

## Question and posture

The question is whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) violates the Second Amendment as applied to Charles Hembree, whose sole predicate felony was simple possession of methamphetamine. The Fifth Circuit reversed Hembree's felon-in-possession conviction. Under its post-*Bruen* precedent, the panel confined the inquiry to the predicate conviction and held that the government had not identified a historical tradition supporting permanent disarmament for simple drug possession.

The petition is unusually narrow. The Solicitor General did not ask for plenary review or defend the Fifth Circuit result as independently certworthy. The government instead asked the Court to hold the case for *United States v. Hemani* and then, if appropriate, grant, vacate, and remand. A GVR requires at least a reasonable probability that the intervening development affected the judgment below.

## Evidence favoring a grant-side disposition

- The United States is the petitioner, and the judgment invalidates a federal criminal statute as applied. Those features ordinarily receive more attention than a private paid petition.
- The petition describes a substantial circuit divergence. Several circuits treat § 922(g)(1) as categorically constitutional, while the Third, Fifth, and Sixth Circuits permit differing forms of as-applied review.
- *Hemani* expressly reserved § 922(g)(1). Justice Alito's concurrence, joined by Justice Kagan, additionally emphasized that the decision should not cast doubt on the presumptive lawfulness of felon-dispossession laws. That gives the government a non-frivolous argument for reconsideration.

## Evidence favoring denial

The intervening decision did not develop as the petition anticipated. The provisioned brief in opposition reports the June 18, 2026 disposition of *Hemani*, and the official opinion retrieved through CourtListener confirms it. All nine Justices affirmed the judgment against the government. A seven-Justice opinion rejected the government's asserted historical analogy and its theory that unlawful drug users could be categorically disarmed as unusually dangerous. Although the Court reserved § 922(g)(1), the reasoning that the petition hoped would unsettle *Connelly* instead largely supports the Fifth Circuit's skepticism about categorical dangerousness.

The decision below also did not rest solely on *Connelly*. It independently rejected the government's contraband analogues, distinguished trafficking from simple possession, and held that the government failed to establish a historical basis for permanently disarming this respondent. The distinction between temporary disarmament under § 922(g)(3) and permanent disarmament under § 922(g)(1) makes it less likely that the Fifth Circuit would alter its judgment on remand.

The opposition identifies three especially close post-*Hemani* signals: denials in *United States v. Doucet*, *United States v. Cockerham*, and *United States v. Mitchell*, each involving government efforts to overturn Fifth Circuit as-applied § 922(g)(1) rulings. CourtListener docket searches did not independently surface those dockets, so I rely on the filed opposition for that procedural history. Their reported denials materially reduce the prospect that the Court will use this case to intervene.

The base rates point the same way, though they deserve less weight than the case-specific signals. The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions, a 4.8% grant rate for modern cert petitions from the Fifth Circuit, and only 0.8% for petitions with no relist. This petition has been distributed for its first conference and has no relist or CVSG signal. The government's institutional status raises it above an ordinary petition, but the adverse intervening precedent and closely analogous denials pull it back toward denial.

## Calibration and significance

I reserve 8% for a GVR or, less likely, a plenary grant prompted by the circuit split and the explicit § 922(g)(1) reservation in *Hemani*. Denial remains the clear modal outcome. I do not predict individual votes because cert votes are not public and the relevant signal is the interaction between the intervening case and the GVR standard, not a merits coalition.

The stakes score is 0.73. The validity of a widely used federal firearms disability and the widening circuit split are nationally significant, but Hembree's as-applied, single-predicate posture and the government's request for only a GVR narrow what this particular docket would decide.
