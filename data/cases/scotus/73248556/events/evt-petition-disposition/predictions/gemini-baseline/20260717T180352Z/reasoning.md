The United States petitioned for certiorari, asking the Court to hold the petition pending the resolution of *United States v. Hemani*, No. 24-1234, and then, if appropriate, grant, vacate, and remand (GVR). *Hemani* presented the question of whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3) (disarming unlawful users of controlled substances) violates the Second Amendment. 

In the decision below, the Fifth Circuit held that § 922(g)(1) (disarming felons) is unconstitutional as applied to respondent Hembree, whose sole predicate offense was a non-violent conviction for simple possession of methamphetamine. In finding that the government failed to establish a historical tradition of disarming individuals based on simple drug possession, the Fifth Circuit relied heavily on its own precedent in *United States v. Connelly*, which had invalidated § 922(g)(3).

The Supreme Court decided *Hemani* on June 18, 2026. As the retrieved opinion and the respondent's Brief in Opposition show, the Court in *Hemani* rejected the government's categorical arguments that drug users are unusually dangerous, effectively striking down the prosecution under § 922(g)(3). Although *Hemani* explicitly reserved the question of whether § 922(g)(1) may constitutionally disarm non-violent felons, the Court's rejection of the government's historical analogues regarding drug users closely tracks the Fifth Circuit's reasoning in *Hembree*. 

Because the Supreme Court's decision in *Hemani* undercuts the government's theory of categorical dangerousness for drug possessors/users, a GVR in favor of the government is unwarranted; the Fifth Circuit's decision is already consistent with *Hemani*'s rationale. Furthermore, the respondent notes that the Court has recently denied certiorari in several other cases raising the constitutionality of § 922(g)(1) as applied to non-violent felons (e.g., *Diaz*, *Duarte*, *Bondi*). As this petition is distributed for the Long Conference (September 28, 2026) with 0 relists, and the intervening precedent cuts decisively against the petitioner's request for a GVR, the petition is highly likely to be denied.
