# Prediction Reasoning

## The Legal Question
The petitioners are non-Indian foster parents who were denied intervention in a custody dispute over Native American twins they had cared for since birth. They bring a direct constitutional challenge to the Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA) and the Minnesota Indian Family Preservation Act (MIFPA), arguing that the race-based placement preferences of these laws violate the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. They also raise a First Amendment claim based on the state court denying their intervention specifically because they raised this good-faith constitutional challenge.

## Governing Standard & Context
In 2023, the Supreme Court decided *Haaland v. Brackeen*, 599 U.S. 255. In that case, the Court upheld ICWA against various challenges (such as anti-commandeering) but explicitly declined to rule on the Equal Protection challenge regarding the placement preferences. The Court held that the *Brackeen* petitioners lacked standing because they were suing federal officials who did not implement the placement preferences. However, the majority opinion and Justice Kavanaugh's concurrence explicitly noted that non-Indians seeking to adopt an Indian child would have standing to raise this exact Equal Protection challenge in state court. 

## Facts from the Snapshot
The petitioners in this case, Nathan and Kellie Reyelts (N.R.), represent exactly the class of plaintiffs the Supreme Court identified in *Brackeen*: foster parents bringing an Equal Protection challenge in a state court custody proceeding.

The case has drawn significant amicus support from high-profile conservative litigation groups, including the Pacific Legal Foundation. On the docket, the state respondent (the Guardian ad Litem) initially waived the right to respond. However, on June 25, 2026, the Supreme Court formally requested a response, indicating that the justices are actively looking at this petition. The case is distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference (the "Long Conference").

## Probability and Predicted Disposition
The base rate for a discretionary cert grant is very low (around 2.5 - 3.0%), but this is no ordinary petition. The Court's request for a response drastically increases the baseline probability of a grant. Furthermore, this case presents a major, unresolved constitutional question that multiple justices (especially Kavanaugh, Thomas, and Alito) have signaled a strong interest in deciding. Because this case is effectively a direct sequel to *Brackeen* and arrives precisely via the procedural vehicle the Court suggested (a state court proceeding), the likelihood of four justices voting to grant certiorari is extremely high. 

I predict a **grant** with a probability of 75%.
