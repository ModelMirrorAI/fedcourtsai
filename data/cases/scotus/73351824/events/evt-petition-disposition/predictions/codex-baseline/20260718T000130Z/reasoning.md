# Prediction

I predict denial, with a 13% probability of any grant.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks (1) whether the Indian Child Welfare Act (ICWA) and Minnesota Indian Family Preservation Act violate equal protection as applied to Indian children and non-Indian prospective custodians, and (2) whether denying intervention because the prospective intervenors challenged those laws violates the First Amendment. The Court's practical certiorari standard favors a cleanly presented, nationally important federal question, especially where lower courts disagree; it disfavors fact-bound error correction, unresolved threshold issues, and decisions supportable on independent state-law grounds. Four votes are required to grant.

## Record and merits signals

The provisioned questions presented and petition describe foster parents who cared for twins with substantial medical needs for more than a year, sought to prevent a placement preferred under ICWA/MIFPA, and later were denied permissive intervention. The petition invokes *Haaland v. Brackeen*, including its observation that a person injured by ICWA could bring an equal-protection challenge in state court. It argues that the state courts then prevented precisely that route and treated the constitutional challenge as evidence that intervention was contrary to the children's interests.

The petition raises a nationally consequential issue, has three supporting amicus filings on the July 17 snapshot, and was filed in the paid docket. Most importantly, after one respondent waived a response, the Court requested a response on June 25. A response request is evidence that at least one Justice or chambers thought the petition warranted adversarial briefing. Those signals lift the case well above an ordinary petition.

The vehicle problems nevertheless dominate. The Minnesota Supreme Court's appended opinion affirmed under a discretionary, state-law best-interests standard. It identified several grounds unrelated to the constitutional position: the parents' expected resistance to other placements, possible delay to an expedited child-protection proceeding, redundancy because the children were represented by the guardian ad litem, Tribe, and mother, and the parents' strong attachment and perceived inability to recognize value in another placement. On the First Amendment issue, the court assumed the asserted principle without deciding it and held both that retaliatory animus was unclear and that the ICWA challenge was not the but-for cause of denial. That makes the second question fact-bound and permits affirmance without endorsing retaliation for protected petitioning.

The first question is an even less suitable vehicle. The state supreme court did not decide the equal-protection merits; the petition must first overcome party-status, standing, preservation, and adequate-and-independent-state-ground disputes. The petition alleges conflicts in lower-court treatment of ICWA, but the provisioned materials do not show a direct split on the judgment below. The Court would also be asked to reach a broad constitutional issue in a sensitive child-custody record after the state court found the would-be challengers were not parties. The Court is unlikely to use this posture to decide ICWA's constitutionality.

## Quantitative calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.4% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions, while the broad modern zero-relist bucket has a 0.8% grant rate. This petition has not yet reached its first conference, so lack of a relist is neutral at this stage rather than adverse evidence. The response request and multiple amici justify a sizable upward adjustment from the paid-petition baseline, but the absence of a developed merits ruling or a clean conflict prevents an adjustment into near-even territory. I assign 13% to any grant. If review were granted, plenary review is more plausible than a GVR because no intervening authority or mootness event appears in the record; nevertheless, denial remains the modal disposition.

No individual cert votes are predicted. The case's potential significance is high because a merits decision could affect ICWA's constitutionality, tribal sovereignty, and child-welfare proceedings across many states, even though the chance of review in this particular posture is modest.
