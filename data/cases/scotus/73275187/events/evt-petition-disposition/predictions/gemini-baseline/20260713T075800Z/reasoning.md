# Reasoning

The petition in *Jouppi v. Alaska* presents an Eighth Amendment Excessive Fines Clause question regarding whether a court determining the gravity of an offense should view the offense in the abstract or consider the specific defendant's actions. 

After its initial distribution for the December 12, 2025 conference, the Supreme Court held the petition without action for over six months. The supplemental brief filed on June 25, 2026, indicates the petition was held pending the resolution of *Pung v. Isabella County* (No. 25-95), which was decided on June 23, 2026. 

The petitioner’s supplemental brief argues that the conflict remains live because *Pung* focused on the threshold question of whether a forfeiture constitutes a "fine" and did not resolve the standard for evaluating whether a fine is "excessive." Because *Pung* did not alter the framework for analyzing excessiveness, the Alaska Supreme Court's reasoning is not undermined by an intervening decision, making a Grant, Vacate, and Remand (GVR) order unlikely. 

With *Pung* resolved on different grounds, the petition essentially returns to the status of a regular certiorari petition. Given the extremely low base rate for cert grants (particularly for state-court judgments, which the statpack estimates at less than 3%), and the fact that the Court has just addressed the Excessive Fines Clause in *Pung*, it is highly unlikely they will grant plenary review in another case on a related issue so soon—especially during the final "cleanup" conference of the Term. 

Therefore, without a direct reason to GVR or a clear indication that the Court wishes to expand upon *Pung* immediately, the most probable outcome is that the Court will deny certiorari.
