# Prediction: denied

I estimate a 30% probability that the petition is granted and a 70% probability that it is denied. “Granted” here includes a plenary grant or a summary grant, vacatur, and remand. I predict denial because the petition presents a serious constitutional issue and unusually strong procedural signals, but the alleged conflict is contestable and the record gives the Court several vehicle-based reasons not to intervene.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the Eighth Amendment permits a court assessing the gravity of an offense to work at the level of the offense in the abstract, or instead requires attention to the particular defendant’s conduct and culpability. Under *United States v. Bajakajian*, a punitive forfeiture violates the Excessive Fines Clause if it is grossly disproportional to the gravity of the defendant’s offense. Legislative judgments receive substantial deference, but the inquiry includes the nature of the particular offense, the defendant’s place within the class targeted by the law, authorized penalties, related criminal activity, and actual or expected harm.

## Record and merits

The provisioned questions presented and petition make the grant case vivid. Jouppi received the minimum executed sentence for a first offense, and the Alaska Supreme Court assumed that his culpability extended to only a visible six-pack. Yet it upheld forfeiture of a $95,000 airplane by emphasizing the $10,000 statutory maximum, the general harms of alcohol in dry villages, and the legislature’s decision to mandate aircraft forfeiture. The petition plausibly argues that this approach conflicts with *Bajakajian* and with decisions of the Ninth Circuit and several state high courts that focus on the specific acts and culpability of the violator. The conflict is especially salient because Alaska lies within the Ninth Circuit. The petition is professionally presented, and the snapshot records three supporting amicus briefs.

The provisioned brief in opposition substantially weakens the grant case. Alaska characterizes the disagreement as application of a shared, fact-sensitive multifactor test rather than a genuine split over a rule of law. It stresses that Jouppi was convicted of knowing or deliberately ignorant conduct in his professional capacity; the airplane was the instrumentality of the offense; the record contained evidence of other alcohol-transport activity; and the Alaska Supreme Court assumed disputed facts in Jouppi’s favor only because the record was incomplete. Those points let the Court view the petition as fact-bound error correction under Supreme Court Rule 10. The criminal, in-personam posture also makes this a less direct vehicle for broader concerns about civil forfeiture.

## Procedural signals and base rate

The committed statpack reports a 4.9% grant rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions in its resolved live/historical slice. This paid petition deserves a large upward adjustment. Alaska initially waived response, but the Court requested one; the petition drew multiple amici; it remained pending after distribution for the December 12, 2025 conference; and the docket then records a June 25, 2026 supplemental brief followed by distribution for the June 29 conference.

CourtListener research identified *Pung v. Isabella County*, decided June 23, 2026. *Pung* held that a fairly conducted tax sale did not require payment based on hypothetical fair-market value under the Excessive Fines Clause, while reiterating that a forfeiture may be a fine if it serves in part to punish. The close timing between that decision and Jouppi’s supplemental filing and renewed distribution strongly suggests that the Court held this petition for related Eighth Amendment property-forfeiture issues. The supplemental brief itself was not provisioned, so I do not assume what it argued. *Pung* does not clearly establish a new proportionality rule that undermines the Alaska judgment: Jouppi’s forfeiture was already undisputedly punitive, while *Pung* largely turned on the history and character of tax sales. A post-*Pung* denial therefore remains more likely than a grant or GVR.
