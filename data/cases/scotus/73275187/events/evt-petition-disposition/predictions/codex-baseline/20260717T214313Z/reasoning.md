# Prediction: denied

I assign a 29% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. That is far above the ordinary paid-petition prior because the Court requested a response, the petition drew three amicus briefs, and—most importantly—the docket reflects an unusual hold from the December 12, 2025 conference through a June 25, 2026 supplemental brief and renewed distribution for the June 29 conference. The hold appears connected to *Pung v. Isabella County*, decided June 23, 2026. But denial remains the likeliest single outcome because *Pung* did not adopt a new proportionality rule that would require a remand, and the asserted conflict can plausibly be characterized as case-specific application of settled law.

## Question and governing standard

The question is whether, when applying the Excessive Fines Clause, a court may assess the gravity of an offense at a generalized statutory level or must assess the particular defendant's conduct and culpability. Under *United States v. Bajakajian*, a punitive forfeiture is unconstitutional when it is grossly disproportional to the gravity of the defendant's offense. The petition argues that *Bajakajian* requires an individualized inquiry and that the Alaska Supreme Court instead attributed the statewide harms of alcohol abuse to Jouppi's offense. Alaska responds that the state court used the conventional four-factor *Bajakajian* framework and considered both Jouppi's circumstances and legislative judgments.

## Record-specific signals

The petition's facts are unusually certworthy. On the Alaska Supreme Court's favorable-to-Jouppi assumption, the State may forfeit a $95,000 airplane for the knowing importation of one six-pack. Jouppi received the minimum executed jail term and fine, and the forfeiture followed a first conviction in this case. The petition also identifies a sharp same-geography tension: the Ninth Circuit instructs courts to examine the violator's specific actions, while Alaska's highest court held that related criminal activity was "not particularly relevant" and relied heavily on the generalized harms of alcohol abuse.

The brief in opposition nonetheless identifies substantial grant obstacles. Alaska says the jury found knowing or deliberately ignorant conduct; nine gallons of beer were aboard; the airplane was the direct instrumentality of the offense; and evidence suggested related importation activity. More fundamentally, Alaska frames the alleged split as disagreement over the weight assigned to common *Bajakajian* factors, not a square conflict over a legal rule. That Rule 10 error-correction argument is credible because the Alaska court expressly articulated an as-applied, multi-factor inquiry. The incomplete and disputed factual record also gives the Court a vehicle reason to deny.

The later procedural history materially raises the forecast above base rates. The petition was initially distributed for the December 12 conference, remained pending while *Pung* was argued and decided, and was redistributed after Jouppi filed a supplemental brief two days after *Pung*. The timing strongly suggests a hold for that lead case. In *Pung*, however, the Court held that a fairly conducted tax sale did not require fair-market-value compensation under the Excessive Fines Clause, relying on the history of tax sales. That decision involved a different threshold and did not revise *Bajakajian*'s gross-disproportionality test for an acknowledged punitive criminal forfeiture. It therefore supplies no clear intervening rule for a GVR. It also shows the Court's interest in economic-sanctions questions, which leaves a meaningful possibility of plenary review, but a post-hold denial is more likely.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack estimates an approximately 3.1% grant rate across modern discretionary-cert petitions and a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term. One ordinary relist has a 7.6% historical grant rate, although this docket's months-long hold pending a related merits case is not an ordinary relist and should not be mapped mechanically to that bucket. A corpus query of recent 2020s SCOTUS dispositions likewise showed that petitions distributed around the same end-of-Term conference produced grants, denials, and a GVR, so repeated distribution alone is not dispositive.

Balancing the low baseline and the plausible no-split/vehicle arguments against the response request, amici, strong facts, and apparent *Pung* hold, I estimate a 29% chance of any grant-side outcome. Denial is the most likely disposition. If the Court acts on the grant side, plenary cert is more plausible than a *Pung*-based GVR because *Pung* did not announce a directly controlling proportionality rule.

The big-case score is 0.67. A merits decision could shape Excessive Fines doctrine nationwide and affect forfeitures and other punitive economic sanctions, but the criminal-aircraft posture and fact-specific record narrow its practical reach.
