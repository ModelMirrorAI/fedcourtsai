This is a highly salient separation-of-powers dispute involving the newly created "Department of Government Efficiency" (DOGE), officially established as the U.S. DOGE Service (USDS) within the Executive Office of the President. The respondent, Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW), sued under FOIA, seeking records from USDS. The district court ordered broad discovery to determine whether USDS exercises "substantial independent authority" making it an "agency" subject to FOIA, or whether its sole function is to advise and assist the President (which would make it exempt under the *Kissinger* and *Soucie* line of cases). The government, represented by the Solicitor General, sought a writ of mandamus, arguing that this discovery violates the separation-of-powers principles articulated in *Cheney v. United States District Court*, 542 U.S. 367 (2004).

The Supreme Court has already demonstrated its intense interest in this exact dispute. According to the petition, the Court previously granted certiorari, vacated the D.C. Circuit's initial denial of mandamus, and remanded with instructions to "narrow" discovery in light of "separation of powers concerns" (a classic GVR). On remand, after respondents withdrew some discovery requests, the D.C. Circuit again denied mandamus, leaving intact significant discovery demands including internal communications and a deposition of the USDS head. The SG has now filed a new petition challenging this second denial.

**Prediction:** Grant (95%).
When the Solicitor General petitions for certiorari, the baseline grant rate is extraordinarily high (historically ~60-70%). Here, the case involves a direct clash between the Executive Branch and the lower courts over the autonomy, operations, and confidentiality of a major, highly publicized Presidential initiative. The Supreme Court's prior GVR signals that it closely scrutinizes lower court discovery orders against the Executive Office of the President. Because the D.C. Circuit largely reaffirmed the discovery order on remand, the Supreme Court is highly likely to grant plenary review (or issue another summary reversal/mandamus) to definitively resolve the question and vindicate its prior *Cheney* instruction. The stakes are immense, and a grant is almost certain.
