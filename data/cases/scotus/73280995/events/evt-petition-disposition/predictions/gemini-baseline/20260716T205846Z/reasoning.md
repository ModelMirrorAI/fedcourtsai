# Reasoning

## Legal Question
Whether the cause of action recognized in *Carlson v. Green* (1980) for deliberate indifference to a prisoner's serious medical needs in violation of the Eighth Amendment extends to an inmate's claim against prison staff at a pretrial detention center for deliberate indifference to chronic pain. This asks whether the Seventh Circuit erred by extending *Bivens* into a "new context" contrary to *Egbert v. Boule* (2022).

## Posture and Facts
The Seventh Circuit recognized a *Bivens* claim against federal prison staff (Brij Mohan, et al.) at a pretrial detention center. The Solicitor General is petitioning on behalf of the federal staff, seeking to reverse the Seventh Circuit. 
The SG's petition explicitly asks the Court to hold this case behind *Nielsen v. Watanabe* (which involves a similar Bivens medical-care issue from the Ninth Circuit) and then dispose of it accordingly.

## Analysis
The Supreme Court has effectively closed the door to new *Bivens* contexts (*Egbert v. Boule*, *Hernandez v. Mesa*, *Ziglar v. Abbasi*). The Seventh Circuit's decision to permit a *Bivens* claim here is highly vulnerable to reversal. 

When the Solicitor General petitions for certiorari, the grant rate is extraordinarily high (historically 60-70%+), reflecting the SG's careful selection of vehicles and the Court's deference to the federal government's interests. Here, the SG is not only petitioning but explicitly asking the Court to hold this petition pending the disposition of *Nielsen v. Watanabe*. 

The standard practice when the SG asks to hold a case behind another pending petition is for the Court to oblige. Given the Court's hostility to *Bivens* extensions, it is highly likely that *Nielsen v. Watanabe* will be granted (or summarily reversed) and that this case will subsequently be Granted, Vacated, and Remanded (GVR'd) in light of the *Nielsen* decision. 

A GVR is technically a grant of certiorari. Therefore, the most probable disposition for this petition is a GVR.

## Predicted Votes
Assuming a GVR, the vote is typically unanimous or without recorded dissent. If it were granted for plenary review, the conservative majority (Roberts, Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett) would almost certainly vote to reverse the Seventh Circuit's extension of *Bivens*.
