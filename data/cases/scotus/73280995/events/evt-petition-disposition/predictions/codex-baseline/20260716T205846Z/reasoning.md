# Prediction

I predict a grant-side disposition, most likely a later grant, vacate, and remand after the Court resolves the cleaner, related petition in *Nielsen v. Watanabe*. My probability of any grant-side disposition is **0.61**. This includes a direct plenary grant or summary reversal, but the modal grant-side path is a hold followed by a GVR; I therefore label the predicted disposition `gvr`.

## Question and governing considerations

The question is whether *Carlson v. Green* permits a Bivens damages claim where federal detention-center staff allegedly showed deliberate indifference to severe pain and swelling after hernia surgery. The Seventh Circuit held that the Eighth Amendment portion of the claim fell within the existing *Carlson* context. The government argues that the different medical episode, pretrial-detention setting, Prison Litigation Reform Act, and Bureau of Prisons grievance process make this a new context under *Ziglar v. Abbasi* and *Egbert v. Boule*.

At certiorari, the central considerations are not simply whether the Seventh Circuit erred, but whether there is a recurring circuit conflict, a clean vehicle, and a reason for four Justices to intervene now. If review follows the related case, the merits direction also matters because a decision narrowing *Carlson* would make a GVR here likely.

## Record signals

The provisioned snapshot shows a paid petition filed by the Solicitor General from a divided Seventh Circuit decision. It was distributed for the June 11, 2026 conference and then redistributed for June 18, an apparent first relist. A relist is materially more favorable than an ordinary cert petition. The petition identifies divergent approaches in the First, Third, Seventh, Ninth, Tenth, and Eleventh Circuits, and quotes multiple appellate judges asking the Court for guidance. It primarily requests that the Court grant *Nielsen v. Watanabe*, No. 25-417, and hold this petition; it asks for a direct grant only in the alternative.

The merits allegations are substantial but the vehicle is imperfect. According to the questions presented, petition, and brief in opposition, Watkins experienced severe postoperative pain and swelling, received ineffective medication, was denied requested surgical follow-up, and later required corrective surgery. The alleged conduct began while he was a pretrial detainee and continued briefly after conviction. That mixed Fifth/Eighth Amendment posture, the interlocutory appeal from dismissal, and the respondent's fact-bound characterization all favor using *Watanabe* instead. The brief in opposition also argues that no circuit has adopted the government's proposed categorical acute/chronic distinction and that the Administrative Remedy Program already existed when *Carlson* was decided.

The Court's doctrinal direction nevertheless favors government intervention. In the official *Goldey v. Fields* opinion retrieved through CourtListener, the Court summarily reversed an extension of Bivens to an Eighth Amendment excessive-force claim, emphasizing congressional primacy, prison-administration consequences, and alternative remedies. *Goldey* preserved the formally recognized *Carlson* context, so it does not decide this case; but it demonstrates both strong hostility to Bivens extensions and willingness to correct lower courts summarily. If *Watanabe* is granted and produces a government-favorable rule, this petition is well positioned for a GVR.

## Calibration

The committed statpack gives a 3.1% grant rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions overall and a 5.4% estimated grant rate for paid 2025 Term petitions. Seventh Circuit petitions are below average at about 2.0%. Those broad priors are substantially displaced here by the Solicitor General's authorship, a developed multi-circuit disagreement, an apparent relist, and an expressly identified companion vehicle. The statpack's one-relist bucket has a 7.6% grant rate, versus 0.8% without a relist, though it does not isolate government petitions or held petitions.

Against those positive signals, the Court recently declined review in an Eleventh Circuit Bivens case identified in the briefs, and the mixed constitutional posture makes a direct grant less attractive. My 0.61 forecast therefore depends heavily on the hold-and-GVR route, not on a prediction that this docket itself will be chosen for plenary review.
