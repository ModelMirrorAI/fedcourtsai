# Prediction: denied

I assign a 24% probability that the petition will be granted and predict denial. This is substantially above the ordinary paid-petition base rate, but denial remains the most likely single disposition.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether FAA § 2 preempts Maryland's rule that an arbitration clause within an otherwise supported contract must have separate consideration, including mutual non-illusory promises to arbitrate. The petition says the Fourth Circuit permits that arbitration-specific requirement while the First, Second, Third, Sixth, and Eighth Circuits reject comparable separate-consideration rules.

At the certiorari stage, the relevant considerations are primarily Supreme Court Rule 10 signals: a genuine and outcome-determinative circuit conflict on an important recurring federal question, a clean vehicle, and the need to correct a lower court departure from Supreme Court precedent. The underlying merits favor the petitioner because *Kindred Nursing Centers*, *Concepcion*, and *Casarotto* strongly disapprove rules that single out arbitration agreements or clauses for special burdens. But merits strength alone does not establish certworthiness.

## Case-specific signals

The July 13, 2026 snapshot shows a paid Term 2025 petition from the Fourth Circuit. It was initially distributed for the November 14, 2025 conference, after which the Court requested a response. That request is a meaningful positive signal: at least one Justice or Chambers wanted adversarial presentation rather than disposing of the petition on a waiver. The American Bankers Association filed an amicus brief supporting review, consistent with the petition's claim that the issue affects standard credit-card and other commercial contracts.

The provisioned `questions-presented.txt` and complete, untruncated 30-page `petition.txt` present a focused legal question. The petition describes a lopsided appellate conflict and relies on the Fourth Circuit's published decision in *Johnson v. Continental Finance Co.*, 131 F.4th 169 (4th Cir. 2025), as well as *Noohi v. Toll Bros.*, 708 F.3d 599 (4th Cir. 2013). CourtListener research confirmed that those Fourth Circuit authorities exist and that *Johnson* is published. The lower-court outcome here is outcome-determinative: eliminating the separate-consideration rule would remove the stated basis for refusing to compel arbitration.

Several factors keep the estimate below 50%. The decision in this case is unpublished and only three pages long. The asserted split may be less direct than the petition suggests because several contrary cases discuss consideration in distinguishable contractual and state-law settings. The earlier *Johnson* petition identified in the petition may be a cleaner lead vehicle, making this case redundant or a hold rather than the case selected for plenary review. No CVSG or relist appears in the snapshot. Most importantly, no brief in opposition is yet available: the response deadline was repeatedly extended, with petitioner's consent, through October 30, 2026. That deprives the current record of the best account of vehicle defects and signals unusually slow progress despite the claimed urgency.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.1% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions and a 5.5% grant rate for modern discretionary-cert petitions originating in the Fourth Circuit. Zero-relist and no-CVSG buckets are much lower than the positive-signal buckets. I raise the estimate sharply above those baselines for the requested response, a plausible multi-circuit conflict, recurring FAA importance, strong merits fit with the Court's equal-treatment precedents, and industry amicus support. I discount for the unpublished vehicle, possible companion-petition redundancy, the lack of a response or relist, and the unusually long consented extension.

The Court could plausibly grant this petition, grant it with a companion, or hold it for another case. On the present record, however, the low certiorari base rate and vehicle uncertainty make ultimate denial more likely.

No individual Justice votes are predicted because cert votes are not public and the record does not support a reliable Justice-by-Justice allocation.
