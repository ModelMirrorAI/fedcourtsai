## Legal and Factual Context
This is a petition for a writ of certiorari arising from Rule 11 and 28 U.S.C. § 1927 sanctions imposed on attorneys (Petitioners) who represented candidates Kari Lake and Mark Finchem in a 2022 lawsuit challenging Arizona's electronic voting procedures. The district court dismissed the underlying complaint for lack of standing, a decision affirmed by the Ninth Circuit. Subsequently, the district court sanctioned the attorneys to "send a message" deterring future baseless election litigation that undermines "public trust." A Ninth Circuit panel affirmed the sanctions, but six judges dissented from the denial of rehearing en banc, sharply criticizing the district court's "weaponization of sanctions to chill politically disfavored litigation."

The petition argues that the sanctions were improperly used to punish "politically disfavored" lawsuits and novel legal arguments, and were based on misconstruing the complaint. Crucially, the petition points to this Court's recent intervening decision in *Bost v. Ill. St. Bd. of Elections*, No. 24-568, slip op. (Jan. 14, 2026), which purportedly held that candidates *do* have standing to challenge election procedures prior to an election.

Respondents' Brief in Opposition contends that the sanctions were justified by the Petitioners' "false, misleading, and unsupported factual assertions" and vexatious multiplication of proceedings, rather than novel legal theories.

## Prediction Reasoning
Base rates for cert petitions, especially at zero relists (0.8%), strongly favor denial. However, several strong case-specific signals push the probability of a grant—specifically a Grant, Vacate, and Remand (GVR)—much higher.

First, the petition highlights an intervening Supreme Court decision, *Bost v. Ill. St. Bd. of Elections*, handed down in January 2026. Because the district court's sanctions were premised, at least in part, on the attorneys advancing legally frivolous arguments regarding candidate standing, the new precedent in *Bost* directly undermines that rationale. While the BIO correctly notes the district court also sanctioned the attorneys for factual misrepresentations, standard Court practice is to GVR when an intervening decision alters the legal landscape underpinning the lower court's judgment, allowing the lower court to untangle which portions of the sanctions order survive.

Second, the case features a vigorous six-judge dissent from the denial of rehearing en banc in the Ninth Circuit. Such a prominent dissent, explicitly accusing the panel and district court of "weaponizing" sanctions against unpopular claims, serves as a powerful signal for the conservative wing of the Court to scrutinize the lower court's action.

Given the combination of a highly salient 6-judge en banc dissent and an intervening Supreme Court decision that touches on the core legal theory of the sanctioned complaint, a GVR is the most probable outcome. A plenary grant is also plausible given the high-profile nature of the election-integrity litigation, but a GVR is the customary vehicle for dealing with judgments potentially disturbed by very recent precedent.

**Predicted Disposition:** GVR
