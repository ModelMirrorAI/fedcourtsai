# Prediction: denial

I assign a 17% probability to any grant, including a GVR, and predict denial as the most likely disposition. If the Court takes grant-side action, a GVR in light of *Bost v. Illinois Board of Elections* is more likely than plenary review.

## Question and governing standards

The petition challenges Rule 11 and 28 U.S.C. § 1927 sanctions imposed on counsel in election litigation. It characterizes the questions as whether a court may sanction attorneys to deter politically disfavored suits, punish novel or long-shot arguments, construe pleadings adversely, or treat a motion filed roughly four months before an election as sanctionable under *Purcell*.

On the underlying sanctions issues, the Ninth Circuit used its two-part *Holgate* inquiry for Rule 11: whether the filing was objectively legally or factually baseless and whether counsel made a reasonable prefiling inquiry. Section 1927 additionally required unreasonable and vexatious multiplication of proceedings with subjective bad faith. On certiorari, the practical question is whether this case presents an important, recurring federal issue or conflict, and whether the record and alternative grounds make it a suitable vehicle, rather than merely an asserted misapplication of settled standards.

## Signals favoring grant-side action

- Six Ninth Circuit judges dissented from denial of rehearing en banc. Their view that the sanctions order misconstrued the complaint and risked chilling disfavored advocacy supplies an unusually substantial lower-court signal.
- The Court requested a response after the petition had initially been distributed. That is a meaningful sign of at least some Justice-level interest, even though it falls well short of the four votes needed to grant.
- *Bost*, decided two days before this petition was filed, held that candidates have a concrete interest in the rules governing vote counting without needing to show that the challenged rules threaten their electoral prospects. That intervening authority weakens any suggestion that the underlying candidates' prospective election challenge was sanctionably speculative merely because injury to the election result was uncertain.
- The petition frames a nationally salient concern about using sanctions to chill election-related or otherwise unpopular advocacy, and the district court's stated deterrent purpose gives that framing some force.

## Signals favoring denial

The complete petition and opposition show substantial vehicle problems. The decision below is an abuse-of-discretion affirmance tied closely to this record, and the petition does not demonstrate a clean, outcome-determinative circuit split. Respondents persuasively identify independent factual bases for the sanctions: allegations implying that Arizona did not use paper ballots, allegations that its voting equipment had not undergone neutral expert testing, speculative assertions about compromised systems, and the alleged failure to make a reasonable inquiry. The opposition also argues that the preliminary-injunction motion repeated these assertions after a Rule 11 warning and unnecessarily multiplied the proceedings.

Those grounds make *Bost* less decisive than the petition suggests. *Bost* addressed candidate standing, not Rule 11, factual accuracy, prefiling investigation, or § 1927 bad faith. Even if *Bost* casts doubt on one standing-related rationale, it may not affect the independent findings supporting either sanctions provision. The *Purcell* question is similarly burdened by alternative grounds and by the respondents' argument that it was not preserved as a standalone challenge. The petition's four questions are also framed more broadly and favorably to petitioners than the detailed sanctions findings permit.

## Calibration

The committed statpack places the 2025 Term paid-petition grant rate at about 5.4%, the modern Ninth Circuit cert grant rate at about 3.0%, and the no-CVSG rate at about 3.0%. This petition deserves a substantial upward adjustment because a response was requested and six judges dissented from en banc denial. I stop at 17% because there is no relist yet, no CVSG, no clear circuit conflict, and multiple record-specific alternative grounds. The likely grant-side route would be a narrow GVR rather than plenary certiorari, but denial remains much more likely.
