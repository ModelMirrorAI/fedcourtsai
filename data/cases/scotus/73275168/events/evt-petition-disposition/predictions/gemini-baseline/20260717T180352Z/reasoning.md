# Reasoning for Prediction

**Case**: Raymond Poore v. United States (No. 25-227)
**Issue**: Whether the limits on agency deference announced in *Kisor v. Wilkie* and *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* constrain the deference courts may accord the Sentencing Commission's interpretation of its own rules via commentary.
**Status**: Pending cert petition with 10 relists (distributions for conference from January to April 2026).

## Qualitative Analysis

1.  **The Substantive Question**: The petition presents a massive and unresolved question of federal law: how the Court's recent watershed administrative law decisions (*Kisor* and *Loper Bright*) apply to the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines and *Stinson* deference. This has resulted in a deep 12-circuit split, marking it as a highly salient issue that the Court will almost certainly need to resolve eventually.
2.  **The Vehicle Problem**: The government's Brief in Opposition argues that the petition is moot because the petitioner completed his term of imprisonment and was released on November 7, 2025. Although the petitioner and some circuits argue that the possibility of a reduced supervised release term prevents mootness, the government correctly points out that this antecedent jurisdictional issue makes the case a poor vehicle for deciding the substantive *Loper Bright* question.
3.  **Docket Activity (Relists)**: The petition was relisted for conference ten consecutive times (from January 9, 2026, through April 17, 2026). In Supreme Court practice, extensive relists—especially when coupled with a vehicle defect on an otherwise cert-worthy issue—strongly suggest that a Justice is drafting a dissent from the denial of certiorari. A straight hold for another case typically does not involve ten explicit distributions.
4.  **Base Rates**: The `statpack.md` corpus base rates indicate that petitions with 3+ relists are granted roughly 21.8% of the time, while the vast majority (76.5%) are denied. 
5.  **Conclusion**: The combination of a severe vehicle problem (mootness) and extensive relists strongly points to a denial of certiorari accompanied by a written dissent from denial. The Court is likely waiting for a cleaner vehicle to resolve the *Stinson* / *Loper Bright* split.

Therefore, the predicted disposition is **denied** with a low probability of a grant (0.15).
