# Reasoning

## Legal Question and Governing Standard
The legal question is whether *Stinson* deference—the doctrine requiring courts to defer to the Sentencing Commission’s commentary interpreting its Guidelines (established in *Stinson v. United States*, 508 U.S. 36 (1993))—survives the Supreme Court's decisions in *Kisor v. Wilkie*, 588 U.S. 558 (2019), and *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 603 U.S. 369 (2024), which heavily limited and overturned *Chevron* deference to federal agencies. Specifically, the case questions whether a prior state conviction for "substantial battery as a party to the crime" qualifies as a "crime of violence" under the career offender guideline (USSG § 4B1.2), based on Application Note 1 which includes "inchoate" offenses, despite the guideline text not explicitly mentioning them. 

The governing standard for granting certiorari is whether there is a compelling reason, such as a circuit split on an important federal question, and whether the case is a suitable vehicle.

## Facts and Record Driving the Outcome
While the petitioner correctly identifies a deep and recurring circuit split on whether *Kisor* and *Loper Bright* apply to Sentencing Guidelines commentary (and specifically *Stinson* deference), the specific application note in this case (Application Note 1 to § 4B1.2) was mooted prospectively. The Sentencing Commission's 2023 amendment moved the substance of former Application Note 1 into the text of Section 4B1.2(d) itself. 

The Solicitor General's Brief in Opposition heavily emphasizes this point, noting that even the petitioner acknowledges that courts will no longer be called upon to defer to this specific commentary. Furthermore, the Commission is currently undertaking a multiyear study specifically to address case law concerning the validity and enforceability of guideline commentary in light of recent Supreme Court decisions.

## Conclusion and Probabilities
The Supreme Court routinely denies certiorari in Sentencing Guidelines cases where the Commission has already amended the Guidelines to resolve the circuit split or fix the underlying issue prospectively. Because this specific commentary was moved into the Guidelines text in 2023, granting certiorari here would only affect a shrinking pool of historical cases. 

Despite the case presenting a poor vehicle, the petition has been relisted an extraordinary ten times (from the January 9, 2026 conference through the April 17, 2026 conference). Such a long streak of relists without a grant or a CVSG order strongly indicates that one or more Justices (such as Justice Gorsuch or Justice Thomas, who have been critical of *Stinson* and *Auer* deference) are drafting a dissent from the denial of certiorari to express their views on the broader methodological question, or they were attempting to build consensus for a summary reversal that ultimately failed. 

Given the poor vehicle problem and the Commission's ongoing multiyear study, the Court is highly unlikely to grant cert. 

Predicted Disposition: **Denied** (Probability of grant: 0.05)
