# Prediction: denied

I assign a 34% probability that the Court grants the petition and predict denial. This is materially above the ordinary paid cert-petition base rate because the docket shows sustained interest, but the case has unusually serious vehicle problems.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether *Kisor v. Wilkie* and *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* constrain the deference courts give Sentencing Commission commentary under *Stinson v. United States*. The immediate dispute concerns former Application Note 1 to U.S.S.G. § 4B1.2, which treated conspiracy and other inchoate offenses as crimes of violence even though the then-operative Guideline text did not expressly do so.

Certiorari is discretionary. The strongest grant considerations are a square and entrenched conflict among federal courts of appeals, recurrence and national importance, and a suitable vehicle. Four votes are required to grant. Even a strong split normally does not overcome mootness, forfeiture, alternative grounds, or the reduced prospective importance of an amended provision.

## Signals favoring a grant

The petition presents a substantial institutional question and documents a six-to-six circuit split. The Third, Fourth, Sixth, Ninth, Eleventh, and D.C. Circuits generally require genuine ambiguity before consulting or deferring to Guidelines commentary; the First, Second, Fifth, Seventh, Eighth, and Tenth Circuits continue to apply *Stinson* more broadly. Several of those rulings are en banc, so further percolation is unlikely to eliminate the conflict. CourtListener searches confirmed representative published decisions on both sides: the Fifth Circuit's en banc *United States v. Vargas*, 74 F.4th 673 (2023), and the Eleventh Circuit's en banc *United States v. Dupree*, 57 F.4th 1269 (2023), as well as the Sixth Circuit's *United States v. Riccardi*, 989 F.3d 476 (2021).

The docket behavior is the strongest case-specific grant signal. After initially waiving a response, the United States was ordered to respond. Following the BIO and reply, the petition was distributed for the January 9, 2026 conference and then redistributed for nine later conferences through April 17. That degree of repeated consideration is far outside the ordinary denial path and strongly suggests that at least one Justice considers the petition worthy of sustained attention.

The committed statpack estimates a 7.2% grant rate for paid modern cert petitions in its incomplete 2025-Term sample (10 grants among 139 estimated resolved paid matters). The case-specific response request, mature split, and repeated redistributions justify a several-fold increase over that prior.

## Signals favoring denial

The BIO identifies multiple independent obstacles:

- Petitioner completed his prison term in November 2025. The government argues that a possible later reduction of supervised release is too speculative to preserve a live controversy. Resolving that threshold Article III dispute would complicate review of the question presented.
- The sentence arose under § 2K2.1, whose own commentary expressly incorporated both § 4B1.2(a) and Application Note 1. The government therefore argues that the case does not cleanly present whether commentary may expand the unambiguous text of § 4B1.2(a).
- Wisconsin party-to-a-crime liability required completion of the underlying substantial battery. The government argues that the conviction therefore independently satisfies the elements clause, and that petitioner forfeited any contrary argument.
- The Sentencing Commission moved the inchoate-offense language into § 4B1.2(d) in 2023. Although that amendment is not retroactive and does not resolve petitioner's case, it substantially reduces the issue's prospective importance.
- The BIO identifies many recent denials of petitions raising related commentary-deference questions and invokes the Court's ordinary reluctance to review Guidelines-interpretation disputes that the Commission can amend.

The unusually long redistribution history is not unambiguously pro-grant. A conventional merits grant ordinarily needs fewer conferences once four votes coalesce. Nine redistributions can instead reflect preparation of a statement respecting denial, a dissent from denial, or consideration of a narrow summary disposition. Given the vehicle defects, that explanation is at least as plausible as a plenary grant.

## Bottom line

The split and docket signals make this a much closer prediction than the low cert base rate would suggest. Still, mootness plus two antecedent merits grounds make this petition a poor vehicle, while the 2023 amendment limits forward importance. I therefore predict denial, with a substantial 34% chance of a grant or grant-like disposition. I do not predict Justice-specific votes because the public record supports no reliable allocation beyond evidence that at least one Justice has sustained interest.
