# Prediction: denied

I assign a 24% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict denial. The procedural history makes this substantially closer than an ordinary cert petition, but the government's vehicle objections make a plenary grant unlikely.

## Question and governing considerations

The petition asks whether *Kisor v. Wilkie* and *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* constrain the deference courts give Sentencing Commission commentary under *Stinson v. United States*. The concrete dispute concerns former Application Note 1 to U.S.S.G. §4B1.2, which included aiding-and-abetting, conspiracy, and attempt offenses within “crime of violence” even though the then-operative guideline text did not expressly include all of them.

Under the Court's usual certiorari considerations, the petition has a strong conflict argument. The petition describes an acknowledged 6–6 division among the federal courts of appeals. Six circuits apply *Kisor*'s genuine-ambiguity inquiry to Guidelines commentary, while six continue to treat pre-*Kisor* applications of *Stinson* as controlling. The CourtListener search I ran confirmed that the issue appears in recent published circuit precedent, including the en banc Eleventh Circuit's *Dupree* decision and Ninth Circuit decisions through 2025. The question is recurring and consequential because commentary can increase advisory sentencing ranges.

The merits framing also has force. *Stinson* expressly analogized Guidelines commentary to an agency's interpretation of its own rule, and *Kisor* tightened the deference attached to that analogy. Notably, the government's brief agrees at a general level that *Kisor* supplies the standard for deciding whether particular Guidelines commentary merits deference. That agreement weakens the Seventh Circuit panel's suggestion that *Kisor*'s effect is simply unclear.

## Case-specific obstacles

The government's brief in opposition identifies several unusually serious vehicle problems:

1. Poore completed his prison term on November 7, 2025. The government argues that his challenge to the length of that term is moot and that possible early termination of supervised release is too speculative to supply redressability. Other circuits, including the Seventh Circuit, take a more permissive view, but the threshold dispute is itself an impediment to reaching the question presented.
2. Poore was sentenced under §2K2.1. The government argues that commentary to that provision independently and expressly incorporated both §4B1.2(a) and former Application Note 1, while Poore did not dispute that §2K2.1's undefined term “crime of violence” was ambiguous. That creates a route to affirm without deciding the broad deference question.
3. The prior Wisconsin party-to-a-crime conviction required a completed substantial battery. The government therefore argues that the conviction independently satisfies the guideline's force clause, regardless of whether commentary can add wholly inchoate offenses. The petition did not preserve a contrary argument.
4. The Commission moved the relevant language into the guideline text in 2023. The amendment is not retroactive and does not eliminate the broader methodological split, but it reduces the prospective importance of this particular Application Note 1 dispute.

These are not minor factual wrinkles: they threaten both Article III jurisdiction and whether a ruling on the presented question would change Poore's sentence.

## Procedural signal and calibration

The docket shows unusually strong indications of Justice-level interest. The Court requested a response after the United States initially waived one. After briefing, the petition was listed for the January 9, 2026 conference and then redistributed for nine more conferences through April 17. A paid filing, a requested response, and repeated conference distributions all lift the case far above the ordinary cert base rate.

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and 21.8% for petitions in the 3+ relist bucket. The Seventh Circuit baseline is only 2.0%, but it is much less informative once the repeated relists are known. Here, however, the exceptionally long sequence is not an unqualified grant signal. Grants commonly follow one or two relists; a months-long sequence can instead reflect work on a statement or dissent respecting denial, especially where a significant legal issue arrives in a poor vehicle. That pattern, together with the government's layered alternative grounds, leaves the grant probability near the 3+ relist reference rate rather than above 50%.

A GVR is possible because the government agrees that *Kisor* governs commentary deference at the appropriate level of generality. Still, the government defends the judgment on independent grounds, and neither *Kisor* nor *Loper Bright* intervened after the Seventh Circuit's April 2025 decision. Denial is therefore the most likely label.

The case's significance score is 0.74. If decided on the question presented, it would settle a nationwide sentencing-methodology split affecting many federal defendants, though it is less publicly salient than a major constitutional or nationwide regulatory case.
