# Reasoning for Prediction

## The Case and Legal Question
This case, *Gator's Custom Guns, Inc. v. Washington* (No. 25-153), is a petition for a writ of certiorari challenging Washington State's magazine capacity limit under the Second Amendment. The specific question presented is whether ammunition feeding devices with a capacity of more than ten rounds are "Arms" presumptively entitled to constitutional protection under the Second Amendment's plain text. The petition follows the Washington Supreme Court's decision upholding the ban.

## Procedural Posture and Key Facts
The petition was filed in August 2025. After the State initially waived its right to respond, the Court requested a response, indicating preliminary interest from at least one Justice. Following the distribution for the December 5, 2025 conference, this petition has been astonishingly relisted at **every subsequent conference** throughout the remainder of the Term—totaling 23 consecutive relists leading up to the final late-June conferences of 2026. 

Additionally, the parties filed supplemental briefs in the spring of 2026. Petitioners filed a supplemental brief in March 2026 concerning "Benson," and Washington filed a supplemental brief on April 23, 2026, explicitly noting a vacatur ("Rule15.8LtrToCtReBensenVacated.pdf"). This strongly suggests that an intervening lower court decision favorable to the petitioners (potentially creating or deepening a circuit split) was vacated (e.g., pending en banc review). 

## Analysis
The base rate for a discretionary cert petition being granted is extremely low (under 5%). A case that has been relisted over twenty times without being granted or summarily reversed (GVR) is virtually certain to result in a denial of certiorari accompanied by a lengthy dissent. 

When the Supreme Court intends to hear a case on the merits, it grants certiorari after one or a few relists (which are typically used to ensure the vehicle is clean or to write a brief summary reversal). The unprecedented 8-month delay here points singularly to a fractured Court where there are not four votes to grant, and where one or more Justices (likely Justices Thomas and/or Alito, who have historically dissented from denials of cert in Second Amendment challenges to assault weapon and magazine bans) have been taking the entire Term to draft a comprehensive dissent from the denial of certiorari. 

Furthermore, the State's Rule 15.8 letter informing the Court of a vacatur (Bensen Vacated) means that a lower court decision that might have provided a persuasive circuit split has been removed from the board, further undercutting the petitioners' vehicle. Without a split, and with the end of the Term having arrived, the petition will be denied.

## Conclusion
The combination of an extreme number of relists, the late-Term timing, the subject matter (Second Amendment), and the intervening vacatur of a potentially favorable lower court case all point overwhelmingly to a denial. The probability of a merits grant at this stage is virtually zero. 

**Predicted Disposition:** Denied (with expected dissents from denial of certiorari).
**Probability of Grant:** 2%
