# Gator's Custom Guns, Inc. v. Washington (No. 25-153) — cert disposition

**Prediction: granted (P = 0.68), most likely via a GVR after the Court decides
the consolidated AR-15-ban cases in June 2027.** "Granted" here follows the
pipeline's labeling convention, under which a grant/vacate/remand order lands on
the granted side of the disposition taxonomy.

## The question presented and the posture

Petitioners (represented by Clement & Murphy) challenge Washington's ESSB 5078,
which bans the manufacture, import, distribution, and sale — though not the
possession — of ammunition feeding devices holding more than ten rounds. The
Washington Supreme Court (568 P.3d 278, May 8, 2025) reversed a superior-court
judgment for petitioners, holding that such magazines are not "Arms" within the
plain text of the Second Amendment and so receive no presumptive constitutional
protection. The QP: whether 10+-round magazines are "Arms" presumptively
protected under the Second Amendment's plain text.

The petition (which I read in the provisioned record) presents a genuine,
acknowledged split on the threshold "Arms" question: the Ninth Circuit en banc
(*Duncan v. Bonta*, 133 F.4th 852 (2025)) and Seventh Circuit (*Bevis*, 85 F.4th
1175 (2023)) hold magazines are not "Arms"; the D.C. Circuit (*Hanson*, 120
F.4th 223 (2024)) and Third Circuit (*ANJRPC*, 910 F.3d 106 (2018)) hold or
strongly suggest they are. The Washington Supreme Court's decision extends the
"not Arms" side to a state court of last resort. The State's BIO (also read)
answers that every appellate court has ultimately *upheld* LCM restrictions
(so the split is methodological, not outcome-determinative), that the Court
denied materially identical petitions three times in two years (*Harrel*,
*Ocean State Tactical*, *Snope*), and that this is a poor vehicle: a
sales-not-possession law, a one-sided record (petitioners submitted no
evidence), a defensive posture ("unclean hands"), and only the step-one half of
the *Bruen* test.

## What the docket reveals — the decisive signals

1. **The Court called for a response** after Washington waived (Sep. 4, 2025) —
   a classic screening signal that at least one chambers is interested.
2. **~19 conference distributions** from September 2025 through June 29, 2026
   without a denial. Petitions the Court intends simply to deny do not get
   relisted for an entire Term.
3. **The supplemental-brief exchange about *Benson***: petitioners' March 11,
   2026 supplemental brief flagged the D.C. Circuit's decision striking down
   D.C.'s materially identical magazine ban (sharpening the split); Washington's
   April 23, 2026 Rule 15.8 letter reported that the *Benson* panel opinion had
   been vacated (en banc rehearing), deflating that split.
4. **The revealed behavior of the June 30, 2026 order list** (from corpus priors
   and public coverage — not this case's own outcome, which does not yet exist):
   the Court **granted** certiorari in *Viramontes v. Cook County* (25-238,
   CA7, 22 distributions) and *Grant v. Higgins* (25-566, CA2, 17
   distributions), consolidating them on a QP **limited to whether the Second
   and Fourteenth Amendments protect possession of AR-15-platform and similar
   semi-automatic rifles** — while **denying** other Second Amendment petitions
   the same day (e.g. *McCoy v. ATF*, 25-24; *West Virginia Citizens Defense
   League v. ATF*, 25-132). Gator's was neither granted nor denied, and the
   snapshot (created July 10, 2026) shows no disposition through the end-of-Term
   clean-up window. *Duncan v. Bonta* (25-198), the parallel California
   magazine-ban petition with its own ~19 relists, is likewise still pending.

The only coherent reading: **the Court is holding the magazine-ban petitions
(Gator's and Duncan) pending its merits decision in the consolidated rifle-ban
cases**, to be argued in OT2026 and decided by roughly June 2027.

## How a hold resolves, and the probability

For a held petition, the terminal dispositions are (a) GVR in light of the
merits decision, (b) denial, or (c) a later plenary grant. The strongest
precedent is the post-*Bruen* sequence: in June–July 2022 the Court GVR'd every
held hardware-ban case — including the magazine-ban cases *Duncan v. Bonta* and
*ANJRPC v. Bruck* — even though *Bruen* itself concerned public carry, a far
more remote question than rifles-to-magazines here. The Washington Supreme
Court's holding rests entirely on the threshold "not Arms" move; almost any
challenger-favorable reasoning in *Viramontes/Grant* about the plain-text scope
of "Arms" and the common-use standard undermines it, and the GVR standard
(reasonable probability the decision below rested on a now-doubtful premise) is
lenient.

Rough decomposition:

- P(petition stays held to the merits decision, rather than being denied at the
  long conference or granted as a companion) ≈ 0.93; P(later plenary grant,
  e.g. alongside a *Duncan* grant) ≈ 0.04; P(early denial) ≈ 0.03.
- P(challengers prevail in *Viramontes/Grant*) ≈ 0.75. The Court granted after
  Justice Kavanaugh's statement respecting denial in *Snope* (June 2025) that
  the Court "should and presumably will" address the AR-15 question soon; a
  grant on this QP from this Court is far more likely a vehicle for reversal
  than affirmance.
- P(GVR | challenger win) ≈ 0.9 (the 2022 precedent, plus the direct doctrinal
  overlap). P(GVR | state win) ≈ 0.2 — even an opinion upholding rifle bans
  could repudiate the *Bevis/Duncan* threshold methodology the Washington court
  adopted, which alone can support a GVR; but denial dominates that branch.

Combining: P(granted-side label) ≈ 0.04 + 0.93 × (0.75 × 0.9 + 0.25 × 0.2)
≈ 0.71; I shade down modestly to **0.68** for the vehicle-specific risks the BIO
identifies (a sales-only law and a bare record make an outright denial-despite-
win somewhat more plausible for *this* petition than for *Duncan*) and for
residual uncertainty about the merits outcome. Predicted disposition:
**granted** (expected form: GVR, ~June–July 2027; a plenary grant is the
low-probability alternative on the granted side).

This sits far above the corpus base rate for modern discretionary cert
petitions (~4.9% granted, per the committed statpack), which is the right
anchor only *unconditionally*; conditioning on a called-for response, a
Term-long relist streak, companion grants on an adjacent QP, and survival of
the end-of-Term clean-up list moves this cell into a small reference class
where granted-side outcomes (mostly GVRs) predominate.

## What would change my mind

A denial at the 2026 long conference (if the Court decides the magazine
question is severable and unworthy even of a hold) is the main downside
scenario and the bulk of the remaining 32%. A state-favorable merits ruling in
*Viramontes/Grant* would likewise flip the expected disposition to denied.

No per-justice votes are predicted: cert-stage votes are not published, and the
disposition target here is the petition's disposition, not a merits vote.
