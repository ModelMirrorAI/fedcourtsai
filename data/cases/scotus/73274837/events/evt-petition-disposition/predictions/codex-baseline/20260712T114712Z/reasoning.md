# Prediction: granted (56%)

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether ammunition feeding devices capable of holding more than ten rounds are “Arms” presumptively protected by the Second Amendment. The Court is deciding whether to grant certiorari, not whether Washington's restriction is ultimately constitutional. The principal certiorari considerations are whether the decision below conflicts with other appellate authority on an important federal question, whether the Washington Supreme Court's threshold analysis is inconsistent with *Heller* and *Bruen*, and whether this record is a suitable vehicle.

## Record and merits signals

The provisioned questions-presented text and petition frame a narrow threshold issue. The Washington Supreme Court held that large-capacity magazines are neither “Arms” nor necessary ancillary components because firearms can operate with magazines holding ten or fewer rounds. The petition argues that magazines actively feed ammunition, are integral to semiautomatic firearms, and are commonly possessed; it identifies differing threshold analyses in the D.C., Third, Seventh, and Ninth Circuits. That is a consequential and recurring question, and the state court's final federal ruling allows review before the consumer-protection remedies proceeding concludes.

The brief in opposition identifies substantial vehicle problems. Petitioners did not rely on their experts at summary judgment, their ownership evidence was excluded, and the record contains the State's unrebutted evidence on historical meaning and self-defense use. Washington regulates new manufacture and sale rather than possession, making this case less restrictive than several challenges for which certiorari was denied. Every cited court of appeals ultimately upheld the relevant magazine restriction, so the disagreement concerns reasoning rather than judgments. Those considerations make a denial—or a grant in a related, better-developed case—quite plausible.

## Procedural signals and calibration

The snapshot materially changes the ordinary low-cert baseline. The Court requested a response after Washington initially waived one. Four amicus filings supported petitioners, including states and major national firearms organizations. Most importantly, after initial distribution for the December 5, 2025 conference, the docket records repeated distribution through the June 29, 2026 conference—roughly twenty-two conference dates—along with supplemental filings by both sides. That extraordinary persistence indicates a hold for related litigation, sustained consideration of a grant, or preparation of separate writing. A long hold can end in a denial accompanied by a statement or dissent, but it is far stronger evidence of serious Court interest than the typical petition supplies.

The committed statpack reports a 4.9% grant rate among resolved modern discretionary-cert petitions. I raise that anchor sharply for the requested response, high-quality advocacy and amici, clean constitutional issue, and exceptionally prolonged conference history. I stop only slightly above even odds because grants usually follow much sooner, while a long hold can reflect fewer than four votes or selection of another magazine case as the vehicle. The resulting estimate is **56% granted** and **44% not granted**, with denial the dominant alternative. “Granted” includes a plenary grant or a grant-vacate-remand disposition; no individual-Justice votes are predicted because certiorari votes are not public on this record.
