# Prediction

I predict denial, with a 14% probability of any grant.

## Question and governing law

The petition presents two questions arising from California's 2022 renaming of Hastings College of the Law and elimination of the statutory board seat for S.C. Hastings's heirs or representatives. First, it asks whether the 1878 statute—enacted on condition that Hastings pay the State $100,000 and using the terms “forever” and “always”—created contractual obligations protected by the Contract Clause. Second, it asks whether legislation that attributes genocidal conduct to a deceased named person and removes associated statutory benefits is a bill of attainder.

The contract issue turns on both statutory intent to contract and the reserved-powers rule that a legislature cannot bargain away essential sovereign authority. The petition relies principally on *United States v. Winstar Corp.*, 518 U.S. 839 (1996), especially for the proposition that damages may remain available even when specific performance would constrain sovereign action. The state court instead treated management of a public educational institution as reserved sovereign authority, drawing on *Newton v. Commissioners*, 100 U.S. 548 (1879). The attainder issue is governed by the historical, functional, and motivational tests described in *Nixon v. Administrator of General Services* and *Selective Service System v. Minnesota Public Interest Research Group*.

## Case-specific signals

The strongest grant signal is procedural. The July 17 snapshot shows that both sets of respondents waived a response, the petition was distributed for the June 25 conference, and the Court then requested a response, now due August 21 after an extension. A response request reflects interest from at least one Justice and materially raises the case above the ordinary paid-petition baseline. The petition also challenges a published state appellate decision on federal constitutional grounds, and the 1878 enactment's exchange structure and emphatic language make the asserted contract more substantial than a bare legislative policy.

The negatives remain stronger. The petition identifies conflict with Supreme Court precedent, but no split among federal circuits or state high courts. Its facts are singular: a nineteenth-century public-law-school charter, a hereditary board seat, and a modern renaming measure responding to contested historical conduct. That makes the case a weak vehicle for broadly recurring Contract Clause doctrine. On the merits, *Newton* supplies a close analogy for a legislature's inability to bind successors on management or location of a public institution. The damages theory may be narrower, but the petition itself reveals waiver and pleading disputes that could prevent clean review. The attainder claim faces the basic difficulty that the named historical figure is deceased, the living petitioners remain eligible for ordinary board service, and the law has an evident nonpunitive restorative-justice rationale. CourtListener research located no Supreme Court merits decision extending the attainder doctrine to this posthumous setting.

There is no brief in opposition in the provisioned documents because the response is not yet due. That makes the forecast less settled than it would be after adversarial briefing. Still, a response request is a one-Justice signal rather than the four votes required for certiorari, and most such petitions are ultimately denied.

## Probability and significance

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions, compared with 2.49% across all fee classes, and a 0.8% grant rate for petitions with no relist. This petition has not been relisted or received a CVSG, but the response request justifies a large uplift from those anchors. Balancing that signal against the absence of a split and the case-specific doctrinal and procedural obstacles produces a 14% grant probability. A GVR is unlikely because the record identifies no intervening authority or mootness development; denial is the likeliest disposition.

The 0.66 big-case score reflects the potentially important limits a merits ruling could set on state contracting power, public-institution governance, and legislative condemnation of historical figures. The dispute is politically salient, but its unusual facts limit its likely national reach.
