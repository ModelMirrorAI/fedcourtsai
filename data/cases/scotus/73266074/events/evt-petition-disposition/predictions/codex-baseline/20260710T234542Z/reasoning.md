# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with a 9% probability of a grant. That is materially above the ordinary cert-petition baseline because the Court requested a response after both sets of respondents had waived one, but the petition still looks more like fact-bound error correction than a case satisfying the Court's usual reasons for review.

## Question and governing standard

The petition presents two federal questions arising from California's 2022 renaming of Hastings College of the Law and elimination of a hereditary board seat. First, it asks whether the 1878 statute, enacted on the condition that S.C. Hastings pay the State $100,000 and using the words “forever” and “always,” created contractual obligations protected by the Contract Clause. Second, it asks whether the later legislature's findings about Hastings's conduct, together with the loss of naming and governance benefits, constitute a prohibited bill of attainder.

At the certiorari stage, the decisive issue is not simply whether the California Court of Appeal may have erred. Review is ordinarily most likely when a lower court has created or deepened a conflict, decided an important recurring federal question that requires resolution, or seriously departed from this Court's precedent. The petition principally alleges conflict with this Court's cases rather than a split among lower courts.

## Record and petition-specific considerations

The provisioned snapshot dated July 10, 2026 shows a paid petition docketed as No. 25-1231. California and the college respondents initially waived responses. After distribution for the June 25 conference, the Court requested a response on June 22; the response was due July 22, and an extension motion was submitted July 10. A response request following waivers means at least one Justice saw enough possible merit to seek adversarial briefing. I treat that as the strongest grant-positive signal in the record. No brief in opposition had yet been filed or provisioned, so the State's vehicle and merits arguments remain an important source of uncertainty.

The complete 46-page petition and its questions-presented extract sharpen several points:

- The Contract Clause argument has intuitive force. The alleged exchange was specific, Hastings allegedly paid the required consideration, and the statute used unusually durable language for the name and board seat. The petition also invokes *United States v. Winstar Corp.* for the narrower proposition that sovereign power may remain intact while the government bears damages for breach.
- The petition does not identify a developed lower-court conflict over either question. Its attempts to distinguish the public-institution cases and extend *Winstar* to this state-law arrangement are largely arguments that the state court misapplied existing law. The idiosyncratic 1878 enactment and a single institution also limit recurrence.
- The damages theory has a substantial vehicle complication. The California Court of Appeal deemed the theory waived on rehearing, forcing the petition to litigate whether that state procedural ruling is adequate and independent before the Court could reach the claimed *Winstar* error.
- The bill-of-attainder theory is novel but weak as a cert vehicle. The legislation names a person who died generations ago, while the asserted punishment of living descendants depends on treating disputed naming, board-seat, monetary, and reputational interests as punitive confiscation. The petition offers historical materials about posthumous attainders but identifies no modern decisional conflict. The State also has an evident nonpunitive restorative-justice rationale, even if petitioners dispute its factual basis and fit.
- The demurrer posture and published state-court opinion make the legal issues relatively clean, but the contested historical narrative and overlapping contract, sovereign-power, remedy, preservation, standing, and punishment questions create multiple off-ramps.

The committed corpus statpack reports grants in 1.4% of its 296 resolved SCOTUS records, but its overall SCOTUS labels are dominated by “other” and “dismissed” dispositions and it contains no resolved recent Terms. I therefore used it only as broad confirmation that grants are rare, not as a case-specific estimate. The response request justifies raising the probability well above that coarse baseline; the absence of a split, the narrow facts, and the vehicle issues keep denial substantially more likely.
