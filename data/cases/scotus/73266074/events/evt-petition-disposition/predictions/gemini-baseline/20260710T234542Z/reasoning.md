# Hastings College Conservation Committee v. California, No. 25-1231 — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(granted) = 0.12.**

## The event

Petition for a writ of certiorari (paid petition, counseled) from the California Court of Appeal (First Appellate District) decision in *Hastings College Conservation Committee v. State of California*, Case No. A170255 (Oct. 15, 2025). Forward-mode cell: the petition is genuinely pending, with the response currently due after a requested extension on July 10, 2026.

## The legal questions

This petition presents two high-profile constitutional questions:
1. **Contract Clause:** Whether a state law that requires a specified name ("Hastings College of the Law") and a governance structure (a board seat reserved for the founder's heirs) for a public college, enacted in exchange for a payment of $100,000 in 1878, creates binding contractual obligations on the state subject to the protections of the Contract Clause of the U.S. Constitution.
2. **Bill of Attainder Clause:** Whether state legislation (Assembly Bill 1936) posthumously declaring an individual (founder Serranus Clinton Hastings) as having promoted and financed Native American massacres in the 1850s and, on that basis, stripping statutory benefits (the name and the board seat) for him and his descendants, violates the Bill of Attainder Clause of the U.S. Constitution.

## Facts from the snapshot and provisioned documents that drive the outcome

- **Background:** S.C. Hastings founded the college in 1878 via legislation that established the school "upon the condition" that he pay $100,000, that the school "forever" be known as Hastings College of the Law, and that his heirs always hold a board seat. In 2022, California passed AB 1936, renaming the school to UC Law SF and eliminating the heirs' board seat to atone for Hastings' involvement in atrocities.
- **Lower Court Holding:** The California Court of Appeal held that (1) the 1878 Act did not create a private contract between S.C. Hastings and the state under California's presumption against statutory contract creation, and (2) the Bill of Attainder Clause did not apply because Hastings is deceased and stripping an honorary, non-paying board seat from his heirs does not constitute "punishment" under established constitutional doctrine.
- **Docket signals (the most probative part of the snapshot):** The State of California and UC Law SF dean David Faigman initially *waived* their rights to respond to the petition (May 27, 2026). The petition was distributed for the June 25, 2026 conference. However, on June 22, 2026, the Supreme Court **Requested a Response** (CFR) from the respondents. On July 10, 2026, the university respondents submitted a motion for an extension of time.
- **The CFR Signal:** A call for response issued after waivers are filed is a strong procedural indicator that at least one Justice (or their chambers) is interested in the petition and wants the matter fully briefed.

## Weighing

### Toward grant:
- **Interest Signal:** The Court's CFR (Call for Response) dramatically increases the likelihood of a grant from the baseline <1% to roughly 8–15%.
- **High-Profile Controversy:** The case is a prominent "culture war" dispute of national interest. The renaming of public institutions over historical grievances touches on highly visible issues of historical legacy and state power.
- **Originalism & Dartmouth:** Originalist-minded Justices (such as Justices Thomas and Gorsuch) have expressed interest in revitalizing the long-dormant Contract Clause. The petition anchors its arguments in the landmark precedent *Trustees of Dartmouth College v. Woodward* (1819) to argue that specific legislative gift-and-condition charters create binding contracts.
- **Bill of Attainder Split:** There is a tension with the D.C. Circuit's broad definition of "punishment" in *Foretich v. United States* (2003) (which held that legislative adjudication of guilt causing severe reputational harm can be unconstitutional). AB 1936 contains direct legislative "findings" of historical guilt.

### Toward denial:
- **No Direct Conflict on Core Holdings:** There is no direct circuit split on the primary holdings. The Contract Clause interpretation depends heavily on California statutory interpretation rules (presumption against statutory contracts), a state-law question the Supreme Court typically avoids.
- **Posthumous Nature:** A Bill of Attainder historically targets living individuals. S.C. Hastings is deceased, and removing an honorary public board seat from his descendants is a mild regulatory action rather than "punishment" under the functional and historical attainder tests.
- **Extreme Base Rate Pressure:** Even among CFR'd cases, the vast majority (85-90%) are ultimately denied. The Court has an exceptionally high threshold for granting cert, and states generally retain broad police powers to govern and rename their own public universities.

### Net:
The CFR moves this petition significantly above the discretionary-cert baseline. However, the state-law-heavy nature of the Contract Clause claim and the lack of a square, direct circuit split on the posthumous attainder question make a denial the most likely outcome. I put P(granted) at **0.12** and predict **denied** with moderate confidence (0.6).
