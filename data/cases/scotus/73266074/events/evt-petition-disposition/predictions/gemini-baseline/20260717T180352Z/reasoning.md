## Reasoning

**Legal Question & Standards:**
The petition presents two constitutional questions arising from California's Assembly Bill 1936 (2022), which renamed UC Hastings College of the Law (removing the name of its founder, S.C. Hastings) and eliminated the board seat reserved for his heirs, following findings of his involvement in the massacre of Native Americans:
1. Whether a state statute establishing a public college's name and board structure in exchange for a donation constitutes a binding contract protected by the Contract Clause.
2. Whether posthumously removing an individual's name from a state college and stripping his heirs of a board seat based on legislative findings of past crimes constitutes an unconstitutional Bill of Attainder.

**Analysis:**
The case involves highly novel and idiosyncratic applications of the Contract Clause and Bill of Attainder Clause, both of which face steep doctrinal hurdles.
1. **Contract Clause:** The Supreme Court has consistently held that there is a strong presumption against interpreting state statutes as contracts that bind future legislatures, especially concerning the organization and governance of public institutions. While the 1878 Act used language of condition ("upon the condition" of $100,000), a state's control over the name and governance structure of its own public university is a core sovereign function. It is highly unlikely the Court would find a perpetual, irrevocable contract in the naming of a state entity.
2. **Bill of Attainder:** The Bill of Attainder Clause prohibits legislative punishment without a trial. The Supreme Court's test for punishment (*Nixon v. Administrator of General Services*) focuses on historical understanding, functional effects, and legislative intent. Removing an honorific name and a board seat does not implicate traditional punishments like imprisonment, banishment, or confiscation of private property. Furthermore, extending the Clause to protect the reputation of a deceased person and the derivative governance rights of his descendants would be an unprecedented expansion of the doctrine.

**Procedural Posture & Signals:**
The respondents initially waived their right to respond on May 27 and June 4, 2026. However, the Court requested a response on June 22, 2026. While a requested response indicates that at least one Justice found the petition interesting enough to warrant briefing—moving it out of the immediate denial pile—the vast majority of such petitions are still denied. The issues presented are deeply localized to a single state institution, lack a circuit split (having been decided by a state appellate court), and present no urgent issue of national federal law. 

**Conclusion:**
Despite the cultural salience of "cancel culture" and institutional renaming, the legal theories are novel, fact-bound, and weak under existing precedent. There is no split of authority. Thus, the Court is highly likely to deny certiorari.
