# Prediction

I predict that the petition will be **denied**, with a **2.5% probability of any grant** (including a GVR). The most plausible grant-side outcome is a GVR in light of *Bowe v. United States*, rather than plenary review, but neither is likely on this record.

## Question and record

The provisioned `questions-presented.txt` and `petition.txt` ask whether 28 U.S.C. § 2244(d)(1)'s one-year limitations period applies when a state prisoner uses § 2241 to challenge the execution of custody rather than the validity of the underlying conviction. Michael St. Clair argues that an interstate Executive Agreement did not permit his return from Kentucky to Oklahoma and that his resulting custody challenge is outside § 2254. He identifies a conflict between the Seventh Circuit and the Second, Fourth, Fifth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits, and asks the Court at least to vacate and remand after *Bowe*.

The snapshot shows a paid petition from an unpublished Tenth Circuit denial of a certificate of appealability. It was filed May 6, 2026 and distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. No brief in opposition, amicus filing, CVSG, or relist appears in the provisioned record. The petition and extracted questions were complete and nonempty; no BIO was provisioned.

## Governing grant considerations

Under the usual certiorari considerations, a square and enduring circuit conflict on an important federal question favors review. Counterweights include whether the judgment actually presents the asserted conflict, whether the issue was fully reasoned below, and whether case-specific procedural or merits problems make the case a poor vehicle. A GVR is possible when an intervening Supreme Court decision creates a reasonable probability that the lower court would reach a different result.

The claimed split supplies a genuine positive signal, and *Bowe* gives the textual argument some freshness. But the statutory distinction is substantial. The *Bowe* majority held that § 2244(b)(3)(E)'s certiorari bar and § 2244(b)(1)'s old-claim rule did not extend to federal prisoners' § 2255 motions. Its reasoning emphasized that § 2244(b)(1) expressly concerns an application “under section 2254” and that § 2255(h)'s cross-reference imports only specified certification procedures. Section 2244(d)(1) instead expressly applies to a habeas application by a person “in custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court.” St. Clair remains imprisoned under Oklahoma murder judgments even though he characterizes the challenged act as the interstate transfer.

The Seventh Circuit's *Cox v. McBride* exception is also not a clean factual match. *Cox* treated extra custody imposed by a prison disciplinary board as distinct from custody under a state-court judgment. The Tenth Circuit's *Dulworth v. Evans* rejected that distinction and applied § 2244(d)(1) to § 2241 execution-of-sentence challenges. Here, the Executive Agreement did not itself impose an additional sentence; Oklahoma's judgments supply the continuing custody. That posture gives the Court a straightforward basis to regard *Bowe* as distinguishable and deny review without resolving the older administrative-custody split.

Vehicle quality further lowers the odds. Review would come from an unpublished COA denial, the petition's underlying demand for immediate release depends on an unusual Executive Agreement, and the provisioned record shows no respondent or amicus presentation sharpening the asserted conflict. The lack of a current relist or CVSG is not independently dispositive before the first conference, but it supplies no selection signal.
