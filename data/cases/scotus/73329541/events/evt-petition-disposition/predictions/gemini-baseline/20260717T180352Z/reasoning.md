This is a paid certiorari petition arising from the Tenth Circuit's dismissal of Michael St. Clair's 28 U.S.C. § 2241 habeas corpus petition as time-barred under the one-year statute of limitations in 28 U.S.C. § 2244(d)(1). The petitioner, a state prisoner serving sentences of life without parole in Oklahoma for murder, challenges his continued custody following a transfer back to Oklahoma from Kentucky pursuant to an Executive Agreement. He argues that the limitations period of § 2244(d)(1) should not apply to § 2241 petitions, citing a circuit split where the Seventh Circuit diverges from the Second, Fourth, Fifth, Ninth, and Tenth Circuits. He also attempts to leverage the recent Supreme Court decision in Bowe v. United States, No. 24-5438 (2026), which held the certiorari provisions of § 2244 inapplicable to § 2255 petitions.

Despite the existence of a split and the clever reference to recent precedent, a grant of certiorari is highly unlikely. First, the underlying merits of Petitioner's claim are extraordinarily weak. He argues that his transfer back to Oklahoma was unlawful under the Executive Agreement. Yet, the agreement explicitly states that if he is not sentenced to death in Kentucky, he "shall be returned to the State of Oklahoma." After receiving a term of years in Kentucky, he was indeed returned to Oklahoma, strictly complying with the agreement's text. Oklahoma retains jurisdiction over his multiple life-without-parole sentences. Because the underlying claim borders on frivolous, the Court will not use this case as a vehicle to resolve the circuit split regarding § 2244(d)(1) and § 2241.

Furthermore, the petition lacks the traditional signals of a grant. It was distributed for the long conference (September 28, 2026) without any relists and without a Call for the Views of the Solicitor General (CVSG). The base rate for certiorari grants is generally low (~2.5% - 3.0%), and cases distributed for the long conference without relists are overwhelmingly denied. Given the poor vehicle and the lack of signals, a denial is nearly certain.
