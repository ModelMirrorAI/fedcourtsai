# Prediction: GVR

I assign a 72% probability to any grant-side disposition and predict a grant, vacate, and remand rather than plenary review.

## Question and posture

The petition asks whether a federal court that already has subject-matter jurisdiction over a pending matter also has jurisdiction to decide motions under Sections 3 and 4 of the Federal Arbitration Act without a separate jurisdictional basis. The provisioned petition says the District of Delaware had undisputed jurisdiction over financialright claims GmbH's 28 U.S.C. § 1782 discovery application, yet dismissed Burford's motions to stay and compel arbitration. A divided Third Circuit affirmed, and rehearing was denied.

The snapshot dated July 17, 2026 is especially important procedurally. The petition was filed May 4; respondent waived a response on June 9; the petitioners filed a supplemental brief on June 16; the matter was distributed for the September 28 conference; and the Court requested a response on July 15, due August 14. The supplemental brief's text was not provisioned, so I do not assume what it says. The response request after a waiver is nonetheless a meaningful sign that at least one Justice is examining the petition rather than treating it as a routine denial.

## Intervening precedent

CourtListener's opinion record shows that the Court decided *Jules v. Andre Balazs Properties*, No. 25-83, on May 14, 2026, after the judgment below and shortly after this petition was filed. The unanimous opinion held that a federal court with jurisdiction over claims it stayed under FAA § 3 retains jurisdiction to decide later §§ 9 and 10 confirmation or vacatur motions. Its central formulation—jurisdiction to decide the case includes jurisdiction to decide motions within it—substantially supports Burford's jurisdictional-anchor theory.

That intervening ruling makes a GVR the natural grant-side vehicle. The petition itself asks the Court to hold and then GVR in light of *Jules*, and reconsideration by the Third Circuit is more plausible and less costly than granting plenary review of a closely related issue.

## Reasons for caution

*Jules* is not perfectly controlling. It involved live federal claims already stayed under § 3 and post-arbitration motions under §§ 9 and 10. This case concerns front-end §§ 3 and 4 motions responding to a § 1782 discovery application. The Third Circuit may still conclude that a § 1782 application is not an arbitrable civil action or does not satisfy § 4's specific language. There is also no provisioned brief in opposition yet, no CVSG, and no relist.

The statistical baseline is low: the committed 2025 Term estimate gives paid petitions a 5.36% grant rate, and the modern Third Circuit bucket is 2.9%. Those broad rates are outweighed here by two highly selected signals—an on-point intervening merits decision and a post-waiver response request—but they counsel against near-certainty. A corpus lookup also confirmed that GVR is an actively used disposition in the current Term, though the returned cases were not close substantive comparators.

Balancing those considerations, I put denial at 28% and a grant-side result at 72%. Conditional on a grant-side result, GVR is much more likely than plenary certiorari. I do not predict individual votes because a summary order ordinarily does not disclose them.
