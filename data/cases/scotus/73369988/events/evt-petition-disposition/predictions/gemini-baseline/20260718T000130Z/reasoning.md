# Reasoning

**The Legal Question:**
The petition presents three questions: (1) whether a court may affirm an arbitration award when it is unclear whether federal or state law governs the standard of review; (2) whether a court may reject claims of arbitrator partiality without applying the objective appearance-of-bias standard from *Commonwealth Coatings*; and (3) whether due process permits affirmance when information bearing on adjudicative neutrality appears on the record but is not addressed. 

**The Governing Standard:**
The Supreme Court grants certiorari for compelling reasons, typically to resolve a circuit split on an important question of federal law, or to address a state court of last resort deciding an important federal question in a way that conflicts with another state court of last resort or a US Court of Appeals (Rule 10). Because the respondent waived the right to respond, the Court will generally only consider a grant if it first calls for a response.

**Facts from the Snapshot:**
1. The petition comes from the Court of Appeal of California, Second Appellate District (an intermediate state appellate court).
2. The respondent (JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A., et al.) waived their right to respond on June 15, 2026.
3. The case was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference on July 1, 2026, with no interim order requesting a response from the respondent.

**Analysis:**
The procedural posture of this case makes a denial virtually certain. The respondent waived their right to respond to the petition. The Supreme Court almost never grants certiorari when the respondent has waived their response unless the Court first issues an order calling for a response. Here, the docket reflects that the case was distributed for the September conference without any such order being issued. Furthermore, the petition originates from a state intermediate appellate court rather than a state court of last resort, and the questions presented regarding arbitration and arbitrator partiality, while important to the petitioner, do not appear to present a clear circuit split or a question of exceptional national importance that would compel the Court's review without a response. Given the base rate for paid cert petitions (which heavily favors denial) and the presence of a waiver without a subsequent call for a response, the probability of a grant is extremely low. Therefore, the predicted disposition is a denial.
