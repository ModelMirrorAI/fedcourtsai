# Prediction: denied

I predict denial with a 0.4% probability of any grant (including a GVR).

## Record and question

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid, pro se petition from an Illinois protective-order proceeding. The petition was filed April 28, docketed May 4, and distributed on June 17 for the September 28 conference. No response or brief in opposition appears in the snapshot, and none was provisioned. I relied on `record/snapshots/2026-07-17.json`, `record/documents/documents.json`, `record/documents/questions-presented.txt`, and the complete, non-truncated 26-page `record/documents/petition.txt`.

The four questions presented ask whether the protective orders violated the petitioners' speech, religious-exercise, firearm, due-process, and equal-protection rights; whether counsel's failure to preserve those claims should be excused; whether the Illinois Domestic Violence Act is constitutionally defective; and whether alleged record and procedural errors warrant review. The petition describes a prayer walk near a family member's home, communications after requests for no contact, ensuing protective orders, and several alleged trial and appellate irregularities.

## Governing considerations

Supreme Court Rule 10 makes certiorari discretionary and ordinarily calls for a developed conflict, an important unsettled federal question, or a lower-court departure requiring supervision. Error correction, disputed facts, and challenges to the application of settled law are poor vehicles. A federal issue rejected as unpreserved in state court also presents an adequate-state-ground problem unless the petitioner can overcome that bar.

The petition does not identify a split among state supreme courts or federal courts. It cites only *Kennedy v. Bremerton School District* as case authority and does not develop why a public-school employee's personal prayer claim controls a protective order entered after an evidentiary hearing. Its broad Second Amendment theory also does not engage *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024), which recognized the constitutional permissibility of temporary disarmament in at least some protective-order settings involving a judicial finding of dangerousness. The state trial court here reportedly found a risk of future harm. Even if factual or statutory distinctions from *Rahimi* exist, the petition does not turn them into a clean doctrinal question.

## Vehicle and grant signals

Several independent defects sharply reduce the odds:

- The petition acknowledges that trial counsel did not raise the constitutional claims. Its invocation of ineffective assistance does not establish a federal right to effective counsel in this civil protective-order proceeding or otherwise remove the state preservation ground.
- Much of the requested review asks the Court to revisit credibility, exhibits, transcript handling, witness access, briefing time, and the lower courts' application of Illinois law. Those are fact-bound and record-dependent issues.
- The petition does not squarely specify a limiting construction, level of scrutiny, or conflict over the Illinois statute; instead it combines numerous constitutional provisions and alleged case-specific errors.
- The petition's own chronology says the final orders lasted two years after the May 21, 2024 hearing. On the materials provided, that creates a possible expiration or mootness problem by the time of disposition. Alleged reputational effects may be relevant to collateral consequences, but the petition does not supply a clean basis for prospective relief.
- There is no provisioned BIO, CVSG, call for a response, prior relist, institutional counsel, government party, or developed split signal. The snapshot reflects only initial distribution to a future conference.

Across the broader modern slice, petitions with no relist have an estimated 0.8% grant rate, petitions without a CVSG 3.0%, and the ten resolved petitions in the Appellate Court of Illinois, First District bucket were all denied. The originating-court bucket is small and the case has not yet reached its first conference, so I treat those cuts as directional rather than deterministic. A general 2020s corpus query returned selected, high-salience dockets rather than close factual comparators and did not justify an upward adjustment.

The severe preservation and vehicle problems, absence of a conflict, weak merits framing, and lack of positive docket signals warrant reducing the paid-petition anchor to 0.4%. A straight denial is much likelier than dismissal or GVR. I omit justice-by-justice votes because cert votes ordinarily are not disclosed and the record does not support responsible individual assignments.

## Significance

I assign a `big_case_score` of 0.38. A clean decision on the interaction between domestic-protection orders and speech, religious exercise, or firearm possession could have meaningful consequences, but this petition's fact-bound intrafamily posture and bundled questions make it unlikely to produce a broad rule.
