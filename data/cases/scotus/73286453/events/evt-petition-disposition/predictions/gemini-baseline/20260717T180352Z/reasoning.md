# Reasoning

## The Legal Question
The petition asks whether, under *County of Sacramento v. Lewis* (1998), a law enforcement officer who injures or kills a civilian through the "intentional misuse" of a police vehicle in a non-emergency situation (here, driving intoxicated without headlights) can be denied qualified immunity because the right to be free from such substantive due process violations is clearly established.

## Governing Standard and Base Rates
The base rate for a paid, discretionary certiorari petition is generally around 2.5% to 3.0%. A direct circuit split significantly elevates this probability, but the vast majority of petitions are still denied. A case with 0 relists currently distributed for its first conference (the September "Long Conference") faces steep odds, though we are predicting its ultimate disposition.

## Analysis of the Record
The petitioner alleges a direct circuit split between the Eleventh Circuit (which granted qualified immunity) and the Tenth (*Browder v. City of Albuquerque*, authored by then-Judge Gorsuch) and Fourth Circuits (*Dean v. McKinney*), which found the right clearly established under *Lewis*. 

While the legal question is compelling and the involvement of a Gorsuch-authored opinion on the other side of the split increases the petition's salience, the respondent's Brief in Opposition effectively raises a vehicle problem. The BIO argues that the facts are distinguishable because the officers in the Tenth and Fourth Circuit cases affirmatively abused the "trappings of power" (e.g., activating emergency lights for personal use), whereas Deputy Locure was merely driving his cruiser drunk off-duty. The BIO frames the latter as a gross negligence state-law tort rather than an abuse of executive power rising to a Fourteenth Amendment violation. 

The Supreme Court often denies certiorari when a claimed circuit split can be reconciled on the facts or when the case is a poor vehicle to resolve the underlying constitutional question. Because Locure was not using emergency lights or actively wielding state authority (other than driving a county vehicle), the Court may view this case as too close to a traditional state tort to warrant constitutionalizing the issue. Thus, while the split is notable, the vehicle issues suggest the Court will likely deny review and wait for a cleaner case involving the affirmative misuse of police authority.

## Conclusion
Given the low base rate for certiorari grants and the factual distinctions raised in the BIO that weaken the circuit split argument, the petition is more likely than not to be denied. I assign a 15% probability of a grant (or GVR), recognizing the split's salience but finding the vehicle imperfect. 

I rate the stakes (`big_case_score`) at 0.70 because if granted, it would resolve a significant qualified immunity and substantive due process question concerning police liability for vehicular misconduct.
