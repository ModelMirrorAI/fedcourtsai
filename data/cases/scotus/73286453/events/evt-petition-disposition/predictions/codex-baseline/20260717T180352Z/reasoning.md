# Prediction: denied

I assign a 6% probability to any grant and predict denial of certiorari. The forecast uses the July 17, 2026 snapshot, the provisioned questions presented, the full petition, and the brief in opposition. The docket is a paid Term 2025 petition from the Eleventh Circuit, distributed for its first conference, with no relist or CVSG shown in the snapshot.

## Question and governing considerations

The petition asks whether *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, particularly footnote 13, clearly established by January 2021 that an officer may incur substantive-due-process liability for intentionally misusing a police vehicle outside an emergency, and whether the Eleventh Circuit conflicts with *Browder* (Tenth Circuit) and *Dean* (Fourth Circuit). The underlying qualified-immunity inquiry asks whether the alleged conduct violated a constitutional right that was clearly established, either through materially similar precedent, a controlling general principle, or an obviously clear violation. At the cert stage, the strongest considerations are a genuine conflict over a recurring federal question and a clean vehicle in which the disputed issue was preserved and outcome-determinative.

## Grant-positive signals

The petition identifies a facially serious disagreement. The Eleventh Circuit held that its precedents foreclosed obvious clarity despite alleged conduct—driving intoxicated at roughly 70 mph in a 25-mph zone, at night without lights, while on no emergency assignment—that *Browder* and *Dean* treat as deliberate indifference capable of defeating qualified immunity. Judge Jordan dissented, accepted the *Browder* analysis, and considered the violation obvious. The case therefore offers a published, divided lower-court decision on a federal constitutional issue, and the petition ties the disagreement to language in this Court's *Lewis* opinion. The issue has moderate national significance because a ruling could clarify the relationship between the conscience-shocking merits standard and clearly established law.

## Denial and vehicle signals

The brief in opposition exposes defects that make this a poor vehicle. It says the petitioner argued only the "obvious clarity" route below, did not cite *Browder* or *Dean* to the Eleventh Circuit, and did not argue that *Lewis* footnote 13 supplied a broader clearly established rule. The petition's principal conflict theory may therefore be unpreserved. The alleged split is also less clean than the petition suggests: *Browder* relied partly on Tenth Circuit precedent, *Dean* arose in the Fourth Circuit, and qualified-immunity notice can legitimately differ with controlling circuit law. The respondent further distinguishes deliberate use of police powers in *Browder* from Locure's use of an official truck without emergency lights or another asserted governmental advantage.

The case is fact-bound and procedurally awkward. The lower court assumed a constitutional violation and resolved only the clearly-established prong; questions about action under color of law, discretionary authority, the meaning of "intentional misuse," and the availability of state tort remedies remain entangled with the proposed rule. The dispute also comes from an interlocutory qualified-immunity posture with state claims continuing below. These complications substantially reduce the chance that four Justices will use this case to address the broader doctrine.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid Term 2025 petitions, a 4.4% grant rate for modern petitions from the Eleventh Circuit, and a 3.0% grant rate for petitions without a CVSG. The case's dissent and plausible conflict justify moving above the most conservative anchors, but the preservation problem, circuit-specific qualified-immunity precedent, narrow facts, lack of a CVSG, and absence of any relist signal keep the forecast close to the paid-petition baseline. Recent corpus priors also show that grants in the retrieved sample generally had several conference distributions; this petition has only its initial distribution as of the snapshot. A plenary grant is more plausible than a GVR if the Court intervenes, because no intervening decision or mootness development supplies a natural remand basis.

No individual vote prediction is offered because cert votes are not public and the record does not support reliable Justice-level assignments.
