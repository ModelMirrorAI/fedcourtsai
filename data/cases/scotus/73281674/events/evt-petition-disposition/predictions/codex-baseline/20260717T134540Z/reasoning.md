# Prediction: denied

I estimate a **4% probability of any grant** and predict denial. I do not predict a GVR: the petition seeks resolution of recurring legal questions, but the record does not identify an intervening ruling that would naturally support vacatur and remand.

## Questions and governing certiorari considerations

The petition asks whether the NLRA permits a bad-faith bargaining finding based only on the substance of proposals, whether the Third Circuit improperly deferred to the NLRB after *Loper Bright*, and whether the Board may award direct or foreseeable pecuniary harms. These are potentially important questions, especially the scope of the Board's remedial authority. But certiorari also requires a cleanly presented conflict or important federal question whose resolution would affect the judgment. This record has substantial vehicle problems on every question.

## Record-specific analysis

The provisioned petition and questions presented frame the case as involving three circuit conflicts. The petition says the Board and Third Circuit inferred bad faith solely from proposals that would leave the union with fewer rights than it would have without a contract. It also identifies a developed disagreement over the Board's *Thryv* remedy, with the Third, Fifth, and Sixth Circuits rejecting broad consequential damages and the Ninth Circuit permitting sufficiently tailored make-whole relief.

The provisioned union brief in opposition materially weakens that presentation:

- The Board separately found that no valid impasse existed because recent proposals had not been fully discussed, the union sought further bargaining, and the company implemented terms more favorable than its final offer. That independent unilateral-change violation supports make-whole relief even if the Court rejected the bad-faith theory.
- The bad-faith finding rested not merely on the proposals' substance but also on the company's persistence, lack of offsetting economic concessions, and premature impasse declaration. A recent D.C. Circuit decision, *District Hospital Partners, L.P. v. NLRB*, 141 F.4th 1279 (D.C. Cir. 2025), likewise approved considering a package of proposals under a totality-of-conduct test. That makes the claimed conflict with the D.C. Circuit look illusory or at least poorly presented.
- The Third Circuit described legal review as plenary and applied deference to fact-finding under the statutory substantial-evidence standard. The case therefore does not cleanly decide whether agency statutory interpretations receive deference after *Loper Bright*.
- The Third Circuit did not reach the *Thryv* question because it found the objection inadequately preserved under NLRA Section 10(e). Reaching the merits would require the Court first to resolve preservation and jurisdictional issues that are not themselves fairly included in the questions presented.
- The union brief reports that the Court denied certiorari in the cleaner *Macy's, Inc. v. NLRB*, No. 25-627, petition on June 15, 2026. That pre-snapshot development sharply reduces the inference that the Court is looking for another immediate vehicle on the same remedial issue.
- The newspaper was sold in May 2026, leaving only residual monetary liability rather than an ongoing bargaining relationship. That does not necessarily moot the case, but it further reduces its practical suitability.

The July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition from the Third Circuit, both respondents' opposition briefs, a reply, and distribution for the September 28 conference. It shows no relist, CVSG, or amicus support. The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid 2025 Term filings, a 2.9% grant rate for modern cert petitions from the Third Circuit, and only a 0.8% grant rate for petitions with no relist. The latter is not fully diagnostic before the first conference, but nothing in the docket supplies an institutional signal strong enough to overcome the vehicle defects.
