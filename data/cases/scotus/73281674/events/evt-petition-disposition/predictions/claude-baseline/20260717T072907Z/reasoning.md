# PG Publishing Co. v. NLRB, No. 25-1192 — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.03.**

## Posture

Paid petition (Term 2025), docketed April 17, 2026, from an **unreported**
Third Circuit decision (Nos. 24-2788, 24-3057, Nov. 10, 2025; rehearing denied
Jan. 14, 2026) enforcing an NLRB order that found the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette
bargained in bad faith with its newsroom Guild and unilaterally implemented
terms after prematurely declaring impasse. BIOs in opposition were filed by
both the federal respondent (Solicitor General) and the intervenor union on
June 17, 2026; petitioner replied July 2. The case was distributed for the
**September 28, 2026 long conference** — its first distribution, so no relist
signal exists yet. No CVSG. The Court previously denied the company's stay
application (No. 25A725, Jan. 7, 2026).

## Questions presented and their cert prospects

**QP1 — bad faith inferred solely from the substance of bargaining proposals.**
Petitioner claims a split with the D.C. and Ninth Circuits, which require
"additional evidence" beyond bargaining positions. The union's BIO lands two
serious blows: (i) the split is contestable — it reads the D.C. Circuit's
recent *District Hospital Partners* (2025) and the Ninth Circuit's cases as
the same totality-of-circumstances test the Third Circuit applied, and the ALJ
in fact cited conduct beyond the proposals (insistence throughout bargaining,
no economic concessions, premature impasse declaration); and (ii) there is an
**independent alternative ground** — the Board expressly found that even
absent bad faith, no valid impasse existed (App. 16a n.1: proposals with
substantive movement never fully discussed, union seeking further sessions,
implemented terms differing from the final offer). That finding alone supports
the make-whole order, which — after the company sold the paper in May 2026 —
is all that remains at stake. A grant on QP1 would likely not change the
judgment: a classic vehicle defect.

**QP2 — whether Loper Bright bars deference to the NLRB.** A genuinely live
and recurring question (the Third Circuit flagged it as open in *Alaris*, and
the Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Tenth Circuits apply independent judgment), but
this is a poor vehicle: the unpublished panel opinion never cited Loper
Bright, decided no contested question of statutory interpretation, and its
"highly deferential" language is most naturally read as substantial-evidence
review of fact-finding — which 29 U.S.C. § 160(e) itself commands. The Court
would have to excavate an implicit interpretive holding from a nonprecedential
opinion.

**QP3 — the Thryv consequential-damages remedy.** The strongest split on paper
(3d, 5th, and 6th Circuits invalidating the remedy vs. the 9th upholding it
over en banc dissents, with a Jarkesy Seventh Amendment overlay). But the
panel held it **lacked jurisdiction under § 10(e)** because the company's
bare-bones exception before the Board did not preserve the objection — so
neither the Board nor the court of appeals decided the question, and reaching
it would require the Court to resolve a preservation fight (and possibly
revisit *Woelke*) first. Decisively, the union's BIO reports that
**certiorari was denied in Macy's, Inc. v. NLRB, No. 25-627, on June 15,
2026** — a petition that squarely presented the Thryv question from the Ninth
Circuit's *Operating Engineers* decision with no preservation bar. The Court
just passed on this exact issue in a cleaner vehicle, which both signals no
current appetite and eliminates the "hold for Macy's" disposition the petition
itself suggested. (A plausible background reason: the Board's remedial regime
may change administratively, mooting the issue.)

## Base rates and adjustment

From the committed statpack (modern discretionary-cert slice,
denial-reweighted): overall grant rate ~2.5–3.0% (Term 2025: 2.5%; Term 2024:
3.0%); CA3-originating petitions grant at 2.9%; no-CVSG petitions at 3.0%;
petitions never relisted grant at 0.8% (relist status here is still unknown —
first conference is Sept. 28). This is a paid petition with capable counsel,
which pulls above the blended rate (paid petitions grant several times more
often than IFP), and the QPs connect to genuine post-Loper Bright/Jarkesy
currents, which is why it merits a probability at rather than below the base
rate despite the defects.

Pulling down from the paid-petition rate: the unpublished, nonprecedential
decision below; the § 10(e) bar on the best QP; the fresh Macy's denial on
that same QP; the independent premature-impasse ground under QP1; the
factbound character of QP2; both respondents (including the SG) opposing; the
prior stay denial; and the sale of the newspaper reducing the case to residual
backpay liability. Netting these, I land at **P(grant) ≈ 0.03** — roughly the
base rate, reflecting real but blocked legal questions in a weak vehicle. The
likeliest disposition is a straight **denial** (a GVR is implausible: no
intervening decision to GVR against, and the Macy's denial removes the natural
hold-and-GVR path).

## Big-case score

0.4 — the underlying issues (deference to the NLRB after Loper Bright;
validity of nationwide Thryv consequential-damages remedies; Seventh Amendment
limits on agency adjudication) would be significant for labor and
administrative law if decided, and the demise-of-local-newspapers backdrop
adds some public salience; but the case as it stands is a single employer's
backpay dispute resolved in an unpublished opinion, and the likely denial will
draw little attention.
