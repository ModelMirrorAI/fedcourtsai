This case presents three questions arising from an NLRB finding of bad-faith bargaining and the subsequent remedial order against the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette.

1. **Surface Bargaining:** Whether bad faith can be inferred solely from the substance of an employer's proposals.
2. **Loper Bright Application:** Whether the Third Circuit's deferential review of the NLRB's standard for bad faith violates *Loper Bright*.
3. **Consequential Damages:** Whether the NLRB's *Thryv* remedy, which authorizes consequential damages for unfair labor practices, exceeds the agency's statutory remedial authority.

Although the issues raised—particularly the application of *Loper Bright* to the NLRB and the recognized circuit split over the *Thryv* consequential damages remedy—are of high national importance, the petition suffers from severe vehicle flaws.

Most notably, the Union's Brief in Opposition points out that the petitioner failed to raise the consequential damages argument before the Board. Under Section 10(e) of the NLRA (29 U.S.C. § 160(e)), this failure renders the third question jurisdictionally barred from judicial review. The Supreme Court routinely denies petitions where a threshold jurisdictional defect prevents it from reaching the merits of the split.

Furthermore, the first and second questions are highly fact-bound. The BIO argues that the Board did not rely solely on the bargaining proposals to find bad faith, but instead evaluated the totality of the circumstances, including table conduct and the premature declaration of an impasse. Because the Third Circuit's decision rests on a substantial-evidence review of these factual findings, the case is a poor vehicle for a clean legal holding on *Loper Bright*'s application to NLRB statutory interpretation.

Given these significant vehicle problems, alongside the generally low base rate for cert grants (around 3%), the petition is highly likely to be denied.
