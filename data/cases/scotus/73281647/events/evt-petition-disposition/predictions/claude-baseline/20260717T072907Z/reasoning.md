# Pena v. City of Los Angeles, No. 25-1163 — cert disposition prediction

**Prediction: deny is the most likely single outcome; P(any grant, GVR included) = 0.22.**

## The legal question

An LAPD SWAT team destroyed the contents of Carlos Pena's North Hollywood print
shop (~$60,000, uninsured) with 25 rounds of tear gas while trying to flush out
a fugitive who had barricaded himself inside after assaulting Pena; the fugitive
had already escaped. The parties agree the police acted reasonably. The Ninth
Circuit (158 F.4th 1033, published, with a Friedland concurrence resting on the
search-and-arrest privilege) held that "public necessity" is an exception to the
Takings Clause, so no compensable taking occurred. The questions presented are
(1) whether the government is exempt from Takings Clause liability when officers
intentionally destroy an innocent person's property while apprehending a
fugitive, and (2) whether "public necessity" is an exception to the Takings
Clause.

## Why this petition is far stronger than the base rate

The Term-2025 estimated grant rate for cert petitions is ~2.5% (statpack), ~3%
for CA9-originating petitions on the reweighted cut. This petition sits well
above that anchor:

- **The Court invited this percolation.** In Baker v. City of McKinney, 145
  S. Ct. 11 (2024), Justices Sotomayor and Gorsuch wrote that the identical
  question "raise[s] an important question that has divided the courts of
  appeals" but "would benefit from further percolation." Since then the Ninth
  Circuit (this case) squarely adopted a necessity exception, the Seventh
  reaffirmed its police-power rule (Hadley v. City of South Bend, 154 F.4th
  549), and the Eleventh rejected an emergency exception (Alford v. Walton
  County, 159 F.4th 844). The petition presents exactly the record the Baker
  statement asked for.
- **A genuine, now three-way doctrinal split**: police-power exemption (7th,
  10th, Fed. Cir.) vs. necessity exception (5th, 9th) vs. search-and-arrest
  privilege (6th), with the 4th, 5th, 6th, and 11th rejecting a categorical
  police-power exemption. The reasoning split is real even though outcomes on
  law-enforcement facts have so far aligned.
- **Excellent vehicle**: paid petition, expert Supreme Court counsel (Institute
  for Justice), published opinion plus a concurrence disagreeing on rationale,
  stipulated facts (reasonableness conceded), a pure legal question with no
  qualified-immunity or factual tangle, and a companion petition (Hadley, No.
  25-1158) filed the same day giving the Court a paired vehicle.
- **Five cert-stage amicus briefs** (Pacific Legal Foundation, Small Property
  Owners of San Francisco/Owners Counsel of America, takings professors Ely/
  Mahoney/Saxer, the Lech family, Chief Tiderington) — a strong salience
  signal; the relist-count statpack cut shows petitions that attract serious
  conference attention (2+ relists) grant at 22–34% versus 0.8% for
  never-relisted ones.
- The current Court has repeatedly taken takings cases (Knick, Cedar Point,
  Tyler, Sheetz, DeVillier), and the sympathetic innocent-owner facts fit its
  property-rights docket.

## Why deny nonetheless remains the modal outcome

- **The Court has denied this exact issue three times**: Lech (2020), Baker
  (2024), Slaybaugh (2025). Only two justices have publicly signaled interest;
  a grant needs four, and the Baker statement's authors themselves voted to
  deny there.
- **The BIO's strongest point is outcome alignment**: no circuit has actually
  awarded compensation for damage from concededly reasonable law-enforcement
  action. Alford is distinguishable as a COVID beach-closure occupation case.
  The Court often tolerates a reasoning split until outcomes actually diverge.
- **Vehicle wrinkles the BIO raises**: the stipulated-judgment posture, and a
  state-law property-interest argument (Customer Co. v. City of Sacramento)
  not pressed below — thin, but the kind of thing used to justify waiting for
  Hadley or a later case.
- **No relist signal yet.** The case was distributed June 24 for the September
  28, 2026 long conference; the classic pre-grant signal (repeated relists)
  cannot be observed at this snapshot, so the probability must stay tethered
  to priors rather than conference behavior.
- A realistic split-the-difference path exists: the Court grants the companion
  Hadley (or another vehicle) and holds this petition, which would end in a
  GVR (still a grant on the binary axis) — or denies both with another
  statement. I fold the hold-then-GVR path into the grant-side probability.

## Calibration

Roughly: P(the Court takes the issue while this petition is pending, via this
case or the Hadley pair) ≈ 30%; conditional on that, this docket ends granted/
consolidated/GVR'd rather than denied ≈ 75%. That yields ≈ 0.22, an order of
magnitude above the ~2.5% Term base rate but still deny-side, reflecting three
prior denials of the same question and the absence of any relist signal yet.
Predicted disposition: **denied**, with `probability` 0.22 expressing P(any
grant including GVR).

No votes predicted: cert votes are not disclosed for denials, so per-justice
vote predictions are not meaningful for this event.
