# Prediction: denied

I assign a 36% probability to a grant-side disposition and predict denial. This is far above the ordinary cert petition, but denial remains more likely for this particular docket.

## Question and governing considerations

The petition asks whether the Takings Clause applies when law-enforcement officers intentionally destroy an innocent third party's property while trying to apprehend a fugitive, and whether public necessity is an exception to the compensation requirement. At the cert stage, the central considerations are the importance and recurrence of the federal question, conflict or disarray among lower courts, the suitability of this record, and whether at least four Justices are ready to intervene.

The merits theories reflected in the filings are sharply opposed. The petition treats the deliberate destruction of property for a concededly public purpose as a compensable taking and argues that necessity historically excused individual tort liability rather than the government's constitutional duty to compensate. The City responds that lawful, reasonable criminal-law-enforcement damage is consequential damage outside the Takings Clause; it invokes public necessity, the search-and-arrest privilege, and the distinction between eminent domain and police power.

## Case-specific signals

The provisioned petition describes a thirteen-hour standoff after an armed fugitive forced Carlos Pena from his print shop. Police deployed twenty-five tear-gas rounds into connected businesses, allegedly destroying about $60,000 of equipment and inventory; the fugitive escaped. Pena concedes that the operation was lawful and reasonable, and the alleged loss was uninsured. The district court entered judgment for the City after denying Pena's summary-judgment motion, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed under a public-safety necessity rule. Judge Friedland concurred on the distinct ground that the search-and-arrest privilege limited the property interest itself. These cleanly presented, undisputed facts make the constitutional allocation question concrete, while the stipulated judgment and alternative doctrinal routes give the City some vehicle arguments.

Several grant signals are unusually strong:

- In *Baker v. City of McKinney*, Justices Sotomayor and Gorsuch called the issue important and complex while explaining that it would benefit from more lower-court percolation. The petition says that the Seventh, Ninth, and Eleventh Circuits have since added decisions, and it was filed alongside the related *Hadley* petition from the Seventh Circuit.
- The lower courts use materially different rules: categorical police-power exclusions, public-necessity defenses, and search-and-arrest privileges. Those rules may have consequences outside SWAT operations.
- Five amicus groups appear on the snapshot, the petition is represented by experienced public-interest counsel, and the concrete burden on an innocent small-business owner makes the issue salient.

The main obstacle is the City's forceful claim that the asserted split is not outcome-determinative. According to the brief in opposition, every identified federal case involving reasonable criminal-law-enforcement destruction has rejected compensation. The Eleventh Circuit authority involved emergency beach-access restrictions rather than a police operation. Thus the Court could again prefer a case in which competing approaches actually produce different judgments. The Court also denied review in *Lech*, *Baker*, and *Slaybaugh*; only two Justices publicly signaled interest in *Baker*. The existence of a companion petition also creates selection risk for this docket even if four Justices decide to take the broader issue.
