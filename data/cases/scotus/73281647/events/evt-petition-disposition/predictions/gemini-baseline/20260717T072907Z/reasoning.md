# Reasoning for Certiorari Prediction

**Case:** *Carlos Pena v. City of Los Angeles*
**Issue:** Whether the government is exempt from liability under the Takings Clause when law enforcement officers intentionally destroy an innocent person’s property in the course of attempting to apprehend a fugitive, and whether the "public necessity" doctrine acts as an exception.

## Analysis of the Record and Prior Precedents

The petition raises a direct question about the Takings Clause, specifically regarding the destruction of an innocent third party's property by law enforcement. The petition explicitly notes that Justices Sotomayor and Gorsuch recognized this exact issue as an "important question that has divided the courts of appeals" in their statement regarding the denial of certiorari in *Baker v. City of McKinney*, 145 S. Ct. 11 (2024). At the time, they suggested the question "would benefit from further percolation in the lower courts prior to this Court’s intervention."

Since *Baker*, that requested percolation has occurred. The Ninth Circuit weighed in on November 4, 2025, in *Pena v. City of Los Angeles* (the case currently on petition), and the Seventh Circuit reaffirmed its previous approach in *Amy Hadley v. City of South Bend, Indiana* (October 7, 2025). The addition of these two major circuit opinions solidifies the "doctrinal disarray" and split in authority that the Justices previously flagged.

## Signals and Base Rates

While the base rate for paid cert petitions is quite low (around 3.0%), this specific petition exhibits exceptionally strong signals for a grant:
1. **Amicus Support:** The petition has attracted five amicus briefs at the cert stage, including filings from property rights organizations (Small Property Owners of San Francisco, Pacific Legal Foundation), a group of law professors, a former police chief, and Leo Lech (the plaintiff in a previously denied petition on the exact same issue, *Lech v. Jackson*). A high number of cert-stage amicus briefs strongly correlates with a cert grant, as it signals to the Court that the issue has high salience and broad interest.
2. **Explicit Prior Call for Percolation:** Having two Justices explicitly state on the record that an issue is important and divided, but needs further percolation, is a highly probative signal. Now that two more circuits have issued published opinions on the issue, the condition for their desired "intervention" has been met.
3. **Distribution:** The case has been distributed for the long conference (September 28, 2026). While cases granted out of the long conference are a minority, the combination of a fully briefed, high-salience split issue makes it a prime candidate. 

## Conclusion

Given the explicit prior interest from at least two Justices, the subsequent development of the circuit split (fulfilling the call for percolation), and the unusually strong amicus support at the cert stage, the likelihood of a grant is extremely high compared to the base rate. 

**Prediction:** Granted (Probability: 85%)
