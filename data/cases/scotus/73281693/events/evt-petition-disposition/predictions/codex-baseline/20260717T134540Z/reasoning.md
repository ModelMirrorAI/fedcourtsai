# Prediction: denied (29% probability of any grant)

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether an employer defeats a Title VII religious-accommodation claim by showing a reasonable basis for expecting undue hardship, or instead must prove that the requested accommodation would actually impose undue hardship. Under *Groff v. DeJoy*, an employer must show a burden that is substantial in the overall context of its business. At the certiorari stage, the principal considerations are whether the decision below squarely conflicts with other courts of appeals, whether the recurring question warrants review now, and whether this record cleanly presents it. Four Justices are required to grant.

## Record and grant signals

The provisioned July 17 snapshot shows a paid petition from a published, final Ninth Circuit judgment. The respondent initially waived a response, but the Court requested one after the petition was distributed; the response is not due until August 3. A requested response is a meaningful indication of interest, although it is not a relist or a grant. The docket also reflects roughly a dozen amicus filings, including briefs from states and religious-liberty organizations. Experienced Supreme Court counsel presents a facially important and recurring statutory question.

The petition identifies recent decisions from six circuits. The Third and Seventh Circuits expressly reject good faith as an independent defense and require proof of undue hardship, while the First Circuit uses reasonableness language when assessing an employer's reliance on contemporaneous medical evidence. The Seventh Circuit's *Kluge* majority and dissent make the disagreement especially visible. That gives the petition substantially more force than an ordinary error-correction request.

## Reasons to expect denial

The case is not a clean example of an employer prevailing merely because it made a reasonable mistake. The pre-decision Ninth Circuit opinion says that merely conceivable or hypothetical hardship is insufficient and that the risk must be realistic. It affirmed on a record that included unrebutted medical evidence, close public contact by firefighter/EMTs, 46 exemption requests among 192 firefighters, operational risks, a threatened correctional-facility contract, and uninsured liability exposure. The panel characterized these as substantial costs in October 2021, not simply a good-faith belief later proven wrong. The respondent therefore has a strong argument that the petition attacks language about contemporaneous risk assessment while overlooking the court's actual-hardship finding.

The comparator cases also arise from materially different records: *Smith* involved a firefighter with a very small chance of being called into fire suppression, *Naylor* found scant evidence of actual reputational harm, and *Kluge* found factual disputes about the effects of an accommodation. Those differences may let the Court regard the asserted 3-3 split as premature or fact-sensitive. The COVID-19 setting, changing public-health evidence, and the unusually large share of the workforce seeking exemptions further complicate the vehicle. The Court decided *Groff* only three Terms ago and may prefer additional percolation or a case in which the alleged good-faith rule is unmistakably outcome-determinative.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a roughly 3.1% grant rate across resolved modern discretionary-cert petitions, 3.0% for petitions originating in the Ninth Circuit, and 5.4% for paid petitions in the 2025 Term. This petition deserves a large upward adjustment for the requested response, published judgment, experienced counsel, extensive amicus support, and plausible circuit disagreement. The vehicle weakness and record-specific alternative ground still make denial more likely than grant. I assign a 29% probability to any grant and predict a straight denial as the most likely disposition. A GVR is unlikely because no intervening authority identified in the pre-decision record supplies an obvious remand basis.

The stakes score of 0.67 reflects the nationwide importance of Title VII accommodation standards and the case's religious-liberty salience, discounted because the dispute is statutory and embedded in a pandemic-specific evidentiary record.
