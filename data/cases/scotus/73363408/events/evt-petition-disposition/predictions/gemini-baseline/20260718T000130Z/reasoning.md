# Reasoning

**Case:** *Dan Schmidt v. City of Omro, Wisconsin, et al.* (No. 25-1293)
**Event:** Petition Disposition

**Legal Question & Governing Standard:**
The petitioner, appearing *pro se*, asks whether the 14th Amendment requires a state trial court judge to explicitly inform a party of their right to appeal in a civil tort case at the time of dismissal, and whether an "equitable exception" exists when the court dismisses a case "with prejudice" assuming the party understands the term. The Supreme Court grants certiorari to resolve circuit splits on important federal questions or to review state court decisions that conflict with federal law. It rarely grants fact-bound, error-correction petitions, particularly in civil matters involving state procedural rules.

**Facts & Posture:**
This is a paid petition, but the petitioner is proceeding *pro se* (the attorney of record is the petitioner himself). The respondents waived their right to respond on May 27, 2026. The petition has been distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference without any call for a response (CVSG or otherwise). 

**Reasoning:**
The likelihood of a grant here is exceedingly low. First, the issue presented—whether civil litigants must be specifically advised by the trial court of their appellate rights—is novel but does not implicate a circuit split or a recognized issue of national importance; it is an attempt to seek error correction regarding state court civil procedure. Second, the petitioner is *pro se*, and the respondents have waived their right to respond. A waiver of response is a strong signal that the respondent views the petition as frivolous and not warranting the Court's resources. The Supreme Court almost never grants a petition without first calling for a response. Given the base rate for modern discretionary cert petitions (around 3%), a paid *pro se* petition with a waived response and no compelling federal question has a probability of grant that approaches zero.

**Conclusion:**
The petition will almost certainly be denied.
