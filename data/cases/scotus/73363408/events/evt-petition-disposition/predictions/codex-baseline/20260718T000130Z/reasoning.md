# Prediction: denied

I assign a 0.25% probability to any grant and predict denial. The prediction uses the July 17, 2026 snapshot, the extracted questions presented, and the complete 42-page petition. No brief in opposition was provisioned; the docket instead records one respondent's waiver of a response.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether the Fourteenth Amendment obliges a state trial judge to tell a civil tort litigant about the right to appeal when dismissing claims, and whether saying only that dismissal is "with prejudice" can excuse the lack of an express notice. Certiorari would ordinarily require an important federal question, a developed conflict, and a suitable vehicle. The underlying constitutional law is unfavorable to the requested rule: the Federal Constitution does not itself guarantee appellate review. Once a state supplies an appeal, due process and equal protection constrain arbitrary or discriminatory barriers, but the Court's strongest access-to-appeal cases concern criminal defendants or exceptional civil interests such as termination of parental rights, not an affirmative judicial-advisement duty in an ordinary damages action.

## Case-specific considerations

Several features push this petition well below even the low certiorari base rate:

- The petition identifies no square split over a constitutional duty to advise civil litigants of appeal deadlines. Its survey mainly shows that civil litigants are generally expected to protect their own appellate rights.
- The case is a poor vehicle. The Wisconsin appeal was dismissed as untimely after litigation over whether an order dismissing two defendants with prejudice was final. State-law rules about finality, the meaning of "with prejudice," later reconsideration proceedings, and the petitioner's delayed notice of appeal would complicate or independently limit review.
- The requested rule is broad but the record is highly fact-bound. The petitioner is pro se, yet the petition does not establish a constitutionally relevant classification or denial of appellate access comparable to the wealth barrier in *M. L. B. v. S. L. J.*
- The asserted prejudice depends partly on the merits of a separate Wisconsin insurance-law dispute over third-party-beneficiary status, further weakening the vehicle.
- There is no institutional grant signal in the snapshot. The petition is paid and has been distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference, but it has no relist and no CVSG. A response waiver is not affirmative support for review.

The petition invokes *Hunter v. United States* as a related pending criminal appeal-waiver case. CourtListener shows that *Hunter* was decided on June 18, 2026, before this snapshot. The Court held that a federal criminal appeal waiver is unenforceable only when enforcement would produce a high-bar miscarriage of justice; it also held that the sentencing judge's erroneous statement that the defendant could appeal did not modify an otherwise knowing and voluntary waiver. That decision arose from federal criminal procedure and the judiciary's supervisory role, not a Fourteenth Amendment duty imposed on state civil judges. It therefore does not create the proposed civil notice rule or materially cure this petition's lack of conflict and vehicle problems.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 2.5% grant rate for all resolved 2025 Term cert petitions and 5.36% for paid petitions, but the much more relevant zero-relist bucket grants only about 0.8%. This petition's absence of a split, ordinary private dispute, weak constitutional foundation, and procedural complications warrant a substantial downward adjustment from that bucket. A routine denial, most likely on the first order list following conference, is overwhelmingly likely. I do not predict individual votes because cert votes are not public and the record gives no reliable justice-specific signal.

The personal property and health allegations are serious to the petitioner, but the case's likely national significance is modest because the proposed issue is narrow, the vehicle is poor, and the underlying dispute is localized.
