# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with a 1.5% probability of any grant (including a plenary grant, summary reversal, or GVR).

## Question and governing law

The petition asks whether 42 U.S.C. § 1981 permits a Black restaurant patron to recover for allegedly discriminatory enforcement of a dress code when she remained, received the meal and service, and completed the transaction. Section 1981(b) protects the making and performance of contracts and enjoyment of their benefits, privileges, terms, and conditions. The Fifth Circuit's published restaurant cases require an actual contractual impairment rather than discriminatory treatment in the abstract. In *Hager v. Brinker Texas*, 102 F.4th 692 (5th Cir. 2024), the court allowed a claim to proceed where a party allegedly was seated but then denied service and left. The panel here treated the materially different fact that Nwosu stayed and completed her meal as defeating the protected-contractual-interest element.

The petition invokes the Sixth Circuit's “markedly hostile” commercial-service framework from *Christian v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc.* and *Keck v. Graham Hotel Systems, Inc.* That line of authority supplies a plausible doctrinal contrast, but the petition does not show a clean, outcome-determinative circuit split involving a customer who received the purchased service in full. *Keck*, for example, concerned repeated failure to consummate a wedding-services contract. The Fourth and Second Circuit authorities cited in the petition likewise do not establish a square conflict on these facts.

## Record and vehicle

The provisioned petition and its appended opinions show evidence favorable to Nwosu: the district court found a genuine issue on discriminatory intent; she says the hostess placed an itchy shawl on her without warning or consent; and she offered evidence that similarly dressed white patrons were not treated alike. Those facts make the alleged treatment serious and supply the best argument that discriminatory enforcement deprived her of equal contractual benefits even though she stayed.

The summary-reversal theory is nevertheless weak. The petition disputes whether her dress violated the policy, how the shawl was placed, and how long she stayed. But the Fifth Circuit's stated rationale was that she identified no cognizable contractual benefit denied to her, received ordinary service and silverware, and remained after the incident. The Court therefore could view the contested details as immaterial to the legal ground of decision, unlike the material summary-judgment errors corrected in *Tolan v. Cotton*.

The vehicle has few institutional grant signals in the provisioned record. The decision is an unpublished per curiam opinion; rehearing drew no requested poll; there is no lower-court dissent; no amicus filing, CVSG, or relist appears; and no brief in opposition was provisioned. The case was newly distributed for the September 28 conference, so the absence of a relist is not independently decisive, but there is presently no positive agenda signal. The petition is also largely fact-bound and at times overstates the lower courts—for example, the panel did not hold generally that every § 1981 restaurant plaintiff must literally be forced to leave.
