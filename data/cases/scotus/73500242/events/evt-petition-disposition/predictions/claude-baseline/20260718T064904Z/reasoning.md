# Nwosu v. 1600 West Loop South, L.L.C. (No. 25-1340) — cert disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(any grant, GVR included) ≈ 0.015.**

## The case

Blessing Nwosu, an African American woman, sued the owner of the Bloom & Bee
restaurant (Post Oak Hotel, Houston) under 42 U.S.C. § 1981 after a hostess
placed a wool shawl on her shoulders — she says forcibly and without warning —
for a claimed dress-code violation, while (she alleges) comparably dressed
non-Black patrons were undisturbed. The Southern District of Texas granted
summary judgment (Judge Hanen, Feb. 21, 2025): she raised a genuine fact issue
on discriminatory intent, but as a matter of law the conduct did not concern an
"enumerated activity" under § 1981(b) because she received full service and the
dress code was itself a term of the dining contract. The Fifth Circuit affirmed
in an **unpublished, non-precedential per curiam** (Barksdale, Willett, Duncan;
Dec. 12, 2025), relying on Hager v. Brinker and Dunaway: the alleged
discrimination did not rise "to the level that would force [her] to leave," and
she remained about four and a half hours. Rehearing was denied Jan. 23, 2026.

## What the petition asks

1. **Summary reversal** for a "flagrant violation of the summary judgment
   standard" (Tolan v. Cotton), pointing to the panel's acceptance of the
   respondent's account (quiet approach, dress-code explanation, ~4.5-hour
   stay) over her deposition testimony (forcible placement, no explanation,
   1–2 hours).
2. **Plenary review** of whether the Fifth Circuit's effective
   "forced-to-leave" requirement conflicts with § 1981(b)'s text ("enjoyment of
   all benefits, privileges, terms, and conditions") and with other circuits'
   standards (6th Cir. Christian "markedly hostile" test; 2d Cir. Lizardo; 4th
   Cir. Eddy).

## Why denial is the strong call

- **Base rates.** Term-2025 paid petitions grant at ~5.4% (statpack per-fee-class
  detail); CA5-originating petitions ~4.8%; petitions never relisted ~0.8%. This
  petition sits well below the paid-petition average on every observable signal.
- **Fact-bound question presented.** The QP is written as a one-party narrative
  ("whether an African American restaurant patron who was singled out and had a
  wool shawl forcibly placed on her ... is barred from recovering ...") — a
  classic error-correction framing, not a cleanly presented legal rule. The
  Court does not sit to correct case-specific summary-judgment errors, and it
  says so routinely.
- **Unpublished, non-precedential decision below.** The Court rarely grants
  plenary review of unpublished per curiams; the decision creates no binding
  circuit law, which also undercuts the split's ripeness.
- **The split is real but shallow as presented.** There is genuine daylight
  among circuits on § 1981 standards for retail/restaurant customers (5th
  Cir.'s enumerated-activity / forced-to-leave approach vs. the 6th Circuit's
  "markedly hostile" test), and the Court has never squarely defined § 1981's
  contours for a completed restaurant meal. But the petition develops the
  conflict in two pages, and the vehicle is poor: petitioner concedes she was
  seated, served without rudeness, and stayed for the meal, and the district
  court alternatively held the dress code was a contract term she was bound
  by — so the outcome may not turn on the circuit disagreement at all.
- **Summary-reversal ask is a long shot.** Tolan-style summary reversals are a
  handful per Term and typically involve qualified immunity or stark
  record-versus-opinion conflicts. The disputed facts here (whether the shawl
  was "forced," 1–2 vs. 4.5 hours) matter less under the panel's legal standard
  than in Tolan, where the standard itself turned on the disputed facts —
  and the panel's ~4.5-hour figure matches the district court's record citation
  to a 2:00 pm arrival and 6:51 pm check close.
- **No grant-side signals.** Solo civil-rights practitioner as counsel of
  record; no amici; no CVSG; first distribution (July 15, 2026) is to the
  September 28, 2026 long conference, where the overwhelming mass of petitions
  is denied; no brief in opposition appears on the docket (response was due
  July 1), and the Court virtually never grants without at least calling for a
  response. Compare the corpus's recent granted petitions (Monsanto, Petersen,
  Viramontes): elite Supreme Court counsel, multiple distributions/relists,
  amicus support, high salience — none present here.

Residual probability (~1.5%) covers a call-for-response followed by an
unexpected relist path, or a summary disposition; there is no plausible GVR
predicate (no intervening decision is identified), so nearly all grant mass is
plenary/summary reversal, and it is small.
