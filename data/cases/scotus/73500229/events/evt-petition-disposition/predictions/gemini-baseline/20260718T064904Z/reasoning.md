# Reasoning

## Legal Question
Whether the Fourteenth Amendment or the Supremacy Clause (via the FDCA's Emergency Use Authorization provisions and the PREP Act) preempts a State and its actors from mandating the administration of an investigational drug (specifically, the COVID-19 vaccine) as a condition of continued employment for healthcare professionals.

## Governing Standard & Facts
The petitioners are former employees of Shriners Hospitals in Washington State who were terminated for refusing to comply with a state COVID-19 vaccine mandate issued by Governor Jay Inslee. They allege that the mandate violated the Supremacy Clause because the vaccines were available only under an Emergency Use Authorization (EUA), which under 21 U.S.C. § 360bbb-3 requires that individuals be informed of "the option to accept or refuse administration of the product." They argue that a state mandate penalizing refusal conflicts with this federal consent framework.

The District Court dismissed the claims under Rule 12(b)(6), and the Ninth Circuit affirmed, relying on *Jacobson v. Massachusetts* and its own recent precedents (*Curtis v. Inslee*, *Health Freedom Defense Fund v. Carvalho*), holding that state actors can mandate vaccines under their police powers and that such mandates survive rational basis review.

## Analysis & Probability
The base rate for a paid cert petition originating from the Ninth Circuit is approximately 3.0%. However, the docket indicates that the Court requested a response on July 14, 2026, after the respondents had initially waived their right to respond. A requested response is a strong signal that at least one Justice (likely one of the more conservative Justices who have expressed skepticism toward broad administrative COVID-19 mandates, such as Justice Thomas, Alito, or Gorsuch) found the petition interesting enough to warrant a closer look.

Despite this signal, the probability of a grant remains very low (estimated at 10%). The petitioners' core legal theory—that the FDCA's EUA informed-consent requirement preempts state or employer vaccine mandates—has been raised repeatedly and rejected uniformly across the circuits during the pandemic (e.g., the Seventh Circuit in *Klaassen v. Trs. of Ind. Univ.* and the Sixth Circuit in *Norris v. Stanley*). Those courts have consistently held that the EUA statute regulates medical providers administering the drug, requiring them to inform patients of their right to refuse, but does not constrain the ability of state or private employers to impose employment consequences for that refusal. 

Because there is no circuit split on this question, and because the petition involves retrospective damages claims over a rescinded pandemic-era policy, the Supreme Court is highly unlikely to grant plenary review. The requested response suggests some internal debate or a potential dissent from the denial of certiorari, but not the four votes necessary to grant.
