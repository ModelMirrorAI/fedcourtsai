# Roberts v. Ferguson (No. 25-1327) — cert petition disposition

**Prediction: denied. P(grant, GVR included) = 0.05.**

## The case

Twelve former employees of Shriners Hospitals for Children (Spokane) sued
Washington's Governor (Inslee, in his individual capacity; Ferguson now
substituted in the caption) and Shriners under § 1983 over Washington's
Proclamation 21-14.1, which conditioned healthcare licensure/employment on
accepting a COVID-19 vaccine then available only under Emergency Use
Authorization. The Eastern District of Washington dismissed under Rule
12(b)(6) (Feb/Mar 2024); the Ninth Circuit affirmed in an **unpublished
memorandum** on December 10, 2025 (No. 24-1949), relying on its published
companion *Curtis v. Inslee*, 154 F.4th 678 (9th Cir. Oct. 6, 2025).

The questions presented recast a federal-preemption theory in Fourteenth
Amendment terms: (1) whether a state may mandate, on penalty of losing
benefits, a drug exempt from 21 U.S.C. § 355(a) when the FDCA (§ 360bbb-3's
"option to accept or refuse" condition, § 355(i)(4), 42 U.S.C. § 289)
requires voluntary informed consent; and (2) whether such a mandate is
consistent with the Fourteenth Amendment when the PREP Act's immunity
clause (42 U.S.C. § 247d-6d(a)(1)) extinguishes tort remedies for injury.

## Why denial is the strong favorite

1. **Genre track record.** This is the EUA-informed-consent / anti-mandate
   preemption theory the Court has declined to take up many times since
   2021 (*Klaassen*, *Dr. A. v. Hochul*, *Johnson v. Brown*, *We The
   Patriots*, and successors). No circuit has accepted it; the petition
   itself frames the fight as error in *Curtis*'s "clinically identical"
   reasoning (EUA-labeled Pfizer product vs. licensed Comirnaty) — an
   intra-circuit, error-correction argument, not a circuit split. Sup. Ct.
   R. 10 cuts hard against it.
2. **Weak vehicle.** Unpublished memorandum below; 12(b)(6) posture; the
   state defendant is sued in his **individual capacity** (qualified
   immunity is an obvious alternate ground); the principal private
   respondent (Shriners) raises a state-action problem; the proclamation
   has been rescinded, so only retrospective damages remain. The QPs'
   Fourteenth Amendment framing sits awkwardly on what is substantively a
   Supremacy Clause argument — a mismatch a BIO will exploit.
3. **Counsel and presentation.** A solo out-of-state practitioner, not a
   cert-stage specialist; no amicus support visible on the docket.
4. **Base rates.** From the committed statpack (live/historical slice,
   denial-reweighted): paid petitions granted ~5.4% in OT2025 (6.9%
   OT2024, 8.0% OT2023); modern cert petitions from CA9 ~3.0% granted;
   zero-relist petitions ~0.8%. The statpack's salience-band table was not
   present in the committed statpack.md, so I anchored on the paid-class
   rate.

## The one genuine upward signal

Both respondent groups **waived** response (June 11 / June 29), the
petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 long conference, and on
**July 14, 2026 the Court requested a response** (due August 13). A CFR
means someone in the building wants the state's answer — it is the classic
pre-relist signal and empirically lifts a paid petition's grant odds
several-fold above baseline. It is why I sit at ~5% rather than the ~1–3%
the genre and the CA9/no-relist cuts would otherwise suggest. But most
CFR'd petitions are still denied, and in this genre the historical pattern
has been CFR → possible relist → denial (sometimes with a statement or
dissent from denial by Justices Thomas or Gorsuch). No intervening decision
of this Court makes a GVR plausible, so I put essentially all non-denial
mass on a plenary grant.

## Net

Anchor ~5–6% (paid class), multiply up for the CFR, multiply down harder
for the genre's unbroken denial record, the unpublished decision below, the
vehicle defects, and the absence of a split: **P(grant) ≈ 0.05; predicted
disposition: denied.**
