# Prediction: denied

I estimate a **9% probability of any grant** and predict denial. The procedural signal is meaningfully better than an ordinary petition, but the Rule 10 case for review is weak and the same theory has been rejected in more than one circuit.

## Question and posture

The petition asks whether Washington could condition healthcare employment and professional practice on receiving a COVID-19 vaccine then available under an Emergency Use Authorization. It argues that the mandate conflicted with the FDCA's informed-consent framework and that PREP Act immunity made vaccination an unconstitutional surrender of access to courts. The Ninth Circuit affirmed Rule 12(b)(6) dismissals in an unpublished memorandum, treating its published decision in *Curtis v. Inslee*, 154 F.4th 678 (9th Cir. 2025), as controlling.

The certiorari standard, rather than the ultimate merits alone, is decisive. Rule 10 favors a developed circuit conflict, an important unsettled federal question, or a departure from this Court's precedent, presented in a clean vehicle. Four Justices must vote to grant.

## Signals favoring review

- Both respondents initially waived a response, but the Court requested one on July 14 after the petition was distributed. That is a genuine selection signal: at least one Justice or chambers saw enough potential interest to require adversarial briefing rather than deny on the waiver record.
- The questions concern bodily autonomy, public-health authority, federal preemption, and the legal consequences of PREP Act immunity. Those issues would be nationally consequential if the Court reached them.
- The petition tries to distinguish *Curtis* by framing the issue as conflict preemption and an unconstitutional condition rather than only as a privately enforceable statutory right.

## Signals favoring denial

- The petition identifies no circuit split. More importantly, the Sixth Circuit's published decision in *Norris v. Stanley*, 73 F.4th 431 (6th Cir. 2023), rejected the same claim that 21 U.S.C. § 360bbb-3 preempts a state-employer vaccine policy. It reasoned that a mandate with employment consequences neither prevents an individual from receiving informed-consent information nor eliminates the practical option to refuse. That agreement across circuits cuts sharply against review.
- The statutory text is an awkward fit for the petition's theory. Section 360bbb-3(e) requires that recipients be informed of the option to accept or refuse; it does not expressly guarantee refusal without employment consequences. Section 360bbb-3(l) withholds compulsory authority from the federal Secretary, not expressly from states or private employers. The FDCA's public-enforcement rule also creates remedial and cause-of-action obstacles.
- *Curtis* is published, factually close, and already rejects the asserted statutory and constitutional sources of rights. The unpublished judgment here chiefly applies that precedent. The Court ordinarily reviews the precedential rule in a clean lead case, not a follow-on memorandum disposition burdened by state-action, private-employer, pleading, remedy, and property-interest questions.
- The second question depends on treating PREP Act immunity as a compelled waiver of court access. Immunity changes the available cause of action by statute; it does not naturally operate as an individual waiver exacted by the state. That makes the proposed constitutional rule novel and vehicle-dependent.
- The challenged 2021 policy is no longer an urgent prospective mandate. This damages-centered employment dispute is therefore a less attractive vehicle for clarifying emergency public-health power.
- No brief in opposition was provisioned because the response was only requested and is due August 13, 2026. The forecast therefore lacks respondents' vehicle and merits arguments, which are likely to sharpen the existing defects.

## Calibration

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 3.0% grant rate for modern petitions from the Ninth Circuit. Petitions with no relist or CVSG signal are lower still in the aggregate. I raise the estimate to 9% because the Court specifically requested a response and because related litigation may prompt a hold or coordinated consideration. I do not raise it further because the precise preemption argument lacks a split, published circuit authority points the same way, and this is a follow-on vehicle.

The most likely disposition is a straightforward denial. There is no apparent intervening ruling that makes a GVR more likely than plenary review, so the 9% grant probability is principally the chance of a plenary grant rather than a GVR.

The `big_case_score` of 0.64 reflects potentially broad consequences for emergency-use medical products and public-health mandates, discounted for the retrospective posture, the case-specific employment setting, and the substantial chance that any ruling would remain narrow.
