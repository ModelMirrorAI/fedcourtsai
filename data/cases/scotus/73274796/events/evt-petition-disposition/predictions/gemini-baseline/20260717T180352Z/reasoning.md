# Reasoning for Renteria v. New Mexico Office of the Superintendent of Insurance (No. 25-113)

**Background:**
Petitioners are members of a Health Care Sharing Ministry (HCSM) who challenged a cease-and-desist order by the New Mexico Office of the Superintendent of Insurance. The Tenth Circuit, applying *Employment Division v. Smith*, denied a preliminary injunction on Free Exercise grounds, finding the state's insurance laws to be "neutral" and "generally applicable," despite petitioners' arguments regarding secular exemptions and hostility.

**CVSG and Hold Recommendation:**
The Court called for the views of the Solicitor General (CVSG). On May 26, 2026, the Solicitor General filed an amicus brief recommending that the Court **hold** this petition pending the outcome of *St. Mary Catholic Parish v. Roy* (No. 25-581). The Court had already granted certiorari in *St. Mary* on April 20, 2026. 

*St. Mary*—also arising from the Tenth Circuit—presents a nearly identical legal question regarding what it takes to show a lack of "general applicability" under *Smith* when secular exemptions exist. Given that the SG explicitly recommended a hold because of the overlap with *St. Mary*, the Supreme Court is highly likely to hold *Renteria*.

**Ultimate Disposition:**
A "hold" is not a final disposition; it defers the disposition until the primary case (*St. Mary*) is resolved. If the Court rules in favor of the religious petitioners in *St. Mary* (narrowing or overturning the general applicability standard in *Smith*), it will almost certainly grant certiorari, vacate the Tenth Circuit's decision, and remand (GVR) *Renteria* for reconsideration in light of the new precedent. 

Given the current composition of the Supreme Court and its recent jurisprudence strongly favoring Free Exercise claims (e.g., *Fulton*, *Carson v. Makin*, *Kennedy*), the probability of the petitioners succeeding in *St. Mary* is very high. Consequently, the ultimate disposition for this petition is highly likely to be a GVR. According to the schema, a GVR is modeled as a grant (`granted = 1`).

I estimate the combined probability of a hold followed by a GVR to be approximately 85%.

**Stakes/Significance:**
Because this case is functionally a tag-along to *St. Mary*, its independent significance is lower. The major doctrinal shift will occur in *St. Mary*, while *Renteria* will simply be a vehicle for the application of the new rule on remand. Therefore, the `big_case_score` is set to 0.4.
