# Prediction: denied

I predict denial of certiorari, with a 0.27 probability that the petition is granted. This is materially above the ordinary modern cert-petition base rate because the Court requested the views of the Solicitor General and, according to the July 11 snapshot, the case remained pending after the United States filed and the petition was distributed for the June 25 conference. The prediction remains denial because the record presents substantial vehicle problems and no clean, mature conflict among federal courts of appeals.

## Questions and governing standard

The provisioned questions-presented section and petition raise three Free Exercise questions and one ACA-preemption question. The religious-liberty issues concern whether neutrality depends on proof of subjective animus, whether general applicability turns on the purposes of secular comparators rather than the risks they pose to the government's interest, and whether official hostility independently establishes a violation. The preemption issue asks whether the ACA's definition and treatment of health care sharing ministries displaces New Mexico's application of its insurance code.

At the cert stage, the relevant standard is Supreme Court Rule 10, not whether petitioners merely have a plausible merits argument. The strongest cert considerations would be a square circuit conflict, an important recurring federal question, or a serious departure from Supreme Court precedent. Four Justices must vote to grant.

On the merits, the petition invokes *Employment Division v. Smith*, *Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye*, *Masterpiece Cakeshop*, *Fulton*, *Tandon*, and *Catholic Charities*. Its best point is that *Tandon* describes comparability by reference to risks posed to the asserted government interest, while the Tenth Circuit emphasized the different purposes of HCSMs and exempt fraternal or labor organizations. It also identifies statements by New Mexico officials and litigation counsel that could be characterized as dismissive of petitioners' beliefs. The respondents' brief answers that the insurance rules apply without regard to religion, the public statements addressed consumer risk rather than faith, and there is no record evidence that exempt secular entities engaged in comparable conduct.

The preemption theory is less certworthy on this record. The petition argues that treating Gospel Light as insurance makes compliance with federal HCSM and nonprofit conditions impossible. The opposition responds that the ACA exempts qualifying individuals from a federal mandate but does not expressly immunize an entity that makes enforceable insurance contracts from state regulation. The hearing officer's undisputed finding that Gospel Light made legally enforceable promises to pay medical expenses gives the State a fact-bound answer and makes the case a poor vehicle for resolving the broader federal question.

## Case-specific grant signals

Several docket facts materially increase the chance of review:

- The Court requested a response after an initial waiver.
- After the first conference, the Court invited the Solicitor General to file a brief. A CVSG is a rare, strong signal of institutional interest.
- The United States filed on May 26, 2026; petitioners filed a supplemental brief; and the matter was distributed for the June 25 conference. The July 11 snapshot records neither a grant nor a denial. Continued pendency after conference can indicate a relist or hold.
- The panel was divided, with Judge Carson dissenting, and religious-neutrality doctrine is important to the current Court.

The provisioned record does not contain the United States' brief, so I do not infer its recommendation as a fact. That missing recommendation is the largest source of uncertainty because the Court often gives it substantial weight after a CVSG.

## Reasons denial remains more likely

The respondents' vehicle objections are substantial. The decision below is unpublished and reviews denial of a preliminary injunction. The record is undeveloped on whether secular organizations actually conduct comparable medical-cost-sharing activity, whether applying the insurance code substantially burdens these two petitioners, and how Gospel Light would respond to regulation. Gospel Light itself is not a petitioner in the Supreme Court. The petition also packages four questions spanning neutrality, general applicability, official hostility, and statutory preemption, increasing the likelihood that the Court will await a cleaner case.

The claimed split is contestable. The opposition plausibly reads the cited Second and Third Circuit cases as considering both intent and objective operation, rather than adopting an intent-only neutrality test. CourtListener searches surfaced little appellate HCSM/free-exercise authority. The closest retrieved recent analogue, *Zion HealthShare, Inc. v. Office of the Insurance Commissioner* (Washington Court of Appeals, Feb. 5, 2026), upheld treatment of a sharing ministry as an insurer and rejected related constitutional challenges. That published state decision shows the issue is current, but it does not create a federal circuit split and its facts and state-law analysis differ.

## Calibration

The committed statpack reports that, among its limited resolved sample of modern discretionary-cert dockets, 9 of 190 were granted; for the 2025 Term sample, 5.6% were granted and 91.3% denied. Those data are incomplete and not conditioned on a CVSG, so I use them only as a starting point. The CVSG, responsive briefing, and apparent post-conference pendency justify a large upward adjustment. The interlocutory posture, undeveloped facts, weak conflict, and broad set of questions bring the final grant probability down to 0.27. Denial is therefore the modal disposition.
