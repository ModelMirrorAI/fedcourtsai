# Prediction: GVR (71% probability of any grant)

## Question and posture

Breanna Renteria and Laura Smith are members of Gospel Light Mennonite Church Medical Aid Plan, a health care sharing ministry (HCSM). They challenge New Mexico's application of its insurance code to Gospel Light. The petition presents Free Exercise questions about neutrality, general applicability, religious hostility, and the comparison of religious conduct with exempt secular conduct. It also asks whether the Affordable Care Act and federal tax law preempt state requirements that allegedly prevent Gospel Light from retaining the features federal law requires of an HCSM.

The case reaches the Court after an unpublished Tenth Circuit decision affirming denial of a preliminary injunction. That posture, the incomplete factual record, ongoing state litigation involving Gospel Light, the absence of a developed circuit conflict on HCSM preemption, and partial preservation problems all weigh against plenary review.

## Governing standards and record

Under *Employment Division v. Smith*, a neutral and generally applicable law ordinarily receives rational-basis review even when it burdens religious exercise. *Lukumi*, *Fulton*, and related cases make laws non-general when they treat comparable secular conduct more favorably, with comparability judged against the government's asserted interest; official hostility can also defeat neutrality. On certiorari, the Court ordinarily looks for an important federal question, a genuine conflict, and a clean vehicle rather than correcting a factbound interlocutory error.

The provisioned petition argues that the Tenth Circuit improperly demanded subjective animus, compared the purposes of secular and religious organizations rather than the risks their conduct posed, and discounted state officials' hostile statements. The provisioned brief in opposition responds that the insurance code distinguishes entities by their activities rather than religion, that the supposed split is illusory, and that the preliminary-injunction record does not establish comparable secular conduct, a substantial religious burden, or irreparable harm. On preemption, the opposition emphasizes traditional state authority over insurance and argues that federal HCSM recognition does not authorize an entity to sell insurance free of state regulation.

The docket snapshot supplies unusually strong selection signals. The Court requested a response after waiver, invited the Solicitor General's views on October 14, 2025, received the United States' brief on May 26, 2026, and distributed the case for the June 25 conference. The paid petition remained unresolved in the July 17 snapshot.

Most importantly, the retrieved CVSG brief recommends that the Court hold this petition for *St. Mary Catholic Parish v. Roy*, No. 25-581, granted April 20, 2026, and then dispose of it as appropriate. The government explains that *St. Mary* concerns whether lack of general applicability requires categorical exemptions for identical secular conduct and uses reasoning similar to the Tenth Circuit's analysis here. It recommends no review of the separate, factbound preemption issue. Petitioners' retrieved supplemental brief accepts a hold as the fallback to an immediate grant. The lack of a disposition following the June conference is consistent with, though it does not itself prove, a hold.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack puts the modern cert grant rate near 3%, the Term 2025 paid-petition rate at about 5.4%, and the historical CVSG-bucket grant rate at 27.1%. This case deserves a much larger adjustment than the generic CVSG bucket because the Solicitor General specifically recommends a hold for an already-granted lead case rather than denial. The Court's recent Free Exercise doctrine and its decision to grant both questions in *St. Mary* make it more likely than not that the lead decision will disturb or clarify the general-applicability reasoning used below.

The likeliest eventual labeled action is therefore a grant, vacate, and remand in light of *St. Mary*, not a plenary grant now. Denial remains meaningful because *St. Mary* could be resolved on a ground that does not affect this case, could leave the relevant Tenth Circuit analysis intact, or could yield no result favorable to these petitioners. The preemption question independently has weak vehicle and conflict characteristics. I assign 71% to any grant (principally a later GVR) and predict `gvr`; the remaining probability is principally denial.

No individual Justice votes are predicted because a hold and later summary disposition do not provide a sound basis for a nine-Justice vote allocation.

## Significance

The case has substantial potential stakes for Free Exercise doctrine and for religious HCSMs operating across state lines. Its standalone importance is moderated by its preliminary-injunction posture, factual disputes, and the Court's use of *St. Mary* as the cleaner lead vehicle, supporting a big-case score of 0.68.
