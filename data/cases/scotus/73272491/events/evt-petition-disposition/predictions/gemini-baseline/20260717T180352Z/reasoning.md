## Legal Question and Standard
The petition asks the Court to overrule *Libretti v. United States*, 516 U.S. 29 (1995), a pre-*Apprendi* decision holding that the Sixth Amendment right to a jury trial does not apply to criminal forfeitability. The petitioner argues that under *Apprendi* and *Southern Union Co. v. United States* (which applied *Apprendi* to criminal fines), a jury must find the facts that dictate the maximum criminal forfeiture amount beyond a reasonable doubt. 

## Facts from the Snapshot
Martin Mizrahi was convicted by a general jury verdict of various crimes including money laundering. The jury instructions allowed conviction if the funds involved were derived from bank fraud, wire fraud, or drug trafficking, but did not require the jury to specify the source. At sentencing, the district judge found by a preponderance of the evidence that over $3.8 million in drug trafficking proceeds and $690k in fraud proceeds were involved, resulting in a forfeiture order of over $4.5 million. The defense preserved an *Apprendi* objection. The Second Circuit affirmed, expressly relying on *Libretti* and noting it was bound by it unless the Supreme Court explicitly overrules it.

The petition was filed on April 27, 2026. The government waived its right to respond. On June 15, 2026, three days before its scheduled conference, the Court requested a response (due August 14). The petition is supported by strong amici, including Former Federal Judges and the Washington Legal Foundation.

## Reasoning
The base rate for a cert grant is extremely low (~3%), but this case presents a set of signals that substantially elevate its grant probability. First, there is a recognized anomaly in constitutional law: *Libretti* stands in deep tension with the Court's modern Sixth Amendment jurisprudence (*Apprendi*, *Southern Union*, *Erlinger*). Lower courts consistently apply *Libretti* because of the *Agostini* doctrine, explicitly inviting the Supreme Court to resolve the conflict. 

Second, the Court's call for a response (CFR) after a government waiver is a strong indicator of interest from at least one Justice. Cases with a CFR are granted at a significantly higher rate than the baseline. 

Third, the vehicle appears clean. The district judge made a preponderance finding on the amount and source of the funds because the jury's general verdict did not specify the predicate for the money laundering conviction, directly implicating the *Apprendi* issue. Furthermore, the large financial penalty underscores the practical stakes. 

Despite these strong signals, the modal outcome for any cert petition—even with a CFR and strong amici—remains a denial. The government's forthcoming brief in opposition may identify vehicle problems (e.g., harmless error arguments based on trial evidence). Therefore, while the probability of a grant is elevated significantly above the base rate to 40%, the most likely single disposition remains a denial.
