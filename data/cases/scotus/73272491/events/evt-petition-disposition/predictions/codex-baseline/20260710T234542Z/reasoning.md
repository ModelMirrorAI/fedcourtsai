# Prediction: denied

I predict denial, with an 18% probability of a grant. That is substantially above the ordinary certiorari baseline because the Court requested a response after the United States waived one, but denial remains more likely.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether *Libretti v. United States*, 516 U.S. 29 (1995), should be overruled insofar as it says there is no constitutional jury right as to criminal forfeiture. The petition argues that *Apprendi* and *Southern Union* require a jury to find beyond a reasonable doubt facts fixing the permissible forfeiture amount, because punitive forfeiture is constitutionally a fine.

At the certiorari stage, the practical question is whether at least four Justices will regard the issue as nationally important, cleanly presented, and ripe for review. The absence of a circuit conflict usually weighs heavily against review, although it is less decisive where lower courts uniformly follow directly controlling Supreme Court precedent and cannot create a split.

## Record and vehicle

The April 27 petition and its questions-presented section show a concrete, preserved dispute. A jury convicted Martin Mizrahi of money laundering through a general verdict that did not identify which alleged source of funds supported the verdict. The district judge then found by a preponderance that $690,000 was associated with bank-fraud proceeds and roughly $3.856 million with narcotics-trafficking proceeds, ordering total forfeiture of $4,545,704. Defense counsel made the Fifth and Sixth Amendment objection, and the Second Circuit rejected it under circuit precedent applying *Libretti*. The asserted error therefore affected a large, identifiable punishment and was preserved below.

The vehicle is comparatively strong. The petition presents a pure constitutional rule, identifies uniform adherence to *Libretti* across multiple circuits, and explains why only this Court can permit further doctrinal movement. Its reliance on the post-*Apprendi* treatment of fines is substantial, and the petition cites recent expressions of interest in jury findings for monetary punishment. The docket also shows two amicus filings and, more importantly, a June 15 request for a government response after an initial waiver. That procedural step indicates that at least one Justice saw enough potential merit to require adversarial presentation.

The weaknesses still predominate. There is no lower-court conflict; the petition explicitly asks the Court to overrule precedent; and the cited separate writings visibly establish interest from fewer than the four votes required for certiorari. The government may also argue that forfeiture is an indeterminate sentencing consequence rather than a punishment with an *Apprendi* maximum, that *Libretti* remains controlling, or that features of the general verdict and underlying convictions complicate relief. The Second Circuit decision is unpublished. Finally, as of the July 10 snapshot, the requested response was not yet due and no brief in opposition was provisioned, so the government's vehicle and merits arguments cannot be weighed.

## Calibration

The committed statpack reports a 1.4% grant share among its small set of resolved SCOTUS records, but it does not contain the prompt-described modern discretionary-cert cut and has no resolved observations for recent Terms or Second Circuit-origin petitions. I therefore treated that figure only as a broad reminder that grants are rare, not as a calibrated denominator. The response request and the unusually clean, recurring constitutional question justify raising the estimate well above that broad baseline, while the lack of a split and the burden of overruling *Libretti* keep denial substantially more likely.

I do not predict individual votes. Certiorari votes are not disclosed in the provisioned record, and assigning Justice-specific votes would add unsupported precision.
