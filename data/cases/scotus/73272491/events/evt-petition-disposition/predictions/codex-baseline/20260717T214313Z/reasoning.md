# Prediction

I predict denial, with an 18% probability of any grant. That is substantially above the ordinary paid cert-petition baseline because the Court requested a response after the United States waived one, but it remains below even odds because the petition lacks a circuit conflict and presents a meaningful vehicle concern.

## Question and governing standard

The petition asks whether *Libretti v. United States*, 516 U.S. 29 (1995), should be overruled insofar as it says that the Sixth Amendment does not protect a right to a jury verdict on criminal forfeitability. The claimed doctrinal conflict is with *Apprendi v. New Jersey*, 530 U.S. 466 (2000), and *Southern Union Co. v. United States*, 567 U.S. 343 (2012): facts that increase criminal punishment beyond the maximum supported by a verdict ordinarily must be found by a jury beyond a reasonable doubt. The petition also relies on *Erlinger v. United States*, 602 U.S. 821 (2024), and the then-recent description in *Ellingburg v. United States* of forfeiture under 18 U.S.C. § 982(a)(1) as criminal punishment.

Certiorari normally requires four Justices and is favored when a case presents an important recurring federal question, a conflict among lower courts, or a need to reconsider Supreme Court precedent. The Court can grant without a split when its own precedent prevents lower-court disagreement, but the lack of disagreement still reduces the likelihood of review.

## Record-specific considerations

The petition and snapshot show that a jury convicted Martin Mizrahi of money laundering based on a general verdict that did not identify which alleged source of funds—bank fraud, wire fraud, or narcotics trafficking—it accepted. The district court then found by a preponderance that $690,000 in bank-fraud proceeds and approximately $3.86 million in narcotics proceeds were involved, producing a $4,545,704 forfeiture order. The Second Circuit affirmed under *Libretti* and circuit precedent.

The case has several grant-positive features:

- The constitutional issue was expressly preserved and decided below.
- The amount and the difference between the jury's general verdict and the judge-found narcotics amount make the question concrete.
- The petition identifies a recurring federal practice with substantial financial consequences.
- Two amicus briefs were filed, including one by former federal judges.
- Most importantly, after the United States waived a response and the petition was distributed, the Court requested a response. That indicates interest from at least one Justice and sharply distinguishes the petition from the ordinary cert pool.
- The petition's doctrinal argument has force: criminal forfeiture is punitive, while *Apprendi* and *Southern Union* emphasize jury findings for facts that fix punishment. Recent decisions such as *Erlinger* make the Court's continued interest in the jury's sentencing role plausible.

The negative considerations remain stronger:

- There is no circuit split. The petition itself says the circuits uniformly continue to apply *Libretti*, and CourtListener research confirmed that the Sixth Circuit's published decision in *United States v. Bradley*, 969 F.3d 585 (6th Cir. 2020), treated *Libretti* as controlling and said the circuits uniformly agree that *Southern Union* did not alter the result.
- The Court has repeatedly denied earlier petitions asking it to revisit *Libretti*. The newer doctrinal developments improve this petition but do not eliminate that history.
- The government has a substantial merits distinction: forfeiture statutes do not state a determinate maximum in the same form as the per-day fine in *Southern Union*. Accepting the petition would therefore require both overruling a Supreme Court precedent and rejecting the circuits' uniform maximum-penalty analysis.
- The trial record says Mizrahi did not request the jury forfeiture determination available under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32.2(b)(5). Although he later preserved the constitutional objection and the Second Circuit reached it, that omission gives the government a waiver, avoidability, or vehicle argument.
- No brief in opposition was provisioned because the Court-requested response was not yet due. The forecast therefore cannot assess whether the government will confess a problem, propose holding the case, or identify additional record defects.

## Calibration and disposition

The committed statpack estimates a 5.36% grant rate for paid petitions in the 2025 Term and a 5.4% grant rate for modern petitions from the Second Circuit. The docket has no CVSG and no completed relist as of the snapshot. I raise the probability considerably for the requested response, amici, preserved recurring question, and post-*Libretti* doctrinal developments, then discount it for uniform lower-court law, prior denials, the indeterminate-maximum argument, and the failure to request a Rule 32.2 jury finding. The resulting forecast is 18% for any grant and 82% against; the most likely realized label is `denied`, not a GVR or dismissal.

I do not predict individual cert votes. The big-case score is 0.58: a ruling could materially change federal criminal-forfeiture procedure, but the dispute is narrower and less publicly salient than the Court's major structural or nationwide policy cases.
