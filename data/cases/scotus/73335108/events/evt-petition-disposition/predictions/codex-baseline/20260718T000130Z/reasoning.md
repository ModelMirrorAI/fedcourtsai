# Prediction: denied

I estimate a 0.2% probability of any grant (including a GVR) and predict denial.

## Record and question

The July 17, 2026 snapshot shows a paid petition from an unpublished Seventh Circuit denial of a certificate of appealability. The respondent waived a response, and the petition was distributed for the September 28, 2026 conference. No brief in opposition was provisioned. I used the complete 27-page petition and its extracted questions presented.

The petition concerns a Wisconsin conviction for negligent handling of a dangerous weapon causing the death of an unintended third party. It says the jury acquitted Watson of recklessly injuring the intended target on self-defense grounds but convicted him on the third-party-death count. Its four questions contend that Wisconsin Jury Instruction 820 and Wis. Stat. § 939.48(3) conflicted; that the State was relieved of its burden to disprove self-defense; that counsel was ineffective; and that the asserted state-law error became a federal due-process violation.

## Certworthiness and vehicle

The petition does not establish a certworthy conflict. It asserts that jurisdictions take different approaches to self-defense burdens, but it does not identify and analyze a concrete split on the same question. Much of the requested review depends on the interaction of one Wisconsin statute, one state jury instruction, and this trial record. The lower federal disposition is an unpublished COA denial, which supplies no developed appellate reasoning for Supreme Court review.

The merits theories also face serious fit problems. *Lockett* concerns consideration of mitigating evidence in capital sentencing, while this is a noncapital guilt-phase case. *Boyde* asks whether there is a reasonable likelihood that jurors understood an instruction in an impermissible way; the differing verdicts alone do not show that the jury disregarded the instruction. *Estelle* makes state-law instructional error noncognizable unless it rendered the trial fundamentally unfair. On habeas review, procedural preservation, exhaustion, AEDPA deference, and the COA posture add vehicle obstacles. The petition's mix of instructional-error, burden-of-proof, ineffective-assistance, statutory-vagueness, and state-law theories further impairs clean review.

The respondent's waiver and the absence, as of the snapshot, of a call for response are powerful negative signals. There is no relist, CVSG, government-party involvement, published conflict, or intervening Supreme Court authority suggesting a GVR. A future call for response remains possible before the conference, but nothing in the current record makes it likely.
