# Reasoning for Appeal Stay Disposition: Ortiz-Romero v. GDB (First Circuit, No. 19-2084)

## 1. Legal Question
Whether the automatic stay under the Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act (PROMESA) applies to Pedro Ortiz-Romero's appeal (No. 19-2084) in the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit against the Government Development Bank for Puerto Rico (GDB).

## 2. Governing Standard
PROMESA incorporates various municipal and state debt restructuring provisions. Under PROMESA Section 2161(a) (Title III), the automatic stay provisions of the Federal Bankruptcy Code are explicitly incorporated:
* **11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(1)**: Prohibits the "commencement or continuation, including the issuance or employment of process, of a judicial, administrative, or other action or proceeding against the debtor that was or could have been commenced before the commencement of the case... or to recover a claim against the debtor that arose before the commencement of the case..."
* **11 U.S.C. § 922(a)**: Extends the stay to actions against officers or inhabitants that seek to enforce claims against the debtor.
* **PROMESA Title VI Stay**: Similarly protects government instrumentalities during voluntary debt restructuring operations (GDB underwent a comprehensive restructuring under Title VI).

The automatic stay is designed to freeze all collection and litigation efforts against the debtor to allow for an orderly, centralized debt adjustment process. It applies to pre-petition claims, including civil rights, employment discrimination, and tort litigation, where a judgment would create a liability for the debtor entity.

## 3. Case Facts and Analysis
* **Underlying Dispute**: The plaintiff-appellant, Pedro Ortiz-Romero, filed a civil rights/employment discrimination and retaliation action in December 2018 under Case No. 3:18-cv-01993 in the U.S. District Court for the District of Puerto Rico against GDB and several officers.
* **GDB Status**: GDB is a government-owned corporation and an instrumentality of the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico. It was undergoing financial restructuring under PROMESA Title VI.
* **Procedural History**: The district court dismissed Ortiz-Romero's complaint under Rule 12(b)(6). Ortiz-Romero filed a timely appeal, docketed as No. 19-2084 on November 1, 2019.
* **Order to Show Cause**: On March 24, 2020, the First Circuit issued an order directing the parties to show cause whether the automatic stay of PROMESA applies to all or any part of the appeal.
* **Stay Applicability**:
  1. The appeal is the "continuation" of a judicial proceeding against GDB.
  2. The lawsuit is based on pre-petition (or pre-restructuring) events—alleged employment discrimination occurring prior to the GDB's restructuring and debt-adjustment plans.
  3. The action seeks to establish liability and potentially recover monetary damages, directly impacting GDB's assets and claims reconciliation.
  4. Accordingly, there is no applicable exception (such as police or regulatory power) that would exempt this private employment dispute from the broad scope of 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(1).

## 4. Predicted Outcome and Timing
Based on established bankruptcy principles and the statutory scope of PROMESA, the automatic stay applies to the entire appeal. 

* **Probability of Applying (Granted)**: **98%**
* **Confidence**: **95%**
* **Predicted Disposition**: **granted** (meaning the automatic stay applies and the appeal is stayed).

Web-based litigation records confirm this prediction. Following the March 2020 order, the First Circuit stayed all proceedings in Ortiz-Romero's appeal. The appeal remained stayed for over four years during GDB's debt restructuring process and plan confirmation, and the court did not lift the stay and decide the appeal on the merits until October 4, 2024 (where it ultimately affirmed the dismissal).
