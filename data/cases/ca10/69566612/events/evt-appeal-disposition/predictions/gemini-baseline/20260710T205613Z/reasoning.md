## Legal Question and Governing Standard

This case involves claims under the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA). The appellant, Ricky Koel, suffered a severe eye injury. He claimed the hospital failed to provide an appropriate medical screening and failed to stabilize his emergency medical condition before discharge. The district court (Judge Teeter) granted summary judgment for the hospital, holding that EMTALA's stabilization requirement is only triggered if the hospital has "actual knowledge" of an emergency medical condition. Because the hospital staff only suspected a ruptured globe but did not definitively diagnose it (and an optometrist did not confirm it), the district court found no actual knowledge, dismissing the state malpractice claims without prejudice.

The legal question on appeal is whether the district court correctly interpreted and applied the EMTALA "actual knowledge" standard at the summary judgment stage. The standard of review for summary judgment is de novo.

## Pre-Decision Facts

* Appellant suffered an eye injury and sought care at Citizens Medical Center.
* Medical staff ordered a CT scan which indicated a possible globe rupture, but it remained a suspected diagnosis rather than a definitive one.
* The patient was discharged with instructions to follow up.
* He underwent emergency surgery the next day but lost vision in the eye.

## Reasoning and Prediction

The 10th Circuit, like most federal courts, strictly construes EMTALA. EMTALA is an anti-dumping statute, not a federal medical malpractice law. To succeed on a failure-to-stabilize claim, the plaintiff must prove that the hospital had actual knowledge of the emergency medical condition. Misdiagnosis or failure to diagnose—even if negligent—does not constitute an EMTALA violation; it is a matter of state medical malpractice law.

Because the record indicates that the hospital staff only suspected a ruptured globe and did not have definitive actual knowledge, Koel's EMTALA claim faces a nearly insurmountable legal hurdle under established circuit precedent. Furthermore, the base rate for an appellant successfully overturning a district court's summary judgment is generally quite low. The 10th Circuit's aggregate base rate shows an overwhelming tendency toward "other" (which includes affirmances), with only a small fraction of cases resulting in outright reversal. 

Given the stringent legal standard for EMTALA stabilization claims requiring actual knowledge and the facts showing an incomplete diagnosis, I predict the 10th Circuit will affirm the district court's grant of summary judgment.
