## Question and governing standards

The event asks for the disposition of the appeal. The limited record indicates that Carl and Marcella Puckett sought appellate review after the district court granted Ain Jeem's voluntary dismissal, dismissed Mr. Puckett's amended counterclaim, and denied attempts to reconsider those rulings.

Appellate jurisdiction is a threshold issue. As the Eleventh Circuit's March 15, 2024 order states, Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4(a)(4)(B)(i) suspended the notice of appeal while the district court had not ruled on the construed post-judgment motions. The district court was directed to decide them before the appeal could proceed. If the court reaches the underlying dismissal ruling, legal conclusions are reviewed without deference, while denial of reconsideration ordinarily receives substantially more deferential review. Reconsideration requires an intervening change in law, new evidence, or a need to correct clear error or manifest injustice; it is not a vehicle to repeat previously rejected arguments.

## Record-based analysis

The strongest signal is the procedural history. The May 24, 2024 district-court order describes the June 29 and July 7, 2023 filings as requests to reconsider earlier denials of reconsideration. It says successive reconsideration requests are improper and finds no new law, new evidence, clear error, or manifest injustice. It also says the challenged orders did not implicate the issues involved in the appeal. Those findings make reversal of the reconsideration ruling unlikely.

The appellate docket reinforces that assessment. The court raised a jurisdictional question, temporarily suspended the notice's effectiveness, and denied the appellant's request to reconsider the resulting panel order. The filings shown in the snapshot are largely procedural and appellant-initiated, including an attempted filing that the clerk said appeared intended for another court. Nothing in the provisioned record supplies a developed merits argument, identifies a legal error in the counterclaim dismissal, or undermines the district court's stated reconsideration standard.

Dismissal remains a meaningful alternative because the record presents notice-of-appeal and finality complications. I nevertheless choose `denied` over `dismissed`: the March 2024 order directed resolution of the pending motions rather than dismissing immediately, and the district court subsequently resolved them, potentially allowing the notice to become effective. If jurisdiction exists, affirmance is much more likely than reversal on this sparse record.

The committed statpack offers only weak calibration: among 45 resolved Eleventh Circuit cases, 95.6% are labeled `other`, 2.2% `denied`, and 2.2% `granted`. That sample is too small and label-heavy to set this case's probability, but it does not justify moving away from the record-specific low reversal estimate.
