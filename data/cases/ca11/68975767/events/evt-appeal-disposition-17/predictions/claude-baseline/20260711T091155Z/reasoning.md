# Ain Jeem, Inc. v. Carl Puckett, Jr. — CA11 No. 23-12267 (evt-appeal-disposition-17)

## The question

Whether the Eleventh Circuit will grant the pro se appellant relief (reverse or
vacate) on his appeal from the Middle District of Florida in a trademark action
(M.D. Fla. No. 8:21-cv-01331), where the appeal — anchored here on the amended
notice of appeal docketed 2023-10-13 — challenges the district court's denials
of the Pucketts' successive motions for reconsideration.

## What the snapshot shows

- **Posture.** Ain Jeem, Inc. sued Carl Puckett, Jr. (and Marcella Puckett) in
  a trademark case (nature of suit 3840). Per the district court's endorsed
  order recited on this docket: the district court granted Ain Jeem's motion to
  voluntarily dismiss its claim against Mr. Puckett (July 6, 2022), denied Mr.
  Puckett's motion for summary judgment and injunctive relief as moot (July 6,
  2022), and dismissed Mr. Puckett's amended counterclaim (Aug. 29, 2022). The
  Pucketts then filed serial motions for reconsideration — including motions to
  reconsider denials of earlier reconsideration motions — and appealed pro se
  (notice of appeal filed 2023-07-10; amended notice 2023-10-13; a companion
  appeal, No. 23-13380, with a consolidation motion pending).
- **Jurisdictional wrinkle, since resolved.** The Eleventh Circuit issued a
  jurisdictional question (appellant responded 2023-11-12) and, on 2024-03-15,
  held the notice of appeal's effectiveness suspended under Fed. R. App. P.
  4(a)(4)(B)(i) because construed Rule 59(e) motions remained pending below,
  directing the district court to rule. The district court did so on 2024-05-24,
  denying both construed motions (docketed here 2024-06-14). That ruling made
  the notice of appeal effective, so outright jurisdictional dismissal became
  much less likely than a merits disposition.
- **The appealed orders and standard of review.** The core targets are denials
  of reconsideration (and reconsideration-of-reconsideration). Denial of a Rule
  59(e)/60(b) motion is reviewed for abuse of discretion, and the district
  court's order rests on settled Middle District and circuit law that successive
  reconsideration motions raising previously available grounds are improper.
  Nothing in the docket suggests an intervening change of law, new evidence, or
  clear error. The appellant is pro se, proceeding IFP (status pending), and the
  panel already denied his motion to reconsider its own 2024-03-15 order
  (2024-05-02), a mild signal of how his arguments are landing.

## Prediction

Affirmance (or a comparable non-relief disposition) is the overwhelmingly
likely outcome: the appellant must show abuse of discretion in refusing to
revisit orders he had already asked the court to revisit, against black-letter
law that successive reconsideration motions are improper, with no indication
of new evidence or changed law. I put **P(granted) = 0.03** — slightly above
zero to account for the pro se-liberal-construction possibility that some
sliver of the appeal (e.g., the counterclaim dismissal reached through a
